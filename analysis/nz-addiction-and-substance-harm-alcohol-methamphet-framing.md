# NZ addiction and substance harm is a burden-and-delivery problem, not one single drug crisis

Discovery framing for stream root #441 (Part of #441). Tests the root's claims about alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling harm against current public evidence, then proposes the child research issues the runner should open.

## Executive answer

- **Alcohol is the biggest measured burden, but the OECD claim needs correction.** New Zealand's recorded alcohol consumption is **not** among the highest in the OECD on the latest OECD country note: OECD reports New Zealand at 8.2 litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+ versus an OECD average of 8.5 litres, while the 2025 Stats NZ release shows the domestic litres-of-alcohol-per-person 15+ series fell to 7.19 litres in the year ended December 2025 [OECD Health at a Glance 2025: New Zealand](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html); [Stats NZ alcohol available for consumption, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025/). The harm burden is still large: Ministry-commissioned NZIER work estimates $6.0b-$9.1b in 2023 societal alcohol harm, and the 2024/25 New Zealand Health Survey found 16.6% of adults had hazardous drinking patterns [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Health, NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey).
- **Methamphetamine is the fast-moving acute-risk strand.** Police wastewater data for Q1 2026 estimated 36.6 kg of methamphetamine consumed per week across sample sites, 15% above the previous four-quarter average, with an estimated weekly social harm cost of $38.4m [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026). The Ministry of Justice says wastewater meth levels doubled across the country in 2024 and gives a conservative 2024 social-harm estimate of $1.5b, while the 2023 Drug Harm Index estimated total methamphetamine harm at $864m and warned that hospitalisation-based harm may miss real harm [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Ministry of Health, 2023 Drug Harm Index](https://www.health.govt.nz/news/2023-drug-harm-index-published).
- **Gambling harm is significant and newly measurable, but it is not just "problem gamblers".** DIA reports gamblers lost $2.792b across TAB, Lotto, class 4 machines and casinos in 2023/24, and the Ministry of Health says the 2024 New Zealand Gambling Survey estimated about 359,000 people aged 16+ were at some risk of harm from gambling and about 381,000 adults experienced at least one wider-family or household gambling harm [DIA gambling expenditure](https://www.dia.govt.nz/gambling-statistics-expenditure); [Ministry of Health, measuring gambling harm](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/research/gambling-harm-research-and-evaluation/measuring-gambling-harm). A 2025 Ministry-commissioned economic and social costing report uses 2023/24 NZ Gambling Survey data and estimates 5.93% of adults were low-risk gamblers, 1.94% moderate-risk and 0.47% problem gamblers, while also warning that children, cultural impacts and unreported family/financial crime are likely undercounted [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf).
- **The common thread is delivery capacity and targeting, not lack of any policy at all.** Current government activity includes a 2026-2029 Substance Harm Action Plan, new mental health and addiction access targets, a methamphetamine ministerial action plan, a 2025/26-2027/28 gambling harm strategy funded through the problem-gambling levy, and the Online Casino Gambling Act 2026 implementation programme [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health, mental health and addiction targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/mental-health-and-addiction-targets); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728); [DIA, Online Casino Gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling). The stream should therefore ask what burdens are comparable, which interventions have the strongest evidence, and where NZ's existing prevention/treatment/enforcement machinery fails to reach the people and places with the highest harm.

**Overall confidence:** Medium-High for the burden baseline, because the headline alcohol, wastewater, gambling-expenditure and strategy claims are from official or Ministry-commissioned sources; Medium for cross-domain comparison and intervention transferability, because the sources use different methods, some costs overlap or are explicitly conservative, and comparable OECD "harm rate" data were not found during framing.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** New Zealand's estimated resident population was 5,361,300 at 31 March 2026 [Stats NZ population indicator](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/); 74.9% of adults drank alcohol in the 12 months before the 2024/25 NZ Health Survey and 16.6% were classified as hazardous drinkers [Ministry of Health, NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey); the Ministry-commissioned NZIER alcohol cost estimate is $6.0b-$9.1b for 2023 and explicitly warns that categories such as FASD, productivity, health and justice costs can overlap [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); Q1 2026 Police wastewater data estimated 36.6 kg/week methamphetamine consumption and $38.4m/week social harm across sample sites [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026); DIA's 2023/24 gambling expenditure total was $2.792b [DIA gambling expenditure](https://www.dia.govt.nz/gambling-statistics-expenditure); and the 2025-2028 gambling harm strategy took effect on 1 July 2025 [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728).

**What is interpretation:** This framing argues that alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling should be researched through a shared "harm burden + intervention delivery" lens rather than as one generic addiction category. That synthesis follows from the different evidence bases: alcohol has the largest health-and-productivity cost estimate, methamphetamine has the sharpest recent wastewater escalation and organised-crime response, and gambling has a regulated-expenditure/social-cost evidence base with online-market change underway [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf); [DIA, Online Casino Gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling).

**Main limits:** This is a framing analysis, not the child research itself. It did not calculate a common percentage-of-GDP burden because the strongest public estimates use different denominators, include overlapping categories, and sometimes mix cost-of-illness, social-harm and lost-expenditure concepts [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Health, 2023 Drug Harm Index](https://www.health.govt.nz/news/2023-drug-harm-index-published); [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf). It did not find a current official Labour policy package matching National-led 2025-2026 government actions, so the child policy issue should distinguish current Government policy, previous Labour-led legislative reforms, and present opposition/party positions rather than imply symmetry [Beehive, Sale and Supply of Alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/). It also did not include lived-experience interviews, unpublished treatment-outcome data, police case files, or identifiable service-user information; those would require human governance and consent before G1.

## Problem statement

New Zealand has a large, uneven addiction and substance-harm burden across alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling, but the public evidence does not support treating these harms as a single undifferentiated "addiction crisis" [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health, measuring gambling harm](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/research/gambling-harm-research-and-evaluation/measuring-gambling-harm). Alcohol is legal, widely used and socially normalised, with 74.9% of adults drinking in the past year and an NZIER societal-harm estimate of $6.0b-$9.1b for 2023 [Ministry of Health, NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey); [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research). Methamphetamine is less prevalent in survey data but creates concentrated acute harm, with Q1 2026 wastewater estimates implying $38.4m/week social harm across sample sites and a justice response focused on transnational supply, enforcement and demand reduction [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/). Gambling is a regulated and partially revenue-returning activity where $2.792b was lost by gamblers in 2023/24, while Ministry material says about 359,000 people aged 16+ were at some degree of gambling-harm risk in 2024 [DIA gambling expenditure](https://www.dia.govt.nz/gambling-statistics-expenditure); [Ministry of Health, measuring gambling harm](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/research/gambling-harm-research-and-evaluation/measuring-gambling-harm).

The stream should establish the real comparative burden, identify which harms fall on whom, and test whether existing prevention, treatment, enforcement and regulation are matched to the strongest available evidence [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728). The immediate audience is a small volunteer project deciding whether there is a tractable intervention to build or support after G1; this means downstream research should privilege public, decision-useful evidence over broad moral arguments about prohibition or personal responsibility [docs/STREAMS.md](../docs/STREAMS.md).

## Who is affected

**The whole population is exposed, but not evenly.** New Zealand had an estimated 5.36 million residents at 31 March 2026, and all three harm domains affect people beyond the individual user or gambler through family, whānau, community, justice, health and productivity pathways [Stats NZ population indicator](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/); [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Health, measuring gambling harm](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/research/gambling-harm-research-and-evaluation/measuring-gambling-harm). The 2026-2029 Substance Harm Action Plan says about one in four New Zealanders use substances in a way that increases their risk of experiencing harm, and that substance harm is intertwined with housing instability, low income and coexisting mental or physical health conditions [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029).

**Alcohol harms people who do not meet an alcohol-use-disorder threshold.** NZIER estimated $3.1b of the 2023 alcohol societal-harm total came from non-disordered alcohol use, and its specific estimates include $281m for intimate partner violence attributable to alcohol use disorder, $74m for child maltreatment attributable to hazardous drinking, $2.1b for road crashes where alcohol was a factor, and nearly $4b in productivity losses [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research). Health NZ-published alcohol-burden work reported 901 deaths, 1,250 cancers, 29,282 hospitalisations and 128,963 ACC claims attributable to alcohol in 2018, and the University of Otago summary says the age- and sex-standardised alcohol-attributable mortality rate was twice as high for Māori as non-Māori [University of Otago summary of Health NZ-published alcohol burden report](https://www.otago.ac.nz/news/newsroom/health-burden-of-alcohol-highlighted-in-report).

**Methamphetamine harm is concentrated but escalating.** The 2021-2023 NZ Health Survey mental-health/substance-use module found moderate or high risk of problematic amphetamine-type stimulant use rose from 1.2% in 2016/17 to 1.8% in 2021-2023, while moderate or high risk of problematic illicit-substance use rose from 10.1% to 11.2% [Ministry of Health, Mental Health and Problematic Substance Use NZHS report](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-06/mental_health_and_problematic_substance_use_report_-_final.pdf). Police wastewater and Ministry of Justice sources indicate that volume is the sharper warning signal than self-report prevalence: wastewater meth levels doubled in 2024, remained elevated through 2025 and Q1 2026, and are treated by government as linked to organised crime and community harm [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026).

**Gambling harm extends to households and communities.** The Ministry of Health says gambling harm includes relationship damage, emotional and psychological distress, disruptions to work or study, loss of income, the financial cost of gambling, fraud and related crimes, and harms to family, whānau and community [Ministry of Health, measuring gambling harm](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/research/gambling-harm-research-and-evaluation/measuring-gambling-harm). The 2025 Ministry-commissioned gambling costing report says Māori and Pacific peoples bear a disproportionate burden of gambling-harm costs based on problem-prevalence data, while also warning that the knowledge base is not strong enough to quantify unique cultural interactions with harm and that children's harms are likely underestimated [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf).

## What's already being done

**Substance harm and addiction services.** The Action Plan to Prevent and Reduce Substance Harm 2026-2029 was published on 18 March 2026 and frames the health response around prevention and early intervention, access to treatment, workforce growth, and system effectiveness [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029). The same plan identifies existing services and supports including school-based education, needle exchange, free drug checking, and specialist community and residential treatment services [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan PDF](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/action-plan-prevent-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029.pdf). The Ministry's mental health and addiction targets include seeing 80% of people accessing specialist mental health and addiction services within three weeks and 80% of people accessing primary mental health and addiction services through Access and Choice within one week [Ministry of Health, mental health and addiction targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/mental-health-and-addiction-targets).

**Alcohol harm reduction.** Cabinet increased the alcohol levy from about $11.5m a year to about $16.6m a year from 1 July 2024 after an independent review found the levy had not kept pace with inflation and had not increased since 2009 [Ministry of Health, independent alcohol levy review Stage 2](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/independent-review-of-the-alcohol-levy-stage-2). The Labour-led Government passed the Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Community Participation) Amendment Bill in August 2023 to remove appeals against provisional local alcohol policies, remove cross-examination at licensing hearings, broaden who can object to licence applications, and extend objection timeframes [Beehive, Sale and Supply of Alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws). Internationally, price policy is a live intervention candidate: Public Health Scotland's 2023 evaluation said minimum unit pricing reduced alcohol-attributable deaths by an estimated 13.4% and hospital admissions by 4.1%, and OECD's harmful-alcohol-use work models pricing and other prevention packages as economically material interventions [Public Health Scotland, MUP evaluation summary](https://publichealthscotland.scot/news/2023/june/minimum-unit-pricing-reduces-alcohol-related-harm-to-health/); [OECD, Preventing Harmful Alcohol Use](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preventing-harmful-alcohol-use_6e4b4ffb-en.html).

**Methamphetamine response.** The Ministry of Justice says the Prime Minister tasked a Methamphetamine Ministerial Group in March 2025, and the resulting action plan combines international supply disruption, enforcement changes, proceeds-of-crime recovery, a nationwide public-awareness campaign, funding to increase services in communities hardest hit by meth, and $23.1m in October 2025 for offshore liaison, money-laundering capability and the Resilience to Organised Crime in Communities programme [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/). The treatment evidence base is not as clean as opioid substitution: a systematic review of reviews found contingency management and some behavioural interventions have evidence for stimulant-use disorder, while pharmacotherapies remain mixed, and a Finland/New Zealand methylphenidate substitution trial exists but is not a settled prescription pathway for methamphetamine dependence [AshaRani et al., stimulant-use-disorder review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7302911/); [PubMed, methylphenidate substitution trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23297867/).

**Gambling harm prevention and regulation.** The Ministry of Health's 2025/26-2027/28 gambling harm strategy is an integrated public-health strategy under the Gambling Act 2003, is funded by the problem-gambling levy, and sets out prevention, intervention, workforce, research and evaluation activity for the three years from 1 July 2025 [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728). Its strategy PDF allocates $5.035m over three years to research and evaluation and says the Ministry will develop a monitoring framework with performance and impact measures [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy PDF](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-09/strategy-prevent-minimise-gambling-harm-2025-26-2027-28-online-v2.pdf). The Online Casino Gambling Act 2026 is now in force, implementation is phased and not expected to be fully operational until 2027, and DIA says the regime is intended to protect consumers, reduce gambling harm, prevent crime and dishonesty, and bring overseas online casino operators under clear rules [DIA, Online Casino Gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling). DIA provider guidance updated 2 July 2026 says up to 15 online casino licences will be available, an auction is expected in September 2026, licence applications are expected in October 2026, operating restrictions start on 1 December 2026, and the licensed regime is expected to be fully operational in 2027 [DIA, online gambling provider guidance](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **research: What is the comparable economic burden of alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling harm in New Zealand?**  
   This should reconcile NZIER alcohol costs, the Drug Harm Index/wastewater-derived meth harm estimates, DIA gambling expenditure, and the 2025 gambling social-cost report without double-counting incompatible concepts [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Health, 2023 Drug Harm Index](https://www.health.govt.nz/news/2023-drug-harm-index-published); [DIA gambling expenditure](https://www.dia.govt.nz/gambling-statistics-expenditure); [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf). It should report health, justice, productivity, family/whānau and fiscal channels separately, and calculate percentages of GDP only where the numerator and denominator are defensible [Stats NZ GDP data via CSV catalogue](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/).

2. **research: How does New Zealand alcohol consumption and alcohol-attributable harm compare with OECD, Australia and UK benchmarks?**  
   This should test the corrected OECD claim by separating consumption, heavy episodic/hazardous drinking, alcohol-attributable mortality/hospitalisation, and policy settings rather than using one "harm" proxy [OECD Health at a Glance 2025: New Zealand](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html); [Ministry of Health, NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey). Start with OECD, WHO, Stats NZ, NZ Health Survey, Health NZ alcohol-attributable burden work, Australia's AIHW, and UK/Scotland alcohol-harm sources [Stats NZ alcohol available for consumption, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025/); [University of Otago summary of Health NZ-published alcohol burden report](https://www.otago.ac.nz/news/newsroom/health-burden-of-alcohol-highlighted-in-report).

3. **research: What is the current methamphetamine situation in New Zealand, and what can public data actually say about organised crime and community harm?**  
   This should combine wastewater trends, survey prevalence, Drug Harm Index estimates, Customs/Police/Justice supply indicators, treatment demand and regional inequities, while explicitly distinguishing evidence of association from causal claims about crime [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Ministry of Health, Mental Health and Problematic Substance Use NZHS report](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-06/mental_health_and_problematic_substance_use_report_-_final.pdf). It should say whether 2024-2026 is a step-change or a temporary market spike, and what would prove that from public sources [NZ Police wastewater Q1 2026](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026).

4. **research: Which interventions have the strongest evidence for reducing alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling harm, and which are realistic in New Zealand?**  
   This should compare population-level alcohol pricing/availability controls, local alcohol policies, screening and brief interventions, methamphetamine treatment models and contingency management, potential stimulant prescribing pathways, drug checking and overdose prevention, gambling harm-minimisation controls, host responsibility, self-exclusion, online-casino licensing and advertising rules [Public Health Scotland, MUP evaluation summary](https://publichealthscotland.scot/news/2023/june/minimum-unit-pricing-reduces-alcohol-related-harm-to-health/); [Beehive, Sale and Supply of Alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [AshaRani et al., stimulant-use-disorder review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7302911/); [DIA, Online Casino Gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling). It should grade evidence separately for effectiveness, equity, implementation cost, political feasibility and time-to-first-useful-result [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728).

5. **research: What is the current New Zealand policy and delivery landscape across government, opposition positions, iwi/Māori and community providers, and local government?**  
   This should map current National-led government actions, previous Labour-led alcohol reforms, any current Labour and minor-party positions that can be verified, and the roles of Health NZ, Ministry of Health, Justice, Police, Customs, DIA, councils, gambling harm services, alcohol harm advocates, drug-checking providers and kaupapa Māori services [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Beehive, Sale and Supply of Alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728); [DIA, online gambling provider guidance](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers). It should identify which delivery gaps are solvable by a small volunteer project and which require legislative authority, clinical governance, lived-experience co-design, or Crown/Māori partnership before the stream can responsibly move past G1 [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current comparable OECD alcohol-attributable harm table showing New Zealand near the top of the OECD would revise the corrected alcohol benchmark; framing found OECD consumption comparators and NZ harm estimates, but not a current cross-OECD harm-rate table [OECD Health at a Glance 2025: New Zealand](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html); [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research).
- A validated common accounting framework that converts alcohol, methamphetamine and gambling harm into non-overlapping GDP, fiscal, health and productivity shares would change the economic burden ranking, because present public estimates are not directly additive [Ministry of Health, alcohol-harm costs](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/costs-of-alcohol-harms-in-new-zealand-updating-the-evidence-with-recent-research); [Ministry of Health, 2023 Drug Harm Index](https://www.health.govt.nz/news/2023-drug-harm-index-published); [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf).
- Public treatment-outcome data showing NZ methamphetamine services reliably reduce use, crime, family harm or hospitalisation at scale would shift the meth focus from "what works and where is access missing" to "how to scale known effective delivery" [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [AshaRani et al., stimulant-use-disorder review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7302911/).
- Evidence that the Online Casino Gambling Act 2026 implementation measurably reduces harm without shifting people to unlicensed platforms would change the gambling intervention question after 2027, because the licensed regime is not expected to be fully operational until 2027 [DIA, Online Casino Gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling); [DIA, online gambling provider guidance](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers).
- Lived-experience, Māori health, Pacific health, clinical, frontline-police and gambling-harm provider input could materially change prioritisation, because public aggregate data undercount cultural harms, family impacts and informal recovery/support pathways [Browne et al., gambling social costing 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/browne-et-al-economic-social-costing-of-gambling.pdf); [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029).
