# NZ's China exposure is real, material, and sector-specific - not a single export dependency

Discovery framing for stream root #438 (Part of #438). The root asks whether New Zealand is economically exposed to China through exports, education, and tourism, and what a deterioration in China-facing demand would mean [Issue #438](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/438).

## Executive answer

- **The corrected problem:** China is New Zealand's largest export destination, but the exposure is not evenly spread across "agriculture" or all services. In the March 2026 quarter, China took about **20.3% of total goods and services exports** ($6.381b of $31.363b), and in the year ended May 2026 it took about **24.1% of merchandise exports** ($19.885b of $82.665b), calculated from Stats NZ's international trade and overseas merchandise trade workbooks [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx); [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx).
- **The sector pattern is uneven:** in the March 2026 quarter, China accounted for about **36.3%** of NZ exports of milk powder, butter and cheese, **60.6%** of logs, wood and wood articles, **27.6%** of fruit, and **18.7%** of meat and edible offal, calculated from Stats NZ's top export-by-destination table [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx). The root's dairy and forestry premise is strongly supported; the meat premise needs correction because the United States was a larger meat/offal destination than China in that quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).
- **Education exposure is current, not just pre-pandemic:** Education New Zealand reported 92,580 international students enrolled with NZ providers in 2025, with China and India the top two source countries at 34% and 14% of enrolments respectively [Education New Zealand, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). The government also has an International Education Going for Growth Plan to double the sector's economic contribution from $3.6b in 2024 to $7.2b by 2034 [Ministry of Education](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/tertiary-and-further-education/international-education).
- **Tourism is important but the "top source" wording is wrong:** Stats NZ reported 3.63m overseas visitor arrivals in the year ended March 2026, including 1.56m from Australia and 297,000 from China; it also reported that March 2026 visitor arrivals were 39% from Australia, 15% from the United States, and 7% from China, compared with 36%, 13%, and 11% respectively in March 2019 [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/). Pre-pandemic, China was New Zealand's second-largest visitor market in the December 2019 year, not the largest: Stats NZ reported 407,100 arrivals from China and 1.54m from Australia in 2019 [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/).
- **The stress test should not be a simple export-share multiplication:** a 25% or 50% fall in China-bound annual goods exports alone would be roughly $5.0b or $9.9b of gross export receipts at the year-ended-May-2026 baseline, but GDP and employment effects depend on substitution to other markets, price changes, inventories, input-output multipliers, exchange-rate responses, and regional labour-market exposure [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx); [Treasury, China's growth and NZ exports](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/outlook-chinas-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-exports-wp-13-16).
- **What is already being done is mainly market-access expansion, not proven diversification:** MFAT says the government is pursuing a goal of doubling export value over the next decade through trade initiatives and economic diplomacy; the same annual-report section cites UAE CEPA signing, GCC negotiations conclusion, India FTA negotiations launch, AANZFTA updating, FTA implementation with the UK and EU, non-tariff-barrier work, and trade missions to China and India [MFAT annual report 2024/25](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/about-us/mfat-annual-reports/mfat-annual-report-2024-25/deliver-an-intensive-targeted-programme-of-trade-and-economic-diplomacy). The stream should therefore test whether these efforts have changed actual shares and resilience, not just list agreements.

**Overall confidence:** Medium-High. Current trade, student-enrolment, and visitor-arrival baselines are High confidence because they come from Stats NZ, Education New Zealand, Ministry of Education, Immigration New Zealand, and MFAT. GDP/employment stress effects are Medium-Low at framing stage because they require explicit modelling assumptions and likely input-output or CGE work, not just export totals.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** China is New Zealand's largest export destination according to MFAT and Stats NZ data [MFAT China trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx). The current dollar baselines used here come from Stats NZ download workbooks for international trade and overseas merchandise trade [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-trade-march-2026-quarter/); [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026/). Education New Zealand's 2025 enrolment data identifies China as the largest international-student source country [Education New Zealand, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). Stats NZ's visitor-arrival data shows Australia, not China, as the largest visitor source in both the March 2026 annual data and the cited 2019 pre-COVID baseline [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/); [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/).

**What is interpretation:** the core framing - that NZ's exposure is material but sector-specific - is my synthesis of the trade, education, tourism, and policy evidence above. The conclusion that current diversification activity is "market-access heavy" is an interpretation of MFAT's listed initiatives and should be tested against export-share time series and firm/sector behaviour [MFAT annual report 2024/25](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/about-us/mfat-annual-reports/mfat-annual-report-2024-25/deliver-an-intensive-targeted-programme-of-trade-and-economic-diplomacy).

**Main limits:** I did not complete an OECD peer-country ranking in this framing, so the root's "one of the most China-dependent OECD economies" wording remains unproven here and becomes a child research question. The commodity shares above use the March 2026 quarter because that is where Stats NZ publishes the combined goods/services and top export-by-destination detail in one workbook; seasonal sectors such as fruit need annualised checking. Tourism revenue by source is proxied here by Stats NZ travel services exports and visitor-arrival data; MBIE International Visitor Survey spending by source should be checked in child research. No private firm, student, tourist, or worker-level data is used.

## Problem statement

New Zealand has a concentrated exposure to Chinese demand across goods exports, international education, and travel services, but the economic risk is not captured by a single headline "share of exports to China." China took $19.885b of NZ's $82.665b annual merchandise exports in the year ended May 2026, while dairy, meat, fruit, and logs/wood together made up $46.541b of annual merchandise exports across all markets [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx). In services, travel exports were $7.035b in the March 2026 quarter and education-related travel exports were $1.288b, while China-specific travel exports were $1.034b [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx). The downstream stream should establish which parts of this exposure are substitutable, which are price- or quality-premium dependent, which are regionally concentrated, and which would transmit quickly into employment or public finances.

## What is already being done

**Trade diplomacy and market access.** MFAT's 2024/25 annual report frames trade diplomacy around the government's goal of doubling export value over the next decade, with work on exports promotion, non-tariff barriers, resilience, and trade missions [MFAT annual report 2024/25](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/about-us/mfat-annual-reports/mfat-annual-report-2024-25/deliver-an-intensive-targeted-programme-of-trade-and-economic-diplomacy). India has moved from a "potential market" idea to a signed FTA: MFAT states New Zealand and India signed the NZ-India FTA on 27 April 2026 [MFAT NZ-India FTA](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement). The UAE CEPA entered into force on 28 August 2025, with MFAT describing the UAE as a Middle East market for dairy, industrial products, meat, horticultural products, and travel services [MFAT NZ-UAE CEPA overview](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/new-zealand-united-arab-emirates-free-trade-agreement/overview). GCC negotiations have concluded, and MFAT says GCC economies were NZ's sixth largest export market at 2.9% of global exports in the year to June 2025 [MFAT GCC trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/nz-gulf-cooperation-council/key-facts-on-new-zealand-gcc-trade).

**International education growth.** The Ministry of Education says the International Education Going for Growth Plan, launched in July 2025, sets actions to double international education value from $3.6b in 2024 to $7.2b by 2034, centred on Education New Zealand marketing and short-, medium-, and long-term sector-growth actions [Ministry of Education](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/tertiary-and-further-education/international-education). That is a growth strategy, not necessarily a China-diversification strategy, because ENZ's latest enrolment data still shows China as the largest source country at 34% [Education New Zealand, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

**Tourism facilitation.** Immigration New Zealand says that from 3 November 2025, people with a Chinese passport do not need a visitor visa to visit New Zealand from Australia for up to three months if they hold an eligible Australian visa, under a 12-month trial using the NZeTA [Immigration New Zealand, 2025](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/). Stats NZ reported that from November 2025 to March 2026, 40% of Chinese-passport visitor arrivals came via Australia, compared with 25% in the same five months a year earlier [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals up in March](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-up-in-march/). This policy increases access for one China-linked visitor channel; it should not be misread as diversification away from China.

## Proposed child research questions

1. **How concentrated is NZ's China exposure by export sector, product, service, and region?** This should establish the current baseline across merchandise exports, services exports, education, and tourism, with sector shares and dollar values. Start with Stats NZ international trade and overseas merchandise trade workbooks, MPI sector reporting, ENZ enrolment data, and MBIE/Stats NZ tourism data.
2. **How has NZ's China share changed over the past decade, and what explains the movement?** This should distinguish price cycles, volume changes, sector mix, COVID-era travel/education disruption, and policy changes. Start with Stats NZ time series, Infoshare/trade dashboard extracts, MPI Situation and Outlook reports, ENZ dashboards, and MBIE tourism datasets.
3. **How would a 25% or 50% China-facing demand shock transmit into GDP, employment, regions, and public revenue?** This should turn gross receipts into economic impact under explicit assumptions about substitution, margins, input-output multipliers, prices, exchange rates, and lag times. Start with Stats NZ input-output tables, national accounts, Treasury/RBNZ scenario material, and sector-specific export margins where public.
4. **Which diversification efforts are underway, and are they changing actual trade, student, or visitor shares?** This should separate market-access announcements from measured diversification outcomes. Start with MFAT FTA pages and annual reports, NZTE/MFAT exporter programmes, Education NZ market-prioritisation material, Immigration NZ visitor settings, and Stats NZ share trends.
5. **Where is China exposure hardest to substitute, and where could alternative markets plausibly absorb a shock?** This should test product-market fit, premium pricing, standards, logistics, buyer relationships, and capacity constraints for dairy, logs, meat, fruit, education, and tourism. Start with MPI market reports, industry-body public data, customs/trade data, and public buyer-market analysis.
6. **How does NZ's China exposure compare with peer OECD and small open economies?** This should test the root's comparative claim using consistent goods-plus-services, exports-to-GDP, and sector-concentration measures. Start with OECD, WTO, UN Comtrade, IMF, national statistics agencies, and Stats NZ for the NZ denominator.

## What would change this conclusion

- A verified OECD-comparator dataset showing NZ is not unusually China-exposed once services and export-to-GDP shares are normalised would weaken the root's comparative premise.
- Annual product-by-destination data showing the March 2026 quarter materially overstates or understates China shares for logs, fruit, dairy, or meat would change the sector ranking.
- Evidence that large exporters can rapidly and profitably redirect China-bound product to other markets at similar prices would reduce the stress-test risk.
- MBIE or Stats NZ source-market spending data showing China visitor spend diverges sharply from China visitor arrivals would change the tourism exposure baseline.
- Input-output or CGE modelling with explicit substitution assumptions could raise or lower the GDP and employment risk materially compared with the gross-receipts framing here.
