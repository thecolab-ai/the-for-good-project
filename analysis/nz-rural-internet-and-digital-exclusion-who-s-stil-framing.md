# NZ rural digital exclusion is now a quality, affordability, and evidence-gap problem

Discovery framing for stream root #433. It tests the root's coverage, impact, programme-effectiveness, comparator, and barrier claims against current public evidence, then proposes the child research questions the runner should open.

## Executive answer

- **Core correction - the stream should not frame rural NZ as simply "still disconnected".** The strongest current evidence says New Zealand has pushed nominal broadband availability very far: UFB fibre reached 87 percent of the population and 412 towns by completion in December 2022, with 73 percent uptake of UFB premises passed at 30 June 2025, and the 2023 MBIE incoming-minister briefing expected government connectivity programmes to leave "up to 3800 households" needing bespoke solutions after programme completion [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/); [MBIE BIM for Media and Communications, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf). The live problem is sharper than simple absence: rural households outside the fibre footprint rely on a mixed stack of 4G/5G fixed wireless, wireless ISP, copper, and satellite services whose price, congestion, latency, data caps, line-of-sight constraints, and local availability differ materially [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).
- **H1: "Rural households still lack reliable broadband or mobile coverage" - partially supported, with a measurement correction.** As of June 2025, the Commerce Commission reported that about 95 percent of rural addresses were within at least one mobile network operator's 4G fixed-wireless coverage area, but it also warned that local capacity and stop-sells can mean a service is unavailable even inside a coverage footprint, that satellite is not shown on its map because local obstructions can block a nominally national service, and that providers' supplied coverage data determine map accuracy [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). Mobile coverage is also "near-universal" only by population: reported 4G mobile coverage reached 99.0 percent of population in 2025, while average 4G landmass coverage was 54.6 percent, so rural black spots remain geographically material even when most people are covered [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [MBIE, broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes).
- **H2: "The impact hits schools, telehealth, farming, and small business" - supported directionally, but weakly quantified.** The best NZ cost estimate found is NZIER's Chorus-commissioned 2022 model, which estimated annual incremental benefits from unconstrained rural connectivity of about $1.79 billion for rural households and $344 million in business time savings, but that is a model rather than observed programme evaluation and should be treated as Medium confidence until replicated [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf). The health channel is independently plausible because the Rural Health Strategy makes digital options part of access-at-a-distance, while the NZ Telehealth Resource Centre says rural internet connectivity can be an issue for telehealth and that better connectivity is needed for equitable access [Ministry of Health, Rural Health Strategy 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf); [NZ Telehealth Resource Centre, Internet in rural areas](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/telehealth-resources/technology/technology/internet-connectivity-in-rural-areas).
- **H3: "UFB and RBI coverage does not equal actual performance" - supported.** UFB is a high-performing fibre programme for the 87 percent population footprint, but the Commerce Commission's rural chapter says rural broadband performance in 2025 differed clearly by technology: ADSL supported only basic online activity, 4G fixed wireless and WISP services were more workable for everyday use, and LEO satellite improved options for some rural addresses while remaining subject to line-of-sight and weather constraints [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/); [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- **H4: "South Island, Bay of Plenty, and Hawke's Bay are especially affected" - partially supported, but not proven as the sharpest geography.** The Commerce Commission's 2025 price table shows rural broadband premiums in Bay of Plenty (16 percent), Gisborne/Hawke's Bay (13 percent), Otago (15 percent), Southland (11 percent), Canterbury (11 percent), and the Tasman/Nelson/Marlborough/West Coast grouping (16 percent), but the largest listed rural-urban price gaps were Taranaki (27 percent), Waikato (26 percent), Northland (22 percent), and Wellington (19 percent) [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). A child issue should map underserved places rather than inherit the root's regional emphasis.
- **H5: "Comparable countries can benchmark NZ" - supported, with care.** OECD's 2025 broadband-divides work shows rural connectivity gaps are a live OECD-wide problem and reports that the OECD fixed-speed gap between metropolitan and far-from-metropolitan regions widened from 22 Mbps in 2019 to 58 Mbps in 2024, while Canada appears among countries with a large urban-rural speed gap and Norway appears as a low-gap comparator [OECD, 2025 statistical release](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/insights/statistical-releases/2025/07/digital-connectivity-expands-across-the-oecd-but-rural-areas-are-falling-further-behind.html); [OECD, Closing Broadband Connectivity Divides for All, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html). The comparator question must compare definitions, minimum-service obligations, performance measurement, and affordability rather than just headline coverage percentages [OECD, Closing Broadband Connectivity Divides for All, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The infrastructure and market baseline is strong because it rests mainly on the Commerce Commission, MBIE, National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Stats NZ, and Ministry of Health sources; the exact economic-cost and underserved-household estimates are Medium or Low because the available public material mixes provider-reported coverage, modelled economic benefits, and definitions of "rural" that do not line up cleanly.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** UFB reached 87 percent of the population and 412 towns by completion in December 2022, with 73 percent uptake of premises passed at 30 June 2025 [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/). The Commerce Commission's 2025 report and connectivity map use provider-supplied coverage and connection data as at 30 June 2025, classify wireless parcel coverage using a 50 percent parcel-overlap rule, exclude satellite from the map, and state that the map may differ from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). Stats NZ's 2023 Census reported household internet access of 90.5 percent, or 1,473,933 households, with 8.4 percent of households having no information for the access-to-telecommunications concept [Stats NZ, 2023 Census population, dwelling, and housing highlights](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-dwelling-and-housing-highlights/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+, access to telecommunication systems](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/fbe47f92-4602-4279-858a-bbbb168e53ff).

**What is interpretation:** The conclusion that rural digital exclusion is now primarily a quality, affordability, resilience, and evidence problem is a synthesis of coverage, performance, price, and satisfaction evidence rather than a single published finding. The strongest support is that rural consumers pay more than urban consumers in every region in the Commission's 2025 table, rural satisfaction trails urban satisfaction across service dimensions, 4G mobile landmass coverage remains far below population coverage, and the official connectivity map contains explicit accuracy limits [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

**Main limits:** The public data does not directly answer "what percentage of rural households lack reliable broadband" because the official sources separately measure nominal coverage, chosen connections, census household access, performance tests by technology, and consumer satisfaction [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+, access to telecommunication systems](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/fbe47f92-4602-4279-858a-bbbb168e53ff); [Commerce Commission, Measuring Broadband New Zealand](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/). Several MBIE assets are bot-protected for direct command-line fetches from this environment, so blocked pages were treated as tooling limits under ADR-0006 and corroborated through WebFetch, official index pages, and alternate official pages rather than called dead [MBIE, broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes); [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/). This framing did not contact providers, schools, health services, farms, iwi, councils, or rural households, so downstream work should include lived-experience and practitioner review before any G1 decision.

## Problem statement

New Zealand's rural connectivity problem in 2026 is best framed as the gap between **nominal access** and **usable, affordable, resilient digital participation** for the population outside the UFB fibre footprint. UFB has completed a large urban and town fibre build that reaches 87 percent of the population, but rural users outside that footprint face a technology mix whose service quality depends on distance to towers or cabinets, local congestion, terrain, line of sight, data caps, installation cost, and provider stop-sell decisions [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/); [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [NZ Telehealth Resource Centre, Internet in rural areas](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/telehealth-resources/technology/technology/internet-connectivity-in-rural-areas).

The central research task is therefore not just counting households with no broadband product. It is separating four different states: no nominal service, a nominal service that cannot be ordered because of capacity or stop-sell constraints, an orderable service that performs too poorly for work, learning, health, and farm operations, and an orderable service that works but is less affordable than the urban equivalent [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

## Who it affects

The exposed population depends on which official rural definition is used: EHINZ's 2024 urban-rural profile says 15.7 percent of New Zealanders lived in rural areas, while the Ministry of Health's Rural Health Strategy says around one in five New Zealanders and one in four Māori live in rural communities under the Geographic Classification for Health [EHINZ, Urban-rural profile](https://www.ehinz.ac.nz/indicators/population-vulnerability/urbanrural-profile/); [Ministry of Health, Rural Health Strategy 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf). NZIER's 2021 rural mapping estimated 834,900 rural residents, 275,496 rural households, and 140,028 rural businesses, but those figures predate the 2023 Census and use an urban-rural allocation rather than the health geography [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

Household internet access has improved nationally, but the census access measure is not a reliability or speed measure: Stats NZ reported household internet access of 90.5 percent in 2023, up from 86.1 percent in 2018, and its DataInfo+ page defines the access-to-telecommunications concept as a working ability to communicate by cellphone, telephone, or internet at the household level [Stats NZ, 2023 Census population, dwelling, and housing highlights](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-dwelling-and-housing-highlights/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+, access to telecommunication systems](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/fbe47f92-4602-4279-858a-bbbb168e53ff). Digital exclusion also intersects with income, disability, age, ethnicity, and settlement type: Motu's four-survey study identified people in social housing, disabled people, Pasifika, Māori, people in larger country towns, older people, unemployed people, and people not actively seeking work as groups prone to relatively low internet access [Motu, Digital inclusion and wellbeing in New Zealand](https://www.motu.nz/our-research/wellbeing-and-macroeconomics/well-being-and-sustainability-measures/digital-inclusion-and-wellbeing-in-new-zealand).

The most obvious rural services at risk are not all at the same point in the stack. School-premises connectivity has had dedicated government and N4L investment, but rural students' home connectivity still matters for remote learning, homework, and studying from home [N4L, Faster safer internet for NZ schools](https://www.n4l.co.nz/); [Stats NZ, 2023 Census population, dwelling, and housing highlights](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-dwelling-and-housing-highlights/). Telehealth depends on both local service design and household connectivity, and the Rural Health Strategy says digital options such as telehealth are not standard options across the health system even where feasible [Ministry of Health, Rural Health Strategy 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf). Farmers, growers, tourism operators, and small rural businesses face business-process risk because NZIER identifies accounting tools, automated milking, harvesting equipment, water-quality monitoring, online booking systems, government compliance, and online supplier or customer contact as connectivity-dependent rural activities [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

## What's already being done

Government and Crown delivery partners have already funded several infrastructure programmes. UFB delivered fibre to 87 percent of the population and 412 towns, MBIE's 2023 incoming-minister briefing described nearly $2.6 billion of government connectivity investment across UFB, Rural Broadband phases 1 and 2, the Mobile Black Spot Fund, Rural Capacity Upgrades, Marae Digital Connectivity, regional connectivity initiatives, the Remote Users Scheme, and 5G rural improvement works, and National Infrastructure Funding and Financing says rural fibre under the Rural Capacity Upgrade programme is now benefiting more than 5,600 rural households and businesses [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/); [MBIE BIM for Media and Communications, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf); [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Rural Capacity Upgrades](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).

The Rural Broadband Initiative, Mobile Black Spot Fund, Rural Capacity Upgrades, and Remote Users Scheme sit closest to this stream. MBIE says the Mobile Black Spot Fund is providing greater mobile coverage on about 1,400 km of state highways and more than 168 tourism locations, that RCU funding was expected to benefit up to 47,000 rural households and businesses and later expanded by up to 26,000 more, and that the Remote Users Scheme supports rural and remote households where broadband is unavailable or very slow and no other government connectivity programme covers them [MBIE, broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes); [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).

The measurement system is also improving. The Commerce Commission now publishes annual telecommunications monitoring reports, a Telecommunications Connectivity Map using provider-supplied data, and the Measuring Broadband New Zealand programme to test performance across fixed line, fixed wireless, and satellite internet [Commerce Commission, annual monitoring reports](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/annual-monitoring-report/); [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/); [Commerce Commission, Measuring Broadband New Zealand](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/). The Digital Inclusion Blueprint defines digital inclusion as more than infrastructure by using four elements - motivation, access, skills, and trust - and says New Zealand lacked agreed measures and reliable comprehensive data when the blueprint was written [Digital.govt.nz, Digital Inclusion Blueprint](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/113-digital-inclusion-blueprint-te-mahere-mo-te-whakaurunga-matihiko/html).

Regulatory settings are changing as well. MBIE says the Government's 2025 telecommunications-regulation changes include reinstating fibre shared-property access rights, mandating dispute-resolution membership for large retail providers, expanding the telecommunications regulatory regime to offshore providers where relevant, and amending local fibre-company constitutions to allow broader wholesale telecommunications services [MBIE, our role in the telecommunications sector](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/our-role-in-the-ict-sector); [Beehive, Boosting rural connectivity and economic growth, 13 March 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/boosting-rural-connectivity-and-economic-growth).

## Evidence test

### H1 - Coverage: supported geographically, corrected at household level

The root's concern about patchy coverage is supported for landmass and local reliability, but the headline rural household story is more complex. The Commerce Commission reported in 2025 that 4G mobile population coverage reached 99.0 percent while average 4G landmass coverage was 54.6 percent, which explains why many rural roads, farms, conservation areas, and tourism routes can remain patchy even when most people are counted as covered [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report says about 95 percent of rural addresses were within at least one MNO's 4G fixed-wireless network, but also says stop-sells can prevent customers inside a footprint from ordering service and that remaining non-covered addresses are likely the most remote and isolated [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The official map itself warns against over-reading coverage. It uses provider-supplied data, represents coverage at a point in time as at 30 June 2025, classifies a wireless technology as covering a parcel if at least 50 percent of the parcel falls within the coverage area, excludes satellite despite theoretical national coverage because obstacles can block signal, and is not inclusive of all providers [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). This means "percentage lacking reliable broadband" needs a constructed measure combining coverage, orderability, performance, and price; no single public source found in this framing gives that number directly.

### H2 - Price and consumer experience: supported

The affordability evidence is strong. The Commerce Commission's 2025 rural price table shows a weighted average monthly rural broadband price of $85.77 versus $72.65 urban, a $13.12 or 18 percent rural premium, and it says rural households consistently pay more than urban households in every region [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report says rural satisfaction trailed urban satisfaction across all service dimensions, with pricing and customer service the weakest areas at 50 percent rural satisfaction [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The region claim needs correction. Bay of Plenty and Gisborne/Hawke's Bay did have rural premiums in the Commission table, and South Island regions or groupings also had premiums, but the largest listed rural-urban price gaps were Taranaki, Waikato, Northland, and Wellington [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). That does not refute the root's regional examples, but it means the first child issue should identify actual current underserved geographies instead of assuming the priority map.

### H3 - Impacts: supported directionally, quantified weakly

NZIER estimated that unconstrained rural digital connectivity would generate annual incremental benefits of about $6,505 per rural household, or $1.792 billion across 275,496 rural households, plus $2,457 per rural business in time savings, or $344 million across 140,028 rural businesses [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf). This is useful as a hypothesis and order-of-magnitude estimate, but it is modelled and commissioned by Chorus, so downstream work should seek independent replication or narrower sector evidence before treating it as the stream's central number [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

The sector channels are plausible and worth separate research. NZIER identifies farm and grower use cases such as automated milking, harvesting equipment, accounting tools, water-quality monitoring, and product marketing, while the Rural Health Strategy and NZ Telehealth Resource Centre identify telehealth and digital support as relevant to rural access-at-a-distance [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf); [Ministry of Health, Rural Health Strategy 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf); [NZ Telehealth Resource Centre, Internet in rural areas](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/telehealth-resources/technology/technology/internet-connectivity-in-rural-areas). The education channel should separate school network connectivity from student household connectivity because N4L provides managed internet to schools and kura, while Stats NZ separately reports household internet access and studying from home in the 2023 Census release [N4L, Faster safer internet for NZ schools](https://www.n4l.co.nz/); [Stats NZ, 2023 Census population, dwelling, and housing highlights](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-dwelling-and-housing-highlights/).

### H4 - UFB/RBI effectiveness: supported for coverage, unresolved for performance value

The UFB evidence is strong: the programme reached 87 percent of the population, 412 towns, and 73 percent uptake of premises passed by 30 June 2025 [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/). The rural programme evidence shows breadth but also the performance gap: MBIE's 2023 briefing says 81,904 rural homes and businesses could access improved broadband through RBI and 1,377 km of state highway plus 128 tourism sites had mobile coverage through MBSF as of September 2023, while the Commission's 2025 report says rural consumers still faced higher prices, lower satisfaction, 4G fixed-wireless congestion, and technology-dependent performance [MBIE BIM for Media and Communications, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf); [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The strongest programme-effectiveness question is not "did the build happen"; it is whether the programmes delivered a service standard that remains adequate as rural households and businesses use video calls, cloud tools, online public services, telehealth, precision agriculture, security devices, and remote work [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf); [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

### H5 - Remaining barriers: supported, but they are multiple

Terrain, distance, density, and economics all remain barriers, but the evidence also points to capacity management, service-orderability, data quality, and affordability. The Commerce Commission says 4G fixed wireless can be affected by distance to the nearest cell tower and local network congestion, copper is constrained by line length and degradation, and satellite can be affected by weather and line-of-sight obstructions [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). NIFF says rural fibre has historically been less viable because of the cost of running long lengths of fibre past widely spaced properties and down long driveways, and the RCU page says capacity pressure caused some rural towers to go into stop-sell where no new users could be supported [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, fibre](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/fibre/); [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Rural Capacity Upgrades](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).

Data is itself a barrier to good policy. The Commerce Commission's map does not include all providers, does not show provider names, updates annually rather than continuously, and differs from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). The Digital Inclusion Blueprint says digital inclusion requires motivation, access, skills, and trust, which means an infrastructure-only map will miss households that have nominal network access but lack affordable devices, skills, confidence, or trusted support [Digital.govt.nz, Digital Inclusion Blueprint](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/113-digital-inclusion-blueprint-te-mahere-mo-te-whakaurunga-matihiko/html); [Motu, Digital inclusion and wellbeing in New Zealand](https://www.motu.nz/our-research/wellbeing-and-macroeconomics/well-being-and-sustainability-measures/digital-inclusion-and-wellbeing-in-new-zealand).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **Who is still unserved or underserved when coverage, performance, resilience, and price are separated?** Build the baseline the root assumes: combine the Commerce Commission connectivity map, annual monitoring data, MBNZ performance reports, Stats NZ rural classifications, and Remote Users Scheme material to estimate the rural households and places with no service, nominal-but-not-orderable service, poor performance, or unaffordable service [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/); [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
2. **What is the real cost of rural digital exclusion for households, farms, health access, education, and small businesses?** Test NZIER's modelled $1.79b household and $344m business figures against independent sources, sector records, and narrow public indicators rather than letting a single commissioned report carry the stream [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf); [Ministry of Health, Rural Health Strategy 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf).
3. **How effective were UFB, RBI, MBSF, RCU, RUS, and rural fibre when judged by actual service outcomes rather than premises or population coverage?** Compare stated programme outputs, current coverage, measured speeds, consumer satisfaction, stop-sell/capacity evidence, and regional price premiums to identify which interventions closed real gaps and which only moved households into a lower-quality technology tier [MBIE, broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes); [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
4. **What happens to rural voice, mobile, emergency, and resilience needs as copper and 3G retreat?** Investigate rural copper dependence, 3G shutdown effects, 4G landmass black spots, emergency calling, farm safety, road/tourism corridors, and backup-power/resilience issues, using ComCom, MBIE, provider, emergency-management, and rural stakeholder sources [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [MBIE, broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes).
5. **How should NZ compare itself with Australia, Canada, and Nordic countries on rural connectivity?** Compare universal-service standards, mapping methods, affordability measures, public subsidy design, satellite treatment, mobile coverage-map standards, and rural performance rather than headline coverage alone, starting with OECD's 2025 broadband-divides work and official Australian, Canadian, and European/Nordic regulator material [OECD, Closing Broadband Connectivity Divides for All, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html); [Australian 2024 Regional Telecommunications Review](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/sites/default/files/documents/2024-regional-telecommunications-review.pdf); [CRTC Broadband Fund](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/internet/fnd/index.htm).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public, address-level dataset showing that nearly all rural households can order a service meeting a current speed, latency, reliability, and affordability standard would weaken the "usable access" framing and shift the stream back toward a small residual remote-users problem [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).
- Provider or regulator data showing low rural stop-sell rates, low congestion, and rural satisfaction converging with urban satisfaction would weaken the claim that nominal coverage is not translating into user experience [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Independent economic analysis that materially refutes NZIER's benefit estimates would lower the impact case, especially for small business and rural household benefits [NZIER, Rural connectivity, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).
- Lived-experience or practitioner evidence from rural schools, hauora providers, farmers, WISPs, iwi, and councils could re-rank the child questions, because this framing used public sources only and did not test local realities directly.
- A change in the regulatory framework for rural copper, offshore satellite providers, Telecommunications Development Levy settings, or local fibre-company activities could materially change the programme-effectiveness and future-barrier analysis [MBIE, our role in the telecommunications sector](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/our-role-in-the-ict-sector).
