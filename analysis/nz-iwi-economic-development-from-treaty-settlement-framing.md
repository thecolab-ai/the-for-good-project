# NZ iwi settlement capital has visible success cases but no simple public track record

Discovery framing for stream root #437 (Part of #437). Tests the root's premise about iwi economic development from Treaty settlement capital, corrects the aggregate redress figure, and proposes child research issues.

## Executive answer

- **The $2.4b premise is supported but out of date.** The Auditor-General reported in April 2025 that about 80 Treaty settlements with about 70 groups had transferred **$2.738b** of financial and commercial redress by late 2024, alongside about 12,000 individual legal and contractual commitments held by about 150 public organisations [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf). A Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau ministerial briefing also gives the same $2.738b total and notes that the Crown negotiates settlements with "large natural groups" rather than individual hapū or whānau [Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau, 2023](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Publications/Corporate/MfTOWN-BIM-for-proactive-release.pdf).
- **Large iwi cases show real balance-sheet growth and mature commercial machinery.** Ngāi Tahu reports $2.191b total assets, $1.745b net assets, an $80.8m FY2025 distribution, and $100m committed to Mint Aotearoa renewable-energy development [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf). Waikato-Tainui reports that its economic base grew from the original $170m settlement to a $2.4b group asset portfolio, with FY2025 total assets of $2.414b, net assets of $1.847b, and a $28.6m annual distribution [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf).
- **Smaller or mid-sized iwi evidence looks different: growth, but also consolidation risk.** Te Rarawa's settlement summary records $33.840m of financial and commercial redress plus interest [Te Tari Whakatau, Te Rarawa settlement summary](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/te-rarawa). Its 2025 annual report says its commercial asset portfolio grew from $32.6m in 2016 to $115.2m in 2025, while management slowed new investments, prioritised liquidity, and described several businesses as still in consolidation phases [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).
- **The stream should not try to answer "did $2.738b work?" as a single ROI question.** Settlements contain financial, commercial, cultural, relationship, and ongoing redress, and the Auditor-General says settlements should be treated as long-term Crown-iwi relationships rather than isolated transactions [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf). The researchable question is which governance, asset allocation, leverage, partnership, distribution, and reporting models appear to convert settlement capital into durable iwi outcomes, and where public evidence is too thin to judge.
- **The main evidence gap is comparability.** Annual reports can support case studies on assets, distributions, debt, sectors, and some member outcomes, but they do not form a standard public venture-by-venture dataset of successes, failures, employment effects, or intergenerational wealth effects [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium-High — the aggregate redress figure and the major annual-report financial facts are high-confidence official or audited sources, but venture-level success/failure rates and causal community impacts are medium-to-low confidence from public sources because reporting is uneven and not designed as a comparable dataset.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** the $2.738b financial and commercial redress total is reported by the Auditor-General and Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf); [Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau, 2023](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Publications/Corporate/MfTOWN-BIM-for-proactive-release.pdf). Stats NZ counted 978,246 people of Māori descent and 887,493 people identifying with the Māori ethnic group in the 2023 Census [Stats NZ, 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-counts-by-ethnic-group-age-and-maori-descent-and-dwelling-counts/). The Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui, and Te Rarawa financial figures cited in this framing are drawn from their published 2025 annual reports [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).

**What is interpretation:** the claim that the public evidence is strong enough for a structured case-study stream but not strong enough for a simple "success rate" is this document's synthesis of annual reports, settlement pages, and Māori-economy policy sources. The interpretation could change if a reliable comparative iwi-investment dataset with venture-level returns, failures, employment effects, and distributions is found.

**Main limits:** this framing used public sources only and does not assess private iwi board papers, commercial contracts, member-register data, or non-public investment performance. It does not judge what iwi "should" do with settlement capital; that would require iwi authority, Māori data-governance review, and lived experience. MBIE and Reserve Bank Māori-economy pages were bot-blocked to raw `curl` but resolved through the built-in web fetch rung, so they are used only for broad policy context rather than as settlement-performance evidence [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports); [Reserve Bank, 2025](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/about-us/how-we-work/te-ao-maori/improved-access-to-capital-for-maori).

## Problem statement

Treaty settlement capital has become a major base for iwi economic development, but the public record does not yet answer the practical track-record question cleanly. The Crown has transferred $2.738b in financial and commercial redress through historical Treaty settlements, and post-settlement governance entities hold and manage settlement redress on behalf of iwi and hapū [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf). Some iwi now publish billion-dollar balance sheets and annual distributions, while other iwi publish smaller portfolios with active liquidity, debt, and consolidation constraints [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).

The stream should therefore frame "track record" as a multi-dimensional evidence question: asset preservation and growth, cash distributions, risk management, governance accountability, venture exits and failures, jobs and procurement, member benefits, and intergenerational wealth. The stream should also separate settlement capital from the wider Māori economy, because MBIE reports a Māori asset base of $126b in 2023 and Māori economic contribution of $32b in 2023, far larger than the Treaty-settlement redress quantum itself [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports).

## Who it affects

The most directly affected people are members of settled iwi and hapū, because post-settlement governance entities act on behalf of claimant groups and hold settlement redress transferred under deeds of settlement [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf). Stats NZ counted 978,246 people of Māori descent and 887,493 people identifying with the Māori ethnic group in the 2023 Census, and Stats NZ explains that Māori descent and Māori ethnicity are related but different concepts [Stats NZ, 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-counts-by-ethnic-group-age-and-maori-descent-and-dwelling-counts/); [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/methods/maori-and-iwi-population-concepts-in-the-2023-census/). Iwi-affiliation evidence matters for this stream, but Stats NZ rates the 2023 Census iwi-affiliation variable as moderate quality and warns that iwi counts can be affected by alternative data sources, multiple affiliations, response rates, and changes in collection methods [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/methods/maori-and-iwi-population-concepts-in-the-2023-census/).

The stream also affects regional economies where iwi portfolios operate. Ngāi Tahu reports commercial activity across primary industries, tourism, seafood, property, listed equities, and renewable energy, with more than 500 kaimahi in FY2025 [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf). Waikato-Tainui reports a Ruakura-focused property and logistics strategy, a tribal registry of more than 94,000 members, 140 registered tribal members supported into employment programmes in FY2025, and 1,221 education grants or scholarships distributed across 68 marae in FY2025 [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf). Te Rarawa reports that its Māhuru Trust housing development provided local employment and workflow opportunities while supporting housing security and whenua retention [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).

## What is already being done

**Crown settlement and delivery oversight.** The Auditor-General's 2025 report assessed whether public organisations are positioned to fulfil Treaty settlement commitments, found about 12,000 commitments under about 80 settlements, and recommended stronger accountability, monitoring, and system-level oversight [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf). Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau maintains public settlement pages and deed documents for iwi including Te Rarawa, Ngāti Awa, Ngāi Tahu, and Waikato-Tainui [Te Tari Whakatau, Te Rarawa](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/te-rarawa); [Te Tari Whakatau, Ngāti Awa](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/ngati-awa); [Te Tari Whakatau, Ngāi Tahu](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/ngai-tahu); [Te Tari Whakatau, Waikato-Tainui Raupatu](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/waikato-tainui-raupatu).

**Iwi reporting and commercial arms.** Ngāi Tahu separates the Office of Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu from Ngāi Tahu Holdings Corporation, and its annual report says commercial activities and assets are managed by NTHC [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf). Waikato-Tainui reports through Waikato Raupatu Lands Trust and Tainui Group Holdings, with TGH acting as chief investment officer for tribal funds and asset manager for the WRLT portfolio [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf). Te Rarawa reports through Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa and Te Waka Pupuri Pūtea Group, whose mandate is to receive, hold, manage, invest, and grow Te Rarawa assets and commercial resources [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf). Ngāti Awa Group Holdings states that its role is to sustainably maximise economic benefits for Ngāti Awa [Ngāti Awa Group Holdings](https://www.naghl.co.nz/).

**Māori economy and capital-access policy.** MBIE's Te Ōhanga Māori reports are intended to inform iwi, Māori, business, and government decision-making, and the 2023 report records a Māori asset base of $126b, a Māori economic contribution of $32b, and nearly 24,000 Māori-owned businesses in 2023 [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports). The Reserve Bank's Māori access-to-capital work programme researches barriers in the financial system, and its 2022 issues paper process was overseen by a multi-agency steering group including Treasury, Te Puni Kōkiri, MBIE, MPI, NZTE, Te Tumu Paeroa, MFAT, and the Ministry of Housing and Urban Development [Reserve Bank, 2022](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/have-your-say/improving-maori-access-to-capital); [Reserve Bank, 2025](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/about-us/how-we-work/te-ao-maori/improved-access-to-capital-for-maori).

**Iwi data infrastructure.** Stats NZ publishes iwi-affiliation data for people of Māori descent and warns that the 2023 iwi-affiliation variable has moderate quality [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/methods/maori-and-iwi-population-concepts-in-the-2023-census/). The data.govt.nz catalogue lists Te Kāhui Māngai as a directory designed to provide information on iwi, including rohe, hapū, marae, and representative organisations [data.govt.nz, Te Kāhui Māngai](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/directory-of-iwi-and-maori-organisations-te-kahui-mangai).

## Hypothesis tests

### H1: "Over $2.4b in commercial assets and capital has been returned to iwi through Treaty settlements."

**Grade: supported but corrected upward and qualified.** The best current official aggregate found is $2.738b of financial and commercial redress transferred through settlements, not $2.4b [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf); [Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau, 2023](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Publications/Corporate/MfTOWN-BIM-for-proactive-release.pdf). The wording also needs care: settlements include financial payments, commercial redress, cultural redress, one-off commitments, and ongoing commitments, so the redress total is not equivalent to a simple pool of investable cash [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf).

### H2: "Major iwi have built significant commercial portfolios in tourism, property, energy, venture investment, and related sectors."

**Grade: supported for large case studies.** Ngāi Tahu reports a portfolio across primary industries, tourism, seafood, property, listed equities, and renewable energy, plus $100m committed to Mint Aotearoa wind, solar, and storage projects [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf). Waikato-Tainui reports a $2.4b group asset portfolio, strong exposure to local real-estate-related assets through WRLT, and a Ruakura partnership designed to retain whenua ownership while reducing debt and interest payments [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf). Te Rarawa reports a commercial asset portfolio that grew from $32.6m in 2016 to $115.2m in 2025, with subsidiaries and a housing-development focus [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf). Ngāti Awa's public settlement page records $42.39m plus interest and cultural-site costs, and its commercial arm says its role is to maximise economic benefits for Ngāti Awa, but its comparable financial performance was less accessible in public machine-readable sources during this framing [Te Tari Whakatau, Ngāti Awa](https://whakatau.govt.nz/te-tira-kurapounamu-treaty-settlements/find-a-treaty-settlement/ngati-awa); [Ngāti Awa Group Holdings](https://www.naghl.co.nz/).

### H3: "The track record can be measured as a success rate of ventures."

**Grade: corrected.** Public annual reports can support case studies of assets, net assets, distributions, debt, sector allocation, and selected venture outcomes, but they do not provide a standard venture-level success/failure denominator across iwi [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf). Ngāi Tahu reports both successful long-term growth and a hard failure signal, noting the 2023 wind-down of Oha Honey due to industry challenges [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf). Waikato-Tainui reports a FY2025 net deficit after tax of $21.377m, a WRLT fund loss of -2%, and a concentration issue because 80% of WRLT value was in local real-estate-related assets [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf). Te Rarawa reports sound performance but explicitly slowed new investment, protected liquidity, and described many businesses as still in consolidation phases [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf).

### H4: "Iwi investments have affected local employment, community outcomes, and intergenerational wealth."

**Grade: partially supported, with causality unproven from public sources.** Annual reports provide output evidence: Ngāi Tahu reports an $80.8m FY2025 distribution and more than 500 kaimahi [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); Waikato-Tainui reports 140 registered members supported into employment programmes, 18 placed into earn-while-you-learn opportunities, and 1,221 education grants or scholarships across 68 marae [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); Te Rarawa reports local employment and workflow opportunities from the Māhuru Trust housing development [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf). These outputs do not by themselves prove causal effects on regional employment, member income, intergenerational wealth, or whānau wellbeing, because public reports do not consistently link investments to counterfactual outcome data [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/methods/maori-and-iwi-population-concepts-in-the-2023-census/).

### H5: "Governance structures are central to the better-performing cases."

**Grade: supported as a mechanism to investigate, not yet proven as causal.** Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui, Te Rarawa, and Ngāti Awa all show separation between iwi authority functions and commercial/investment entities or arms [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf); [Ngāti Awa Group Holdings](https://www.naghl.co.nz/). Waikato-Tainui's report explicitly links diversification, debt reduction, and the Ruakura Brookfield partnership to future distribution sustainability, while Te Rarawa links its refreshed investment strategy to disciplined capital allocation and balancing financial returns with social and intergenerational outcomes [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf). The child research should test which governance features matter rather than assume professionalised boards automatically produce better outcomes.

### H6: "Evidence can inform future settlement structures or iwi-led development models."

**Grade: partially supported and authority-limited.** Public evidence can inform questions about reporting comparability, capital access, relativity mechanisms, settlement-size effects, and distribution sustainability [Te Arawhiti/Te Tari Whakatau, 2023](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Publications/Corporate/MfTOWN-BIM-for-proactive-release.pdf); [Reserve Bank, 2025](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/about-us/how-we-work/te-ao-maori/improved-access-to-capital-for-maori). It cannot by itself prescribe future iwi development models, because settlement assets are held for iwi and hapū, iwi data requires appropriate governance, and public annual reports are not a substitute for iwi mandate or member decision-making [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf); [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/methods/maori-and-iwi-population-concepts-in-the-2023-census/).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **What did major settled iwi receive, what do their commercial portfolios look like now, and how have assets, returns, debt, and distributions changed over time?** This should establish the baseline for Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui, Te Rarawa, Ngāti Awa, and 2-4 additional iwi where public reports are strong enough. It should begin with Te Tari Whakatau settlement pages, OAG/Te Arawhiti aggregate material, iwi annual reports, and any reliable comparative iwi-investment reports.
2. **Which post-settlement commercial ventures succeeded, struggled, or failed, and what patterns explain the outcomes?** This should move beyond balance-sheet totals to venture-level evidence, including exits, write-downs, closures, COVID/tourism shocks, property cycles, farming/forestry exposure, fisheries, and energy or logistics partnerships. It should start with iwi annual reports and then triangulate with Companies Office records, media, sector reports, and iwi-published statements.
3. **How do iwi commercial governance models affect risk, accountability, distributions, and long-term asset protection?** This should compare structures such as tribal authority, charitable trust, commercial holding company, asset-holding company, independent board, investment charter, and member accountability. It should not assume "professional management" is always the answer; it should test what the public evidence links to performance, trust, and mandate.
4. **What evidence links iwi settlement capital to jobs, procurement, housing, education grants, marae support, and other member or community outcomes?** This should test the social-outcome claims using annual reports, Stats NZ/Te Whata/iwi data, MBIE/TPK sources, and local project reporting. It should distinguish outputs, outcomes, and causal claims.
5. **How do settlement size, relativity mechanisms, asset mix, geography, and starting conditions shape returns and inequality between iwi?** This should test whether large early settlements, relativity clauses, urban/property location, resource endowments, debt capacity, and partnership access create structural differences between iwi outcomes. It should start with Waikato-Tainui and Ngāi Tahu relativity material, then compare mid-sized and smaller settlements.
6. **What public reporting standard would make iwi economic-development performance comparable without undermining rangatiratanga or Māori data sovereignty?** This should identify the minimum comparable public metrics a reviewer could use while respecting iwi control over data and purpose. It should begin with annual-report practice, charity and company reporting requirements, Stats NZ iwi data-quality guidance, and Māori data-sovereignty sources.

## What would change this conclusion

- A reliable public dataset of iwi venture-level investments, returns, write-downs, exits, employment, and distributions would replace the current case-study framing with a more quantitative success/failure analysis.
- Audited annual reports from a broader and more representative iwi sample could show that Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui, Te Rarawa, and Ngāti Awa are atypical rather than indicative.
- Iwi-approved data or interviews could materially change the interpretation of public financial reports, especially where commercial confidentiality or tikanga-based outcomes are not visible in annual accounts.
- Legal or governance review by Māori commercial lawyers, iwi governors, or PSGE practitioners could correct the framing of governance, accountability, or settlement-structure implications.
- More current Crown data could change the aggregate redress baseline, because the OAG figure is as of late 2024 [Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf).
