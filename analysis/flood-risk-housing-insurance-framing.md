# Flood-risk housing and insurance retreat in NZ needs a five-track research baseline

## Executive answer

- This is a valid discover stream because recent market evidence points in a counter-intuitive direction: RNZ reported on 3 July 2026 that Cotality data showed NZ homes with high flood scores selling at a discount while their values had risen faster than no-risk properties since the 2023 severe weather events; in Auckland, RNZ reported a $70,000-$100,000 discount for flood-zoned homes and 18 percent growth since 2020 versus 13 percent for nearby unaffected properties. [RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties)
- The exposure is large enough to justify work: Earth Sciences New Zealand reported in October 2025 that more than 750,000 people live in locations exposed to one-in-100-year rainfall flooding events and that about $235 billion of buildings are exposed under the current climate. [Earth Sciences NZ, 2025](https://www.earthsciences.nz/news/nationwide-study-reveals-escalating-flood-risk)
- The insurance/lending cliff-edge is plausible but not yet fully measured: RBNZ says insurers are adopting more risk-based flood pricing as data improves, complete insurance withdrawal could happen relatively quickly because policies are typically annual, and banks need greater scrutiny of ongoing property insurability. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)
- The public-data baseline is improving but still incomplete: MfE says New Zealand has no single public map that combines flood information clearly and consistently, and the first public generation of the New Zealand Flood Map is expected by May 2027. [MfE New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/)
- I opened five research issues from this root: the value paradox (#260), insurance retreat (#262), exposed communities and deprivation (#263), lending and disclosure (#264), and managed retreat/adaptation policy (#261). [#260](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/260); [#262](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/262); [#263](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/263); [#264](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/264); [#261](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/261)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the exposure, insurance, lending, LIM, and policy baselines are supported by official or established sources, but the market-value paradox currently rests on RNZ's reporting of Cotality data rather than a public reproducible dataset.

## Problem statement

The stream should test whether parts of the NZ housing market are still capitalising affordability discounts in flood-prone homes while insurers and lenders begin to price, restrict, or monitor the same physical risk more tightly. [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)

The core risk is not simply that some homes flood; it is that a buyer may accept a lower purchase price without seeing the future affordability, availability, lending, resale, and adaptation implications with the same clarity as an insurer or lender. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2); [Natural Hazards Commission home-buyer guidance](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/be-prepared/home-buyers/understand-the-risks/)

## Evidence

### Market signal to verify

RNZ reported that Cotality combined average monthly sale prices since 2020 with Cotality flood scores from zero to five, using property history, council data, and other information. [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties)

RNZ reported that Cotality compared flood-zone houses in Auckland and Hawke's Bay with unaffected properties in a 500-metre buffer, and that Auckland flood-zoned houses had a $70,000-$100,000 discount but 18 percent growth since 2020 compared with 13 percent for nearby unaffected properties. [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties)

Those figures should be treated as a research trigger rather than a settled project finding because this discovery pass did not obtain Cotality's underlying data, sampling frame, hedonic controls, or full regional results. [#260](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/260)

### Exposure and data

Earth Sciences New Zealand reported that more than 750,000 people are exposed to one-in-100-year rainfall flooding under the current climate, that this could rise to more than 900,000 people with 3 degrees of additional warming, and that exposed buildings are valued at about $235 billion today and $288 billion under the same warming scenario. [Earth Sciences NZ, 2025](https://www.earthsciences.nz/news/nationwide-study-reveals-escalating-flood-risk)

MfE says the New Zealand Flood Map will combine existing council flood information with new national modelling, provide homeowners, buyers, and sellers access to relevant flood-risk information, and identify gaps in existing data. [MfE New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/)

MfE also says the first public generation of the New Zealand Flood Map is expected by May 2027, with a technical-user national view of existing council flood information expected by the end of 2026. [MfE New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/)

NZDep2023 is available as an SA1-level area-deprivation source through the University of Auckland / Stats NZ ArcGIS service, and the vendored `deprivation-nz` skill reports that decile 1 is least deprived and decile 10 is most deprived. [NZDep2023 ArcGIS item](https://www.arcgis.com/home/item.html?id=d0caefba5f8f42d6918fc52faacec00b); [deprivation-nz skill](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/tree/main/skills/deprivation-nz)

The social-exposure question therefore needs an aggregate hazard-by-area overlay, not anecdotes or address-level examples. [#263](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/263)

### Insurance and Natural Hazards Cover

RBNZ says New Zealand insurers historically took a partly community-rated approach to residential flood risk because modelling and data were insufficient, but insurers are now using combinations of data sources as flood-risk modelling improves. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)

RBNZ says higher premiums for elevated flood-risk properties are being introduced by some insurers and that pressure can increase on other insurers to follow risk-based pricing to avoid a disproportionate share of high-risk properties. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)

RBNZ says full insurance retreat is uncommon so far but that complete withdrawal could happen relatively quickly because insurance contracts are typically annual. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)

Natural Hazards Commission says a homeowner has Natural Hazards Cover if they have a home insurance policy that includes fire insurance, and that the levy is included in the premium paid to the private insurer. [Natural Hazards Commission, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)

Natural Hazards Commission says the current building cover cap is generally $300,000 plus GST, while any cover above that amount is provided by the private insurance policy. [Natural Hazards Commission, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)

The Natural Hazards Insurance Act provides building cover for earthquake, hydrothermal activity, landslide, tsunami, volcanic activity, and consequential fire, but for storm or flood it provides cover for residential land rather than direct residential-building flood damage. [Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/1/en/latest/)

Natural Hazards Commission says land cover is limited to certain areas under and around the home and accessway, is not intended to be comprehensive, and additional land cover cannot be bought from a private insurer. [Natural Hazards Commission, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)

### Lending and disclosure

RBNZ's 2022 residential mortgage flood-risk assessment found that 2.5 percent of participating banks' mortgaged properties were exposed to a 1-in-100-year coastal flood zone with 50 centimetres of sea-level rise, rising to 3.8 percent with 1 metre of sea-level rise. [RBNZ, 2022](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2022/nov-2022/fsr-nov-2022-box-c)

The same RBNZ assessment found that Hawke's Bay had 15 percent of regional mortgage lending in the coastal flood zone at 50 centimetres of sea-level rise and almost 20 percent at 1 metre of sea-level rise, while Auckland river and surface-water flooding under a severe climate outcome placed more than a quarter of participating banks' Auckland mortgage lending in the flood zone. [RBNZ, 2022](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2022/nov-2022/fsr-nov-2022-box-c)

RBNZ's 2023 climate stress-test bulletin says banks generally do not monitor residential-mortgage insurance status after the first 12 months of a loan and recommends cost-effective ways to track mortgage insurance status as climate risk and insurance retreat increase. [RBNZ Climate Stress Test Results, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/bulletins/2024/rbb-2024-87-05.pdf)

Natural Hazards Commission's home-buyer guidance says buyers can get flood-risk information from local or regional councils and that it should be present on a LIM report. [Natural Hazards Commission home-buyer guidance](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/be-prepared/home-buyers/understand-the-risks/)

Horowhenua District Council's September 2025 explanation of the new LIM rules says councils are required to include all natural-hazard information known to them, including information held by other agencies where available, and that the regulations are intended to create clearer and more comparable LIM reports. [Horowhenua District Council, 2025](https://www.horowhenua.govt.nz/News-Events/News/Changes-to-natural-hazard-reporting-in-LIMs)

The lending and disclosure track should distinguish legal availability of information from whether buyers, lenders, insurers, and conveyancers actually use it early enough to change a purchase decision. [#264](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/264)

### Policy and managed retreat

MfE says the New Zealand Flood Map is a key action under the wider National Adaptation Framework. [MfE New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/)

MfE's 2025 Climate Sigma report page says the report estimates the number and value of residential properties expected to experience at least one damaging flood event between 2026 and 2060, that the results are regional and national rather than property-specific, and that the report does not represent government policy. [MfE Climate Sigma report page, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/estimated-number-and-valuation-of-residential-properties-within-inundationflood-zones-impacted-by-climate-change/)

The National Policy Statement for Natural Hazards 2025 came into force on 15 January 2026 and applies to hazards including flooding, coastal inundation, and coastal erosion. [MfE NPS Natural Hazards, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/)

The same National Policy Statement says natural hazard risk from subdivision, use, and development must be managed proportionately, very high natural hazard risk must be avoided, and decisions must be based on the best available information even where that information is uncertain or incomplete. [MfE NPS Natural Hazards, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/)

The managed-retreat track should separate enacted policy, modelling inputs, buyout precedents, planning direction, and live political choices about who pays. [#261](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/261)

## What would change this conclusion

- Public or shared Cotality/CoreLogic methods and data showing that the RNZ value-growth pattern disappears after stronger controls for suburb, dwelling type, renovation, sale mix, land value, or post-flood repair status would weaken the value-paradox premise. [#260](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/260)
- A representative aggregate overlay showing that high flood exposure is not concentrated in lower-income, higher-deprivation, or otherwise lower-resilience communities would weaken the equity concern. [#263](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/263)
- Evidence that insurers continue to offer affordable flood-inclusive cover for high-risk homes, or that lenders routinely verify ongoing flood cover across loan life, would weaken the cliff-edge concern. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2); [RBNZ Climate Stress Test Results, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/bulletins/2024/rbb-2024-87-05.pdf)
- A clear funded adaptation or buyout framework that allocates risk before insurance withdrawal would change the policy conclusion, because this discovery pass found active national-map and planning work but did not establish a settled funding pathway for already-exposed homes. [MfE New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [MfE NPS Natural Hazards, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/)
- I could not verify who is buying flood-risk homes by income, tenure, investor status, ethnicity, or first-home-buyer status; that likely requires linked administrative, mortgage, or survey data and human governance before publication. [#263](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/263)
- I did not publish or inspect address-level examples, because the issue's guardrails prohibit personal or property-level identifying data. [#254](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/254)
- Human review is needed before ideation because the stream touches household financial vulnerability, future insurability, legal disclosure, lending conduct, and managed retreat choices. [docs/STREAMS.md](../docs/STREAMS.md)
