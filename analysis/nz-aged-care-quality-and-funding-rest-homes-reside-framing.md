# NZ aged residential care is a rising-demand, quality-transparency, workforce, and resident-cost problem

Discovery framing for stream root #442 (Part of #442). This frames the residential-aged-care part of the wider aged-care system: rest-home, dementia, psychogeriatric and continuing/hospital-level care funded through Health NZ contracts, resident contributions, income/asset testing, and related regulation [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care); [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html).

## Executive answer

- **Demand pressure is supported.** Health NZ reports around 32,000 people aged 65+ and a further 1,400 younger people living in aged residential care (ARC), and its projection workbook's dashboard scenario rises from about 35,600 average residents in 2024/25 to about 52,200 in 2035/36 [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024); [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- **The quality premise is partially supported but needs a data caveat.** The Aged Care Commissioner says aged care is inconsistent, fragmented and inequitable, HDC reports residential care is disproportionately represented in complaints about older people, and Ngā Paerewa implementation evidence points to resource pressure in aged care providers; however, public audit/certification data is not yet a simple national quality-score dataset for resident outcomes [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).
- **The funding premise is supported with a correction.** Residents with assets above the subsidy thresholds pay contracted care costs up to the regional maximum contribution, and residents under the threshold usually contribute most income while the subsidy pays the balance; the "family co-payment" framing should be corrected to resident/partner means testing plus family/whānau exposure through home equity, residential-care loans, guarantees, navigation, and optional/private charges [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution from 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html).
- **The workforce-stretched premise is supported.** The Aged Care Commissioner links workforce shortages and stretched resources to aged-care challenges, the Ngā Paerewa evaluation says aged-care providers reported disproportionate resource barriers to implementing the standards, and a Health NZ briefing released under the OIA says ARC providers remained concerned funding rates were too low and the sector depended on internationally qualified nurses [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf); [Health NZ briefing released by Ombudsman, 2024](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-11/OIA-202400209-Health-NZ-Briefing-Aged-Residential-Care-29012024.pdf).
- **Comparator research is useful but should not assume Australia or England has the answer.** Australia exposes residential-care fees through My Aged Care and publishes Star Ratings by Residents' Experience, Compliance, Staffing and Quality Measures, while England's CQC publishes rating methods and the NHS describes self-funding thresholds; those systems are comparison surfaces, not proven solutions for NZ [My Aged Care, aged care home costs and fees](https://www.myagedcare.gov.au/aged-care-home-costs-and-fees); [Australian Government, Star Ratings](https://www.health.gov.au/our-work/star-ratings-for-residential-aged-care); [Care Quality Commission, how we reach a rating](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/assessing-quality-and-performance/reach-rating); [NHS, self-funding care](https://www.nhs.uk/social-care-and-support/money-work-and-benefits/paying-for-your-own-care-self-funding/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium-High. The demand, subsidy, maximum-contribution, certification and policy-process claims rest mainly on official NZ sources; the weaker parts are the still-unquantified extent of quality variation, provider-level staffing impacts, and what families actually pay beyond the contracted-care maximum [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** ARC includes rest-home, dementia, psychogeriatric and continuing/hospital-level care; older people access long-term ARC after a needs assessment; audits against Health and Disability Services Standards are used for certification; and contracted care can be paid by Health NZ, ACC or the resident depending on eligibility [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care). The Ministry of Health's 2026 notice sets the maximum weekly resident contribution for long-term contracted ARC from 1 July 2026 and states that it replaces the 2025 notice [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution from 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026). Work and Income administers a residential-care subsidy and loan regime based on age, care need, income and assets [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html).

**What is interpretation:** the best stream frame is not "rest homes are bad" or "families pay an adult-child co-pay", but "NZ needs to know whether rising demand, uneven quality signals, workforce constraints, and means-tested resident costs are aligned with a sustainable and fair care model." That synthesis follows from official demand projections, HDC/Aged Care Commissioner quality signals, subsidy rules, and current reform work, but it is this framing's judgement rather than a quoted policy conclusion [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).

**Main limits:** the projection workbook gives a national demand model but does not by itself explain regional bed supply, ownership, Māori/Pacific equity or workforce capacity [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). HDC complaints, HealthCERT audits and standards-certification evidence are quality signals, but they are not equivalent to resident outcome measures without careful denominator, severity and provider-context work [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf). The public funding data found here includes a 2021/22 system-flow estimate from the Sapere phase-one review, so any current dollar split should be rechecked against 2026/27 funding, provider accounts and official appropriations before G1 decisions [Sapere, Review of Aged Care Funding and Services Models](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/6374386fb05848183f325148/t/65417f990c5b8f0a615a3dfa/1698799521490/Review+of+Aged+Care+Funding+and+Services+Models.pdf); [Beehive, 2026 funding uplift](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector). This framing uses only public aggregate information and does not include resident or staff personal data.

## Problem statement

NZ's residential aged-care problem is becoming a combined capacity, quality-transparency, workforce and resident-cost problem: the number of residents is projected to rise sharply, regulatory quality data is fragmented across audits and complaints, providers report workforce and funding pressure, and residents/partners face means-tested contributions that can require using income, assets or home equity [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html).

The stream should not start by assuming a single fix, because the policy question cuts across demand forecasts, service quality, provider viability, staffing, resident affordability, and the balance between ageing at home and moving into ARC [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review); [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).

## Who it affects

The directly affected group is the roughly 32,000 people aged 65+ in ARC and the further 1,400 younger people with long-term conditions or disabilities who live in ARC, alongside residents' whānau and unpaid supporters who navigate needs assessment, financial assessment, placement and ongoing care decisions [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024); [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care). Health NZ's Health and Independence Report says about 48,000 older people access needs-assessment services each year and more than 80,000 older people receive funded home and community support services, so ARC demand sits inside a larger continuum of older-person care [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024).

The future affected group is materially larger: the same Health NZ report says people aged 65+ were 16.5% of the population in 2024 and are projected to be 18.3% by 2034, while the 85+ population is projected to almost double from 105,000 to 202,000 by 2044 [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024). Health NZ's ARC projection workbook, read from its dashboard worksheet, projects the national all-care-levels "base pop + aligned" series from about 35,632 average residents in 2024/25 to about 52,174 in 2035/36, with rest-home residents rising from about 13,857 to about 20,549 and continuing/hospital-level residents from about 13,985 to about 20,831 over the same period [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

The cost exposure is substantial for residents with assets above the subsidy threshold: from 1 July 2026, the maximum weekly contribution for long-term contracted ARC ranges across listed territorial/local statistical-area regions from $1,513.33 to $1,634.43, inclusive of GST, and the Ministry says the maximum contribution is the maximum weekly amount a needs-assessed resident is required to pay for contracted care services in the region of their facility [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution from 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026).

## What is already being done

The regulatory baseline already exists: the Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001 establishes certification for health and disability service providers, provides for audit requirements, and allows cancellation of certification on specified grounds [Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2001/0093/latest/whole.html). Health NZ states that ARC services are assessed through audits against Health and Disability Services Standards, and the Ministry publishes rest-home audit reports through the certified-provider system [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care).

The reform baseline is active rather than settled: Health NZ's Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review examined the current state across consumer journey, provider landscape, workforce, quality and outcomes, funding and financial flows, and Health NZ says review findings were handed to the Ministerial Advisory Group [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review); [Sapere, Review of Aged Care Funding and Services Models](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/6374386fb05848183f325148/t/65417f990c5b8f0a615a3dfa/1698799521490/Review+of+Aged+Care+Funding+and+Services+Models.pdf). The Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group began work in January 2026, is expected to report by mid-2026, and is scoped to advise on sustainable aged-care beds, the sharing of care costs between recipients and government, contracting, regulation, service innovation, and integration with wider health and disability services [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).

The near-term funding baseline has also moved: on 17 June 2026, Ministers announced a 4% 2026/27 funding uplift worth about $79 million for ARC providers, including a 2.66% base increase and targeted investment for service delivery, responsiveness to demand and access to care [Beehive, 2026 funding uplift](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector). That uplift is current evidence of government action, but it does not itself answer whether the underlying funding model is sustainable, equitable, or aligned with workforce and quality outcomes [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).

## Hypothesis checks

### H1 — "How many Kiwis are in residential aged care and how many are projected by 2035?"

**Supported, with a precise baseline.** Health NZ's 2024 Health and Independence Report gives a current point-in-time headline of around 32,000 people aged 65+ in ARC plus 1,400 younger residents, while the Health NZ projection workbook gives a 2035/36 national projection around 52,200 average residents under the base-population-plus-aligned dashboard series [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024); [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). The child research should establish whether 2035 is best treated as financial year 2034/35 or 2035/36, because the workbook gives about 50,647 in 2034/35 and about 52,174 in 2035/36 under that dashboard series [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

### H2 — "Quality varies significantly, staffing is stretched, and inspection data can show it"

**Partially supported.** The Aged Care Commissioner's 2023 report says aged care is inconsistent and fragmented, support structures vary significantly across the country, and workforce shortages and stretched resources contribute to challenges [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/). HDC's 2025 annual report says people aged 65+ are over-represented in complaints and that 31% of complaints about older people related to residential care compared with 4% of complaints about the general population [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). The correction is that complaints and audits are not a clean outcome dataset by themselves, so the research task is to translate HealthCERT audit findings, HDC complaints/decisions and certification conditions into a defensible quality-variation picture with clear denominators and caveats [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care); [Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2001/0093/latest/whole.html).

### H3 — "The funding split is government subsidy plus family co-payment"

**Supported on complexity, corrected on who formally pays.** The formal split is not a simple family co-payment: Work and Income says eligibility for the Residential Care Subsidy depends on age/circumstances, assessed long-term care need, and income/asset tests, and residents under the threshold generally contribute most income while keeping specified allowances [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html). Residents above the asset threshold pay the full cost of contracted care up to the regional maximum contribution, while the Ministry says government pays extra costs above the maximum contribution for hospital or dementia-level care [Ministry of Health, Long-term residential care booklet](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/long-term-residential-care-for-older-people-what-you-need-to-know-2012); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution from 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026). Families and whānau still matter because the asset test can include the home and vehicle depending on partner/home circumstances, residential-care loans can be secured against a home and repaid when the home is sold or the estate is settled, and providers may ask for a guarantor even though the Ministry says a guarantor cannot be a condition of admission [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html); [Ministry of Health, Long-term residential care booklet](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/long-term-residential-care-for-older-people-what-you-need-to-know-2012).

### H4 — "Staffing shortfalls affect care quality"

**Supported as a research direction, not yet quantified in this framing.** The Aged Care Commissioner links workforce shortages and stretched resources to aged-care challenges, the Ngā Paerewa evaluation says aged-care providers reported the least sector support for implementing the standards and that resources, time, workforce and finance were major barriers, and a Health NZ briefing released by the Ombudsman says the sector depends on internationally qualified nurses and that a 2022-23 campaign supported 551 overseas-trained registered nurses into ARC by the end of November 2023 [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf); [Health NZ briefing released by Ombudsman, 2024](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-11/OIA-202400209-Health-NZ-Briefing-Aged-Residential-Care-29012024.pdf). The child research needs to quantify staff mix, vacancies, dependence on internationally qualified nurses, turnover, pay-equity/funding settings and any credible links between staffing measures and audit, complaint or resident-outcome indicators [Health NZ briefing released by Ombudsman, 2024](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-11/OIA-202400209-Health-NZ-Briefing-Aged-Residential-Care-29012024.pdf).

### H5 — "NZ should compare itself to Australia, the UK or OECD models"

**Supported as comparator research, with caution.** Australia is a strong comparator because My Aged Care publicly explains fee types, means assessment and government contribution, and Australia publishes Star Ratings overall and across Residents' Experience, Compliance, Staffing and Quality Measures [My Aged Care, aged care home costs and fees](https://www.myagedcare.gov.au/aged-care-home-costs-and-fees); [Australian Government, Star Ratings](https://www.health.gov.au/our-work/star-ratings-for-residential-aged-care). England is a useful but non-identical comparator because the Care Quality Commission publishes rating methods for services and the NHS explains when self-funding applies for social care, including the upper capital limit used in England [Care Quality Commission, how we reach a rating](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/assessing-quality-and-performance/reach-rating); [NHS, self-funding care](https://www.nhs.uk/social-care-and-support/money-work-and-benefits/paying-for-your-own-care-self-funding/). The child research should compare instruments and tradeoffs, not assume that a rating system or means-test transplant would solve NZ's workforce, regional-access or resident-affordability constraints [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **research: How many people use NZ aged residential care now, who is projected to need it by 2035, and where will pressure concentrate?** Establish the demand baseline across care levels, regions, age groups and any publicly available ethnicity or deprivation dimensions, starting with Health NZ's ARC projection workbook and Health and Independence Report [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024).
2. **research: What does public quality evidence show about variation in NZ aged residential care?** Translate HealthCERT audit reports, certification conditions, HDC/Aged Care Commissioner evidence and Ngā Paerewa implementation findings into a defensible picture of quality variation, while separating complaint rates, audit non-conformance, enforcement and resident outcomes [Health NZ, Aged residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/older-peoples-health/aged-residential-care); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).
3. **research: How does NZ's residential care funding split work in practice, and what do residents and families typically pay?** Map subsidy, income and asset testing, maximum contribution, higher-care public top-ups, residential-care loans, guarantees, optional/premium charges, and current public-private funding flows using Work and Income, Ministry of Health and Health NZ/Sapere sources [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Sapere, Review of Aged Care Funding and Services Models](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/6374386fb05848183f325148/t/65417f990c5b8f0a615a3dfa/1698799521490/Review+of+Aged+Care+Funding+and+Services+Models.pdf).
4. **research: What staffing constraints does NZ aged residential care face, and how do they affect care quality and viability?** Quantify registered nurse, caregiver, allied-health and manager constraints; examine dependence on internationally qualified workers; and test any link to delayed admissions, audit findings, HDC complaints or provider viability with sources that avoid anecdote-only conclusions [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [Health NZ briefing released by Ombudsman, 2024](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-11/OIA-202400209-Health-NZ-Briefing-Aged-Residential-Care-29012024.pdf).
5. **research: What can NZ learn from Australia, England and OECD long-term-care models without importing their failures?** Compare quality transparency, staffing measures, resident means testing, public subsidies, accommodation charges, regulatory ratings and known implementation problems, starting with My Aged Care, Australian Star Ratings, CQC, NHS care-funding guidance and OECD long-term-care affordability work [My Aged Care, aged care home costs and fees](https://www.myagedcare.gov.au/aged-care-home-costs-and-fees); [Australian Government, Star Ratings](https://www.health.gov.au/our-work/star-ratings-for-residential-aged-care); [Care Quality Commission, how we reach a rating](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/assessing-quality-and-performance/reach-rating); [OECD, Is Care Affordable for Older People?](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/is-care-affordable-for-older-people_450ea778-en.html).
6. **research: Which current policy reforms and organisations are changing NZ aged care, and where are the live decision points?** Map the Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group, Health NZ funding-and-service-model review, Health Committee inquiry input, HDC/Aged Care Commissioner recommendations, 2026/27 funding uplift, pay-equity and immigration settings, and decision points before the stream reaches G1 [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group); [Beehive, 2026 funding uplift](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current official dataset showing stable or falling ARC demand by 2035 after modelling home-and-community-substitution would weaken the capacity-pressure frame; this framing found Health NZ's workbook pointing the other way [Health NZ, ARC projection model](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- Provider-level quality research showing little variation after adjusting for resident acuity, provider size and complaint/reporting propensity would weaken the quality-variation frame; this framing found only directional evidence from complaints, audits and standards implementation [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).
- Current 2026/27 funding-flow data showing the 2021/22 Sapere public/private split has materially changed would alter the funding question and should be checked before any synthesis relies on dollar shares [Sapere, Review of Aged Care Funding and Services Models](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/6374386fb05848183f325148/t/65417f990c5b8f0a615a3dfa/1698799521490/Review+of+Aged+Care+Funding+and+Services+Models.pdf); [Beehive, 2026 funding uplift](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector).
- Lived-experience input from residents, whānau, care workers, providers, Māori and Pacific aged-care organisations, and disability advocates is needed before G1 because official datasets do not show the full experience of placement, cultural safety, daily care, family stress or end-of-life dignity [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group).
