# NZ hospital waiting pressure is real, but the public data does not yet show which fixes move the system

Discovery framing for stream root #434 (Part of #434). This frames the problem of public hospital waiting times and emergency department (ED) crowding in New Zealand, tests the root's claims against current public evidence, and proposes child research questions for the runner to open.

## Executive answer

- **H1: "Current ED wait times and elective backlog figures are available for 2024-2026" -- supported, with a measurement correction.** New Zealand has current quarterly health-target reporting for ED six-hour performance, first specialist assessment (FSA) waits, and elective-treatment waits, but the current headline figures are percentages meeting targets, not a single transparent "backlog" count for all patients waiting [Ministry of Health, health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets); [Health NZ, health targets](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-targets). For January-March 2026, the Government reported 74.4% of ED patients admitted, discharged, or transferred within six hours, 61.2% of FSA patients seen within four months, and 64.9% of elective-treatment patients treated within four months, all below the 95% target but improved year-on-year [Beehive, 23 June 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets). For October-December 2025, the equivalent reported results were 74.2% for ED, 62.2% for FSA, and 64.5% for elective treatment [Beehive, 10 April 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/health-targets-delivering-new-zealanders).
- **H2: "NZ hospital wait times compare poorly with OECD/Australia/UK benchmarks" -- partially supported, but not one-to-one.** OECD 2025 says New Zealand and Slovenia were the notable countries where elective-surgery waiting times continued to increase on both presented metrics after the pandemic, and OECD's New Zealand country note records fewer hospital beds than the OECD average, but ED target definitions differ across countries [OECD waiting-times chapter, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html); [OECD New Zealand country note, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html). Australia reports ED time-to-clinical-care by triage category and elective-surgery admission waits, while England reports A&E four-hour and referral-to-treatment standards, so the stream needs a careful comparator design rather than direct headline ranking [AIHW ED care 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments); [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times); [NHS England A&E statistics](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/ae-waiting-times-and-activity/); [NHS England RTT statistics](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/rtt-waiting-times/).
- **H3: "Fast-track EDs, satellite theatres, GP access funds and similar interventions have been tried, and some may work" -- partially supported.** New Zealand's current public intervention menu includes ED staffing investment, fast-tracked hospital wards, aged-residential-care capacity, urgent and after-hours healthcare, Elective Boost private-sector purchasing, expanded cataract sessions, theatre-productivity work, an FSA Accelerate Programme, and musculoskeletal triage pathways [DPMC ED target factsheet, September 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf); [DPMC elective-treatment factsheet, March 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf); [Health NZ Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf). What is not yet supported is a claim that any one 2024-2026 intervention has causally moved national wait times; current public releases mostly pair intervention descriptions with before/after target results, which is not enough for attribution [Beehive, 23 June 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets); [Ministry of Health planned-care briefing, January 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/H2025060008%20Output%20measures%20for%20elective%20treatment%20and%20first%20specialist%20assessment%20volumes.pdf).
- **H4: "The cost of delayed care includes clinical, mental-health and productivity harm" -- supported for clinical harm; incomplete for NZ mental-health and productivity quantification.** A New Zealand ED cohort study of 25 acute hospitals and 5,793,767 ED visits found higher seven-day mortality when more than 10% of current ED patients had access block, while a Health Research Council summary of an earlier NZ six-hour-target study reported about 700 fewer ED deaths than predicted after the target was introduced [Jones & van der Werf, *Emergency Medicine Australasia*, 2020 abstract via ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/347569522_Emergency_department_crowding_and_mortality_for_patients_presenting_to_emergency_departments_in_New_Zealand); [HRC, 2016](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/fewer-deaths-eds-when-length-stay-reduced). The root's mental-health and productivity-cost claims are plausible but need specific public sources for NZ public-hospital waits; this framing did not find a current official national estimate that turns delayed ED/FSA/elective care into productivity dollars or mental-health burden.
- **H5: "Staffing shortfalls correlate with wait-time spikes" -- plausible, but not yet supported by public national evidence.** Health NZ says general nursing vacancies improved from an estimated 10.8% in 2023 to 6.8% in 2024, while still noting specialist nursing needs and future demand pressure [Health NZ workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context). Health NZ's nursing analysis still estimated a current all-nurse shortage of 2,250, the Medical Council reported 20,530 practising doctors in 2025 and a 37.1% increase in emergency-medicine vocational-scope doctors since 2020, and Health NZ's workforce implementation plan covers 2024/25-2026/27 actions [Health NZ nursing analysis](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/workforce-plan-profession-specific-analysis/health-workforce-plan-profession-nursing-analysis); [Medical Council workforce survey 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf); [Health NZ workforce implementation plan](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-workforce-implementation-plan). None of those sources, by itself, establishes a district-level staffing-to-wait-time correlation.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The headline NZ target definitions and 2025-2026 results are well supported by official sources; the comparator and intervention-attribution questions are weaker because public systems use different definitions and public releases do not isolate causal effects.

## Problem statement

New Zealand's public hospital waiting-time problem is best framed as a whole-system flow problem, not just as people "waiting in ED." The current official targets cover three connected points: ED patients should be admitted, discharged or transferred within six hours; referred patients should receive a first specialist assessment within four months; and patients committed to elective treatment should receive that treatment within four months [Ministry of Health, health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets). The latest public results show improvement but still a large gap from target: 74.4% ED six-hour performance, 61.2% FSA under four months, and 64.9% elective treatment under four months in January-March 2026 [Beehive, 23 June 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets).

This affects anyone relying on public hospital care, with particular pressure on people who cannot substitute private specialist or surgical care. Hato Hone St John reported 706,194 emergency 111 ambulance calls in 2025, up nearly 30% over five years, and 551,399 emergency ambulance incidents, up 17% since 2020 [Hato Hone St John, 27 January 2026](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/). Planned care also touches large numbers: 178,349 first specialist assessments and 51,089 elective-waitlist treatments were completed in January-March 2026, both higher than the same quarter a year earlier [Beehive, 23 June 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets).

The core research need is to separate five things that are often blended together: (1) whether public waiting-time performance is improving or deteriorating by measure and district; (2) whether New Zealand is an outlier once comparable definitions are used; (3) which interventions have credible evidence of effect; (4) what delayed care costs patients and the economy; and (5) whether workforce or bed capacity explains the district-level pattern.

## What is already being done

The Government reintroduced five health targets from 1 July 2024, including ED six-hour performance, FSA waits and elective-treatment waits, and the Ministry says the targets sit in the Government Policy Statement on Health 2024-2027 [Ministry of Health, health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets). Health NZ says the targets are ambitious and that high-level implementation plans set out short- and medium-term activities to improve performance [Health NZ, health targets](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-targets).

The ED-side intervention list includes a $20 million ED boost for frontline staffing, expanded Nelson ED treatment spaces, 140 fast-tracked hospital beds planned for the second half of 2026 across Middlemore, Waikato, Wellington, Nelson and Hawke's Bay hospitals, Budget 2025 funding to increase aged-residential-care capacity, Budget 2025 funding for urgent and after-hours healthcare, and a new 24/7 digital health service [DPMC ED target factsheet, September 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf). Hato Hone St John says ambulance demand is rising because of wider system pressures, ageing population and rising acuity, but the public ambulance source found here does not publish hospital handover-delay minutes or ramping hours for New Zealand [Hato Hone St John, 27 January 2026](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/).

The planned-care intervention list includes Health NZ's Elective Boost partnership with the private sector to provide an additional 21,000 hip, knee, cataract and other procedures through June 2026, a focus on long-waiting patients, expanded Hutt Valley and Porirua cataract sessions, and theatre-productivity improvements [DPMC elective-treatment factsheet, March 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf). Health NZ's Q1 2025/26 factsheets also cite an Accelerate Programme committing to 800 additional FSA clinics by mid-December 2025 and a musculoskeletal pathway operating in 13 districts to triage non-urgent orthopaedic FSA referrals to physiotherapy-led care [Health NZ Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).

Health NZ's workforce response sits alongside the flow response: its workforce implementation plan, published 13 January 2026, covers 2024/25 to 2026/27 and implements the Health Workforce Plan 2024 and Mental Health and Addiction Workforce Plan [Health NZ workforce implementation plan](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/publications/health-workforce-implementation-plan). The workforce baseline is mixed: Health NZ reports improvement in general nursing vacancies but continuing specialist nursing needs, and the Medical Council reports a growing doctor workforce with emergency-medicine vocational-scope doctors increasing from 350 in 2020 to 480 in 2025 [Health NZ workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context); [Medical Council workforce survey 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf).

## Evidence and hypothesis tests

### H1 -- Current NZ wait-time figures exist, but "backlog" needs definitions

The root's request for current ED and elective-surgery data is supported because official target definitions and quarterly results are public [Ministry of Health, health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets); [Health NZ, health targets](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-targets). The correction is that "wait times" is not one metric: ED reporting uses the share admitted, discharged or transferred within six hours, FSA reporting uses the share waiting less than four months from referral, and elective-treatment reporting uses the share waiting less than four months after commitment to treatment [Ministry of Health, health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets).

The current national picture is below target despite recent improvement. The January-March 2026 results were 74.4% for ED six-hour performance, 61.2% for FSA, and 64.9% for elective treatment [Beehive, 23 June 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets). The October-December 2025 results were 74.2% for ED, 62.2% for FSA, and 64.5% for elective treatment [Beehive, 10 April 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/health-targets-delivering-new-zealanders). Health NZ's Q1 2025/26 factsheets reported 62.3% for FSA and 54.7% as the national elective-treatment result in the district chart text extracted by the built-in web tool, while the DPMC March 2026 elective factsheet reported 64.5% for the quarter ending March 2026, so a child issue must reconcile quarter naming, extraction dates and target definitions before making trend claims [Health NZ Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf); [DPMC elective-treatment factsheet, March 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf).

**Grade: supported with correction.** Data exists, but the stream should not use "backlog" as a single number unless it specifies FSA over-four-month waits, elective over-four-month waits, long waits over 365 days, or another denominator.

### H2 -- International comparisons are useful, but headline comparisons are fragile

OECD 2025 gives a strong signal that New Zealand's elective-surgery waiting-time trend is concerning: after pandemic increases, OECD says waiting times returned or started returning to pre-pandemic levels in most countries, but continued to increase in New Zealand and Slovenia on both elective-waiting metrics presented [OECD waiting-times chapter, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html). OECD's New Zealand country note also records 2.5 hospital beds per 1,000 population compared with an OECD average of 4.2, while reporting 3.7 practising doctors per 1,000 population against an OECD average of 3.9 and 11.7 practising nurses per 1,000 against an OECD average of 9.2 [OECD New Zealand country note, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html).

Australia and England are relevant comparators but use different measures. Australia reported in 2024-25 that 67% of ED patients were seen on time for their triage category, with median ED waiting time of 18 minutes and 90th percentile waiting time of 1 hour 58 minutes, while elective-surgery reporting says 6.0% waited more than 365 days and media-release text says the median public elective-surgery wait was 45 days [AIHW ED care 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments); [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times); [AIHW media release, December 2025](https://www.aihw.gov.au/news-media/media-releases/2025/2025-december/elective-surgery-admissions-continue-to-rise-while-wait-times-stabilise). England reports A&E attendance and emergency-admission statistics separately from consultant-led referral-to-treatment statistics; UK Parliament's June 2026 briefing states the A&E target is 95% within four hours and that 39% of major A&E attendances in 2025/26 lasted longer than four hours [NHS England A&E statistics](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/ae-waiting-times-and-activity/); [UK Parliament NHS key statistics, June 2026](https://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/CBP-7281/CBP-7281.pdf).

**Grade: partially supported.** NZ appears poor on some elective-wait trends and has low beds per capita, but ED and elective comparisons require a methods-first child issue.

### H3 -- Interventions exist; causal attribution is the weak point

The historical six-hour ED target is the clearest NZ intervention with published outcome evidence. The Health Research Council summary of the HRC-funded study says the target was introduced in 2009, DHBs invested about $52 million between 2008 and 2012, average hospital length of stay reduced by about seven hours, ED stays reduced by over an hour, and the intervention was associated with about 700 fewer ED deaths than predicted [HRC, 2016](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/fewer-deaths-eds-when-length-stay-reduced). That source also reports mechanisms that matter for current research: process improvements, ED-to-hospital flow, investment in ED facilities, and short-stay capacity [HRC, 2016](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/fewer-deaths-eds-when-length-stay-reduced).

For 2024-2026, the public record is stronger on inputs than on outcomes. ED initiatives include staffing, beds, aged-residential-care capacity, urgent after-hours care and digital care [DPMC ED target factsheet, September 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf). Planned-care initiatives include Elective Boost, long-waiter focus, cataract sessions and theatre productivity [DPMC elective-treatment factsheet, March 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf). FSA initiatives include the Accelerate Programme and musculoskeletal pathway [Health NZ Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf). The Ministry's January 2025 planned-care briefing warns that percentages can mask activity volume and says the number of FSA assessments completed in Q4 2024/25 was about 12,000 higher than Q4 2023/24 even though the proportion seen within the target timeframe decreased [Ministry of Health planned-care briefing, January 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/H2025060008%20Output%20measures%20for%20elective%20treatment%20and%20first%20specialist%20assessment%20volumes.pdf).

**Grade: partially supported.** The stream can study interventions now, but it must distinguish activity increases, target-percentage movement and causal impact.

### H4 -- Delayed care has clinical harm; other costs need NZ quantification

Clinical harm from ED crowding is supported by NZ evidence. Jones and van der Werf's NZ retrospective cohort study reported 25 of 28 eligible acute hospitals, 5,793,767 ED visits, and higher seven-day mortality when more than 10% of current ED patients had access block; its abstract concludes access block had the strongest association with seven-day mortality [Jones & van der Werf, 2020 abstract via ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/347569522_Emergency_department_crowding_and_mortality_for_patients_presenting_to_emergency_departments_in_New_Zealand). The HRC target study summary independently supports the proposition that reducing ED length of stay and crowding can affect mortality [HRC, 2016](https://www.hrc.govt.nz/news-and-events/fewer-deaths-eds-when-length-stay-reduced).

The public record found here is weaker on mental-health and productivity costs from delayed hospital care. The root should not assume a single national dollar cost until research checks ACC, Health NZ, Treasury, MSD, productivity-commission-style material, peer-reviewed NZ studies, and international economic models that are transferable to NZ.

**Grade: supported for clinical harm; incomplete for mental-health and productivity quantification.**

### H5 -- Workforce matters, but correlation needs district-level data

Health NZ's workforce documents support a nuanced staffing baseline. Health NZ says it made major progress closing general nurse workforce gaps, moving from 26,505 nursing FTE and estimated 10.8% vacancies in 2023 to 29,400 nursing FTE and estimated 6.8% vacancies in 2024, while still needing specialist nursing roles and noting stronger hospital growth than primary/community growth [Health NZ workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context). Health NZ's nursing analysis states the nursing workforce had about 78,700 practising-certificate holders in March 2024, with a current all-nurse shortage estimate of 2,250 and a projected 2033 shortage of 4,120 on top of the current pipeline [Health NZ nursing analysis](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/workforce-plan-profession-specific-analysis/health-workforce-plan-profession-nursing-analysis).

The medical workforce is also growing but uneven by scope. The Medical Council reports 20,530 practising doctors in 2025, up 2.6% from 2024, and says emergency-medicine vocational-scope doctors rose from 350 in 2020 to 480 in 2025 [Medical Council workforce survey 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf). That growth does not prove adequate staffing in the places or shifts where ED and planned-care bottlenecks occur, and no public source found here directly correlates district vacancies with quarterly ED/FSA/elective performance.

**Grade: plausible but unproven.** A child issue should test staffing, beds, occupancy and demand together rather than treating vacancies as the sole driver.

## Proposed child research questions

1. **What is the 2024-2026 baseline for NZ ED, FSA and elective-treatment waits by district, population group and specialty?** This should reconcile Health NZ downloadable data, DPMC factsheets and ministerial releases, and turn percentages into people where public denominators allow it.
2. **How does NZ compare with Australia, England and OECD countries once waiting-time definitions are made comparable?** This should avoid false rankings by mapping NZ's six-hour ED target, Australia's triage-time measures, England's four-hour A&E/18-week RTT measures, and OECD elective-surgery metrics.
3. **Which NZ interventions since 2024 have credible evidence of improving waits or throughput, and which are only activity announcements?** This should test ED staffing/beds, urgent after-hours access, Elective Boost, cataract-session expansion, theatre productivity, Accelerate FSA clinics, and musculoskeletal triage pathways against trend data.
4. **What is the clinical, mental-health and productivity cost of delayed ED, specialist-assessment and elective care in NZ?** This should start with NZ ED mortality studies, then look for NZ hospital-wait economic burden, productivity loss, whānau cost and mental-health evidence, marking where only international transfer estimates exist.
5. **Do workforce, bed capacity, ambulance demand and aged-care discharge constraints explain district-level wait-time spikes?** This should combine Health NZ workforce data, Medical Council/Nursing Council data, hospital-bed and occupancy indicators, Hato Hone St John demand, and any public discharge-delay or aged-care-capacity measures.

## What would change this framing

- A public Health NZ downloadable dataset showing materially different March 2026 target results from the ministerial releases would change the current baseline and confidence rating.
- District-level analysis showing strong improvements before specific interventions, or no improvements after them, would change the intervention framing from "uncertain effect" to "weak evidence of impact."
- A robust NZ economic study linking delayed public hospital care to productivity, welfare, ACC or mental-health outcomes would strengthen the cost-of-delay question and may add a dedicated child issue.
- Public ambulance offload/ramping data by hospital would sharpen the ED-overcrowding framing; this framing found ambulance demand data but not a national ramping time series.
- Clinical review by emergency physicians, surgeons, nurses, ambulance leaders, hospital operations managers and patient advocates could correct the operational model before G1.
