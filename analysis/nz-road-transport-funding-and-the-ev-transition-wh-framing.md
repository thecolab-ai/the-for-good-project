# NZ road funding faces a staged RUC transition, not an immediate petrol-tax collapse

Discovery framing for stream root #444 (Part of #444). This tests the founder premise against current official evidence and proposes child research questions for the road-funding, EV-transition, RUC, charging-infrastructure and comparator-country workstream.

## Executive answer

- **The core problem is real, but the timing is corrected.** Petrol excise duty has not yet collapsed: in 2024/25 the National Land Transport Fund recorded net road user charges (RUC) of $1.998b and net fuel excise duty (FED) of $1.926b, only slightly below 2023/24 net FED of $1.933b, while Ministry of Transport modelling says FED is expected to remain stable in the short to medium term, peak around 2035, and decline over the longer term as fewer vehicles use petrol [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf); [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).
- **The current funding model is more than petrol tax.** The NLTF is funded primarily by road users through FED, RUC, motor vehicle registration and licensing fees, track user charges and other revenues, and it is topped up by Crown grants/loans and local-government funding; Ministry advice says local government contributes about $1b annually towards the local road network and public transport, mainly through rates [Ministry of Transport, Road User Charges system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system); [Ministry of Transport, Revenue Action Plan cabinet paper, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Proactive-Release-Cab-Revenue-Action-Plan-for-web.pdf).
- **The policy direction is not just "RUC for EVs"; it is "RUC for all light vehicles eventually".** Light electric vehicles and plug-in hybrids entered RUC from 1 April 2024, and the Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill is now in Parliament to modernise RUC and tolling before any full petrol-fleet transition; as at 4 July 2026 the Bill had completed second reading on 30 June 2026 and had not reached third reading or Royal Assent [Beehive, EVs to pay RUC, 2024](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/electric-vehicles-pay-road-user-charges); [Parliament, Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/d31385e9-dd9d-4cbd-c52d-08de22297aa7?Tab=history&lang=en).
- **The EV-charging concern is supported, but the urban/rural gap still needs proper mapping.** Government says New Zealand had a bit over 1,800 public charge points in March 2026, is targeting 10,000 by 2030, and announced 2,574 additional charge points through $52.7m in zero-interest loans plus private co-investment; EECA's dashboard says it is updated quarterly and draws on EVRoam, EECA co-funded projects, NZTA registrations and Stats NZ population data, but it also warns that some charge-point counts are calculated and may understate total infrastructure [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/charging-ahead-2500-ev-chargers-way); [EECA Public EV Charger Dashboard](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/data-tools/public-ev-charger-dashboard/).

**Overall confidence: Medium-High.** The revenue, legislation and programme facts are grounded in official NZ sources, but the future revenue path depends on model assumptions, the current charger datasets have definitional caveats, and distributional impacts cannot be settled without more detailed modelling and human policy judgement.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** Net 2024/25 NLTF revenue by source was RUC $1.998b, FED $1.926b, motor vehicle registration and annual licensing fees $287m, and track user charges $17m [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf). RUC applies to diesel vehicles, heavy vehicles and electric vehicles as a pre-paid per-kilometre charge, while FED applies to petrol and is included in the pump price [Ministry of Transport, Road User Charges system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system). The standard petrol excise rate returned to 70.024 cents per litre from 1 July 2023, and RUC applies to electric and plug-in hybrid vehicles from 1 April 2024 [Ministry of Transport, rates of petrol excise duty and RUC](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges).

**What is interpretation:** This framing treats the real problem as *transition design* rather than an already-open revenue hole. That interpretation follows from the combination of stable recent FED accounts, official long-run modelling, and government work to modernise RUC before shifting the 3.5m remaining light petrol vehicles [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf); [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf); [Beehive, next steps on replacing petrol tax, 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/next-steps-replacing-petrol-tax-electronic-road-user-charges).

**Main limits:** The Ministry's vehicle-fleet and revenue forecasts are modelled scenarios, not observed outcomes, and the RIS says the full transition date has not been set [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). Public EV-charger counts vary by definition: EECA distinguishes charging units, points and sites, and says some charge-point counts are calculated because EVRoam does not currently record charge points directly [EECA Public EV Charger Dashboard](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/data-tools/public-ev-charger-dashboard/). This framing does not make legal advice about the Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill, and it does not settle privacy, equity, rural access or compliance design; those are child research tasks.

## Problem statement

New Zealand is moving from a land-transport revenue model where petrol use acts as a rough proxy for road use to one where distance, vehicle type and vehicle weight are intended to carry more of the funding load. In 2024/25, RUC and FED together supplied $3.924b of the NLTF's $4.228b net land-transport revenue, but the same system already relies on motor vehicle registration/licensing fees, local rates, Crown support and tolling decisions [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf); [Ministry of Transport, Revenue Action Plan cabinet paper, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Proactive-Release-Cab-Revenue-Action-Plan-for-web.pdf).

The founder premise should therefore be reframed: the question is not "what happens now that petrol tax has collapsed?", but "can New Zealand move from fuel-based charging to a universal, enforceable, fair, privacy-credible and administratively cheap RUC model before petrol, hybrid and EV fleet changes materially weaken FED?" The Ministry's 2025 RIS says EV uptake is expected to be the primary future driver of declining excise revenue, could add 0.7m to 1.1m EVs by 2035, and could see light vehicles subject to RUC surpass petrol vehicles in the early 2040s [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

## Who is affected

The affected population is effectively all New Zealand residents because the land-transport system funds road maintenance, road improvements, public transport and road safety, and Stats NZ estimated New Zealand's population at 5,361,300 on 31 March 2026 [Ministry of Transport, Road User Charges system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system); [Stats NZ, population indicator](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/).

Vehicle owners are directly affected because the Ministry estimated that, in 2023, about 3.3m of more than 4.5m vehicles were light petrol passenger or goods vehicles paying FED, about 200,000 motorcycles also used petrol and contributed via FED, and about 1.18m vehicles already paid RUC, including over 900,000 light diesel vehicles, 180,000 heavy vehicles, 70,000 light battery EVs and 30,000 light plug-in hybrids [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

Ratepayers and taxpayers are affected because local government contributes about $1b annually toward local roads and public transport, primarily through rates, while the Government's own Revenue Action Plan says recent reliance on Crown funding has increased and that the revenue system needs reform to reduce reliance on direct Crown funding [Ministry of Transport, Revenue Action Plan cabinet paper, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Proactive-Release-Cab-Revenue-Action-Plan-for-web.pdf).

EV drivers and prospective EV buyers are affected because light EV and plug-in hybrid owners entered RUC from 1 April 2024, while public charging availability remains a stated barrier to electrifying the fleet and is the target of the Government's 10,000-public-charge-point programme [Beehive, EVs to pay RUC, 2024](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/electric-vehicles-pay-road-user-charges); [Ministry of Transport, Supercharging EV Infrastructure](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/environment-and-climate-change/supercharging-ev-infrastructure).

## What is already being done

- **EVs and plug-in hybrids were moved into RUC.** Light EV owners were told to pay $76 per 1,000km from 1 April 2024, and Cabinet accepted the select committee change setting plug-in hybrid RUC at $38 per 1,000km rather than the originally proposed $53 [Beehive, EVs to pay RUC, 2024](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/electric-vehicles-pay-road-user-charges); [Ministry of Transport, Cabinet paper and minute on Light Electric RUC Vehicles Bill, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Road-User-Charges-Light-Electric-RUC-Vehicles-Amendment-Bill.pdf).
- **RUC modernisation is in train.** The Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill would remove physical RUC labels, enable private RUC providers, allow broader electronic distance recorders, enable subscriptions or post-pay plans, and prepare for a future petrol-vehicle transition [Ministry of Transport, amendment bill summary, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/news/amendment-bill-makes-changes-to-road-user-charges-and-tolling). Proposed RUC regulations covering electronic distance recorders, provider approval, performance, privacy and alternative payment schemes closed for consultation on 12 June 2026 [Ministry of Transport, RUC regulations consultation, 2026](https://consult.transport.govt.nz/policy/proposed-regulations-for-road-user-charges/).
- **Full petrol-fleet transition is deliberately not dated.** The Ministry's RIS says the 2025 reform does not itself transition petrol light vehicles to RUC, and that a transition date and arrangements will be determined after cost-effective, user-friendly RUC services exist for light vehicles [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). The Minister said in August 2025 that no date had been set for the full transition of the light vehicle fleet because the Government was focused on getting the system right before rollout [Beehive, next steps on replacing petrol tax, 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/next-steps-replacing-petrol-tax-electronic-road-user-charges).
- **Public charging is being accelerated.** The Government aims for 10,000 public charge points by 2030 and has created a concessionary-loan programme of about $66.1m, with $52.7m in zero-interest loans announced for ChargeNet and Meridian projects that are expected to deliver more than 2,500 chargers [Ministry of Transport, Supercharging EV Infrastructure](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/environment-and-climate-change/supercharging-ev-infrastructure); [NIFF, EV Charging Infrastructure Loans programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/ev-charging-infrastructure-loans-programme/).

## Proposed child research questions

1. **research: What has actually funded NZ land transport since 2015, and what revenue gap appears under plausible EV, hybrid and fuel-efficiency scenarios?** This should build a year-by-year baseline for FED, RUC, motor vehicle fees, tolling, Crown grants/loans, local-government contributions and major deductions/refunds. It should use NLTF annual reports, Treasury tax outturns, Ministry of Transport revenue papers and GPS/NLTP documents, then compare official projections with a transparent scenario model. The stream needs this first because it determines whether the real issue is revenue volume, revenue volatility, fairness, or the political acceptability of new charges.
2. **research: Is the current and proposed RUC system adequate for EVs and a future all-light-vehicle transition?** This should test rates, compliance, bad debt, administration cost, privacy, provider-market design, enforcement and user burden for light EVs, plug-in hybrids, efficient petrol hybrids and eventual petrol entrants. Start with the RUC retail-market RIS, the Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill, the 2026 regulations consultation, NZTA performance reports and submitter evidence. The output should identify what would make the system fail even if the revenue arithmetic works.
3. **research: What is NZ's real EV uptake trajectory after the Clean Car Discount ended and EV/PHEV RUC began?** This should separate battery EVs, plug-in hybrids, ordinary hybrids, petrol and diesel vehicles across new imports, used imports, fleet stock and vehicle-kilometres travelled. It should use Ministry of Transport fleet statistics, NZTA Motor Vehicle Register data, Clean Car Standard reporting, EECA data and credible market data. The stream needs this because "rapid EV growth" is too blunt: the revenue risk depends on stock turnover, kilometres, fuel efficiency and policy responsiveness.
4. **research: Where are NZ's public EV charging infrastructure gaps, and do current programmes close them for urban, rural and inter-regional users?** This should map charge points, charger power, uptime/proxy quality, BEVs per charger, regional and district penetration, highway corridors, electricity-distribution constraints and planned co-funded sites. Start with EECA's Public EV Charger Dashboard datasets, EVRoam, NIFF loan programme material, distribution-business connection policies and regional transport plans. The output should distinguish genuine rural/corridor gaps from simple low-demand areas.
5. **research: How are Norway, the UK, the Netherlands and California replacing road-funding revenue as EVs grow, and what lessons are transferable to NZ?** This should compare distance charging, annual registration/ownership taxes, tolls/congestion charges, EV tax-incentive phaseouts, privacy design, rural equity and administrative cost. It should use official tax/transport sources first, then OECD/IEA material and peer-reviewed work where available. The stream needs this to avoid copying a high-EV market, a dense European country or a US state without checking whether its institutional conditions match New Zealand.

## What would change this framing

- Evidence from Treasury or the NLTF that FED has materially fallen after 2024/25 would strengthen the "revenue gap now" framing and weaken the "transition design before decline" framing [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf).
- A passed Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Act with final regulations, provider approvals and a declared petrol-fleet transition date would change the child research from "is the model ready?" to "is implementation working?" [Parliament, Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/d31385e9-dd9d-4cbd-c52d-08de22297aa7?Tab=history&lang=en).
- Strong evidence that EV uptake has stalled despite chargers and Clean Car Standard settings would reduce the urgency of FED replacement but increase the importance of charging and climate-policy questions [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail market RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).
- Detailed EECA/EVRoam evidence showing adequate rural and inter-regional charger coverage would narrow the infrastructure child issue toward reliability, power rating and grid connection rather than geography [EECA Public EV Charger Dashboard](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/data-tools/public-ev-charger-dashboard/).
- Lived-experience and frontline evidence from rural drivers, disabled drivers, low-income drivers and small businesses could materially change the equity framing, because public sources describe charges and infrastructure but do not show household-level coping strategies or trip substitution.
