# NZ youth mental health: high distress, inequitable suicide risk, and a service system whose youth impact is still under-proven

Discovery framing for stream root #443 (Part of #443). It tests the root's premise against current public evidence and proposes child research issues; it does not answer the child questions.

## Executive answer

- **H1: "NZ youth suicide rates have been a persistent concern" — supported, with a correction.** The latest national suicide picture is not a simple year-on-year worsening story: official 2023/24 Cabinet material recorded 617 suspected self-inflicted deaths, a rate of 11.2 per 100,000, and said this was 3.6% lower than the 15-year average but not statistically significant; the 2026 draft Mental Health and Wellbeing Strategy cites a 2024/25 suspected suicide rate of 11.0 per 100,000, with higher rates for males and Māori [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); [Draft Mental Health and Wellbeing Strategy 2026-2036, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-04/Draft-Mental-Health-and-Wellbeing-Strategy-2026-2036.pdf). The youth-specific concern is still real: young people aged 20-24 had the highest suspected suicide rate in 2023/24 at 19.9 per 100,000, and OECD/UNICEF comparators place New Zealand among the worst wealthy-country performers for adolescent/youth suicide [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html); [UNICEF Aotearoa, 2025](https://www.unicef.org.nz/stories/report-card-19-what-it-means-for-aotearoa).
- **H2: "Māori and Pasifika youth experience disproportionately high rates of mental health crisis and suicide" — partially supported, and needs tighter wording.** Māori youth inequity is strongly supported: a 2025 Hauora Māori Advisory Committee insight paper reports that 15-19-year-old Māori had the highest reported youth self-harm/attempted-suicide hospitalisation rate in 2023/24 at 75.2 per 10,000, and that confirmed 2020 suicide deaths among rangatahi Māori were 24 per 100,000 compared with 14 per 100,000 for non-Māori youth [Hauora Māori Advisory Committee, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/priority-4-key-insights%E2%80%93june-2025.pdf). Pacific youth inequity is supported for distress and self-reported attempts: Youth19 reported Pacific students' significant depressive symptoms rising from 14% to 25% and past-year suicide attempts from 7% to 12% between 2012 and 2019, compared with 3% for Pākehā/other European peers in 2019; the 2025 suicide-prevention Cabinet paper says Pacific people aged 15-24 have about 1.5 times the suspected suicide rate of Pacific people aged 25-44 and 45-64, but this framing did not verify a current Pacific-versus-non-Pacific youth suicide mortality ratio [Youth19 mental health report](https://www.youth19.ac.nz/s/Youth19-Mental-Health-Report.pdf); [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf).
- **H3: "Youth mental health services were established but their impact has been questioned" — supported, with nuance.** Youth Access and Choice expanded primary mental-health options: the Auditor-General reported 22 Youth Access and Choice services operating across all 20 former DHB districts by mid-2023, and Te Hiringa Mahara says Access and Choice reached 236,300 people in 2024/25, up from 207,600, while still below the 325,000 annual-reach goal [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf). The unresolved impact question is that specialist access for youth moved the other way: Te Hiringa Mahara reports 4,919 fewer 19-24-year-olds using specialist services over five years, 0-18-year-olds below the 80% three-week wait target at 69.9% in 2024/25, and 0-18-year-olds with the highest declined-referral rate at 14.9% [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf).
- **H4: "A systematic look at evidence and funding is warranted" — supported.** The Ministry's suicide-prevention evidence synthesis says large population reductions are unlikely from isolated, unsustained interventions outside an integrated multi-level strategy, and it calls for real-time self-harm/suicide surveillance and stronger evaluation; Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 youth early-intervention review found modest evidence for many low-intensity and "light touch" interventions but an uneven evidence base and a need for stronger evaluation, especially for communities facing access barriers [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).
- **H5: "Culturally responsive approaches matter" — supported for access/engagement; direct outcomes evidence is thinner.** Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 review says culturally appropriate, co-designed and Indigenous-led approaches are likely to improve acceptability and engagement for rangatahi Māori, but the same review says none of the included evaluations directly tested whether culturally tailored programmes produced stronger symptom improvements [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf). That distinction should shape the stream: culturally safe design is not optional, but the research question is which models improve outcomes, for whom, and under what conditions [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence: Medium-high.** The scale, service-access and policy baseline rest on official NZ and established institutional sources; the main limits are the lag and small-number instability in suicide data, incomplete regional youth breakdowns in this framing, and uneven evidence on culturally specific intervention outcomes [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara suicide/self-harm indicators working paper, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Suicide-and-self-harm-October-2025/Te-Hiringa-Mahara-Review-of-suicide-and-self-harm-indicators-Working-Paper.pdf).

## Problem statement

New Zealand's youth mental-health problem is not one problem: it is a high-prevalence distress problem, a smaller but severe self-harm/suicide problem, and a service-fit problem that affects different youth populations differently. In 2024/25, 22.9% of 15-24-year-olds, about 156,000 young people, reported high or very high psychological distress in the previous four weeks, the highest proportion of any adult age group in the New Zealand Health Survey [Te Hiringa Mahara NZ Health Survey summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Health-Survey/MHWC-NZ-Health-Survey-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf). Youth19 shows this is not only a post-pandemic survey artefact: among secondary students, significant depressive symptoms rose from 13% in 2012 to 23% in 2019 and past-year suicide attempts were reported by 6% in 2019, with higher rates among young people in high-deprivation communities [Youth19 mental health report](https://www.youth19.ac.nz/s/Youth19-Mental-Health-Report.pdf).

The suicide baseline is more complex than the word "crisis" implies. All-age suspected suicide rates have been broadly persistent rather than clearly rising in the latest official material, but New Zealand remains an international outlier for adolescent/youth suicide, 20-24-year-olds had the highest suspected suicide rate among five-year age groups in 2023/24, and Māori youth face materially higher suicide and self-harm hospitalisation rates [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html); [Hauora Māori Advisory Committee, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/priority-4-key-insights%E2%80%93june-2025.pdf). UNICEF Aotearoa's summary of Innocenti Report Card 19 says New Zealand ranked 36th of 36 countries for mental wellbeing, and that New Zealand's most recent comparable 15-19 suicide rate in that report was 17.1 per 100,000, almost three times the high-income-country average of about 6.5 [UNICEF Aotearoa, 2025](https://www.unicef.org.nz/stories/report-card-19-what-it-means-for-aotearoa).

The service problem is that the front door expanded while the higher-acuity system still appears constrained. Access and Choice was designed for mild-to-moderate needs and includes Youth, Kaupapa Māori, Pacific, GP and community streams; independent monitoring says it substantially increased access and choice, with rangatahi and young people making up 20% of Access and Choice users and Māori making up 27% [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice programme page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/). But Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 access summary says 19-24-year-olds were the only age group whose specialist-service use did not increase in 2024/25 and that 0-18-year-olds had the longest waits and highest declined-referral rate; the Auditor-General also found capacity constraints in specialist child/adolescent services flowing back into primary services and Youth One Stop Shops [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf); [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).

The stream should therefore establish four things before ideation: the current quantitative baseline by age, ethnicity, gender, deprivation and region; the true international comparison once age bands and data lags are harmonised; which intervention types have evidence strong enough to guide a small volunteer or policy-facing effort; and where current funding/coverage diverges from need, especially for Māori, Pacific, disabled, rainbow, care-experienced and high-deprivation youth [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).

## Who it affects

The broad affected population includes roughly 5.36 million New Zealand residents, because suicide prevention and youth mental wellbeing are population-level issues, and Stats NZ's latest national estimate was 5,361,300 residents at 31 March 2026 [Stats NZ population indicator, accessed via vendored stats-nz skill](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/). The immediate youth distress group is much smaller but still large: Te Hiringa Mahara's NZ Health Survey synthesis estimates about 156,000 15-24-year-olds had high or very high psychological distress in 2024/25, while another 24.8% of 15-24-year-olds reported moderate psychological distress [Te Hiringa Mahara NZ Health Survey summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Health-Survey/MHWC-NZ-Health-Survey-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf).

Māori youth are a priority population on current evidence. In Youth19, rangatahi Māori significant depressive symptoms rose from 14% to 28% between 2012 and 2019, and the proportion reporting a past-year suicide attempt rose from 6% to 13%; the 2025 Hauora Māori insight paper reports 15-19-year-old Māori with the highest youth self-harm/attempted-suicide hospitalisation rate in 2023/24 and a 2020 confirmed suicide death rate higher than non-Māori youth [Youth19 mental health report](https://www.youth19.ac.nz/s/Youth19-Mental-Health-Report.pdf); [Hauora Māori Advisory Committee, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/priority-4-key-insights%E2%80%93june-2025.pdf). Te Hiringa Mahara also reports Māori adults had among the highest 2024/25 high/very-high psychological distress rates at 22.5%, and were significantly more likely than non-Māori adults to report high/very-high distress after age/gender adjustment [Te Hiringa Mahara NZ Health Survey summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Health-Survey/MHWC-NZ-Health-Survey-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf).

Pacific youth are also a priority population, but the stream must distinguish distress/self-harm evidence from suicide-mortality evidence. Youth19 reported Pacific students' significant depressive symptoms at 25% and past-year suicide attempts at 12% in 2019, with higher rates among Pacific females and students living in high-deprivation areas; Te Hiringa Mahara's Pacific wellbeing report says Pacific people experience inequities in mental-health outcomes and that Pacific-by-Pacific services and sustainable contracting models are part of better Pacific wellbeing outcomes [Youth19 mental health report](https://www.youth19.ac.nz/s/Youth19-Mental-Health-Report.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara Pacific wellbeing report page, 2024](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/achieving-equity-of-pacific-mental-health-and-wellbeing-outcomes/). What this framing did not verify is a current, public, age-specific Pacific-versus-non-Pacific suicide mortality rate; that should be answered by the first child issue using Health NZ's suicide web tool and, if needed, official data requests [Health NZ suicide data page](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/health-data/data-sets-and-collections/suicide-data-and-statistics).

Other youth groups are material but under-specified in the root: Youth2000's 2024 synthesis says higher need is seen among Māori, Pacific, Asian, rainbow and disabled young people, low-income communities, and young people facing intersecting identities; the Ministry's Child and Youth Mental Health Study is explicitly designed to focus on disabled, high-deprivation, Māori, Pacific, Asian and rainbow youth aged 5-24 [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study). The stream should avoid a binary Māori/Pacific-only lens while still treating Te Tiriti obligations and Pacific equity as core, not add-ons [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara Pacific wellbeing report page, 2024](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/achieving-equity-of-pacific-mental-health-and-wellbeing-outcomes/).

## What is already being done

National suicide-prevention policy is active. Every Life Matters is the 2019-2029 suicide-prevention strategy, and the second action plan, Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029, sets out 21 Health-led actions and 13 cross-agency actions focused on access, workforce, prevention/early intervention, and effectiveness/understanding; the Ministry says implementation is supported by existing Vote Health suicide-prevention investment of $20 million per year plus more than $16 million per year from 2025/26 [Ministry Suicide Prevention Action Plan page, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029). The Ministry is also publishing quarterly monitoring updates for the 2025-2029 action plan, which gives this stream a live accountability source rather than only a strategy document [Ministry monitoring page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/information-releases/monitoring-progress-on-the-suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029).

Primary/community mental-health support has expanded through Access and Choice. Te Hiringa Mahara says Access and Choice was rolled out from 2019/20 over five years for mild-to-moderate mental health and addiction needs, including Kaupapa Māori, Pacific, youth, GP and community settings; its 2025 monitoring found substantial increases in access and choice, but recommended reaching the 325,000-per-year target and improving pathways between Youth Access and Choice and ICAMHS [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice programme page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/). Health NZ's public wellbeing-support page presents Access and Choice as free support, including GP-based, Kaupapa Māori, Pacific-led and Youth services, although its map/service interface needs separate coverage analysis before the stream can say where youth services are actually reachable [Health NZ wellbeing support](https://www.wellbeingsupport.health.nz/).

Specialist services remain the key bottleneck to test. ICAMHS serves 0-17-year-olds with moderate-to-severe concerns and adult specialist services generally cover young people aged 18 and over; the Auditor-General reported that young people were waiting longer for specialist care than in 2018, that the average first-appointment wait for 12-19-year-olds rose from 15 days in 2017/18 to 23 days in 2022/23, and that an average 12-19-year-old waited over 60 days from referral to starting treatment in 2022/23 [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm). The same report said only some ICAMHS offer separate kaupapa Māori teams or services, that it was aware of only one ICAMHS team specifically for Pacific young people, and that Te Whatu Ora intended to raise the child/adolescent service age limit to 24 but had not set a timeline at the time of the audit [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).

The evidence base is being updated, but not settled. The Ministry's 2024 suicide-prevention evidence synthesis reviewed 285 reviews plus 22 Māori/Pacific/NZ intervention studies and concluded that universal, selective and indicated interventions need to be integrated and sustained; it also said NZ evidence for many local suicide-prevention activities is often based on community evaluations rather than methodologically rigorous outcome research [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf). Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 review of youth early intervention screened 20 studies covering 16 interventions and found many low-intensity interventions had at least modest effectiveness, while also saying the evidence base is uneven and many studies rely on non-randomised designs, small samples or limited subgroup analyses [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).

The data system is also being rebuilt. The Ministry says the Child and Youth Mental Health Study will collect data from about 6,000 households on children and young people aged 5-24, covering prevalence, service use, barriers, unmet need, and protective/contextual factors, with a focus on disabled, high-deprivation, Māori, Pacific, Asian and rainbow youth [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study). That study should not be treated as available baseline evidence yet; it is a future source that may change several conclusions once published [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study).

## Proposed child research questions

1. **What are the current NZ youth mental-distress, self-harm, suicide and service-need statistics by age, ethnicity, gender, region and deprivation?** This should build the stream's quantitative baseline from Health NZ suicide data, NZ Health Survey, hospitalisation/ED measures, Youth19/Youth2000 and the forthcoming Child and Youth Mental Health Study metadata, while flagging where small counts make regional or ethnic suicide rates unstable [Health NZ suicide data page](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/health-data/data-sets-and-collections/suicide-data-and-statistics); [Te Hiringa Mahara suicide/self-harm indicators working paper, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Suicide-and-self-harm-October-2025/Te-Hiringa-Mahara-Review-of-suicide-and-self-harm-indicators-Working-Paper.pdf).
2. **How do NZ youth suicide and mental-health outcomes compare with Australia, the UK and OECD/high-income peers after harmonising age bands, definitions and data lag?** This needs to test the "NZ is among the worst" claim using UNICEF, OECD, WHO mortality data, AIHW and UK ONS/NHS sources, and should separate adolescent suicide, 20-24 suicide, distress, self-harm and service-access indicators [UNICEF Aotearoa, 2025](https://www.unicef.org.nz/stories/report-card-19-what-it-means-for-aotearoa); [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html); [AIHW young people suicide/self-harm page, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/suicide-self-harm-monitoring/population-groups/young-people/suicide-self-harm-young-people).
3. **Which youth suicide-prevention and early mental-health interventions have the strongest evidence, and what implementation conditions matter in NZ?** This should synthesise universal, selective and indicated suicide-prevention interventions, youth early-intervention models, brief/digital/peer/school/whānau approaches, and the difference between evidence of efficacy, evidence of engagement, and evidence of scalable NZ implementation [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).
4. **What is the coverage, wait-time, declined-referral, outcomes and equity performance of Youth Access and Choice, ICAMHS/CAMHS, youth crisis responses and school/community supports?** This should distinguish primary mild-to-moderate support from specialist moderate-to-severe care, map regional coverage where public data allows, and test whether expanded access translated into youth-relevant outcomes rather than only higher contact counts [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf).
5. **What do Māori, Pacific, rainbow, disabled, care-experienced and high-deprivation youth evidence sources say about culturally safe and identity-responsive care, and where does implementation match or miss that evidence?** This should treat Te Tiriti and Pacific equity as core design tests, while being honest about which claims are supported by outcome evaluations versus engagement, acceptability, youth voice or rights-based evidence [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara Pacific wellbeing report page, 2024](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/achieving-equity-of-pacific-mental-health-and-wellbeing-outcomes/).
6. **Where is public and philanthropic money going for youth mental health and suicide prevention, and does it align with need and evidence?** This should map Vote Health suicide-prevention investment, Access and Choice funding/reach targets, relevant education/community programmes, crisis/postvention funding and major contracted NGO/philanthropic services, then compare that spend to the evidence and priority-population need established by the other child issues [Ministry Suicide Prevention Action Plan page, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029); [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice programme page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/).

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** the 2024/25 NZ Health Survey psychological-distress figures for 15-24-year-olds, Māori and Pacific adults; the published 2023/24 suspected suicide rate and 20-24 highest-age-group figure in Ministry Cabinet material; the 2024/25 Access and Choice and specialist-service access figures in Te Hiringa Mahara's February 2026 access summary; the Auditor-General's 2024 findings on Youth Access and Choice coverage and ICAMHS capacity; and the existence/scope of the Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029 and Child and Youth Mental Health Study [Te Hiringa Mahara NZ Health Survey summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Health-Survey/MHWC-NZ-Health-Survey-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf); [Ministry Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf); [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Ministry Suicide Prevention Action Plan page, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029); [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study).

**What is interpretation:** the central framing that the stream should focus on the gap between distress prevalence, high-risk youth suicide/self-harm inequity, and a split service system where primary access expanded but specialist youth outcomes remain uncertain. That synthesis follows from the cited evidence, but the exact point of highest leverage cannot be chosen until the child issues quantify regional baseline, service coverage, funding and intervention evidence [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf); [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf).

**Main limits:** (1) suicide is a low-count event, and official sources warn that small population-group rates are variable and that confirmed suicide data lag suspected self-inflicted death data [Ministry Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); (2) this framing did not extract full age-by-ethnicity-by-region tables from the Health NZ suicide web tool, so the first child issue must verify current Pacific, regional and small-population rates directly [Health NZ suicide data page](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/health-data/data-sets-and-collections/suicide-data-and-statistics); (3) Youth19 is detailed and rigorous but is a 2019 secondary-school survey from Auckland, Northland and Waikato plus specific youth cohorts, not a 2026 national prevalence survey [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); (4) Ministry/Health NZ web pages were partly Cloudflare/CloudFront-blocked to raw `curl`, so I used the built-in WebSearch/WebFetch-equivalent results and direct PDF fetches where possible, treating blocked raw HTTP as tooling rather than a dead citation under ADR-0006; (5) lived-experience, whānau, Pacific community and clinical interpretation should be checked by humans before this stream crosses G1, because service acceptability and cultural safety cannot be settled from published documents alone [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).

## What would change this framing

- Full Health NZ suicide-web-tool extraction showing that Pacific youth mortality is not elevated versus non-Pacific peers would narrow the Pacific-suicide premise to distress, attempts, service access and age-within-Pacific risk; a verified elevated Pacific youth mortality ratio would strengthen the root's original wording [Health NZ suicide data page](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/health-data/data-sets-and-collections/suicide-data-and-statistics).
- Publication of the Child and Youth Mental Health Study could update the prevalence, unmet-need and priority-population baseline, especially for 5-14-year-olds and youth outside secondary schools [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study).
- Outcome data showing Youth Access and Choice, ICAMHS changes or crisis-response models reduce distress, self-harm, suicide attempts, hospitalisation, wait harm or inequity would shift the stream from "what works and where is coverage weak" toward implementation scaling [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice programme page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/); [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).
- Stronger NZ evaluations of culturally specific Māori, Pacific, rainbow, disabled or care-experienced youth interventions would sharpen the recommendations beyond the current engagement/acceptability evidence [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).
