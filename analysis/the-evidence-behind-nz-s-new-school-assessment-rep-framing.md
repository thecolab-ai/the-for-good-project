# NZ's 2026 school assessment reset is better framed than National Standards, but its evidence base has unresolved delivery and equity risks

Discovery framing for stream root #636 (Part of #636). Tests the premise that New Zealand's new nationally consistent assessment and reporting system may repeat the National Standards pattern, and proposes child research questions for the runner to open.

## Executive answer

- **The new system is a real national standardisation move, but not a simple restoration of National Standards.** From 2026, state and state-integrated schools must assess Year 3-8 students twice yearly in reading, writing and maths using specified tools, and SMART is a new bilingual digital tool for Year 3-10 assessment in reading, writing, maths, pānui, tuhituhi and pāngarau; the 2026 tool choices are SMART, PATs or e-asTTle, with e-asTTle discontinued at the end of 2026. [Tāhūrangi, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p); [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p)
- **The strongest correction to the root premise is that reporting explicitly remains multi-evidence and teacher-judgement based.** The Ministry tells parents that no single evidence source can give the full picture, and that teachers should combine observations, conversations, tasks and assessment-tool data when selecting progress descriptors; National Standards also used overall teacher judgements, but the new public guidance puts "multiple sources" and progress-over-time graphics closer to the front of the parent-facing design. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf)
- **The evidence base is mixed: there is an official rationale and some trial/consultation evidence, but Treasury documents show unresolved implementation risks.** The Minister says the new approach followed consultation with principals' associations and teachers and trials in 85 schools involving about 12,000 student assessment engagements; Treasury supported consistent and regular assessment in principle but warned in released advice that key building blocks were unlikely to be in place for early Cabinet pre-commitment, that the timeline was being driven by the 2026 mandate, and that the Ministry did not have enough time, stakeholder engagement or item-development capacity for the proposed schedule. [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- **National Standards did not produce clearly attributable achievement gains, but "failed" is too blunt without separating outcomes, information use and policy harm.** The Ministry's later e-asTTle analysis found at least a third of Year 8 students were below expected curriculum levels in writing and mathematics and warned that e-asTTle use was uneven and non-causal as research data; PIRLS 2016 found New Zealand's Year 5 mean reading score stayed above the PIRLS centrepoint but fell by 8 points from 2011 to 2016; NZCER found some useful school-level data use but persistent concerns about robustness, curriculum narrowing, league tables and student labelling. [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling); [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)
- **The core risk for this stream is not "standardised assessment is always bad"; it is whether low-stakes assessment stays low-stakes once it feeds parents, schools, the Ministry and system targets.** OECD distinguishes standardised tests used for formative, monitoring and summative purposes, while also warning that high-stakes assessment can encourage teaching to the test and narrow what is valued; the 2026 NZ design says SMART is "low-stakes, light-touch", but it is explicitly linked to Government Target 7 and Ministry system-level visibility. [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf); [OECD, 2009](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2009/07/assessment-and-innovation-in-education_g17a1cff/222814543073.pdf); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The current policy requirements, funding scale, and Treasury implementation warnings are well supported by official sources; the downstream behavioural risk is an interpretation from NZ and international assessment literature, and needs child research on actual 2026 uptake, reporting practice, data use and sector experience.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** From 2026, schools must carry out twice-yearly Year 3-8 assessment in reading, writing and maths using specified tools, with kura kaupapa Māori, Ngā Kura ā-Iwi and specified hapū/iwi-based special-character schools moving to the requirement from 1 January 2027. [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) SMART is designed for Year 3-10 assessment in reading, writing, maths, pānui, tuhituhi and pāngarau, and detailed reports from the first 2026 assessment window were released on 3 July 2026. [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) The Government's Target 7 is 80% of Year 8 students at or above expected curriculum level in reading, writing and maths by December 2030, against low current baselines: 47% in reading in 2023, 22% in maths in 2023, 23% in maths in 2024, and 24% in writing in 2024. [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/improving-achievement)

**What is interpretation:** The new system is better designed than National Standards on parent-facing progress language and explicit multi-evidence judgement, but it can still reproduce the National Standards failure mode if standardised check-ins become a de facto accountability/ranking instrument rather than a support mechanism. That inference combines Ministry design claims, Treasury implementation-risk advice, National Standards evaluations, and OECD/UNESCO assessment principles; it should be tested by the child research rather than treated as settled. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [OECD, 2009](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2009/07/assessment-and-innovation-in-education_g17a1cff/222814543073.pdf)

**Main limits:** This framing did not obtain full Cabinet papers beyond the Treasury OIA release, did not analyse the SMART item banks psychometrically, and did not verify unpublished Ministry trial reports from the 85 schools. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) Sector reactions are current but uneven: NZEI has publicly alleged AI-marking accuracy and data-storage concerns, while PPTA deputy-principal feedback reports workload and timetable concerns; those are important leads, not representative outcome evidence. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback)

## Problem statement

New Zealand is replacing a largely school-selected assessment/reporting ecosystem with a more nationally consistent design for Years 0-10 reporting and Years 3-8 twice-yearly checks in reading, writing and maths. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) The affected population is large: Budget 2025 material says the new Year 3-10 assessment tool will support consistent assessment and reporting for approximately 540,000 students, and Education Counts records 466,547 primary-sector students and 73,144 composite-sector students on the July 2025 school roll. [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment); [Education Counts school rolls, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/school-rolls); [Figure.NZ / Ministry of Education school roll data, 2025](https://figure.nz/chart/rTaa8YdYdaCbHKnh)

The problem is not whether parents should receive clear information, because existing law and policy already require schools to report at least twice yearly in plain language, and the 2017 abolition of National Standards explicitly retained twice-yearly parent reporting. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Beehive, 2017](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/national-standards-ended) The problem is whether a system designed to identify and support learners can keep its assessment information formative and context-sensitive once it is standardised, centrally visible, connected to national achievement targets, and publicly explained as a nationwide measure of progress "regardless of which school" a child attends. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

## What is already being done

The Government's achievement package includes refreshed English, Te Reo Rangatira, Mathematics and Pāngarau curriculum content, required reading/writing/maths teaching time in primary and intermediate settings, action plans in reading, writing and maths, the Curriculum Insights and Progress Study as the national target indicator, parent-reporting guidance, and SMART as the new assessment/aromatawai tool. [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/whats-new-term-1-2026); [Ministry foundational learning action plan, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/foundational-learning-action-plan); [Tāhūrangi Curriculum Insights, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/curriculuminsights2025); [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

Schools can meet the 2026 twice-yearly Year 3-8 requirement through SMART, PATs or e-asTTle; SMART is free, PATs are not Ministry-funded, and e-asTTle remains available in 2026 but is not aligned with the new curriculum and is due to be discontinued at the end of 2026. [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) The reporting framework asks schools to include progress descriptors, a progress-over-time visual, explanation of descriptor selection and next steps, assessment results, and attendance information, while allowing existing school report templates to continue if they include those components. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning)

Several non-government actors are already contesting or defending the approach. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [New Zealand Initiative, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/opinion/smart-educational-assessment/) NZEI has warned parents about SMART accuracy, AI marking and data storage, PPTA deputy-principal feedback has raised readiness, workload and timetable concerns, and the New Zealand Initiative argues the tool can improve parent clarity, formative feedback, resource allocation and accountability if used well. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [New Zealand Initiative, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/opinion/smart-educational-assessment/)

## Hypothesis tests

### H1: The new system requires national testing and reporting that closely mirrors National Standards

**Grade: partially supported, with a key correction.** The resemblance is real at the level of policy purpose: both systems promise clearer, more nationally consistent parent information in reading, writing and maths, and National Standards were explicitly intended to give parents clear and consistent information while producing system-level information about patterns of performance. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress)

The correction is that the new reporting vocabulary uses five progress descriptors and progress-over-time visuals, while Ministry guidance says descriptor selection should draw on multiple sources and professional judgement rather than a single test. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress) National Standards also relied on overall teacher judgements rather than a single national test, so the relevant comparison is not "test versus teacher judgement"; it is whether the standardised tool and central data use create pressure similar to the National Standards public-achievement-information era. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380)

### H2: The new system is evidence-based where National Standards were not

**Grade: partially supported, but not proven.** There is a clearer official evidence chain than the root implies: the Government target is tied to Curriculum Insights results, SMART is linked to ERO and NZAI arguments about assessment information, and the Ministry says the new approach followed consultation and trialling in 85 schools with about 12,000 student assessment engagements. [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/improving-achievement); [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning); [NZAI, 2021](https://www.nzai.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/NZAI_Assessment-Principles-Practices-and-Proof-Final.pdf)

The support is limited because Treasury's released advice records significant reservations: it recommended against early pre-commitment, said key building blocks were unlikely to be in place, rated delivery as only "Partial", warned that 19,200 new English-language items and te reo Māori item development created high delivery risk, and said there was insufficient time for stakeholder engagement. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) Treasury also said the benefit of funding in the Māori-medium context was unclear until the intervention logic distinguished how twice-yearly standardised assessment would improve learning support and policy compared with more flexible arrangements, and until the implications for aromatawai were clearly articulated. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### H3: National Standards failed

**Grade: partially supported, with a narrower claim.** National Standards failed to establish a trusted, low-burden, improvement-producing assessment system; the evidence is weaker for a simple claim that National Standards alone caused national achievement to fall. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling)

The achievement record is at best disappointing: the Ministry's e-asTTle analysis of 2011-2016 found at least a third of Year 8 students below expected curriculum levels in writing and mathematics, while PIRLS 2016 showed an 8-point statistically significant fall in Year 5 reading from 2011 to 2016 but still an average above the PIRLS centrepoint. [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling); [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement) The implementation record is more clearly problematic: NZCER reported early concerns about no trial, unrealistic timeframes, weak moderation infrastructure, league-table risks and labelling; its 2016 survey found principals using National Standards data to identify need, including 77% for Māori students' needs, but also found only 31% agreed National Standards drove what they did in school and reported altered or softened reporting language in many schools. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

### H4: The new system risks the same standardise-test-rank trap

**Grade: supported as a risk, not yet as an outcome.** The risk is structurally plausible because the same data can serve multiple purposes: OECD distinguishes formative assessment, monitoring/evaluation and summative/high-stakes assessment, and warns that high-stakes assessment and accountability incentives can encourage teaching to the test and narrow valued learning. [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf); [OECD, 2009](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2009/07/assessment-and-innovation-in-education_g17a1cff/222814543073.pdf)

The counterweight is also real: UNESCO describes learning assessment as useful for improving quality and equity at system level, and NZ curriculum guidance defines assessment for learning as using evidence to improve teaching, identify needs, plan next opportunities and support learners sooner. [UNESCO, accessed 2026-07-06](https://www.unesco.org/en/learning-assessments); [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p) The decisive question for child research is whether SMART and the new reports are governed, communicated and resourced as assessment-for-learning, or whether they become accountability evidence for ranking schools, teachers, cohorts or learners. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf)

### H5: There is a way to identify and support children without a harmful national benchmark

**Grade: supported in principle, unresolved in NZ implementation.** Assessment-for-learning approaches explicitly identify needs and next steps without requiring a public ranking frame, and NZ's own guidance says teachers should combine classroom evidence, assessment tools and professional judgement. [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p); [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress)

The unresolved part is allocation: the Government says consistent data will enable action and support across parents, schools, the Ministry and the sector, but public documents do not yet show a fully specified pathway from twice-yearly scores to additional tutoring, learning support, teacher PLD, school resourcing or culturally grounded Māori-medium support. [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

## Proposed child research issues

1. **What exactly does the 2026 assessment and reporting system require, and how does it differ from National Standards?** This should map legal/regulatory requirements, Ministry guidance, SMART/PAT/e-asTTle choices, reporting descriptors, data flows, and what is required versus encouraged. It should produce a side-by-side comparison with National Standards, including both policy intent and classroom/reporting mechanics. Start with Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, SMART guidance, 2010 National Standards guidance, and the 2017 abolition material.
2. **What public evidence, consultation, trial and assurance record supports SMART and the new reporting framework?** This should test the Government's claim of consultation and 85-school trialling against released business cases, Treasury advice, procurement material, trial reports, ERO/NZAI evidence and any OIA releases. It should explicitly evaluate whether the evidence answers National Standards-era concerns about moderation, workload, labelling, curriculum narrowing, Māori-medium/aromatawai and data misuse.
3. **What did National Standards actually achieve or damage in New Zealand?** This should separate achievement trends, data-use benefits, workload, curriculum narrowing, student labelling, equity effects, league tables and implementation quality. It should use Ministry evaluations, NZCER national surveys, RAINS/Thrupp work, PIRLS/e-asTTle/NMSSA where relevant, and be careful not to infer causation from national trend data alone.
4. **How do international systems use standardised assessment without turning it into harmful high-stakes ranking?** This should review assessment-for-learning, low-stakes national monitoring, public accountability and standardised testing evidence, including systems that improved equity and systems where testing narrowed curriculum. It should identify design features that matter: sampling versus census testing, stakes, privacy, moderation, professional learning, resourcing triggers, and public reporting rules.
5. **Can NZ identify and support learners who need help without a one-size-fits-all benchmark?** This should compare pathways from assessment evidence to support: classroom formative practice, learning support, tutoring, PLD, resource allocation, Māori-medium aromatawai and whānau reporting. It should ask what additional support actually follows when a learner is below expectation, and whether the new system has a funded response or mainly a measurement response.
6. **What are teachers, principals, Māori-medium leaders, parents and education researchers saying about the 2026 rollout, and how does that compare with early National Standards reactions?** This should distinguish union/media claims from representative evidence, gather sector submissions and surveys, and look for early 2026 uptake, workload, technical, data-sovereignty, AI-marking and timetable issues. It should compare the pattern of concern with 2009-2012 National Standards evidence rather than assuming the same politics.

## What would change this conclusion

- A published independent evaluation showing SMART trial validity, item quality, inter-rater reliability, Māori-medium/aromatawai fit, accessibility, and positive classroom use would strengthen the evidence-based-policy claim. [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning); [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- Clear rules preventing school league tables, punitive accountability, or unsupported labelling would weaken the National Standards-repeat concern.
