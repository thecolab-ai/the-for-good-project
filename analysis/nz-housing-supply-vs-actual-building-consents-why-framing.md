# NZ housing supply targets need to be measured against capacity, consents, completions, and affordability

Discovery framing for stream root #435 (Part of #435). This framing tests the premise that government housing targets are missing actual building consents, corrects the target baseline, and proposes child research issues that separate enabled capacity, consented homes, completed dwellings, and affordability outcomes.

## Executive answer

- **The founder premise is directionally useful but needs correction.** The current central-government housing-growth target is not a national delivery target like "120,000 homes by 2028"; the active Going for Housing Growth target requires Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity in district plans using high population projections, with related monitoring, price-indicator, infrastructure-funding, and council-incentive changes [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [HUD, GfHG consultation Q&A](https://www.hud.govt.nz/about-us/have-your-say/past-consultations/going-for-housing-growth-providing-for-urban-development-in-the-new-resource-management-system). The closest historical analogue is KiwiBuild: Budget 2018 described a programme to deliver 100,000 affordable houses over 10 years, and Budget 2019 performance material referred to progress toward 100,000 KiwiBuild affordable homes by 2028; that was an affordable-home programme target, not a whole-of-market building-consent target [Treasury, Budget Speech 2018](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budget-speech/budget-speech-2018); [Treasury, Vote Housing and Urban Development 2019/20](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2019-05/est19-v10-houudev.pdf).
- **The current consent baseline is stronger than the root implies, but still not enough to settle the delivery question.** Stats NZ reported 39,737 new homes consented in the year ended May 2026, up 19 percent on the year ended May 2025, with 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents nationally; the record per-capita consent rate was 13.4 per 1,000 residents in the year ended December 1973 [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ, Annual number of home consents up 19 percent](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/annual-number-of-home-consents-up-19-percent/).
- **Consents are a leading indicator, not delivered housing.** Stats NZ dwelling estimates use building consents with a 12-month lag from April 2018 onward, and the private-dwelling estimate applies a 0.92 weighting factor to allow for unfulfilled consents, demolitions, destruction, and other stock changes [Stats NZ DataInfo+, Estimated private dwellings and households](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/ded8075e-2713-4876-84eb-b2a7b54e8b4a).
- **The regional pattern is uneven enough to justify a council-by-council stream.** In the year ended May 2026, Auckland had 16,862 new dwellings consented and Canterbury had 8,468, while Stats NZ's regional per-capita rates were 9.3 per 1,000 residents for Auckland, 12.1 for Canterbury, 11.1 for Otago, 5.9 for Waikato, and 4.0 for Wellington [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/). A territorial-authority calculation from the same Stats NZ May 2026 downloadable CSV shows much sharper outliers: Queenstown-Lakes District at 35.5 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents, Selwyn District at 26.4, Christchurch City at 10.8, Auckland at 9.3, and Wellington City at 2.5 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents downloadable data](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).
- **The stream should reframe the "gap" as four linked gaps:** whether councils have enabled enough feasible and infrastructure-ready capacity; whether that capacity becomes consented homes; whether consented homes become completed, occupied dwellings; and whether the resulting stock improves affordability for renters, first-home buyers, and high-need households. Housing affordability remains acute: in the year ended June 2024, 45.9 percent of non-owner-occupier households spent at least 30 percent of disposable income on housing costs, and the national median house-sale-price-to-income ratio was 14.6, with Auckland at 17.2 [Stats NZ, Current state of housing](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/the-current-state-of-housing-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The current consent count, consent methodology, Going for Housing Growth target design, and housing-cost burden facts are high-confidence because they come from Stats NZ, HUD, MBIE, Treasury, and Te Waihanga. The ranking of constraints is medium-to-low confidence at framing stage because public evidence confirms several plausible constraints but does not yet establish which one binds hardest in each council or region.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** the year-ended May 2026 national and regional building-consent numbers published by Stats NZ; the Stats NZ dwelling-estimates method that treats consents as lagged and weighted inputs rather than completions; the current Going for Housing Growth target design for Tier 1 and 2 council planning capacity; the historical KiwiBuild 100,000 affordable-home target; the 2024 housing-cost burden figures; the MBIE 2025 construction-pipeline forecast; the Te Waihanga infrastructure-consenting and land-use/infrastructure coordination statements; and the Budget 2026 housing investment announcements [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/ded8075e-2713-4876-84eb-b2a7b54e8b4a); [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Treasury, Budget Speech 2018](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budget-speech/budget-speech-2018); [Stats NZ, Current state of housing](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/the-current-state-of-housing-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report); [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan](https://tewaihanga.govt.nz/national-infrastructure-plan-online/6-making-it-easier-to-build-better/61-making-resource-management-work-for-infrastructure); [HUD, Budget 2026 housing](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system).

**What is interpretation:** that the root should not be framed as "government target versus actual consents" alone. That interpretation follows from the mismatch between current capacity targets, building-consent indicators, completion methodology, and affordability outcomes, but the child research needs to quantify each link before the stream can support policy conclusions.

**Main limits:** This framing did not perform a full council-by-council Housing Growth Target compliance audit, did not verify completion rates by territorial authority, did not rank land, infrastructure, finance, labour, materials, and consenting delays by causal weight, and did not test whether new dwellings are well-located or affordable to high-need households. It uses only public, aggregate sources and does not include personal or identifying data. A planner, housing economist, consenting practitioner, or council infrastructure finance specialist should review any later policy-facing conclusion before the stream crosses the G1 gate.

## Problem statement

New Zealand does not have one clean "housing target versus building consents" gap in 2026. The current central target is a planning-capacity target for Tier 1 and 2 councils, while the headline Stats NZ metric is the number of new dwellings consented, and Stats NZ dwelling estimates treat consents as a lagged and discounted input into the dwelling stock [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/ded8075e-2713-4876-84eb-b2a7b54e8b4a). The stream should ask where capacity, consents, completions, location, and affordability diverge by council and region, then identify the constraint that explains the divergence.

The affected population is broad but not evenly affected. Stats NZ reported that average annual housing costs rose 31 percent between the June 2020 and June 2024 years while average annual disposable income rose 24 percent, and that in the June 2024 year 45.9 percent of non-owner-occupier households spent at least 30 percent of disposable income on housing costs compared with 26.6 percent of owner-occupier households [Stats NZ, Current state of housing](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/the-current-state-of-housing-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/). Stats NZ also reported that about one in four households that did not own their dwelling spent more than 40 percent of income on housing costs in the June 2024 year, and that non-owner-occupier Maori and Pacific households were especially exposed to the 30-percent burden threshold at 64.1 percent and 69.9 percent respectively [Stats NZ, Current state of housing](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/the-current-state-of-housing-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/). HUD's March 2026 homelessness insights reported 8,799 households with children on the social housing register and 1,101 previously emergency-housing households housed since May 2024, including 2,367 children, which is why total private-market consents cannot be treated as sufficient evidence of meeting housing need [HUD, Homelessness insights to March 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Homelessness/Homelessness-insights-report-to-end-March-2026.pdf?m=572f7739f02450ea54793fedd7ec85b57727299e).

## What's already being done

Central government is pursuing **Going for Housing Growth** through three pillars: freeing up land for development, improving infrastructure funding and financing, and providing incentives for councils and communities to support growth [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). Under Pillar 1, Tier 1 and 2 councils will need to zone for at least 30 years of housing demand rather than a minimum of three years, while capacity must also be infrastructure-ready across short, medium, and long-term horizons [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). Under Pillar 2, the government says development levies and related funding tools are intended to give councils and developers a more flexible toolkit for infrastructure needed to support land zoned for housing development [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). Under Pillar 3, Budget 2026 created a $400 million tagged-contingency Incentives for Growth Fund, with first payments expected from 1 April 2027 and payments linked to building consents in the preceding 12 months [HUD, Budget 2026 housing](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system); [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).

The building and planning system is also being loosened at the small-dwelling margin. MBIE says the granny-flat exemption took effect on 15 January 2026, allowing simple standalone dwellings up to 70 square metres to be built without building or resource consents if they meet the Building Code and are built by authorised professionals [MBIE, Granny flats exemption](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats). MBIE says the exemption is expected to save up to $5,650 in direct costs and speed up the process by up to 14 weeks, but the child research should test whether this materially affects the national supply gap or mostly creates a small secondary-dwelling channel [MBIE, Granny flats exemption](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats).

Public and affordable housing investment is proceeding separately from the market-consent baseline. HUD says Budget 2026 adds $69.2 million to the Flexible Fund to deliver 1,800 to 2,250 additional social and other homes over three years starting in 2028/29, and HUD's Housing Investment Plan 2025 sets purchasing intentions for 675 to 770 social homes and affordable rentals from 1 July 2027 in named high-need locations [HUD, Budget 2026 housing](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system); [HUD, Housing investment plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/housing-investment-plan-2025). HUD's May 2026 dashboard says Budget 2024 funded 1,500 new social homes over 2025/26 and 2026/27, Budget 2025 committed funding for around 550 to 600 Auckland social homes in 2025/26, and $426 million has been approved since December 2023 for Maori-led delivery of around 1,000 homes [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, May 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-may-2026).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **research: What is the measurable gap between NZ housing capacity targets, building consents, and completed dwellings?** This should establish the baseline the stream will use: current Housing Growth Targets and NPS-UD/HBA capacity measures, current Stats NZ consent counts, and the best public completion or dwelling-stock proxy. Start with HUD's Going for Housing Growth material, Stats NZ building-consents downloadable data, Stats NZ dwelling-estimates methodology, and council Housing and Business Development Capacity Assessments.

2. **research: Which councils are housing-supply outliers once capacity-target compliance, consent volume, per-capita consents, and population growth are combined?** This should identify where the stream should look hardest, because high raw consent counts, high per-capita consents, and compliance with 30-year capacity rules are different tests. Start with Stats NZ territorial-authority consent data, HUD Housing Growth Target guidance, council HBAs and Future Development Strategies for Auckland, Christchurch/Selwyn/Waimakariri, Wellington, Hamilton, Tauranga, and Queenstown-Lakes.

3. **research: Which constraint binds hardest: zoned land, infrastructure funding, development finance and demand, construction capacity, or consent processing?** This should rank the root's proposed constraints rather than treating them as equal. Start with HUD Going for Housing Growth Cabinet and programme papers, Te Waihanga's National Infrastructure Plan, MBIE's National Construction Pipeline Report, Stats NZ construction-cost and labour indicators, and public council consenting and development-contribution material.

4. **research: How do Auckland, Canterbury, Queenstown-Lakes, and Wellington differ in supply dynamics?** This should compare the largest-volume market, high-growth South Island outliers, and a low-per-capita capital-city case rather than averaging them into a national story. Start with Stats NZ regional and territorial consent data, Auckland Council and Greater Christchurch growth documents, Queenstown-Lakes spatial/infrastructure plans, and Wellington capacity and consenting evidence.

5. **research: How much do public, social, and affordable housing programmes change the supply gap in high-need places?** This should test whether aggregate consents are going where high-need households are, and whether public and affordable delivery is material relative to the register, emergency-housing exits, and private-market construction. Start with HUD's Housing Dashboard, Housing Investment Plan 2025, Budget 2024-2026 funded-housing dashboards, Kainga Ora and community housing delivery data, and HUD homelessness insights.

6. **research: What evidence exists that specific delivery levers actually increase completed homes in New Zealand or close comparators?** This should stay research-stage by evaluating evidence for fast-track consenting, building-consent exemptions, modular or offsite construction, infrastructure funding tools, public housing procurement, and council incentive payments before any solution is proposed. Start with MBIE granny-flat and building-consent reforms, Fast-track Approvals material, HUD's Incentives for Growth Fund, MBIE/BRANZ pipeline evidence, and overseas evaluations only where NZ evidence is thin.

## What would change this framing

- A current official national target for completed or consented homes, distinct from Housing Growth Targets and historical KiwiBuild targets, would change the baseline from "capacity-consent-completion-affordability chain" back toward a direct target-versus-delivery audit.
- Public territorial-authority completion data, if available and current, would reduce reliance on the Stats NZ 12-month-lag and weighting-factor methodology for estimating dwelling-stock changes.
- Council evidence showing that capacity-target compliance reliably predicts actual consents and completions would weaken the need to treat capacity, consents, and completions as separate gaps.
- Strong evidence that one constraint, such as infrastructure finance or labour capacity, dominates across most regions would narrow the stream from a multi-constraint diagnosis to a sharper delivery-barrier stream.
- Lived-experience or frontline-provider evidence could change how the stream weights public and affordable supply, especially where aggregate consent numbers rise but high-need households remain on the social housing register or in temporary accommodation.
