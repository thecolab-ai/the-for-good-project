# Are most NZ statutory amendments actually repeals?

Discovery framing for stream root #667 (Part of #667). Tests the premise that a recent open-data analysis "leans on the idea that ~most NZ legislative amendments are some kind of repeal" against what that analysis and its source claim actually say, establishes the official NZ taxonomy for classifying amendments, surveys the data infrastructure available to sample and classify them, and proposes the child research questions.

## Executive answer

- **The premise as stated is corrected, not supported.** Neither the 07 July 2026 Spinoff piece that prompted this issue, nor the political line it is built on, actually asserts that most NZ statutory amendments are repeals. The Spinoff piece's own headline finding is that legislative *churn* (total amendment volume per parliamentary term) is roughly flat across governments — "not red or blue" — and the author explicitly flags: "this is every amendment, not just reversals. Consequential tidy-ups and routine maintenance are in here too" [The Spinoff, 07-07-2026](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/07-07-2026/are-we-really-the-fastest-repealers-in-the-west-i-counted). Deliberate policy *reversals* — the closest thing to "repeals" in the political sense — are tracked as a separate, much smaller count (about 30) on a companion tool, not folded into the churn headline [whiplash.jonno.nz](https://whiplash.jonno.nz); [jonnonz1/whiplash README](https://github.com/jonnonz1/whiplash).
- **"Fastest repealers" is Geoffrey Palmer's phrase, and it is about policy churn, not amendment taxonomy.** Sir Geoffrey Palmer told The Spinoff in June 2024: "We've gone from being the fastest lawmakers in the west to the fastest repealers in the west," in a piece about governments unwinding predecessors' major programmes (citing the transaction costs of "enormous programmes binned when a new government comes in") [The Spinoff, 25-06-2024](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/25-06-2024/geoffrey-palmer-on-rapid-reforms-you-should-learn-from-history-not-repeat-it). Nothing in that quote, or in the 07-07-2026 piece that tests it, is a claim about the *share of individual amending provisions* that are technical repeals (of a whole Act, a section, or a schedule item) versus insertions, substitutions, or textual tidy-ups. The issue's framing conflates two different phenomena — party-political policy reversal ("repealer" as in undoing a predecessor's law) and PCO drafting taxonomy ("repeal" as a specific amendment operation) — that happen to share a word.
- **That said, the underlying empirical question Jake raised is real, distinct from the article, and worth answering on its own terms.** Nobody in the sources found here has actually measured what share of NZ's ~15,000+ individual amendment events are technical repeals versus other operations. The official NZ drafting taxonomy for this is well documented and current: PCO's standard amendment vocabulary distinguishes **Repeal** (removing a whole enactment — an Act, Part, Subpart, Schedule, section or subsection), **Delete** (removing sub-section elements: paragraphs, words, figures — formerly called "omit"), **Replace** (removing text and substituting new text in one operation — formerly "substitute"), and **Insert** (adding new text, whether mid-provision or at the end — formerly split into "insert" and "add") [PCO, "A new approach to describing how amendments are made in legislation", last updated 08-10-2025, in force since 01-01-2012](https://web.archive.org/web/20260201011527/https://pco.govt.nz/instructing-the-pco/drafting-practice-and-model-clauses/new-amending-styles) (direct fetch 403-blocked; fetched via Wayback snapshot, tooling block not a dead link — see Confidence & limits). This four-way split — repeal / delete / replace / insert — is corroborated independently by the Law Commission's 1996 Legislation Manual, which describes NZ's preferred "textual amendment" method as operating "whether by repeal, alteration, substitution or incorporation of new provisions" [NZLC Report 35, Appendix B, para B4](https://web.archive.org/web/20241222125322/http://www.nzlii.org/nz/other/nzlc/report/R35/R35-Appendix-2.html) (also Wayback-fetched after a direct 403).
- **The crux definitional question — and likely the single biggest lever on any future headline — is what to do with "Replace".** A "Replace" operation textually removes existing provisions and substitutes new ones in the same instruction; under the pre-2012 terminology this was literally described as "repealed and the following section substituted" [PCO amending-styles page, cited above]. Whether a stream counts "Replace" as a repeal (pushing any "mostly repeals" estimate up) or excludes it (pushing it down, since new operative text survives) will likely swing the headline more than any other single choice — this is exactly the kind of "if the headline flips depending on definition, that is the finding" case CONTRIBUTING.md's method anticipates.
- **A reproducible full- or large-sample classification looks genuinely feasible**, not just aspirational. The country's amendment history is already mirrored and parsed at scale by an independent open-data pipeline: [jonnonz1/nz-statute-book](https://github.com/jonnonz1/nz-statute-book) renders "every public Act of the New Zealand Parliament, every electronic consolidation, as a commit" — 4,001 acts across 15,282 consolidations, current to 2026-06-06, deterministically regenerated from PCO XML — and its sibling pipeline, [jonnonz1/whiplash](https://github.com/jonnonz1/whiplash), already parses PCO "history notes" into an "amendment graph". The Spinoff piece's 241,456-change figure comes from the same underlying open-data corpus [The Spinoff, 07-07-2026](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/07-07-2026/are-we-really-the-fastest-repealers-in-the-west-i-counted). Official bulk access exists independently of that project too, via PCO's keyless bulk XML (catalogued on data.govt.nz) and a free-key developer API [data.govt.nz catalogue entry](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/new-zealand-legislation); [PCO developer API docs](https://api.legislation.govt.nz/docs/). John Gregoriadis (@jonnonz1) has been invited as a contributor per the issue — looping him in on method before a full-corpus attempt is sound, since he has already solved several of the hard mirroring/parsing problems.

**Overall confidence: Medium.** The correction of the premise (the article does not claim "mostly repeals") is High confidence — it rests on directly reading both cited pieces. The official taxonomy is High confidence — it rests on PCO's own current drafting-practice page, corroborated independently by the Law Commission's manual. What remains genuinely unknown — the actual empirical share of repeal-type versus other amendments across NZ's statute book — is Low confidence by construction, because nobody has sampled it yet; that gap is precisely what the child research issues below are for.

## Problem statement

A claim is circulating that New Zealand has become "the fastest repealer in the West" — a line Sir Geoffrey Palmer used in 2024 to describe how quickly governments unwind their predecessors' major programmes, and which a 07 July 2026 Spinoff data analysis set out to test using the country's full open-data statute book [The Spinoff, 25-06-2024](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/25-06-2024/geoffrey-palmer-on-rapid-reforms-you-should-learn-from-history-not-repeat-it); [The Spinoff, 07-07-2026](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/07-07-2026/are-we-really-the-fastest-repealers-in-the-west-i-counted). The issue that opened this stream took a further step and asked whether the analysis implies that *most individual legislative amendments* are technical repeals — of whole Acts or of specific provisions — as opposed to insertions, substitutions, or minor textual tidy-ups.

That further step doesn't survive contact with the sources: the 07-07-2026 piece counts total amendment volume ("churn") per parliamentary term and finds it essentially flat across governments, while explicitly stating its count includes "consequential tidy-ups and routine maintenance", and while tracking genuine policy reversals separately and at a much smaller scale (~30) on a companion site. Neither the churn count nor the reversal count is a classification of amendment *type* in the PCO drafting-practice sense (repeal / delete / replace / insert). So this stream should not set out to verify or refute an unmade claim. Instead, civic transparency is better served by answering the question on its own honest terms: **independent of what anyone has claimed, what share of NZ's individual statutory amendment events are genuine repeals — under a clearly stated definition — versus other kinds of amendment, and how sensitive is any headline number to how "repeal" is defined?** That is a real, previously unanswered, and now demonstrably feasible-to-answer question, given the taxonomy PCO already publishes and the open-data infrastructure (both official and the community-built nz-statute-book/Whiplash pipeline) already available to sample from.

## Evidence

### The claim and where it actually comes from

Palmer's exact words, as reported: "We've gone from being the fastest lawmakers in the west to the fastest repealers in the west" — made in the context of concern about the cost of governments reversing large programmes, not a statement about drafting mechanics: "You can't have enormous programmes binned when a new government comes in, because the cost of doing the replacements are enormous, and the time to do them is enormous, and the transaction costs to the economy are very high" [The Spinoff, 25-06-2024](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/25-06-2024/geoffrey-palmer-on-rapid-reforms-you-should-learn-from-history-not-repeat-it).

The 07-07-2026 piece by John Gregoriadis takes that line as its title question and tests it using "every act of parliament and every regulation made under one… downloaded" from New Zealand's open-data legislation corpus, examining 241,456 recorded changes dating back to 1872, focused on amendment events to Acts since 2008 lined up against the electoral calendar. Its headline finding is that amendment counts per parliamentary term since 2008 range roughly 12,710–17,538 with no partisan pattern: "The churn is not red or blue. It is the machine." Critically, the author states the caveat plainly: "this is every amendment, not just reversals. Consequential tidy-ups and routine maintenance are in here too" — and points to a companion interactive tool, Whiplash, which "handles the deliberate U-turns" separately, currently showing "30 reversals and counting" [The Spinoff, 07-07-2026](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/07-07-2026/are-we-really-the-fastest-repealers-in-the-west-i-counted); [whiplash.jonno.nz](https://whiplash.jonno.nz).

Nowhere in either piece is there a claim that most individual amendments are technical repeals. The word "repealer" is being used the way Palmer used it — a government reversing a predecessor's policy programme — not as a count of PCO amendment operations. The issue's founder read a plausible-sounding folk inference into "repealer" rhetoric; that inference is exactly the kind of widely-shared, untested claim this project exists to check, and checking it here means first correcting what's actually being claimed before testing anything with data.

### The official taxonomy — this is well documented and current

New Zealand's Parliamentary Counsel Office publishes the operative drafting vocabulary for amendments, current since a change that took effect gradually from 1 January 2012 and was last updated 8 October 2025. Its four operations are:

- **Repeal** (or "revoke" for secondary legislation) — used "for all text that isn't in quotation marks," i.e. removal of a whole enactment or defined sub-part (an Act, Part, Subpart, Schedule, section, or subsection). Example given: the old style "The item relating to Austria in Schedule 2 is omitted" becomes "In Schedule 2, repeal the item relating to Austria."
- **Delete** — the modern term for what used to be called "omit": removing text *smaller* than a subsection (a paragraph, word, figure, or expression) — e.g. "In section 17(2), delete 'or dog'."
- **Replace** — the modern term for "Substitute": removing existing text and putting new text in its place in one instruction, "without stating that the text to be replaced is repealed" — the old style made the repeal explicit ("Section 13 is repealed and the following section substituted"); the new style just says "Replace section 13 with:".
- **Insert** — adding new text, whether mid-provision or at the end of a provision (formerly two different words, "insert" and "add"; now unified) [PCO, "A new approach to describing how amendments are made in legislation"](https://web.archive.org/web/20260201011527/https://pco.govt.nz/instructing-the-pco/drafting-practice-and-model-clauses/new-amending-styles).

This is corroborated independently, and predates the 2012 style change, by the Law Commission's Legislation Manual: "An amending law repeals, alters, or substitutes existing provisions in an enactment, or it incorporates new provisions in the text" and New Zealand's preferred method, textual amendment, "enables express alterations to be made to an enactment, whether by repeal, alteration, substitution or incorporation of new provisions" [NZLC Report 35, Appendix B, paras B1 and B4](https://web.archive.org/web/20241222125322/http://www.nzlii.org/nz/other/nzlc/report/R35/R35-Appendix-2.html). Two independent official sources, thirty years apart, describe the same four-way split — this is a solid, load-bearing basis for the taxonomy the root issue asks for.

The taxonomy also surfaces the crux ambiguity directly: the pre-2012 style for a "Replace" operation was literally to say the old text "is repealed and the following section substituted." Whether that operation should be scored as a "repeal" (old text is destroyed) or as something distinct (new operative text survives, so nothing is left un-replaced) is not settled by the taxonomy itself — it is a choice a classifier has to make, and different reasonable choices will produce very different "mostly repeals" answers from the same underlying data. There is also a separate, non-textual category worth keeping distinct from all four operations above: whole-Act repeal of *spent* legislation via the periodic Statutes Repeal Bill process, illustrated by a 2015 exposure draft seeking submissions on Acts and provisions identified by departments as "redundant" or "spent" [PCO, Statutes Repeal Bill Exposure Draft Explanatory Material (2015)](https://web.archive.org/web/20231202160552/https://www.pco.govt.nz/srb-explanatory-material). This is "housekeeping" repeal of dead law, conceptually different from a repeal-and-replace inside an active policy amendment, and conflating the two would distort any headline share.

### Data infrastructure — a large or full-corpus classification looks feasible

Two independent routes exist to sample or classify NZ's amendment history:

1. **Official PCO bulk data.** All NZ legislation is available as bulk XML, catalogued on data.govt.nz, alongside a free-key developer API exposing `/works`, `/versions`, and version history programmatically [data.govt.nz catalogue entry for New Zealand Legislation](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/new-zealand-legislation); [PCO developer API docs](https://api.legislation.govt.nz/docs/). This project's own vendored `legislation-nz` skill already wraps keyless access to search, per-section text, version history, and an update feed, which is enough to pull individual amending provisions' text for classification (`python3 .skills/skills/legislation-nz/scripts/cli.py get-section ...`).
2. **The community-built nz-statute-book / Whiplash pipeline.** [jonnonz1/nz-statute-book](https://github.com/jonnonz1/nz-statute-book) mirrors "every public Act of the New Zealand Parliament, every electronic consolidation, as a commit authored by the government of the day" — 4,001 acts across 15,282 consolidations, current through 2026-06-06, deterministically regenerated from the same PCO XML ("identical inputs produce identical commit SHAs"), with repealed provisions rendered as their official "[Repealed]" tombstone. Its sibling repo, [jonnonz1/whiplash](https://github.com/jonnonz1/whiplash), documents a pipeline stage that parses PCO "history notes" into an "amendment graph" (`pipeline/build_git_repo.py`, referenced against `specs/whiplash/03-data-pipeline.md`) — meaning at least one working implementation already extracts structured amendment metadata from the same official history-note text a classifier would need to read. The issue notes @jonnonz1 has just been invited as a contributor; looping him in before scaling past a hand-classified sample is the efficient path, since key mirroring and parsing infrastructure already exists rather than needing to be built from scratch.

Both routes are consistent on rough scale — the Spinoff piece's 241,456 total recorded changes since 1872 (all Acts and regulations) versus nz-statute-book's 15,282 consolidation-level commits for the 4,001 *currently in-force* public Acts since an unstated start point — these are different units of counting (individual line/clause changes vs. whole-of-Act reprint events vs. Acts-plus-regulations vs. Acts-only) and reconciling exactly what each counts is itself one of the child questions below, since headline comparability depends on it.

## Open follow-up questions

Proposed child research issues (each `Part of #667`), most important first:

1. **Draw and classify the indicative random sample (the core proof-of-method).** Define the taxonomy precisely — Repeal (whole Act / whole provision), Delete, Replace, Insert, plus a residual "consequential/transitional" tag — state a sampling frame (e.g. amending provisions enacted over a fixed period, drawn from legislation.govt.nz or the `legislation-nz` skill), a sample size N, and a fixed random seed. Classify each sampled amendment against the taxonomy from the actual legislative text, citing each item or providing a reproducible script, and report the breakdown with uncertainty appropriate to N. This is the deliverable the whole stream hinges on.
2. **Definition-sensitivity: does "mostly repeals" flip depending on how "Replace" is scored?** Using the same sample as question 1 (or a matched one), compute the "repeal share" twice — once counting only clean whole-enactment Repeal operations, once also counting Replace operations (which functionally repeal old text) — and report how much the headline moves. If it flips from a minority to a majority framing (or vice versa) depending on this one choice, that is the single most important finding this stream can produce, per the root issue's explicit instruction to surface definition-sensitivity as a finding in its own right.
3. **Reconcile what the Spinoff piece, nz-statute-book, and official PCO data each actually count.** The 241,456-change figure (Acts + regulations, since 1872, unspecified granularity), nz-statute-book's 15,282 consolidations (currently in-force public Acts only, one commit per reprint event), and whatever unit PCO's own bulk XML/history-note structure uses are not obviously the same denominator. Reconstruct each method precisely enough to state whether they're comparable, and whether the "churn is flat across governments" finding would look different if measured in units of individual amendment instructions (repeal/delete/replace/insert operations) rather than reprint/consolidation events.
4. **Full-corpus feasibility, in collaboration with @jonnonz1.** Assess whether the nz-statute-book/Whiplash pipeline's existing "history note" parser (or PCO's own bulk XML/API) can be extended or queried to classify amendment *type* at full-corpus scale rather than a sample — what the history-note text structure actually looks like, whether it already distinguishes repeal/delete/replace/insert or would need new parsing, and what a reproducible full-corpus run would require in effort and access (API key, compute, mirrored XML volume). This is the natural next step once question 1's method is validated on a sample, and is explicitly flagged as a strong candidate for a federated multi-agent run.
5. **How much "repeal" activity is housekeeping (spent-Act cleanup) versus live policy amendment?** Using the Statutes Repeal Bill / statute revision programme mechanism as a lead, establish how much of NZ's total whole-Act repeal volume over a recent period is PCO/Treasury-driven removal of redundant or spent legislation (a distinct, low-salience "tidying" phenomenon) versus repeal-and-replace embedded in active, politically contested policy amendments — since the two are easy to conflate under one "repeal" headline but reflect very different things about "how NZ governs."

## What would change this conclusion

- If a careful re-read of the 07-07-2026 Spinoff piece (or its underlying dataset/scripts, if published) turns out to include an explicit repeal-type breakdown this framing missed, the "corrected" verdict above would need to soften to "partially supported."
- If question 1's sample shows Repeal-and-Replace operations (narrowly defined) are a small minority even under the broadest reasonable definition, that would argue against there being any real "mostly repeals" phenomenon to ground — a genuinely negative, still-valuable result.
- If @jonnonz1 confirms the Whiplash history-note parser already classifies amendment type reliably at full-corpus scale, question 4 could be answered immediately rather than requiring new engineering, changing the stream's second-step recommendation.
- Direct, unblocked access to `pco.govt.nz`'s revision-programme and Statutes Repeal Bill pages (rather than 2015–2026-dated Wayback snapshots) could update or correct the "housekeeping repeal" scale claims in question 5.
