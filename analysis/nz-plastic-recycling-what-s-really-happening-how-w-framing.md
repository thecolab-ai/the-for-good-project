# NZ plastic recycling is a low-recovery, data-poor system whose strongest levers are still unfinished

Discovery framing for stream root #390 (Part of #390; Stream: #390). This frames the question "what really happens to New Zealand plastic recycling?" as a flow problem: plastic placed on the market, plastic collected, material sorted at MRFs or other channels, onshore reprocessing, export, landfill, stockpiling, litter, and data confidence.

## Executive answer

- **H1: New Zealanders participate in recycling - supported.** MfE's 2025 behaviour monitoring survey found 97% of respondents reported recycling and 84% used council kerbside recycling bins, so the household effort premise is real even though survey behaviour is self-reported [MfE behaviour monitoring, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/2025-waste-and-resource-efficiency-behavioural-trend-monitoring-report.pdf).
- **H2: There is a single current answer to "how much NZ plastic is actually recycled" - corrected.** MfE's January 2025 national recycling-rate report says MfE does not regularly produce a national recycling rate, most comprehensive recycling-rate data is low or medium quality outside levy data, some TA reporting, and product-stewardship data, and plastic recovery specifically has regular export data but missing local recycling data [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf).
- **H3: Most plastic packaging is not recycled - supported for packaging, not all plastics.** MfE's current product-stewardship page says New Zealand produced an estimated 263,000 tonnes of plastic packaging in 2022 and recycled 17%; the PPPS scheme report gives the underlying estimate as 263,231 tonnes placed on market and 17.1% recycled, with 28,954 tonnes recycled onshore and 16,159 tonnes exported [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). These two citations likely trace to the same Valpak/PPPS baseline, so the number is the best current official-facing estimate, not independent corroboration.
- **H4: "Recycled" often means exported - partially supported and scope-dependent.** A 2019 PMCSA page, using the National Resource Recovery Project estimate, said 45,000 tonnes of plastic was collected for recycling and 90% was exported, but the 2025 PPPS packaging estimate says 16,159 of 45,113 tonnes of plastic packaging recycled in 2022 was exported, so the export-heavy claim is historically supported but not safe as a current packaging-wide statement [PMCSA, Rethinking Plastics data page](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/topics/rethinking-plastics/quantifying-aotearoas-plastic/); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
- **H5: Plastics 1, 2 and 5 are the practical kerbside core; other grades are the problem - mostly supported, with a correction.** Since 1 February 2024, council-managed household kerbside recycling accepts plastic bottles, trays and containers numbered 1, 2 or 5, while MfE's plastic guide describes 1, 2 and 5 as easier to recycle, 3, 6 and 7 as difficult, and 4 as possible rather than uniformly non-recyclable [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/); [MfE plastic phase-out page](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/plastic-phase-out/).
- **H6: Export destination matters because NZ cannot verify final treatment - supported.** MfE's 2023 aide-memoire says the only mechanism to track plastic-waste exports is Customs self-declaration, there is no ability to track waste once it leaves New Zealand, Malaysia and Indonesia were the largest 2022 destination markets, and officials did not have official information about processing after arrival [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf).
- **H7: NZ clearly underperforms comparable systems - partially supported, because definitions change the answer.** MfE's commissioned recycling-rate model estimates NZ's Australia-compatible all-material recycling rate at 39.4% against Australia's 63.0%, while its OECD-compatible municipal-waste rate is 37.6% and described as "middle of the pack"; for plastics specifically, Australia's 2023-24 recycling rate was 13% overall and its business-to-consumer packaging recovery rate was 22%, while NZ's plastic packaging baseline was 17.1% [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf); [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).
- **H8: The likely high-leverage changes are structural, not more household sorting alone - supported.** Standardised kerbside materials are in force, but four other 2022 household-recycling policies did not proceed; plastic packaging remains at scheme-development stage; a beverage container return scheme is only a future possibility; and Cabinet has agreed to new waste legislation with an extended producer responsibility framework intended before the 2026 general election [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/); [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/); [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. Confidence is High that NZ plastic packaging recovery is low and that all-plastic flow data is weak; Medium on exact onshore/export shares because the current plastic-packaging mass balance appears to rely heavily on one Valpak/PPPS survey-based baseline; Low on destination-country "actual recycling" outcomes until child research pulls customs, import-country, and processor evidence together.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** MfE does not regularly publish a national recycling rate and its commissioned model identifies low or medium data quality for much comprehensive recycling-rate data [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf). Council-managed kerbside standard materials have included plastics 1, 2 and 5 since 1 February 2024 [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/). Plastic packaging is a declared priority product, and MfE states that New Zealand produced an estimated 263,000 tonnes of plastic packaging in 2022 and recycled 17% [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/). Cabinet has agreed to new waste legislation covering an EPR framework, monitoring and enforcement, levy allocation, litter and role clarification, with an intended pre-election 2026 enactment [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/).

**What is interpretation:** The central interpretation is that the stream should not start from "householders are failing to recycle properly"; it should start from "the system has incomplete data, limited markets, export opacity, and unfinished producer-responsibility rules." That interpretation combines the household behaviour survey, MfE's data-gap findings, the PPPS packaging baseline, export-tracking limits, and the current status of kerbside/EPR/CRS policy [MfE behaviour monitoring, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/2025-waste-and-resource-efficiency-behavioural-trend-monitoring-report.pdf); [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf); [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/).

**Main limits:** I did not obtain a live 2024/25 customs extract for HS 3915 because the public UN Comtrade API required a subscription key during this run, so export-destination claims rely on MfE's 2023 Customs-based aide-memoire and the PPPS 2022 packaging baseline [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). The NZ Parliament skill timed out on bill search in this run, so legislation status is sourced from MfE's December 2025 legislation announcement and waste work programme rather than the bills API [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/); [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/). ## Problem statement

New Zealand's plastic-recycling problem is not simply whether people put the right bottle in the right bin; it is whether a small, geographically dispersed country can measure, sort, reprocess, sell, regulate and verify plastic flows well enough for recycling to be a real circular-economy pathway rather than a partial diversion pathway. New Zealand had an estimated resident population of 5,361,300 at 31 March 2026, and Stats NZ estimated 2,064,700 households at 31 March 2026, so even a small per-household packaging-flow error scales nationally [Stats NZ population indicator, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/); [Stats NZ dwelling and household estimates, Mar 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/). Public behaviour is not the obvious weak link: 97% of surveyed people reported recycling and 84% said council kerbside bins were their main recycling route [MfE behaviour monitoring, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/2025-waste-and-resource-efficiency-behavioural-trend-monitoring-report.pdf).

The sharper problem is that plastic packaging alone was estimated at 263,000 tonnes in 2022 with only 17% recycled, while MfE's own national recycling-rate work says local plastic recycling data is missing and would need regular collection [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf). The stream should therefore establish a current, scope-explicit mass balance before debating solutions: all plastics versus plastic packaging, household versus commercial, plastics 1/2/5 versus 3/4/6/7, onshore reprocessing versus export, and sorted-for-recycling versus actually reprocessed.

## Who it affects

**Households and renters:** NZ households are asked to separate recycling under a nationally standardised kerbside list, and the accepted household plastic category is now bottles, trays and containers numbered 1, 2 or 5 [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/). The 2025 behaviour survey also found persistent confidence and knowledge gaps: less than half of respondents were confident their items were actually recycled, and around a third found recycling rules confusing, felt they knew what happened after collection, or believed most recycling ended up in landfill [MfE behaviour monitoring, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/2025-waste-and-resource-efficiency-behavioural-trend-monitoring-report.pdf).

**Councils and ratepayers:** MfE says the financial and environmental burden of managing post-consumer plastic packaging currently falls on councils and communities, while the December 2025 waste-legislation package proposes changes to levy allocation for territorial authorities and an EPR framework to keep producers accountable through product life cycles [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/).

**Producers, importers, retailers and brand owners:** Plastic packaging has been a declared priority product since 2020, the PPPS project released a proposed scheme design in May 2025, and MfE says it is working with industry and stakeholders on the next stages of scheme development [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [PPPS final-report page, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/final-report/).

**Rural communities and primary producers:** Agrichemicals, their containers and farm plastics are also priority products, and Cabinet has approved drafting regulations for a Rural Recycling Scheme after consultation, with the January 2026 Cabinet paper saying some rural areas still rely on avoidable burning, burial or storage where take-back and recycling services are unavailable [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [CAB 601 proactive release, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/CAB-601-MfE-proactive-release.pdf).

**Export destinations and the environment:** MfE's 2023 aide-memoire says NZ exported plastic waste to at least ten countries in 2022, with Malaysia and Indonesia the largest destination markets, and also says NZ lacks official information about processing once material arrives [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf). That makes destination-country regulation, processor quality, and illegal dumping or incineration risks part of the NZ system question, not a separate overseas issue [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf).

## What's already being done

**Kerbside standardisation is live but narrower than the 2022 package.** MfE says only one of the five November 2022 household-recycling policies has come into force: standard materials for household recycling; the other four policies - urban household recycling access, urban food scraps access, private provider reporting, and a performance standard - will not go ahead at this time [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/). The live standard accepts tins and cans, glass bottles and jars, paper and cardboard, and plastic bottles, trays and containers numbered 1, 2 or 5 [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/).

**A Recycling Leadership Forum exists for excluded materials.** The RLF was established by MfE in May 2024, includes brand-owner, retail, packaging, grocery, recycling and local-government representatives, and focuses on materials explicitly excluded from kerbside collections from 1 February 2024 [RLF annual progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf). Its first report says lids and caps are no longer accepted despite being recyclable because they are often less than 5 cm, too small to hand-sort, or fall through sorting lines into general waste [RLF annual progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).

**Some hard-to-recycle and single-use plastics have been phased out, but the third tranche was delayed.** MfE says October 2022 bans covered items such as single-use plastic drink stirrers and cotton buds, polystyrene takeaway food and drink packaging, expanded polystyrene food and drink packaging, some PVC food trays, and plastics with pro-degradant additives; July 2023 bans covered single-use plastic tableware and plastic produce bags and restricted plastic straws [MfE waste policy update, 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/news/update-on-waste-policies/). MfE also says the Government removed the 2025 deadline for the third tranche that had included remaining PVC and polystyrene food and drink packaging not covered by earlier phase-outs [MfE waste policy update, 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/news/update-on-waste-policies/).

**Plastic packaging stewardship is recognised but not operating as a regulated scheme.** The Government declared plastic packaging a priority product in 2020, the 2025 PPPS report proposed a producer-led scheme, and MfE says it is working with industry and stakeholders on next-stage scheme development [MfE priority product stewardship, updated 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [PPPS final-report page, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/final-report/). The PPPS recommended Option 3 would expand source separation, improve MRFs or create plastics recovery facilities, increase producer demand for recycled content, and aim for 50% plastic-packaging recovery over 10 years, but its cost-benefit analysis reported a benefit-cost ratio of 0.97 and a net present value of -$17.2 million under its assumptions [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

**A beverage container return scheme is possible but not enacted.** MfE's 2024-26 work programme says New Zealanders use over 2.5 billion single-use drink containers each year, estimates over half end up in landfills, unused stockpiles or litter, and proposes developing a framework for a potential CRS through wider product-stewardship legislative changes [MfE waste and resource efficiency work programme 2024-26](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/waste-and-resource-efficiency-work-programme-2024-26.pdf). The current MfE waste work programme says future plans might include a beverage container return scheme made possible by waste-legislation improvements [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/).

**New waste legislation is planned around EPR and enforcement.** MfE says public consultation on amendments to the Waste Minimisation Act 2008 and Litter Act 1979 ran in April and May 2025, Cabinet has agreed to amend the WMA and replace the Litter Act, and the proposed legislation will cover levy allocation, an EPR framework, stronger monitoring and enforcement, litter and mismanaged waste controls, and role clarity [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/). MfE's submission summary says 95% of submitters supported a modern EPR framework, while 69% supported ending central-government accreditation of voluntary product-stewardship schemes as a legislative function [MfE summary of submissions, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/proposed-amendments-to-waste-legislation-summary-of-submissions.pdf).

## Evidence framing

### 1. The plastic-flow baseline is the first research task

The old all-plastics public baseline is stark but dated: the PMCSA page lists 45,000 tonnes of plastic collected for recycling and 380,000 tonnes of plastic waste landfilled, and says the National Resource Recovery Project estimated 45,000 tonnes of recycled plastic with 90% exported [PMCSA, Rethinking Plastics data page](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/topics/rethinking-plastics/quantifying-aotearoas-plastic/). The current packaging baseline is narrower and more recent: PPPS/Valpak estimated 263,231 tonnes of plastic packaging placed on the NZ market in 2022, 45,113 tonnes recycled, 28,954 tonnes onshore, and 16,159 tonnes exported [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). These estimates cannot be treated as a direct trend because they use different scopes, different years, and different data sources [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf).

### 2. Kerbside plastics are now deliberately narrower

The standard kerbside list accepts plastic bottles, trays and containers numbered 1, 2 or 5 [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/). MfE's 2023 export aide-memoire says only plastics 1, 2 and 5 would be accepted in household kerbside recycling because they were most likely to be recycled onshore and had good end markets [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf). MfE's plastic guide classifies PET 1, HDPE 2 and PP 5 as easier to recycle, PVC 3, PS 6 and other 7 as difficult, and LDPE 4 as possible, which means the stream should avoid treating "3-7" as one material class [MfE plastic phase-out page](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/plastic-phase-out/).

### 3. Export is smaller for packaging than the old public shorthand suggests, but destination opacity remains real

MfE's 2023 aide-memoire says annual plastic-waste exports declined after 2016 and stabilised, shredded/flaked plastic exports rose after China's National Sword policy, PE polymers were 69% of 2022 plastic-waste exports, and Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand and China were the four largest destination shares shown for 2022 [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf). The same aide-memoire says exports are tracked through Customs self-declaration, consignments valued under $1,000 do not require export entries, there is no ability to track waste after it leaves NZ, and MfE had no official information about processing in the receiving country [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf). That supports a child question on export traceability rather than a framing conclusion that all exported material is dumped.

### 4. International comparisons are useful only when the denominator is explicit

MfE's recycling-rate report estimates NZ's Australia-compatible all-material recycling rate at 39.4% versus Australia's 63.0%, its EU-compatible municipal-waste rate at 41.6% versus the EU's 48%, and its OECD-compatible municipal-waste rate at 37.6%, while warning that municipal-waste definitions vary by jurisdiction and that no ready-made international approach captures the full range of NZ waste [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf). Eurostat's 2026 article says the EU recycled 48% of municipal waste in 2024 under its longstanding dataset and 43.3% under the 2023 municipal-waste reporting-obligation data, and also says the voluntary and mandatory EU datasets can produce different results because the mandatory rules are stricter [Eurostat municipal waste statistics, data extracted Jan 2026](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Municipal_waste_statistics). For plastics, Australia's official 2023-24 data says its plastics recovery rate was 14%, recycling rate excluding energy recovery was 13%, business-to-consumer packaging recovery rate was 22%, and around 87% of plastics leaving use went directly to landfill [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).

### 5. The practical levers cluster around data, demand, design, collection channels, and producer responsibility

MfE's work programme says resource recovery and recycling have been hampered by a lack of infrastructure, lack of household confidence, confusion about what can be recycled, and non-recyclable and harmful materials entering supply chains [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/). PPPS identifies source separation, MRF or plastics recovery facility upgrades, improved material quality, producer demand for recycled content, labelling, education, and commercial/community collection systems as components of its preferred option [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). Australia's 2023-24 plastics report is a useful comparator because it separates consumption, end-of-life generation, recovery, local reprocessing, export, capacity and recycled content in one annual reporting framework [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **research: What is the best current mass balance for NZ plastics from placed-on-market to collection, onshore reprocessing, export, landfill and leakage?** This must reconcile the dated PMCSA all-plastics estimate, the 2022 PPPS plastic-packaging baseline, MfE's January 2025 recycling-rate model, and any accessible Customs/Stats NZ export series [PMCSA, Rethinking Plastics data page](https://www.pmcsa.ac.nz/topics/rethinking-plastics/quantifying-aotearoas-plastic/); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf). It should explicitly split all plastics from plastic packaging, household from commercial, and plastics 1/2/5 from 3/4/6/7 where public data allows [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/).
2. **research: Where do NZ plastic-waste and plastic-raw-material exports go, and what evidence exists about their actual destination treatment?** This should update MfE's 2022 destination picture, distinguish HS 3915 "plastic waste" from shredded/flaked or other "plastic raw material" exports, and test the gap between exporter due diligence claims and official verification limits [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf). It should use Customs/Stats NZ/UN Comtrade or mirror datasets where accessible, plus destination-country controls where public [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf).
3. **research: How does NZ plastic recycling performance compare like-for-like with Australia, the EU and selected high-performing EPR or CRS systems?** This should keep plastic-specific recovery separate from all-material municipal recycling, and should explain denominator differences before ranking NZ [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf); [Eurostat municipal waste statistics, data extracted Jan 2026](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Municipal_waste_statistics); [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting). It should identify which better-performing systems use mandatory EPR, deposit return, recycled-content demand, disposal restrictions, or public data obligations [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
4. **research: Which NZ policy levers are real, delayed or missing for plastic recycling, and what would each change in the flow?** This should map standardised kerbside, plastic phase-outs, the Recycling Leadership Forum, PPPS, farm-plastics stewardship, waste-legislation amendments, waste levy settings, and CRS status against the mass-balance stages they can affect [MfE household recycling page, updated 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/improving-household-recycling-and-food-scrap-collections/); [MfE waste policy update, 2024](https://environment.govt.nz/news/update-on-waste-policies/); [RLF annual progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf); [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/). It should distinguish enacted policy from work-programme intentions [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/).
5. **research: What structural barriers make NZ plastic recycling economically hard, and which interventions look highest leverage?** This should test scale, transport distance, contamination, MRF capability, polymer mix, coloured PET, soft plastics, recycled-content demand, virgin-plastic economics, and product design using MfE, PPPS, RLF, recycler, council and comparable-country evidence [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [RLF annual progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf). It should end by ranking interventions for a small volunteer team to investigate after the G1 human gate, not by opening ideation work now.

## What would change this framing

- A current MfE, Stats NZ or Customs release that gives a complete all-plastics mass balance by polymer, source, destination and actual treatment would replace the current "data-poor" conclusion [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf).
- Independent verification that the PPPS/Valpak 2022 onshore/export split materially undercounts or overcounts onshore reprocessing would change the packaging baseline [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
- Evidence that exported NZ plastic is routinely tracked through to audited recycling outputs would weaken the export-opacity concern [MfE aide-memoire BRF-3444, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/OIA/Files/Doc3-BRF-3444_Aide_Memoire-Information_on_plastic_waste_exports_out_of_New_Zealand-for_release.pdf).
- Enacted EPR or CRS legislation before the stream reaches synthesis would shift the "unfinished levers" assessment from policy-design to implementation-performance research [MfE waste legislation announcement, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/news/government-confirms-new-waste-and-litter-legislation/); [MfE waste work programme](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/work-programme-on-waste/).
- Lived experience from council waste managers, MRF operators, recyclers, community resource-recovery groups and packaging producers would be needed before this stream turns evidence into solutions, because published sources do not show contract-level economics or operational constraints consistently [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf).
