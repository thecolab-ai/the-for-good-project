# NZ Super is not unusually costly by OECD standards, but unchanged settings make it a rising long-term fiscal claim

Discovery framing for stream root #440 (Part of #440). Tests the root's claims about NZ Superannuation's design, generosity, ageing pressure, fiscal trajectory, and reform politics against current official and OECD evidence, then proposes child research questions.

## Executive answer

- **Design claim - supported, with eligibility nuance.** New Zealand Superannuation is a cash pension for people aged 65+ that is not means-tested, but it is not literally paid to "everyone over 65": applicants must satisfy citizenship/residence-class, ordinary-residence, and residence-duration rules, the residence requirement is phasing from 10 years to 20 years for younger cohorts, and overseas pensions can reduce payments [Work and Income, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html); [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super); [MSD NZS/VP data tables, Mar 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx).
- **"One of the most generous" - corrected.** NZ Super is unusually simple and universal, but OECD 2025 does not show New Zealand as unusually generous for average earners: its modelled net replacement rate is 43.8% for an average earner against an OECD average of 63.2%, while low earners receive 67.0% and high earners 23.7% [OECD, net pension replacement rates, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html). New Zealand's projected public pension expenditure is also below the OECD average: OECD 2025 puts New Zealand at 5.3% of GDP in 2025 and 6.5% in 2050, compared with OECD32 averages of 9.0% and 10.0% [OECD, long-term pension expenditure projections, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html).
- **Ageing claim - partially supported, with date correction.** Stats NZ's 2024-base national population projections put the 65+ population at 0.87 million, or 17%, in 2024, and give a 90% probability range of 1.44-1.55 million, or 21-24%, in 2051; the "25%+" threshold is in the 2078 range of 25-33%, not the median 2050 picture in the latest release [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/).
- **Current fiscal baseline - supported.** Budget 2026 appropriates $26.481 billion for NZ Super in 2026/27 and nearly $132 million for Veteran's Pension; MSD counted 967,176 NZ Super recipients and 971,865 NZS/VP recipients in March 2026; against Stats NZ's provisional resident population of 5.361 million at 31 March 2026, the NZ Super appropriation is roughly $4,940 per resident and roughly $27,400 per NZ Super recipient, as a transparent calculation from those sources [Vote Social Development Estimates 2026/27, Budget 2026](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/estimates/v9/est26-v9-socdev.pdf); [MSD benefit fact sheets, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/index.html); [Stats NZ population indicator, 31 Mar 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/).
- **Long-term fiscal pressure - supported, but not a prediction.** Treasury's 2025 Long-term Fiscal Statement says unchanged current policies are not sustainable, models government spending per person rising from $18,300 to $35,900 in inflation-adjusted terms by 2065, and projects NZS expenditure rising from 5.1% of GDP now to around 8% by 2065; Treasury explicitly frames this as a current-policy projection, not a forecast of what will happen [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).
- **Reform politics - unsettled, not absent.** The current Government's Speech from the Throne says the NZ Super age remains 65, while National's party policy page says the age would remain 65 until 2044 and then gradually rise to 67; the Retirement Commission's 2025 review therefore leans first on a cross-party retirement-income accord and a 10-year roadmap rather than a single ready-to-legislate NZS cut [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697); [National Party, commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [Retirement Commission 2025 review media release](https://retirement.govt.nz/news/latest-news/a-roadmap-to-a-better-retirement-review-calls-for-cross-party-action).

**Overall confidence:** Medium-high. The design, Budget 2026, MSD recipient, Stats NZ projection, OECD comparison, and Treasury long-term-fiscal claims are high-confidence because they rest on official or OECD sources. The reform-politics framing is medium-confidence because this analysis did not interview parties, older people's organisations, unions, iwi, or fiscal officials, and because 2026 election-positioning may shift after this 2026-07-04 snapshot.

## Problem statement

NZ Super is a broad, tax-funded, wage-linked entitlement whose annual cost is mechanically pushed by recipient numbers and indexation: in BEFU 2026, core Crown expenses rise from $147.2 billion in 2025/26 to $154.8 billion in 2026/27, and Treasury attributes $1.8 billion of that increase to NZ Super, split roughly equally between more recipients and indexation [Treasury, BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf). The near-term fiscal question is not whether NZ Super already makes New Zealand an OECD pension-spending outlier, because OECD 2025 puts New Zealand below the OECD average on public pension expenditure as a share of GDP [OECD, long-term pension expenditure projections, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html). The hard policy question is whether unchanged settings remain acceptable when the 65+ population rises from 17% in 2024 to 21-24% in 2051, the tax-heavy 15-64 age bracket grows more slowly than NZS recipients, and Treasury's current-policy projection pushes NZS from 5.1% of GDP now to about 8% by 2065 [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/); [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).

The stream should therefore frame NZ Super sustainability as a distributional and political-economy problem, not just an accounting problem. A reform that saves money by raising the age, changing indexation, reviving a surcharge, means-testing, shifting more weight to KiwiSaver, or increasing NZ Super Fund pre-funding will have different effects on current retirees, near-retirees, renters, lower-income workers, carers, disabled people, Māori and Pacific peoples, migrants with shorter residence histories, and younger taxpayers; the 2025 Retirement Commission review treats retirement-income policy as a system spanning NZ Super, KiwiSaver, decumulation, work after 65, housing, and stewardship [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies); [NZIER for Retirement Commission, Aotearoa New Zealand in 2050](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).

## Who it affects

- **Current recipients:** MSD counted 967,176 NZ Super recipients and 4,689 Veteran's Pension recipients in March 2026, for 971,865 NZS/VP recipients in total [MSD NZS/VP data tables, Mar 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx).
- **Older people with limited private income:** The Retirement Commission says 40% of people aged 65+ have virtually no income besides NZ Super and another 20% have only a little more; this is a load-bearing welfare-risk claim that child research should verify against the underlying 2022/2025 review evidence before using it as a policy conclusion [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super).
- **Taxpayers and future cohorts:** Treasury says the ratio of people aged 15-64 to people aged over 65 has moved from seven in the 1960s to four now and is projected to be two by 2065, so fewer working-age people will carry each retiree under unchanged tax-funded settings [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).
- **Migrant and overseas-pension cohorts:** The residence test is phasing by date of birth from 10 years to 20 years from age 20, and Work and Income says qualifying overseas pensions can affect NZ Super payments [Work and Income, NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html).
- **Women, carers, self-employed people, low-income workers, and people outside stable home ownership:** The 2025 Retirement Commission review foregrounds gender, self-employment, KiwiSaver contribution gaps, decumulation, housing, and older people's financial voices as retirement-income issues, so downstream research should avoid treating "retirees" or "taxpayers" as homogeneous groups [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies).

## What's already being done

- **Official fiscal monitoring:** Treasury publishes Economic and Fiscal Updates twice a year, says BEFU 2026 was the most recent forecast as at 28 May 2026, and says the next forecast will be PREFU 2026 on 29 September 2026 [Treasury forecasts page, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budgets/forecasts). Treasury's 2025 Long-term Fiscal Statement gives the current 40-year sustainability baseline and makes the NZS/health/debt tradeoff explicit [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).
- **Budget and recipient-data baselines:** Budget 2026 publishes Vote Social Development appropriations for NZ Super and Veteran's Pension, while MSD publishes quarterly NZS/VP recipient tables, including age, gender, ethnicity, territorial authority, and overseas-pension deductions [Vote Social Development Estimates 2026/27, Budget 2026](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/estimates/v9/est26-v9-socdev.pdf); [MSD benefit fact sheets, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/index.html).
- **Pre-funding through the NZ Super Fund:** Treasury's BEFU 2026 NZ Super Fund contribution-rate model calculates capital contributions under the legislated formula and includes historical, forecast, and projected NZS expenditure in nominal dollars and as a share of nominal GDP; the Guardians describe the Fund's purpose as adding Crown wealth and reducing future taxpayers' burden for superannuation [Treasury, NZSF contribution-rate model BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026); [NZ Super Fund, purpose and mandate](https://nzsuperfund.nz/about-the-guardians/purpose-and-mandate/).
- **Eligibility tightening already enacted:** The residence requirement is already increasing by cohort to 20 years for people born on or after 1 July 1977, while the qualifying age remains 65 under current Work and Income rules [Work and Income, NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html).
- **Retirement-income stewardship work:** The Retirement Commission's 2025 review recommends a cross-party retirement-income accord, a Parliamentary working group for a 10-year roadmap, and a pan-sector group led by the Commission, with the roadmap explicitly addressing KiwiSaver, NZ Super, and retirement-planning innovation [Retirement Commission 2025 review media release](https://retirement.govt.nz/news/latest-news/a-roadmap-to-a-better-retirement-review-calls-for-cross-party-action).

## Evidence check against the root claims

### H1 - "Flat-rate, no means test, paid to everyone over 65"

**Grade: supported, with correction.** The Retirement Commission describes NZ Super as a pension for people aged 65 that is not means-tested and has residence-based eligibility criteria [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super). Work and Income adds that applicants must be aged 65+, meet citizenship or residence-class rules, be ordinarily resident in New Zealand/the Cook Islands/Niue/Tokelau when applying, and meet a residence-duration table that rises to 20 years for people born on or after 1 July 1977 [Work and Income, NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html). MSD's March 2026 workbook also notes that NZS is not income tested except where a non-qualified partner is included, and that overseas pensions can reduce NZS/VP [MSD NZS/VP data tables, Mar 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx).

### H2 - "One of the most generous universal pension schemes in the OECD"

**Grade: corrected.** New Zealand is distinctive because its Tier 1 public pension is universal and simple, and the Retirement Commission says NZ Super protects against poverty in old age [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super). But "one of the most generous" is not supported if generosity means replacement income for an average worker or pension spending as a share of GDP: OECD 2025 puts New Zealand's average-earner net replacement rate at 43.8% against an OECD average of 63.2%, and it identifies New Zealand as one of the lowest replacement-rate systems for high earners [OECD, net pension replacement rates, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html). OECD 2025 projects New Zealand public pension expenditure at 5.3% of GDP in 2025 and 6.5% in 2050, below the OECD32 averages of 9.0% and 10.0% for those years [OECD, long-term pension expenditure projections, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html). The more defensible framing is that NZ Super is broad and adequate for low earners, not that it is currently among the OECD's most generous by average-earner replacement or fiscal-cost metrics.

### H3 - "65+ now around 17%, projected to hit 25%+ by 2050"

**Grade: partially supported, corrected on timing.** Stats NZ's 2024-base projections support the 17% starting point for the 65+ population in 2024 and show strong ageing pressure [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/). The latest Stats NZ release gives a 90% probability range of 21-24% aged 65+ in 2051, and 25-33% in 2078, so "25%+ by 2050" is too strong for the current national-projection release [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/). Treasury's fiscal concern still holds because it is the ratio of NZS recipients to the working-age tax base, not the exact 2050 percentage, that drives the current-policy fiscal path [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).

### H4 - "Treasury projections show a sustainability issue"

**Grade: supported.** Treasury's 2025 Long-term Fiscal Statement says unchanged current policies are not sustainable, projects spending per person nearly doubling in real terms by 2065, and projects net core Crown debt excluding the NZSF and advances reaching 104% of GDP by 2050 and 200% by 2065 in the Long-term Fiscal Model [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). The same statement says NZS expenditure was 3.9% of GDP in 2006, is 5.1% now, and would grow to around 8% by 2065 if current policies are maintained [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). This is not Treasury predicting a passive drift to 200% debt; Treasury says the projection is not its prediction and expects policy action to alleviate fiscal pressures [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).

### H5 - "Reforms on the table include age increases, means testing, and tapering"

**Grade: partially supported, but current official policy is narrower.** New Zealand has already used two big pension-setting levers: the NZS age rose from 60 to 65 over 1992-2001, and Treasury's 2005 working paper links that age rise to a sharp increase in older people's labour-force participation [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09). New Zealand also operated a superannuation surcharge before it was abolished in 1998, and a Treasury/MSD background paper states current NZS is neither income nor asset tested [Treasury/MSD retirement-income framework paper](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf). In current politics, however, the Government's formal 2023 programme said the entitlement age would remain 65, National's party policy delays a rise to 67 until 2044, and the Retirement Commission's 2025 review calls for an accord and roadmap rather than recommending an immediate age rise or means test [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697); [National Party, commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [Retirement Commission 2025 review media release](https://retirement.govt.nz/news/latest-news/a-roadmap-to-a-better-retirement-review-calls-for-cross-party-action).

## Proposed child research issues

1. **research: What is the reconciled current and projected cost of NZ Super, gross and net, through 2065?** This should establish one clean fiscal baseline across Budget 2026 appropriations, MSD recipient counts, Treasury's LTFS/LTFM, the NZSF contribution-rate model, and OECD pension-expenditure series. It should separate gross appropriations from net-of-tax cost, per-resident cost, per-recipient cost, and percentage-of-GDP measures so later reform work does not mix incompatible denominators. Start with Vote Social Development 2026/27, BEFU 2026 tables 2.3/5.2, Treasury's 2025 LTFS and LTFM spreadsheet, the BEFU 2026 NZSF model, MSD benefit fact sheets, and Stats NZ population projections [Vote Social Development Estimates 2026/27](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/estimates/v9/est26-v9-socdev.pdf); [Treasury BEFU 2026 PDF](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf); [Treasury LTFS 2025 PDF](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Treasury NZSF contribution model BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026); [MSD benefit fact sheets, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/index.html); [Stats NZ 2024-base national projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/).
2. **research: How does NZ Super actually compare with OECD pension systems on generosity, targeting, age, poverty protection, and fiscal cost?** This should test the corrected "not one of the most generous by average-earner replacement rate" finding against the full OECD profile, including low-earner adequacy, old-age poverty, public/private pension split, pension age, means testing, and Australia as the closest comparator. Start with OECD Pensions at a Glance 2025, the Retirement Commission's NZ Super/OECD material, and NZIER's Australia-New Zealand comparison paper [OECD Pensions at a Glance 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4.html); [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super); [NZIER for Retirement Commission, Aotearoa New Zealand in 2050](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
3. **research: What NZ Super reform options have credible public evidence on fiscal savings and distributional effects?** This should compare raising the age, changing indexation, reviving a surcharge or income test, asset-testing or partial tapering, changing residence rules, increasing NZSF pre-funding, and shifting more adequacy onto KiwiSaver. It needs fiscal estimates and distributional evidence, not just a list of proposals. Start with Treasury LTFS/LTFM scenario tools, the NZSF contribution model, the 2025 Retirement Commission review and background papers, party policy documents, and past Treasury/MSD papers on the surcharge and age changes [Treasury LTFS 2025 PDF](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Treasury NZSF contribution model BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026); [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies); [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09); [Treasury/MSD retirement-income framework paper](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf).
4. **research: Who bears the cost or risk under each reform path?** This should identify which groups would lose income, delay retirement, pay more tax, or face higher private-saving risk under plausible reforms, with attention to renters, women, carers, disabled people, Māori and Pacific peoples, manual workers, migrants, self-employed people, and current versus future cohorts. Start with MSD NZS/VP recipient tables, Stats NZ population projections, Retirement Commission 2025 research on women, self-employed people and older people's voices, NZIER 2050, and public health/life-expectancy evidence [MSD NZS/VP data tables, Mar 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx); [Stats NZ 2024-base national projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/); [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies); [NZIER for Retirement Commission, Aotearoa New Zealand in 2050](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
5. **research: Why has New Zealand resisted means testing and major age reform, and what would make a durable accord politically feasible?** This should examine the 1985-1998 surcharge, the 1992-2001 age rise, the 1993 accord and its collapse, NZ First/National/Labour/ACT/Green positions, older people's organisations, and public-trust arguments around universality. It should distinguish "technically feasible" from "politically durable". ## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** NZ Super's current eligibility is age 65 plus residence/status rules [Work and Income, NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html); the 2026/27 Vote Social Development NZ Super appropriation is $26.481b [Vote Social Development Estimates 2026/27](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/estimates/v9/est26-v9-socdev.pdf); MSD's March 2026 NZS/VP workbook records 967,176 NZS recipients and 971,865 NZS/VP recipients [MSD NZS/VP data tables, Mar 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx); Stats NZ's 2024-base projection puts 65+ at 17% in 2024 and 21-24% in 2051 [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/); OECD 2025 puts New Zealand's average-earner net replacement rate below the OECD average and projected pension spending below the OECD average [OECD, net replacement rates, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html); [OECD, pension expenditure projections, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html); Treasury's current-policy LTFS scenario projects NZS to about 8% of GDP by 2065 and net core Crown debt to about 200% of GDP by 2065 [Treasury, He Tirohanga Mokopuna 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).

**What is interpretation:** The central reframing - that NZ Super is not currently an OECD outlier but is a rising long-term fiscal claim under unchanged settings - is a synthesis across Budget, OECD, Stats NZ, MSD, and Treasury sources. The reform-politics judgement - that durability likely requires a cross-party roadmap rather than a single-agent answer to "raise age or means test?" - follows from the current Government's age-65 commitment, National's delayed age-rise policy, the Retirement Commission's accord recommendation, and the history of past surcharge/age reforms [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697); [National Party, commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [Retirement Commission 2025 review media release](https://retirement.govt.nz/news/latest-news/a-roadmap-to-a-better-retirement-review-calls-for-cross-party-action); [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09).

**Main limits:** This framing used public sources only and did not interview policymakers, older people, iwi/Māori organisations, disability advocates, unions, employers, or fiscal officials. It did not extract the BEFU 2026 NZSF model workbook or Treasury LTFM spreadsheet into a reconciled cost table, so the first child issue must do that before the stream quotes a definitive gross/net/pathway number. It also did not verify every current party's 2026 election position; the next hard update point is Treasury's PREFU 2026 release scheduled for 29 September 2026 [Treasury forecasts page, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budgets/forecasts).

**What would change the conclusions:** A new PREFU 2026 or party manifesto that materially changes NZS age, indexation, means-testing, or NZSF contributions would alter the political-realism section [Treasury forecasts page, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budgets/forecasts). A Treasury LTFM/NZSF model reconciliation showing materially different net-of-tax NZS paths from the headline LTFS text would change the cost baseline [Treasury, NZSF contribution-rate model BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026). Stronger distributional evidence on life expectancy, ability to keep working, retirement wealth, and housing costs by ethnicity, gender, disability, occupation, and tenure would change which reform options look fair enough to investigate after G1 [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies).
