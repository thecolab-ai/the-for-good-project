# NZ teacher supply is a subject, language and place mismatch, not a uniform headcount crisis

Discovery framing for stream root #439 (Part of #439). This frames the NZ teacher-supply problem, tests the root's claims against current public evidence, and proposes child research issues for the runner to open.

## Executive answer

- **The root's "teacher supply crisis" premise is partially supported, but needs narrowing.** The Ministry's 2025 Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, published in February 2026, projects a **primary** teacher surplus of 530 in 2026 rising to 1,350 by 2028 under the medium scenario, while **secondary** has a shortfall of 710 in 2026 narrowing to 190 by 2028; the same Ministry release says the system has more qualified teachers than at any point in the last 20 years [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/teacher-demand-and-supply-2025-report-released); [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc). The useful stream question is therefore not "is NZ short of teachers everywhere?", but "where is the system failing to put the right teachers into the right roles?".
- **Secondary subject shortage is strongly supported.** The Ministry says secondary teachers are not easily interchangeable across subjects and shortages in science, technology, mathematics and te reo Māori cannot be offset by surplus in other subjects [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc). PPTA's June 2026 survey of 155 secondary and composite principals reported that 20% of advertised positions with known outcomes could not be filled, 8% were filled by Limited Authority to Teach holders, 47% of principals had teachers working outside specialist areas, and the unfilled/LAT subject lists included mathematics, science, Te Reo Māori and hard materials technology [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
- **Māori-medium and te reo Māori supply pressure is strongly supported.** Māori-medium enrolment grew to 28,382 students across 362 schools as at 1 July 2025, up 4.6% from July 2024, while the Ministry's projection warns that Kaupapa Māori, Māori-medium, rumaki reo and reo rua settings face persistent shortages because language and cultural expertise make recruitment and retention especially difficult [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc). The Ministry's EBITE funding page explicitly describes the programme as designed to address a critical shortage of qualified teachers in immersion settings and te reo Māori subject teaching [Education Workforce, EBITE, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award).
- **The "decile 1-3" claim is corrected rather than accepted as stated.** Decile was replaced by the Schooling Equity Index from 2023, and Ministry evidence says individual school socio-economic status does not by itself determine turnover, although turnover is related to socio-economic status across groups of high-Equity-Index schools and is also shaped by age, employment type, school size and rurality [Education Counts, teacher turnover and Equity Index, 2021](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/he-whakaaro/he-whakaaro-teacher-turnover-and-equity-index); [Education Counts, teacher turnover definitions, 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover). The current operational analogue is "priority staffing schools": 630 state and state-integrated schools/kura, 26% of the sector, were eligible for the 2025/26 Teacher Bonding Scheme using retention, readvertising and regional/kura nomination criteria [Education Workforce, TBS eligible schools, 2025](https://assets.workforce.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/TBS%20Eligible%20schools%20-%202025-26%20Cohort.pdf?VersionId=nsoqra3SK8D5d2E4xIhwL90eeDN9FzDa).
- **Retention is not a simple national attrition crisis, but workload and local mobility are real risks.** Education Counts reports a 2025 entry rate into teaching of 9.7% and a 2024 leaving rate of 7.3%, which it describes as the lowest leaving rate since 2004 except for the 2020 COVID dip [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement). At the same time, TALIS 2024 found New Zealand full-time Year 7-10 teachers worked 48 hours per week, Year 1-6 teachers worked 51 hours per week, 33% of Year 7-10 teachers experienced stress "a lot", and teachers in socioeconomically disadvantaged schools reported stress "a lot" at 45% versus 28% in more advantaged schools [Education Counts, TALIS 2024](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report).

**Overall confidence:** Medium-high. The national and regional projection, Māori-language enrolment, ITE, movement and TALIS claims rest on current official sources; the secondary vacancy/suitability evidence is a large recurring sector survey rather than an official census; current live vacancy counts from Education Gazette are mutable and should be treated as a lead for a child issue, not as this framing's core evidence.

## Problem statement

New Zealand's teacher-supply problem is best framed as a **mismatch problem**: the national stock of teachers is close to demand in aggregate, but shortages persist where the job needs a secondary subject specialist, te reo Māori or Māori-medium capability, a rural or isolated placement, or a school context that has repeated recruitment and retention difficulty [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc); [Education Workforce, TBS eligible schools, 2025](https://assets.workforce.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/TBS%20Eligible%20schools%20-%202025-26%20Cohort.pdf?VersionId=nsoqra3SK8D5d2E4xIhwL90eeDN9FzDa). This affects secondary students whose schools cannot staff specialist classes, Māori-medium learners whose pathway depends on qualified kaiako with te reo Māori and cultural expertise, rural and isolated communities where placement and housing costs deter candidates, and high-Equity-Index communities where teacher stress and turnover pressures are more acute [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Counts, TALIS 2024](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report).

The scale is material even after correcting the headline crisis claim. The Ministry projects secondary demand at about 30,650 teachers in 2026 and says the medium-scenario shortfall of 710 secondary teachers is equivalent to more than one teacher per secondary school on average across 491 secondary schools [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc). PPTA's 2026 survey reported 249 advertised secondary/composite vacancies among responding schools from the start of Term 1 to 9 March 2026, and separately estimated 209 unadvertised vacancies nationally from its respondent pattern [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655). Māori-medium schooling served 28,382 students in 2025, and the wider Māori-language-in-English-medium category served 266,199 students, so te reo Māori workforce constraints are not limited to immersion kura [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling).

This stream should produce an evidence base that distinguishes four different shortages: **vacancy shortage** (unfilled roles), **specialist shortage** (roles filled by non-specialists or LAT holders), **language/cultural capability shortage** (te reo Māori and Māori-medium kaiako), and **distribution shortage** (teachers available nationally but not for specific regions, rural schools or priority staffing schools). That distinction is interpretation from the evidence above, and it should be tested by the child issues rather than assumed.

## What is already being done

The Ministry is already using a targeted workforce toolkit rather than a single national recruitment campaign. The 2025 projection says workforce initiatives are expected to add 4,140 teachers over 2026-2028 under the medium scenario, including 1,740 primary and 2,400 secondary teachers [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/teacher-demand-and-supply-2025-report-released). The specific initiatives named in the Ministry report include TeachNZ scholarships, employment-based ITE programmes, Teacher Education Refresh, the Overseas Relocation Grant and the School Onsite Training Programme [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc).

Current targeted programmes map onto the shortage types this stream should test. Around 460 TeachNZ scholarships are awarded each year and are prioritised for difficult-to-recruit subjects including STEM and te reo Māori [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/teacher-demand-and-supply-2025-report-released); Te Tipu Whakarito offers 130 annual scholarships for students aiming to teach in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings or teach te reo Māori in English-medium secondary schools [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); EBITE is a two-year employment-based route for LAT holders in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium and te reo Māori roles [Education Workforce, EBITE, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award); Go Rural provides a $4,000 grant for eligible student teachers taking five-week-plus placements at 454 rural or isolated schools, with up to 123 grants in 2026 [Education Workforce, Go Rural, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/go-rural-isolated-placements-fund); and the Teacher Bonding Scheme offers up to $40,000 before tax over five years for teachers recruited into eligible priority-staffing roles, with 185 places per year [Education Workforce, Teacher Bonding Scheme, 2025](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/principals-and-schools/initiatives-schools-and-kura/teacher-bonding-scheme).

The open policy question is whether these programmes are large enough, targeted enough and sticky enough. That is not answered by their existence. The child research should test whether funded places actually translate into qualified teachers in shortage subjects and settings, whether incentive values are material relative to housing and workload barriers, and whether the programmes reduce reliance on LAT holders and non-specialist teaching.

## Comparator scan

Australia is a direct comparator because it is explicitly responding to national teacher workforce shortages through a National Teacher Workforce Action Plan agreed by education ministers in December 2022, and its Commonwealth Teaching Scholarships provide up to A$40,000 for undergraduate teacher education students and up to A$20,000 for postgraduate students [Australian Government Department of Education, National Teacher Workforce Action Plan](https://www.education.gov.au/national-teacher-workforce-action-plan); [Australian Government Department of Education, Commonwealth Teaching Scholarships](https://www.education.gov.au/teaching-scholarships). Its relevance for NZ is strongest around national workforce data, priority cohorts, scholarships and rural/remote incentives; its weaker fit is that Australian federal/state responsibilities and larger labour markets differ from NZ's centralised schooling workforce.

England is a direct comparator for subject-specific retention incentives and workload policy. The UK Government's 6,500 additional teachers delivery plan includes targeted retention incentives focused on disadvantaged areas and priority subjects such as physics and mathematics, and England also ran a workload reduction taskforce to reduce teacher and leader working hours by five hours per week within three years [UK Department for Education, 6,500 teachers plan, 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/every-child-achieving-and-thriving/6500-additional-teachers-delivery-plan-html-version); [UK Department for Education, workload reduction taskforce](https://www.gov.uk/government/groups/workload-reduction-taskforce). Its relevance for NZ is strongest around subject-and-place retention payments and workload governance; its weaker fit is that England's academy/trust system and teacher labour market are structurally different from NZ's school boards and national collective agreements.

Singapore is a less direct shortage comparator and a stronger workforce-design comparator. Singapore's Ministry of Education publishes three teacher career tracks for teaching, leadership and specialist development, while TALIS 2024 shows Singapore combines relatively high social valuation and salary satisfaction with long working hours and stress risks [Singapore MOE, career tracks](https://www.moe.gov.sg/careers/become-teachers/pri-sec-jc-ci/professional-development); [Singapore MOE, TALIS 2024 press release](https://www.moe.gov.sg/news/press-releases/20251007-singapore-teachers-embrace-digital-technologies-and-benefit-from-strong-professional-development-oecd-talis-2024-study); [OECD, TALIS 2024 Singapore country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/singapore_8e88e29a-en.html). Its transferable lessons are more likely to be career structure, induction, mentoring and professional status than a plug-in solution for NZ's Māori-medium and rural recruitment constraints.

## Proposed child research questions

1. **What is the current teacher shortage signal by subject, region and school setting once vacancy listings, Ministry projections and secondary staffing survey data are reconciled?** This should separate live vacancies, unadvertised vacancies, unfilled appointments, LAT-filled roles and non-specialist teaching, because each measure captures a different kind of shortage [Education Gazette vacancies](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/vacancies/); [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
2. **How large is the Māori-medium, Kaupapa Māori and te reo Māori teaching pipeline relative to learner demand?** This should connect Māori-language schooling growth, Māori-medium ITE enrolments, EBITE, Te Tipu Whakarito and LAT reliance, then identify whether the binding constraint is language proficiency, training capacity, placement geography, retention or competition with English-medium te reo Māori roles [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Workforce, EBITE, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship).
3. **Which hard-to-staff schools are most affected, and how should the old "decile 1-3" premise be translated into current Equity Index and priority-staffing data?** This should quantify the overlap between high Equity Index, rural/isolation, retention/readvertising criteria, Māori-medium/kura nominations and regional shortages rather than treating disadvantage as a single cause [Education Counts, teacher turnover and Equity Index, 2021](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/he-whakaaro/he-whakaaro-teacher-turnover-and-equity-index); [Education Workforce, TBS eligible schools, 2025](https://assets.workforce.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/TBS%20Eligible%20schools%20-%202025-26%20Cohort.pdf?VersionId=nsoqra3SK8D5d2E4xIhwL90eeDN9FzDa).
4. **Where does NZ's teacher pipeline lose or redirect people between ITE enrolment, graduation, registration, first job, returning-to-teaching and overseas recruitment?** This should test whether rising 2025 ITE enrolments are likely to relieve shortage subjects/settings, and whether employment-based and career-switch pathways are producing classroom-ready teachers for the roles that need them [Education Counts, ITE statistics, 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics); [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement); [ERO, Ready, set, teach, 2024](https://evidence.ero.govt.nz/media/s4hh1mg0/ready-set-teach-how-well-prepared-and-supported-are-new-teachers-report.pdf).
5. **What retention and working-condition factors are actually driving teacher exits, mobility and hard-to-staff vacancies?** This should distinguish leaving the profession from moving schools, and test workload, change fatigue, pay satisfaction, leadership, housing/location, early-career support and subject-specialist labour-market alternatives against public datasets and sector surveys [Education Counts, TALIS 2024](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report); [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
6. **Which policy instruments from Australia, England and Singapore are transferable to NZ's STEM, Māori-medium and hard-to-staff shortage points?** This should compare scholarships, bonded incentives, subject-specific retention payments, workload reduction, career ladders, mentoring and workforce data systems, then rule out options that depend on institutional features NZ lacks [Australian Government Department of Education, National Teacher Workforce Action Plan](https://www.education.gov.au/national-teacher-workforce-action-plan); [UK Department for Education, 6,500 teachers plan, 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/every-child-achieving-and-thriving/6500-additional-teachers-delivery-plan-html-version); [Singapore MOE, career tracks](https://www.moe.gov.sg/careers/become-teachers/pri-sec-jc-ci/professional-development).

**What is fact (Medium):** the 2026 PPTA secondary staffing survey results, because the survey covers 155 principals and a 30% response base rather than every school, but it is current, recurring and directly referenced by the Ministry's projection as sector feedback [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc).

**What is interpretation:** that NZ's teacher-supply problem should be framed as a subject/language/place mismatch rather than a single national headcount crisis. This interpretation follows from official aggregate projections, subject-specialist evidence, Māori-medium growth, priority-staffing criteria and TALIS workload evidence, but it should be refined by the child research.

**Main limits:** no official current public dataset was found that gives real-time vacancies by subject, region, Māori-medium status and Equity Index group; Education Gazette is a live listing source and should be snapshotted or parsed with a dated method before being used as a statistic [Education Gazette vacancies](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/vacancies/). The Ministry projection excludes day relief teachers and is scenario-based rather than a guarantee, so it can understate relief-pool and school-level shortages [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc). The comparator scan is a framing lead rather than policy advice, and any Māori-medium workforce conclusions need review by people with Kaupapa Māori and te reo Māori education expertise before the stream passes G1.

**What would change this framing:** an official vacancy census showing large primary shortages across many regions would weaken the "not a uniform headcount crisis" correction; Teaching Council or Ministry data showing that 2025 ITE growth flows into STEM, te reo Māori and priority-staffing roles would reduce the pipeline-risk conclusion; evidence that Teacher Bonding Scheme, EBITE, Go Rural or SOTP have high completion and retention in shortage settings would shift the stream toward scaling and targeting rather than problem diagnosis; and a validated vacancy-by-Equity-Index analysis showing no disadvantage relationship would overturn the current "priority staffing / high Equity Index" lens.
