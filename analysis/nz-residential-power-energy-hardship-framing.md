# NZ residential power affordability needs five research tracks — energy hardship is real and measured, and the data-centre pressure on household bills is a genuine but structurally different risk than overseas comparators

This is the discover framing note for Stream #244, which is a `stage: discover` root issue about NZ residential power affordability — winter energy hardship today, and emerging pressure on household supply and cost, including data-centre demand [Issue #244](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/244). It establishes the sourced baseline for who experiences energy hardship, what existing schemes do, and what the downstream health/economic cost is, then fans out four of the issue's original six candidate questions into research children. It answers the sixth candidate question — large new loads (data centres) and residential power — directly, to the full method standard, as this issue's own narrowed core question, because it is a bounded, self-contained evidence check rather than a wide survey like the other five.

## Executive answer

- **Energy hardship is real, measured, and disproportionately borne by renters, Māori and Pacific households, and low-income households.** MBIE's most recent report (year ended June 2024, published 12 December 2025) finds 6.7% of households (132,000) could not afford to keep their home adequately warm in the previous 12 months; renters are 3–4 times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy hardship measures, and Māori/Pacific households are 2–3 times more likely than the general population [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures – Year ended June 2024*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).
- **The picture is improving, not worsening, on MBIE's five measures since 2019** — three of five measures show statistically significant improvement — even though the DEP-17 child-material-hardship measure (a related but different, broader Stats NZ measure) rose to 14.3% in the year to June 2025 [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024); [Stats NZ Child Poverty Statistics via the repo's `child-poverty-nz` skill, retrieved 3 Jul 2026]. These measure different things over different periods and should not be read as contradictory without care — flagged for the child issues.
- **Existing schemes each cover a different slice and none covers renters directly.** Warmer Kiwi Homes pays 50–90% of insulation/heat-pump costs but only to owner-occupiers of pre-2008 homes meeting income/deprivation criteria [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/); [Beehive, 11 August 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/over-50000-more-kiwis-be-eligible-insulation-grants). The Winter Energy Payment ($20.46/week single, $31.82/week couple, 1 May–1 October) reaches beneficiaries and superannuitants only, regardless of housing quality [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html). The Electricity Authority's Consumer Care Guidelines protecting medically dependent and vulnerable consumers from disconnection became **mandatory only from 1 April 2025**, after a 2023 review found just 47% of retailers fully complying [RNZ, 15 May 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/489951/fewer-than-half-of-electricity-retailers-complying-with-consumer-care-guidelines-report).
- **Cold/damp housing has a real, quantified cost, but two distinct NZ evidence bases should not be conflated.** Broad excess winter mortality averaged ~1,450 deaths/year in the 2010s (4.5% of all deaths), with the authors explicit that causation is not attributable to any single factor [Telfar-Barnard et al., 2023, *International Journal of Biometeorology*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00484-023-02573-6). A narrower, housing-specifically-attributed study estimates 229 deaths/year and NZ$938.9 million annual societal cost from dampness/mould, cold, and crowding specifically, with dampness/mould hospitalisations alone costing NZ$35.8 million/year [Riggs et al., 2021, *Bulletin of the World Health Organization*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8085632/).
- **On data centres: the international evidence is real but the mechanism doesn't transfer cleanly to NZ.** Where large data centres are already operational, documented, measurable effects on grid capacity and cost exist (Ireland's data centres are ~21–22% of national electricity demand and drove a multi-year Dublin connection moratorium; PJM's own market monitor attributes 40% of a recent US$16.4bn capacity-auction cost to data-centre load) — but the *channel* through which these costs bite (capacity-market auctions in the US, national demand-share/interconnector limits in Ireland) does not exist in the same form in New Zealand's small, hydro-heavy, isolated, nodal-spot-priced grid. This is evidence for taking the DataGrid Makarewa proposal's [(#212)](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/212) grid impact seriously, not evidence that NZ households will see Ireland- or Virginia-style bill effects by default.
- **The stream fans out into five research questions:** deeper prevalence/demographic depth [#326](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/326), drivers (housing vs pricing vs income vs split-incentive) [#327](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/327), whether the now-mandatory Consumer Care code actually protects vulnerable consumers [#328](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/328), who existing interventions reach and miss [#329](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/329), and reconciling the health/economic cost evidence [#330](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/330).

**Overall confidence:** Medium-High — the NZ energy-hardship baseline rests on MBIE's own primary report and is High confidence; the existing-schemes mapping is High confidence (official programme pages); the health-cost reconciliation is Medium confidence because it flags an unresolved tension between two legitimate but differently-scoped studies; the data-centre comparator analysis is Medium confidence, with one comparator (Amsterdam/TenneT) explicitly flagged as partially unverified.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact:** MBIE's five energy-hardship measures and their year-ended-June-2024 values, the renter/Māori/Pacific/low-income multipliers, the Warmer Kiwi Homes and Winter Energy Payment eligibility rules and rates, the Consumer Care Guidelines' mandatory-from-1-April-2025 status and the pre-mandate 47% compliance figure, the excess-winter-mortality and housing-attributable-hospitalisation figures, Ireland's data-centre demand share and Dublin connection moratorium, PJM's own market monitor's capacity-cost attribution, Virginia's SCC large-load rate class, Amsterdam's 2019 moratorium and the Netherlands' hyperscale (≥70MW/>10ha) restriction, and NZ's 85.5% renewable generation share and lack of international electricity interconnection — all cited inline below with primary or near-primary sources.

**What is interpretation:** that these facts together support the specific claim that NZ's grid structure (small, hydro-heavy, isolated, nodal-priced) means overseas data-centre-driven residential cost effects won't transfer directly — this is a reasoned inference from the cited structural differences, not a measured NZ outcome, because no NZ data centre at DataGrid's scale has yet gone live to observe.

**Main limits:** This framing does not (by design) do the deep, two-source-verified research each child issue owns — regional/demographic depth of hardship, apportioning hardship between housing/pricing/income/split-incentive causes, post-mandate Consumer Care enforcement evidence, intervention-reach mapping, and full reconciliation of the health-cost literature. On the data-centre question specifically: I could not independently verify a primary EirGrid/CRU-published wholesale or residential price impact figure (only the demand-share and connection-policy facts are confirmed); the TenneT Noord-Holland grid-capacity claim for Amsterdam is single-sourced via search-summarised content after a direct fetch was bot-blocked; and the widely circulated "~$70/month by 2028" PJM-area residential increase figure is an unaudited third-party projection, not a measured PJM/regulator outcome — all flagged explicitly below, not presented as settled fact.

## Evidence

### Scale: who experiences energy hardship today

MBIE defines energy wellbeing as "individuals, households and whānau [being] able to obtain and afford adequate energy services to support their wellbeing in their home or kāinga," with energy hardship as its opposite [MBIE, Defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship). MBIE monitors five measures; for the year ended June 2024 (published 12 December 2025, its second report in the series): 6.7% of households (132,000) reported they could not afford to keep their home adequately warm in the previous 12 months; 6.3% (126,000) put up with feeling cold a lot to save costs; 6.0% (118,000) paid a utility bill late more than once; 5.2% (102,000) reported a major problem with heating; and 3.7% (74,000) reported a major problem with damp/mould [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures – Year ended June 2024*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). Three of the five measures improved by a statistically significant margin between 2019 and 2024; only the late-utility-bill-payment measure rose (not statistically significant) [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

Renters were 3–4 times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy hardship in the year ended June 2024, though renters saw larger percentage-point improvements over 2019–2024 than owner-occupiers on most measures [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). Households with Māori and Pacific peoples were 2–3 times more likely than the general population to experience hardship measures, though both groups saw statistically significant housing-related improvements since 2019 — e.g. Pacific households reporting a major heating problem fell from 20.9% to 9.8% [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). Households reporting inadequate income were 3–5 times more likely to experience hardship than the average household, with almost a third unable to afford to keep their home adequately warm [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). Crowded households and one-parent families also showed elevated rates [MBIE, *Report on energy hardship measures*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

This national picture sits alongside a separate, broader Stats NZ measure: the DEP-17 child material-hardship index (which asks about 17 items including "put up with feeling cold to save money," not energy specifically) reached 14.3% of children (169,300) in the year to June 2025, up from 13.5% the year before [Stats NZ Child Poverty Statistics, measure MEASC, retrieved via the repo's `child-poverty-nz` Colab skill, 3 Jul 2026 — verified directly: `python3 .skills/skills/child-poverty-nz/scripts/cli.py national --measure MEASC --from 2023 --to 2025`]. This is a different, broader, rising measure over a more recent and shorter period than MBIE's five energy-specific measures, which improved 2019–2024. The two should not be read as contradicting each other — one tracks overall child material hardship most recently to mid-2025, the other tracks energy-specific hardship for all households to mid-2024 — but reconciling exactly how much of the DEP-17 rise is energy-driven is unresolved and a natural extension for [#326](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/326).

### What's already being done

**EECA Warmer Kiwi Homes** pays 50–90% of ceiling/underfloor insulation costs and up to 90% of an approved heat pump, for owner-occupied homes built before 2008, where the owner holds a Community Services Card or SuperGold Combo Card, or lives in an area EECA classifies as low/middle income; those in Deprivation Index areas 9–10 or with a Community Services Card get the 90% insulation rate [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/). An August 2023 expansion extended eligibility to Deprivation Index level 7 areas and made up to 58,000 more lower-income homeowners eligible; by end-2022 the programme had delivered over 122,000 insulation/heating installations [Beehive, 11 August 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/over-50000-more-kiwis-be-eligible-insulation-grants). **Structurally, this scheme reaches owner-occupiers, not renters** — the population MBIE finds is 3–4 times more likely to experience hardship. Whether and how renters benefit (via landlord uptake) is unresolved here and a natural question for [#329](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/329).

**The Winter Energy Payment** is a non-taxable seasonal payment of $20.46/week for singles and $31.82/week for couples or people with dependent children, paid automatically from 1 May to 1 October to recipients of NZ Super, Veteran's Pension, Jobseeker Support, Sole Parent Support, Supported Living Payment, and several related benefits [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html). It is a cash top-up unconditional on housing quality or actual energy spend, so it does not address the housing-stock driver of hardship.

**The Electricity Authority's Consumer Care Guidelines** protect medically dependent consumers (people who rely on electricity to live) from disconnection for non-payment, and set expectations for how retailers treat vulnerable consumers generally [RNZ, 15 May 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/489951/fewer-than-half-of-electricity-retailers-complying-with-consumer-care-guidelines-report). The guidelines were **voluntary** until becoming **mandatory on 1 April 2025** [Simpson Grierson, "Consumer Care Guidelines to become mandatory for electricity retailers"](https://www.simpsongrierson.com/insights-news/legal-updates/consumer-care-guidelines-to-become-mandatory-for-electricity-retailers). Before that, a May 2023 Electricity Authority compliance review found only 18 of 38 retailers (47%) fully complying, nine retailers did not respond to inquiries, and roughly a quarter of households with a medically dependent consumer lacked full disconnection protection [RNZ, 15 May 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/489951/fewer-than-half-of-electricity-retailers-complying-with-consumer-care-guidelines-report). Whether post-mandate compliance has actually improved is unverified here — that is [#328](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/328). Separately, the Electricity Authority's December 2025 disconnections dashboard reports postpay accounts averaging 810 disconnections/month versus prepay accounts averaging 27,000 disconnections/month affecting roughly 10,000 (about 35%) of prepay customers [Electricity Authority, 15 December 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/general-news/strengthening-visibility-of-disconnections-in-the-retail-electricity-market/) — prepay self-disconnection is structurally different from a retailer-initiated disconnection-for-non-payment and may sit partly outside the Consumer Care framework, a gap worth [#328](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/328) checking explicitly.

### Consequences: the health and economic cost of cold homes

Two NZ evidence bases quantify the cost of cold/damp housing, and they measure different things — conflating them would overstate or understate the case depending on which direction the error runs. **Excess winter mortality (EWM)** — the broad seasonal pattern of more deaths in the four coldest months than the rest of the year — averaged 1,450 deaths/year in the 2010s (4.5% of all deaths, down from 7.9% in the 1920s and 7.4% in the 1950s), across a 145-year, 1876–2020 dataset [Telfar-Barnard, Baker, Wilson & Howden-Chapman, 2023, "The rise and fall of excess winter mortality in New Zealand from 1876 to 2020," *International Journal of Biometeorology* 68:89–100](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00484-023-02573-6). The authors are explicit that **"attributing causation is more difficult"** and the long-run EWM decline "is not clearly associated with any single societal intervention" — this figure should not be read as "1,450 deaths caused by cold housing" [Telfar-Barnard et al., 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00484-023-02573-6).

A separate, narrower study specifically attributes disease burden to substandard housing conditions: using 2010–2017 hospitalisation data, dampness/mould accounted for 6,276 hospitalisations/year (36,649 hospital nights, NZ$35.8 million), cold housing for 625 hospitalisations/year (NZ$2.3 million), and crowding for 526 hospitalisations/year (NZ$1.4 million); pneumonia/lower-respiratory-tract infection was the largest single contributor within the dampness/mould category (2,486 hospitalisations/year, NZ$23.7 million) [Riggs, Keall, Howden-Chapman & Baker, 2021, "Environmental burden of disease from unsafe and substandard housing, New Zealand, 2010–2017," *Bulletin of the World Health Organization*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8085632/). The same study estimates 229 deaths/year attributable to these housing conditions combined, with total societal mortality cost of approximately NZ$938.9 million/year [Riggs et al., 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8085632/). This is a **causally-attributed, methodologically distinct, and far more conservative figure than the raw EWM count** — 229 versus 1,450 deaths/year — because it isolates housing-specific pathways rather than all-cause seasonal excess. Reconciling these, and checking for any post-2017 update, is [#330](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/330).

### Large new loads (data centres) and residential power — an evidence check

*(This is this issue's own narrowed core question — the fifth of the original six candidate questions became child issues; this one is answered directly here because it is a bounded, self-contained comparator study rather than an open-ended NZ survey.)*

**Ireland.** Data centres consumed 21.2–22% of Ireland's metered electricity in 2024, up from about 5% in 2015, and accounted for 88.2% of the growth in Irish electricity demand since 2015 — confirmed independently by Ireland's Central Statistics Office [CSO survey via Silicon Republic, 10 June 2025](https://www.siliconrepublic.com/enterprise/data-centres-cso-survey-2024-electricity) and by SEAI's own *Energy in Ireland 2025* report [SEAI, Energy in Ireland 2025](https://www.seai.ie/sites/default/files/publications/Energy%20in%20Ireland%202025%20v1.0%20complete.pdf). The Commission for Regulation of Utilities (CRU) operated a de facto moratorium on new data-centre grid connections in the Greater Dublin Area from 2021, driven by EirGrid warnings that Dublin-area load was approaching physical grid capacity; on 12 December 2025 the CRU published a new connection policy ending the blanket moratorium but requiring new data centres to source at least 80% of demand from new Irish renewables within six years, with Greater Dublin connections remaining case-by-case only [CRU, "The CRU Publishes its Decision on New Electricity Connection Policy for Data Centres"](https://www.cru.ie/about-us/news/the-cru-publishes-its-decision-on-new-electricity-connection-policy-for-data-centres/); corroborated by [DataCenterDynamics, "EirGrid says no new applications for data centers in Dublin until 2028"](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/eirgrid-says-no-new-applications-for-data-centers-in-dublin-till-2028/). **What this evidence supports:** a measured, regulator-confirmed grid-capacity constraint driven substantially by data-centre demand growth. **What it does not establish here:** a specific, independently verified figure for the effect on Irish wholesale or residential prices — that would need a follow-up primary-source check (e.g. EirGrid's capacity statements or CRU price-control decisions) before being treated as settled.

**Virginia / PJM Interconnection.** PJM's own Independent Market Monitor (Monitoring Analytics) found data-centre load accounted for US$6.5bn of the US$16.4bn (40%) in total cleared costs in PJM's December 2025 base capacity auction, and US$21.3bn of US$47.2bn (45%) across PJM's last three base auctions [Utility Dive, citing Monitoring Analytics directly](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/data-centers-pjm-capacity-auction/808951/). PJM's own clearing prices rose from US$28.92/MW-day (2024–25 delivery year) to US$329–333/MW-day (2026–27 and 2027–28), hitting the FERC-approved price cap [PJM news release, 17 December 2025](https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/about-pjm/newsroom/2025-releases/20251217-pjm-auction-procures-134479-mw-of-generation-resources.pdf); corroborated by [Utility Dive](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/pjm-interconnection-capacity-auction-data-center/808264/). Monitoring Analytics — PJM's regulator-mandated, not an advocacy, monitor — states data-centre load growth is "the primary reason for recent and expected capacity market conditions," a high-confidence causal attribution from the most authoritative available source, though still one organisation's interpretation rather than a controlled study. On the residential side, Virginia's State Corporation Commission (Case No. PUR-2025-00058) created a new large-load rate class (effective 1 January 2027) requiring data centres ≥25MW to pay at least 85% of transmission/distribution costs, explicitly framed as avoiding cost-shifting onto residential ratepayers [Virginia SCC, "SCC Data Center Initiatives," February 2026](https://www.scc.virginia.gov/media/sccvirginiagov-home/about-the-scc/fact-sheets/scc-data-center-initiatives-02-2026.pdf), corroborated by [Clean Virginia, 26 November 2025](https://www.cleanvirginia.org/2025/11/26/news-state-corporation-commission-approves-dominion-rate-hike-punts-on-data-center-costs/). Widely circulated longer-run projections — cumulative US$100–163bn in PJM-wide costs through 2033, or "~$70/month" average residential increases by 2028 — are estimates from secondary analysis (e.g. IEEFA, American Action Forum), not measured PJM/SCC outcomes, and should be treated as Low–Medium-confidence projections, not fact.

**Amsterdam / Netherlands.** The Municipality of Amsterdam and neighbouring Haarlemmermeer imposed an immediate moratorium on new data centre developments on 12 July 2019, lifted a year later; the municipality's own stated reason at the time was primarily about **space and architectural fit**, not grid capacity [DataCenterDynamics, "The ongoing impact of Amsterdam's data center moratorium," 22 August 2024](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/analysis/the-ongoing-impact-of-amsterdams-data-center-moratorium/) (fetched via a text-proxy after a direct request was bot-blocked). Separately, and more recently, the Dutch government has banned new hyperscale developments of ≥70MW or >10 hectares across most of the Netherlands, which it has explicitly attributed to a **lack of power grid capacity**, and Amsterdam/Haarlemmermeer imposed a further round of restrictions in December 2023 aimed at controlling data-centre energy use [DataCenterDynamics, 22 August 2024](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/analysis/the-ongoing-impact-of-amsterdams-data-center-moratorium/). Industry group DDA has pushed back that this is "symbol politics" blaming data centres for wider grid congestion that "will not disappear" regardless [DataCenterDynamics, 22 August 2024](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/analysis/the-ongoing-impact-of-amsterdams-data-center-moratorium/). **This is the clearest correlation-not-isolated-causation case of the three**: national transmission operator TenneT has reportedly said there is no additional grid capacity in large parts of Noord-Holland for the next decade, a constraint that — per secondary reporting — also blocks new housing and school connections, not data centres alone; I could not directly verify TenneT's own statement here after a bot-blocked fetch, so this specific claim is flagged as a **lead, not independently verified**, pending a follow-up direct source check.

**Why this doesn't transfer directly to New Zealand.** New Zealand's grid differs from all three comparators in ways that matter for the mechanism, not just the numbers. Generation mix: 85.5% of NZ electricity was renewable in 2024 (hydro ~58%, geothermal and other renewables making up the rest), down slightly from 88.1% in 2023 because low hydro inflows were partly offset by increased coal/gas generation [MBIE, *Energy in New Zealand 2025 — Electricity*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/energy-in-new-zealand/energy-in-new-zealand-2025/electricity) — a different marginal-cost profile than Ireland's gas-heavy or PJM's fossil-heavy grids, where a large new load more directly raises fossil-fuel-driven prices. Isolation: NZ has no international electricity interconnection, unlike Ireland's EU interconnectors or PJM's 13-state pooled market that can draw on neighbouring capacity to absorb a large new load [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/). Market design: NZ prices wholesale electricity through half-hourly, location-specific (nodal) spot pricing at 52 injection and 196 exit points [Electricity Authority, "Spot market"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/industry/wholesale/spot-market/) — a different mechanism from PJM's capacity-market auctions (the actual channel through which the largest documented data-centre price effects above occurred) and from Ireland's connection-policy rationing. The plausible NZ-specific channel, per this framing, is a *localised nodal-price and transmission-constraint effect near a large new connection, interacting with existing hydro dry-year risk* [Electricity Authority, dry-year risk explainer](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/) — a distinct hypothesis from either the Irish demand-share story or the PJM capacity-auction story, and one that has not yet been observed here because no NZ data centre at DataGrid's proposed scale (up to 280MW consented, a stated ambition of up to 1GW) has gone live [stream #212 baseline](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-factual-baseline.md). This is evidence for treating the question seriously in a future NZ-specific research issue once DataGrid's energy findings [#215](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/215) land, not evidence that a comparable NZ effect is already measured.

## What would change this conclusion

- A primary EirGrid or CRU-published wholesale/retail price series showing the measured (not projected) effect of data-centre load on Irish household bills would sharpen or overturn the "evidence check, not scare" framing for the Ireland comparator.
- A direct, verified TenneT statement on Noord-Holland grid capacity (rather than search-summarised secondary reporting) could confirm or weaken the Amsterdam comparator's grid-capacity causal claim.
- DataGrid Makarewa's confirmed real MW-by-phase and grid-connection details [#215](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/215) — once that research lands — would let a future issue test the "localised nodal-price effect" hypothesis against an actual NZ case rather than an analogy.
- MBIE's next energy hardship report (expected 2027, per MBIE's own page) or an interim release could confirm whether the 2019–2024 improvement trend continues or reverses, especially given the DEP-17 child-hardship measure's more recent rise.
- Post-1-April-2025 Electricity Authority compliance monitoring data on the now-mandatory Consumer Care Guidelines — not yet published at the time of this framing — would directly answer whether the pre-mandate 47% compliance problem has been fixed; this is exactly what [#328](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/328) should find.
- I could not verify current uptake of Warmer Kiwi Homes by tenure (i.e., how many landlords apply on behalf of tenants) — this materially affects whether the renter/owner-occupier hardship gap is being closed by the scheme at all, and is flagged for [#329](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/329).
- A human with NZ electricity-market and grid-planning expertise should review the data-centre section before it is relied on for any DataGrid-adjacent policy conversation — nodal pricing, nodal-vs-national effects, and nascent-connection dynamics are easy to oversimplify, and this framing is a survey-level evidence check, not a market-modelling exercise.
