# NZ council earthquake-retrofit debt is a discoverability problem before it is a single liability number

Discovery framing for stream root #432 (Part of #432). It tests the root's claims against current public evidence and proposes child research questions for the runner to open.

## Executive answer

- **The stream is valid, but the premise needs tightening.** New Zealand has 78 local authorities: 11 regional councils and 67 territorial authorities [Local Government Commission, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.lgc.govt.nz/about-us/about-local-government-in-new-zealand/). Territorial authorities, not all 78 councils, are the local bodies that determine and record earthquake-prone buildings in MBIE's EPB Register [MBIE EPB Register, last updated 18 May 2026](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register). Councils also own community facilities, parks and land assets, but state-school property and public hospitals sit mainly with central-government/Health NZ portfolios: the new school-property agency is to manage the education property portfolio as a Crown entity, and Health NZ says it owns 86 hospital sites [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm); [Ministry of Education, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/property-and-infrastructure/school-property-group/new-zealand-school-property-agency); [Health NZ, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/public-hospitals).
- **There is no obvious public all-council "seismic retrofit liability" line item.** Stats NZ's Local authority financial statistics dataset exposes balance-sheet categories such as land and buildings, current debt, term debt, and total liabilities, but it does not expose a seismic-strengthening or earthquake-retrofit obligation category [Stats NZ Local authority financial statistics year ended June 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). The Audit Office says the 2024-34 LTPs forecast total council debt peaking at $50.9 billion in 2032 including Auckland Council, and $28.8 billion in 2033 excluding Auckland Council, but that is whole-of-council borrowing, not retrofit debt [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
- **The national EPB obligation is being softened, not simply tightened.** Under the current system, seismic risk areas set identification and remediation timeframes, with high, medium and low seismic-risk areas and shorter deadlines for priority buildings [MBIE, "How the earthquake-prone building system works", accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works). But current remediation deadlines that had not lapsed as at 2 April 2024 are being extended by four years while a review proceeds, and the 2025/26 reform bill is explicitly framed as a more proportionate, risk-based system covering only high-risk building types in medium and high seismic zones [MBIE, "How the earthquake-prone building system works"](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [New Zealand Parliament, Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Bill, accessed 2026-07-04](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en).
- **The fiscal-pressure question is well supported.** The Audit Office says councils own and operate 26% of New Zealand's infrastructure assets, worth at least $76 billion, and are already dealing with deferred maintenance, infrastructure deficits, higher insurance costs in earthquake-risk regions, rates increases, and historically high planned borrowing [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm). The same report says councils' 2024-34 LTPs forecast $91.9 billion of capital expenditure across 58 councils, including $39.5 billion renewals, while climate-related severe-weather risk makes low stormwater-renewal ratios a concern [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
- **Overall confidence: Medium.** The statutory, population, council-count, EPB-system and council-finance baseline is supported by official sources, but the specific aggregate retrofit-liability question cannot be answered from the framing pass because public financial datasets do not isolate seismic obligations [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).

## Problem statement

New Zealand's councils serve a resident population of about 5.36 million people, and local government holds a material share of the public infrastructure base [Stats NZ population indicator, period 31 March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/); [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm). The earthquake-prone building regime places regulatory work on territorial authorities and remediation duties on building owners, while councils are themselves owners of public buildings such as libraries, halls, pools, grandstands, civic buildings, bridges and wharves [MBIE EPB Register, 2026](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register); [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings). The practical problem is that council balance sheets and LTPs make total borrowing and asset ownership visible, but they do not make seismic retrofit exposure visible in a comparable all-council form [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).

The stream should therefore ask: what council-owned or council-controlled assets still face seismic remediation, what cost has been budgeted or deferred, how that competes with climate adaptation and ordinary renewals, and how much post-event fiscal headroom would remain if a significant earthquake hit before the work was complete.

## Who it affects

- **Ratepayers and residents:** local authorities cover all of New Zealand, and Stats NZ's provisional national resident population estimate was 5,361,300 at 31 March 2026 [Stats NZ, accessed via the repo's Stats NZ skill on 2026-07-04](https://www.stats.govt.nz/indicators/population-of-nz/). Council debt, rates and service levels affect residents because councils fund infrastructure through rates, borrowing, subsidies, grants, fees and other revenue [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
- **Users of public buildings and public spaces:** councils own community facilities, parks and land assets, and Hutt City Council's public register illustrates the asset mix by listing halls, libraries, pavilions, pools, museums, grandstands, a wharf and other council-owned buildings with %NBS ratings [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm); [Hutt City Council, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings).
- **Building owners and tenants outside council ownership:** the EPB system covers non-residential buildings and some larger residential buildings, and owners must strengthen, remove or otherwise remediate earthquake-prone buildings within statutory timeframes unless deadlines are changed by the current reform [MBIE, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works).
- **Emergency-management and post-disaster finance decision-makers:** NHC cover provides a first layer for insured residential homes and limited residential land after earthquake and other natural hazards, but council infrastructure and public buildings are financed through council insurance, borrowing, reserves, Crown assistance and recovery decisions rather than household NHCover [NHC Toka Tu Ake, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).

## What's already being done

The national EPB system requires territorial authorities to identify potentially earthquake-prone buildings, determine whether buildings are earthquake prone, and record determined buildings in MBIE's EPB Register [MBIE EPB Register, 2026](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register). The system classifies areas into high, medium and low seismic risk, gives shorter deadlines for priority buildings, and applies to non-residential buildings plus some larger residential buildings [MBIE, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works).

The Government has already paused part of the cost pressure by extending non-lapsed remediation deadlines as at 2 April 2024 by four years during the review [MBIE, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works). MBIE's proposed reform would remove about 55% of EPBs, around 2,900 buildings, from the EPB system; give around 1,440 more affordable remediation requirements; leave 840 with no mandatory remedial-work requirement; and require full retrofit for only around 80 buildings [MBIE proposed EPB changes, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system). The bill before Parliament says it would regulate only high-risk building types in medium and high seismic zones [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-04](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en).

Some councils are publishing their own asset-level seismic information: Hutt City Council's register lists council-owned buildings, seismic ratings and assessment links, including assets rated below 34% NBS or otherwise marked earthquake prone [Hutt City Council, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings). Wellington City Council has current official project pages for the Wellington Town Hall redevelopment, the Te Ngakau civic precinct programme and the Central Library strengthening/modernisation project, which shows seismic work is managed as major project work rather than a single national liability line [Wellington City Council Town Hall page, fetched via `scripts/fetch.mjs` plain HTTP on 2026-07-04](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/earthquake-strengthening-projects/town-hall-strengthening/about-the-project); [Wellington City Council Te Ngakau page, fetched via `scripts/fetch.mjs` plain HTTP on 2026-07-04](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/te-ngakau-civic-precinct-programme); [Wellington City Council Central Library page, fetched via `scripts/fetch.mjs` plain HTTP on 2026-07-04](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/te-ngakau-civic-precinct-programme/central-library).

The adjacent climate-adaptation baseline is already visible at LTP level: the Audit Office says councils are considering climate-change impacts in infrastructure strategies, that severe-weather risk makes stormwater renewal underinvestment a concern, and that some councils made unreasonable assumptions about government funding for projects such as flood and shoreline protection [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm). Prior For Good work has already framed council consenting and climate-adaptation incentive problems in stream #370, so this stream should not duplicate property-level flood adaptation research except where it compares budget pressure with seismic work [analysis/council-consenting-climate-adaptation-framing.md](council-consenting-climate-adaptation-framing.md).

## Proposed child research questions

1. **What council-owned or council-controlled assets still have seismic remediation exposure, and what is the best public aggregate estimate of remaining cost?** This should build a council-by-council register from the MBIE EPB Register, LTPs, annual reports, asset registers, major-project pages and council-controlled organisation disclosures, distinguishing council-owned buildings from private EPBs that councils regulate [MBIE EPB Register, 2026](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register); [Hutt City Council, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings).
2. **How visible are seismic-retrofit obligations on council balance sheets, LTPs and financial strategies?** This should test whether obligations appear as term debt, capital-project budgets, provisions, contingent liabilities, depreciation, insurance notes or unquantified asset risks, using Stats NZ Local authority financial statistics as the common balance-sheet spine [Stats NZ, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).
3. **How does seismic remediation compete with climate adaptation, water, stormwater and ordinary renewal programmes in 2024-34 LTPs?** This should code a sample of high-seismic and high-climate-risk councils' LTP capex into comparable categories, because the Audit Office shows budget pressure but not a seismic-vs-climate split [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
4. **What legal obligations will remain if the Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Bill passes, and which council assets move in or out of scope?** This should compare current EPB rules with the reported bill and MBIE review material, then test asset-level implications for selected councils in high and medium seismic zones [MBIE proposed changes, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system); [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-04](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en).
5. **Which councils are materially ahead or behind on seismic-risk transparency and remediation delivery?** This should compare at least Wellington City, Hutt City, Christchurch City, Dunedin City and one smaller high/medium-risk territorial authority on asset registers, %NBS disclosure, project status, budget certainty, and service-critical assets [Hutt City Council, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings); [Wellington City Council Town Hall page, fetched 2026-07-04](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/earthquake-strengthening-projects/town-hall-strengthening/about-the-project).
6. **What happens fiscally and operationally if a significant earthquake hits before council retrofits are complete?** This should model a scenario for 2-3 councils using remaining seismic-risk assets, insurance arrangements, LGFA/debt headroom, emergency-response roles, NHC/private-insurance boundaries and likely service outages [Audit Office, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm); [NHC Toka Tu Ake, accessed 2026-07-04](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/).

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** the 78-local-authority structure [Local Government Commission](https://www.lgc.govt.nz/about-us/about-local-government-in-new-zealand/); the EPB Register role for territorial authorities [MBIE EPB Register](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register); the current EPB timeframes and deadline extension [MBIE](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); the existence of the 2025/26 bill and its risk-based purpose [New Zealand Parliament](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en); the Audit Office's aggregate council debt/capex observations [Audit Office](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm); and the non-council ownership signal for state-school and public-hospital portfolios [Ministry of Education](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/property-and-infrastructure/school-property-group/new-zealand-school-property-agency); [Health NZ](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/public-hospitals).

**What is interpretation:** that the stream's highest-value first move is a discoverability/accounting register rather than a policy conclusion. That follows from the mismatch between asset-level council pages, EPB data, and aggregate financial datasets, but the register has not yet been built [Stats NZ](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [Hutt City Council](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings).

**Main limits:** I did not scrape all 67 territorial-authority LTPs, annual reports or EPB records in this framing; I did not quantify private building-owner liabilities; I did not test council insurance policies or LGFA covenants beyond the Audit Office's public summary; and I did not provide legal advice on the pending bill. The Wellington pages were fetched by the repo's `scripts/fetch.mjs` via plain HTTP after the quick web extraction gave only metadata/noisy HTML, and the Hutt City and MBIE pages fetched via quick HTTP/WebFetch. A human local-government finance specialist, structural engineer, or resource-management/building-law practitioner could materially improve the later interpretation.

## What would change this conclusion

- A current official MBIE, DIA, Stats NZ, LGFA or Audit Office dataset that already tags seismic-remediation costs by council would change the first child issue from "build the register" to "validate the register" [Stats NZ](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).
- Passage of the Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Bill in a form materially different from the reported bill would change which assets remain in mandatory remediation scope [New Zealand Parliament](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en).
- Evidence that council annual reports consistently provision seismic obligations in a comparable way would raise confidence in an aggregate liability estimate [Audit Office](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
- Council insurance schedules or recovery plans showing robust post-event cover and borrowing headroom would narrow the "next event" fiscal-risk concern [NHC Toka Tu Ake](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [Audit Office](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm).
