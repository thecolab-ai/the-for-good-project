# NZ's peptide market is not unregulated but mid-reform — the real gaps are the peptides Medsafe still can't seize and the near-total absence of NZ harm data

Discovery framing for stream root #709 (Part of #709). The root — prompted by a *60 Minutes Australia* documentary — asks "what is New Zealand actually doing about peptides, where are the gaps, and what can we learn from countries that have moved (especially Australia)?" It offers six candidate directions and one strong implicit premise: that peptides are a "booming, largely-unregulated grey market". This document tests that premise against the current NZ regulatory record and proposes the child research issues. It does **not** give usage, dosing, or how-to-obtain guidance.

## Executive answer

- **The headline premise — "largely unregulated" — is partially supported but needs correcting. NZ is mid-reform, not absent.** Several of the best-known peptides are already prescription medicines: BPC-157 was classified as a prescription medicine at the 70th Medicines Classification Committee (MCC) meeting on 25 May 2023 and gazetted from November 2023 [Medsafe MCC minutes, 70th meeting, 2023](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/mccMin25May2023.htm); [Medsafe, recent classification notices](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp); SARMs are a prescription-medicine group entry, and Medsafe's own June 2025 committee paper states "Thymosin beta-4 and TB-500 are classified as prescription medicines in both New Zealand and Australia" [Medsafe, Classification of Unscheduled Peptides submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). So the market is *partly* scheduled, and the reform is active.
- **What survives of the premise is real and specific: a defined class of peptides remains legally unscheduled, and for those Medsafe cannot seize at the border.** Medsafe's June 2025 submission proposed classifying whole groups of currently-unscheduled peptides (thymic peptides, BPC-157 analogues, ACTH analogues like Semax, pineal peptides like epitalon, antimicrobial peptides, myostatin and mitochondrial peptides, kisspeptins, Selank and others) as prescription medicines — precisely because, for an unscheduled peptide, "Medsafe does not have the grounds, pursuant to the Medicines Act 1981, to seize them" and instead releases them to the individual with a high-risk-medicine letter [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf); [RNZ, 27 Jul 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/568172/medsafe-considers-crackdown-on-import-of-unregulated-peptide-medications).
- **The reform is unfinished — and its exact status is the single most important thing this stream must pin down.** At the 74th MCC meeting (23 July 2025) the Committee agreed classification "would help to reduce this risk of harm" but **deferred the decision "pending additional information"** about impacts on external parties (e.g. the cosmetics sector) [Medsafe MCC minutes, 74th meeting, 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm). As of the most recent classification notices I could verify, the group reclassification has **not** yet been gazetted — only BPC-157 appears in the peptide classification record [Medsafe, recent classification notices](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp). So the enforcement gap the reform is meant to close was, on the latest verifiable record, still open.
- **Supply is demonstrably running ahead of controls — this limb of the premise is supported.** Border interceptions of parcels containing peptides or SARMs rose roughly 15-fold, from 15 in 2022 to 370 in 2025, with ~200 already seized in the first months of 2026 [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). Medsafe has since issued a consumer health warning (21 May 2026) naming BPC-157, CJC-1295, GHRP-6, ipamorelin, kisspeptin, melanotan II, retatrutide, Selank, Semax, TB-500 and thymosin, and singling out black-market retatrutide as linked to "fatal overdose, contamination, severe neurological symptoms" [Medsafe consumer advisory, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp).
- **The Australia-has-moved comparator is well-founded.** The TGA created a Schedule 4 (prescription-only) entry for BPC-157 — a final decision published 22 May 2024, in force 1 June 2024, via the ACMS #43 scheduling process — removed GLP-1 receptor agonists from the pharmacy-compounding exemption from 1 October 2024 after reports that >20,000 Australians were injecting compounded "replica" weight-loss products, and now treats unapproved peptides as an active safety and law-enforcement priority [TGA, ACMS #43 final scheduling decision, 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/publication/scheduling-decisions-final/notice-final-decision-amend-or-not-amend-current-poisons-standard-acms-43-accs-37-joint-acms-accs-35); [TGA, GLP-1 RA compounding changes, 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/update-glucagon-peptide-1-receptor-agonists-glp-1-ras-pharmacy-compounding-changes); [Australian Dept of Health / Minister Butler, 2024](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-mark-butler-mp/media/protecting-australians-from-unsafe-compounding-of-replica-weight-loss-products). (Note: the "March 2025" BPC-157 date that circulates on peptide-retail blogs does not match the TGA's own record and is not used here.) NZ's design choices — schedule-by-group vs case-by-case, and what to do about compounding and clinics — are live and comparable.
- **The sharpest genuine gap after "unscheduled peptides" is evidentiary: there is almost no NZ-specific harm data.** Sports-doping risk is clear (BPC-157 is WADA S0; growth-hormone secretagogues and TB-500 sit in S2; two named NZ athletes were banned in 2024–25 after Customs intercepted peptide parcels) [WADA Prohibited List](https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list); [Sport Integrity NZ, rugby case, 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/rugby-player-gets-anti-doping-ban-for-importing-banned-substances); [Sport Integrity NZ, trafficking case, 2025](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/athlete-sanctioned-for-trafficking-banned-substances). But NZ National Poisons Centre call data, CARM adverse-reaction tallies, hospitalisation reports, and any prevalence survey are **not published** for peptides — the hard harm evidence is Australian (TGA) and international lab studies. That absence is itself a finding and shapes the child issues.

**Overall confidence:** Medium–High on the NZ regulatory chain and the overseas comparators (primary Medsafe, MCC, WADA, TGA and Sport Integrity sources); Medium on scale (Customs figures reach us via RNZ quoting Medsafe/Customs rather than a published dataset); and deliberately Low where NZ harm data simply does not exist. Where 2025–2026 dates appear, they are the dates the live official pages display and are flagged below.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High confidence, primary sources):** BPC-157's classification as a prescription medicine at the 70th MCC (25 May 2023) and its presence in the classification record [Medsafe, 2023](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/mccMin25May2023.htm); the June 2025 Medsafe submission proposing group classification of unscheduled peptides and its explicit statement that Medsafe cannot currently seize unscheduled peptides at the border [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf); the 74th MCC's deferral "pending additional information" [Medsafe, 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm); the personal-importation rule (max three months' supply of a prescription medicine, with an authorised-prescriber prescription/letter) [Medsafe, importing medicines](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consumers/miet/importmedicines.asp); the Medsafe consumer advisory content [Medsafe, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp); the two named NZ anti-doping cases [Sport Integrity NZ, 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/rugby-player-gets-anti-doping-ban-for-importing-banned-substances); [Sport Integrity NZ, 2025](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/athlete-sanctioned-for-trafficking-banned-substances); the GLP-1 approval/funding split (Medsafe-approved, not Pharmac-funded) [Pharmac, semaglutide](https://www.pharmac.govt.nz/news-and-resources/news/semaglutide-wegovy-ozempic); the TGA and Australian-government actions cited above; and the peer-reviewed contamination findings (online semaglutide: endotoxin in all samples, measured purity 7.7–14.37% against 99% claimed; SARMs: only 52% of products contained the labelled compound) [Ashraf et al., JMIR 2024](https://www.jmir.org/2024/1/e65440); [Van Wagoner et al., JAMA 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29183075/).

**What is interpretation:** that these facts add up to "mid-reform with two sharp gaps" rather than "unregulated" is this document's argument, and it partially *corrects* the root's premise — the useful question is not "why is nothing being done" but "will the proposed group classification be enacted, will it actually bite (border seizure powers, clinic/compounding oversight), and can NZ measure the harm it is trying to prevent". The ranking of the gaps (unscheduled-peptide enforcement first, missing NZ data second) is a judgement, not a sourced fact.

**Main limits / what would change the conclusions:** (1) the **current enacted status** of the June 2025 group-classification proposal is the key uncertainty — I found deferral (July 2025) and no subsequent Gazette notice for the group, but a later meeting or gazettal after my search window would change the "gap still open" conclusion; a definitive answer needs the MCC's post-74th record and the NZ Gazette classification notices, and legislation.govt.nz bot-blocked direct fetching so some statutory wording (Medicines Act 1981 ss 29, 43) is verified via Medsafe explainers rather than the primary Act. (2) **Scale figures** (15→370 parcels) are Medsafe/Customs numbers reported through RNZ, not a published Customs dataset broken out for peptides — a Customs/Medsafe OIA would firm them up; note the apparent tension between "56 parcels in the year to May 2025" and "370 in 2025" reflects different reporting windows and should be reconciled, not treated as contradictory. (3) **No NZ harm data** (Poisons Centre, CARM, hospitalisations, prevalence) is published — a genuine evidence gap, so harm claims lean on Australian and international sources. (4) A vendor's or clinic's claim about legality or safety is a claim, not confirmation; blogs and peptide-shops were excluded as sources. (5) Clinical, legal and regulatory practitioners should validate any policy-facing conclusion before this stream crosses G1 — this is evidence-gathering, not medical or legal advice. No personal data is used; the two athletes named are from published official anti-doping decisions.

## Problem statement

"Peptides" now names a fast-growing consumer market spanning GLP-1 weight-loss analogues, muscle/recovery and "anti-ageing" compounds (BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, ipamorelin, epitalon, Semax, Selank and many more), and injectables sold online and through wellness/anti-ageing clinics and gyms, frequently labelled "for research purposes only" [Medsafe consumer advisory, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp); [Healthify, unapproved peptide products](https://healthify.nz/medicines-a-z/u/unapproved-peptide-products). In New Zealand the regulatory response is genuinely underway — BPC-157 and SARMs are already prescription medicines, GLP-1 drugs are Medsafe-approved (though not Pharmac-funded), and Medsafe has proposed sweeping the remaining unscheduled peptides into prescription-only status — but that final step was deferred in July 2025 and, on the latest verifiable record, not yet enacted, leaving a defined class of peptides that Medsafe cannot seize at the border even as interceptions rise 15-fold [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf); [Medsafe MCC minutes, 74th meeting, 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm); [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). Meanwhile the harm the reform is meant to prevent is essentially unmeasured in NZ. This stream should establish the precise legal map (what is scheduled, what is not, and what happened to the group proposal), how peptides enter and who supplies them, what harm can actually be evidenced here, and which overseas levers NZ could realistically adopt.

## Evidence

### 1. The NZ legal map: partly scheduled, actively reforming, one class still open

Under the Medicines Act 1981, a peptide with no NZ consent to distribute is an "unapproved medicine"; if it is classified as a prescription medicine, it cannot lawfully be imported, supplied, possessed or procured without authorisation, and a prescriber may supply an unapproved medicine to a named patient under s29 with notification to Medsafe [Medsafe, unapproved medicines](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/riss/unapp.asp); [Medsafe consumer advisory, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp). Three tiers are visible:

- **Already prescription medicines:** BPC-157 (70th MCC, 25 May 2023 — "the Committee agreed to Medsafe's proposal to classify BPC-157 as a prescription medicine") [Medsafe, 2023](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/mccMin25May2023.htm); SARMs (prescription-medicine group entry); and, per Medsafe's own submission, thymosin beta-4 / TB-500 and the growth-hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, ipamorelin, tesamorelin) [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf).
- **Approved and regulated (the licit end):** GLP-1 weight-loss drugs — Medsafe has approved Wegovy (semaglutide) and Mounjaro (tirzepatide) for weight loss, but "neither of these products are publicly funded", so patients pay privately, a pricing gap that plausibly drives grey-market substitution (Medsafe warns specifically about black-market retatrutide, an unapproved Eli Lilly candidate) [Pharmac, semaglutide](https://www.pharmac.govt.nz/news-and-resources/news/semaglutide-wegovy-ozempic); [Medsafe consumer advisory, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp).
- **Still unscheduled (the gap):** epitalon/epithalamin, Semax/Adamax, Selank, kisspeptins, antimicrobial peptides (LL-37), myostatin modulators (GDF-8, ACE-031), mitochondrial peptides (humanin, MOTS-c, SS-31) and others named in the June 2025 submission [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). The 74th MCC agreed group classification "would help to reduce this risk of harm" but **deferred** it pending impact information [Medsafe MCC minutes, 74th meeting, 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm); no subsequent Gazette notice for the group was found in the classification record [Medsafe, recent classification notices](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp). None of these four best-known peptides appear in the Misuse of Drugs Act 1975 schedules — they are regulated (or not) as medicines, not controlled drugs.

### 2. How they enter NZ, and the border-enforcement gap

Individuals may import at most a three-months' supply of a prescription medicine and only with an authorised prescriber's prescription or letter that "must match exactly the name, strength, quantity, and form"; importing a prescription medicine without that reasonable excuse is unlawful [Medsafe, importing medicines](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consumers/miet/importmedicines.asp); [Medsafe, importing FAQs](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/compliance/faqs.asp). The "research chemical / not for human consumption" label is a marketing device, not a legal shield: Medsafe states such wording "has no legal effect" and does not make supply or use lawful [Medsafe consumer advisory, 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp); [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). The binding constraint is classification: for a peptide that is *not* a scheduled prescription medicine, Medsafe "does not have the grounds… to seize" it and releases it to the importer with a high-risk-medicine letter — the exact mechanism the group proposal is meant to close [Medsafe submission, 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf); [RNZ, 27 Jul 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/568172/medsafe-considers-crackdown-on-import-of-unregulated-peptide-medications). Interceptions have surged (15 in 2022 → 370 in 2025 → ~200 in early 2026), and NZ Drug Foundation drug-checking reports peptides "brought online weren't what they were marketed as" [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides).

### 3. Who supplies and uses them; clinics, compounding, and the GLP-1 boom

Demand is driven by international social-media and influencer promotion, including NZ influencers using "for research and information purposes only" disclaimers [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). The supply/oversight side — anti-ageing and wellness clinics, compounding pharmacies, and gyms — is the least-documented part of the picture in official NZ sources and is a priority for the stream (Australia's experience shows compounding is where the weight-loss market bent the rules, prompting the 1 October 2024 removal of GLP-1s from the compounding exemption after >20,000 patients were found injecting compounded replicas) [Australian Dept of Health, 2024](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-mark-butler-mp/media/protecting-australians-from-unsafe-compounding-of-replica-weight-loss-products). What professional oversight (Medical Council, Pharmacy Council, Health & Disability Commissioner, compounding rules) actually applies to NZ clinics prescribing or administering peptides is an open question.

### 4. Sports integrity

BPC-157 has been on the WADA Prohibited List under S0 (non-approved substances) since 1 January 2022; growth-hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, ipamorelin, tesamorelin, MK-677) and growth factors such as TB-500/thymosin beta-4 fall under S2, prohibited at all times [WADA Prohibited List](https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list). NZ enforcement is live: a rugby player was banned two years after Customs intercepted a parcel containing chorionic gonadotropin, BPC-157 and CJC-1295/ipamorelin [Sport Integrity NZ, 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/rugby-player-gets-anti-doping-ban-for-importing-banned-substances), and a surf-lifesaving/athletics competitor was banned three years for trafficking after a September 2024 parcel of tesamorelin, ipamorelin, TB-500 and BPC-157 was intercepted [Sport Integrity NZ, 2025](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/athlete-sanctioned-for-trafficking-banned-substances). Both cases were determined through the Sports Tribunal of New Zealand; a full sweep of its decisions would map prevalence among sanctioned athletes.

### 5. Harm evidence — strong internationally, a genuine gap in NZ

The quality problem is documented in peer-reviewed work: a 2024 analysis of online, no-prescription semaglutide found endotoxin in every sample and measured purity of 7.7–14.37% against a 99% label claim, judging all vials "probable substandard and falsified" [Ashraf et al., JMIR 2024](https://www.jmir.org/2024/1/e65440); a JAMA analysis of internet-sold SARMs found only 52% contained the labelled compound [Van Wagoner et al., JAMA 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29183075/). Australia's TGA documents real adverse events — severe allergic reactions requiring adrenaline and hospitalisation, systemic inflammatory response syndrome, and musculoskeletal injuries — and hospitalisations from compounded GLP-1 replicas [TGA, importing unapproved peptides warning](https://www.tga.gov.au/safety/safety-monitoring-and-information/safety-alerts/tga-warning-risks-importing-unapproved-peptide-products); [Australian Dept of Health, 2024](https://www.health.gov.au/ministers/the-hon-mark-butler-mp/media/protecting-australians-from-unsafe-compounding-of-replica-weight-loss-products). By contrast, no NZ National Poisons Centre call data, CARM adverse-reaction tally, confirmed NZ hospitalisation, or prevalence/needle-exchange figure specific to peptides is published — the NPC releases statistics only on request [NZ National Poisons Centre, statistics](https://poisons.co.nz/poisons-centre-info/statistics/). NZ officials describe the risk in general terms (Ministry of Health clinical chief advisor Dr Anna Skinner: peptides "had not been through human trials" and "could be contaminated with mould, heavy metals and bacteria") [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). Closing this data gap is itself a useful deliverable.

### 6. Overseas lessons

Australia is the natural comparator and has moved decisively: the TGA created a Schedule 4 (prescription-only) entry for BPC-157 through the ACMS #43 process (final decision 22 May 2024, in force 1 June 2024), citing that BPC-157 "poses a high risk of misuse within athletic, fitness, wellness and anti-ageing consumer markets" [TGA, ACMS #43 final scheduling decision, 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/publication/scheduling-decisions-final/notice-final-decision-amend-or-not-amend-current-poisons-standard-acms-43-accs-37-joint-acms-accs-35); it removed GLP-1 receptor agonists from the pharmacist-compounding exemption from 1 October 2024 ("compounded GLP-1 RA products no longer being able to be compounded by pharmacists and supplied to patients") [TGA, GLP-1 RA compounding changes, 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/update-glucagon-peptide-1-receptor-agonists-glp-1-ras-pharmacy-compounding-changes); and it warns on personal-importation of unapproved peptides while "actively monitoring this issue from both safety and law enforcement perspectives" [TGA, unapproved-peptide safety alert, 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/safety/safety-monitoring-and-information/safety-alerts/understanding-your-responsibilities-when-importing-compounding-and-supplying-unapproved-peptide-products). The US FDA route runs the other way in 2026: several peptides (BPC-157, CJC-1295, TB-500, ipamorelin, kisspeptin and others) were flagged in "category 2" bulk-substance compounding as potentially presenting "significant safety risks", but those nominations were later withdrawn and a July 2026 Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee is reconsidering access — so the US position is genuinely contested [FDA, bulk drug substances that may present significant safety risks](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/certain-bulk-drug-substances-use-compounding-may-present-significant-safety-risks); [FDA, Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting, Jul 2026](https://www.fda.gov/advisory-committees/advisory-committee-calendar/july-23-24-2026-meeting-pharmacy-compounding-advisory-committee-07232026). The UK MHRA has focused on the GLP-1/counterfeit end, seizing hundreds of fake Ozempic pens and thousands of illegal weight-loss products and warning that fakes "may contain insulin rather than semaglutide" [MHRA, unsafe fake weight-loss pens](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/mhra-warns-of-unsafe-fake-weight-loss-pens). The contrast — Australia tightening, the US contested, the UK focused on counterfeits — frames the transplant question for NZ: schedule-by-group vs case-by-case, how to treat compounding and clinics, and how to pair scheduling with border powers.

## Proposed child research issues

Each is `Part of #709`; none crosses the G1 gate into ideation. Most important first (the runner opens at most six).

1. **The NZ peptide classification map and the fate of the June 2025 group proposal.** Build the authoritative, current table of which peptides are prescription medicines vs unscheduled under the Medicines Act 1981, and establish exactly what happened to Medsafe's June 2025 "Classification of Unscheduled Peptides" submission after the 74th MCC deferral (23 July 2025) — was it enacted, gazetted, or still pending, and what is the enforcement effect either way? Start at the MCC minutes/agendas, the NZ Gazette classification notices, and legislation.govt.nz.
2. **How peptides enter NZ and the border-enforcement gap.** Quantify import flows and the seizure trend from primary Customs/Medsafe records (an OIA will likely be needed to reconcile the 56-vs-370 figures and get a clean series), document the "high-risk-medicine letter" release practice for unscheduled peptides, the "research chemical" labelling loophole, and what changes operationally when a peptide is scheduled. Start at the Medsafe submission, NZ Customs drug-interception statistics, and Medsafe compliance pages.

Start at TGA scheduling decisions, FDA PCAC materials, and MHRA guidance.

Start at the WADA list, Sport Integrity Commission, and the Sports Tribunal decisions index.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A Gazette notice enacting the group peptide classification** (or a later MCC decision) would close the "unscheduled peptides Medsafe can't seize" gap and shift the story from "reform stalled at deferral" to "reform delivered — does it bite?". This is the first thing question 1 must settle.
- **Published NZ harm data** (Poisons Centre, CARM, hospitalisations) would replace the reliance on Australian/international evidence and could raise or lower the assessed severity for NZ specifically.
- **Evidence of active clinic/compounding oversight** (or its absence) would determine whether the supply-side risk sits with border imports or with domestically-administered products — changing where any intervention should land.
- **A clean Customs dataset** reconciling the 56-vs-370 parcel figures would firm up (or revise) the "15-fold rise" scale claim, currently sourced via RNZ quoting Medsafe/Customs rather than a published series.
