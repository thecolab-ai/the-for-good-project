# NZ hazard planning is risk-based on paper but structurally rewards avoidance over property-level adaptation

Discovery framing for stream root #370 (Part of #370). Tests the founder's four hypotheses — H1 framing, H2 granularity, H3 mitigation ignored, H4 disincentive — against the current statutory and administrative record, and proposes the child research issues.

## Executive answer

- **H1 (framing) — partially supported, with an important correction.** The operative instruments are formally *risk-based, not avoidance-based*: the National Policy Statement for Natural Hazards, gazetted 18 December 2025 and in force 15 January 2026, mandates avoidance only at "very high" risk and expressly requires decision-makers to consider "existing and proposed mitigation measures" [NZ Gazette, 2025](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045); [MfE NPS-NH Implementation Guide, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/RMA/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-implementation-guide.pdf). But the "enable" pathway is one-directional and penalty-priced: consent remains discretionary, and the Building Act's build-anyway route (s72) tags the title with an entry the Court of Appeal called "a blot on the title" with "significant insurance implications", under which the Natural Hazards Commission may lawfully decline cover [MBIE natural hazard provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); [NHC, section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/).
- **H2 (granularity) — supported, with nuance.** Council hazard layers are genuinely extent-based polygons that can clip part of a parcel, but the disclosure and consent trigger operates at whole-property level once any part intersects: Hamilton says flood information enters the property record "when any portion of a property falls within a mapped flood hazard area" [Hamilton City Council flood mapping](https://hamilton.govt.nz/property-rates-and-building/district-plan/flood-mapping/), and Waikato Regional Council concedes LIM-disclosed regional data "may not even be applicable to a particular parcel of land" [WRC position statement, 2025](https://www.waikatoregion.govt.nz/assets/WRC/RegionalHazardsPortalPositionStatementProvisionNaturalHazardInformationLIMs.pdf).
- **H3 (mitigation ignored) — strongly supported for business-as-usual.** Auckland Council states its flood maps "are produced for a whole catchment… not site specific", "don't include flood mitigations to a building", and that LIM flood content "does not include any mitigations that may have occurred at the property" [Auckland Council OurAuckland, Mar 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2026/03/understanding-auckland-s-regional-flood-maps/); the 2025 LIM regulations expressly require no further analysis beyond information councils already hold [Local Government (Natural Hazard Information in LIMs) Regulations 2025](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2025/0068/16.0/whole.html); [Simpson Grierson, 2025](https://www.simpsongrierson.com/insights-news/legal-updates/streamlining-natural-hazard-information-in-lims-what-new-requirements-mean-in-practice-for-both-councils-and-customers). The counter-precedent is post-Gabrielle recategorisation: designations demonstrably moved when mitigation was built (45 Waiohiki properties 2C→1; 11 Ōmāhu properties lifted) — responsive designation is administratively possible, just not institutionalised outside the recovery framework [Inside Government NZ, 2025](https://insidegovernment.co.nz/cyclone-homes-recategorised-in-waiohiki/); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/580182/the-end-of-category-3-cyclone-red-zones-to-be-phased-out).
- **H4 (disincentive) — largely supported.** Raising a typical timber house up to 2 m costs roughly $132k–$357k [NIWA / WT Partnership, 2023](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf); Suncorp told Consumer NZ home adaptations are "not currently reflected in our standard pricing"; the NHC levy is flat regardless of risk or mitigation [Consumer NZ, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding); [AA Insurance, levies](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/levies). Yet Auckland's own 2P scheme shows the arithmetic favours mitigation: council lifted its mitigation grant cap from 25% to 40% of capital value in February 2026 explicitly to avoid ~$14m of buyouts at ~$1.2m cost [RNZ, Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/585755/auckland-council-looks-at-rule-change-to-avoid-flood-buyout-costs); [1News, Feb 2026](https://www.1news.co.nz/2026/02/05/auckland-council-raises-flood-mitigation-limit-to-avoid-costly-buyouts/).
- **The sharpest gap is not consenting new builds — it is the missing reward machinery for adapting existing homes.** The October 2025 National Adaptation Framework contains no property-level adaptation programme and leaves "who pays" unanswered [MfE National Adaptation Framework, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/climate-change/national-adaptation-framework-final-web.pdf); [Newsroom, 2025](https://newsroom.co.nz/2025/10/16/long-awaited-climate-adaptation-plan-leaves-who-pays-unanswered/), while comparators (US NFIP elevation credits, Alabama's statutory FORTIFIED discounts, UK Flood Re Build Back Better, Queensland's $741m Resilient Homes Fund) all connect mitigation to premiums or funded delivery [FEMA Risk Rating 2.0, 2025](https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_rr-2.0_04-2025.pdf); [FORTIFIED incentives — Alabama](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives-alabama/); [Flood Re, Build Back Better](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/); [Queensland Reconstruction Authority, Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qra.qld.gov.au/resilient-homes-fund).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the statutory and council-source claims rest on primary or official documents, but several load-bearing items (insurer pricing practice, exact statutory wording where legislation.govt.nz bot-blocked fetching, current select-committee status of the Planning and Natural Environment Bills) are single-source or unverified as at 2026-07-03 and are flagged below.

## Confidence & limits

**What is fact (High):** the NPS-NH 2025 gazettal, objective, policies and mitigation-crediting clauses [NZ Gazette, 2025](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045); [MfE implementation guide](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/RMA/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-implementation-guide.pdf); Building Act ss 71–74 mechanics including removable-entry provisions, quoted verbatim in MBIE's official guidance [MBIE, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); Auckland Council's own description of its flood maps and LIM content [OurAuckland, Mar 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2026/03/understanding-auckland-s-regional-flood-maps/); the 2P grant scheme and its February 2026 cap change [OurAuckland, Nov 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/11/auckland-council-confirms-mitigations-for-2p-properties-will-be-funded/); [RNZ, Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/585755/auckland-council-looks-at-rule-change-to-avoid-flood-buyout-costs); the Waiohiki/Ōmāhu recategorisations [Inside Government NZ, 2025](https://insidegovernment.co.nz/cyclone-homes-recategorised-in-waiohiki/); and the NIWA/WT Partnership house-elevation costings [NIWA, 2023](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf).

**What is interpretation:** that these facts combine into a structural disincentive — formally risk-based consenting plus title/insurance penalties, parcel-level disclosure, mitigation-blind maps, and absent premium/rates rewards jointly make property-level adaptation economically irrational for most owners. That synthesis is this document's argument, not a sourced fact, and it partially *corrects* the founder's H1: the framing problem is less "the law says avoid" than "the law permits adaptation but nothing rewards it".

**Main limits:** (1) legislation.govt.nz, several council pages and web.archive.org bot-blocked direct fetching, so some statutory wording (RMA s6(h)/s106/s106A verbatim text, NHI Act 2023 s77, the LIM regulations' clause numbering) is corroborated through official secondary documents rather than the primary page; (2) insurer practice rests heavily on one Consumer NZ investigation and Tower's own pages; (3) whether the Environment Committee has reported back the Planning Bill and Natural Environment Bill as at 2026-07-03 could not be confirmed; (4) no NZ data was found on consent refusal rates for adaptation works, or on how many s73-tagged titles exist or have ever had entries removed — those absences are themselves findings and drive the child issues below; (5) resource-management lawyers and practising planners must validate any policy-facing conclusions before this stream advances past G1 — this framing is evidence-gathering, not legal advice. No personal or property-identifying data is used; the one live dispute cited (Bayview, Auckland) is from published news reporting [Insurance Business NZ, Mar 2026](https://www.insurancebusinessmag.com/nz/news/property/auckland-owners-fight-very-high-hazard-tags-on-lims-568663.aspx).

## Problem statement

New Zealand's hazard-planning stack — RMA consenting, district-plan overlays, Building Act ss 71–74, LIM disclosure, NHC/private insurance — was built to stop *new* risk being created, and its instruments (refusal powers, title tags, parcel-level disclosure) all point that way. Around 750,000 people and about $235 billion of buildings already sit in 1-in-100-year flood-exposed locations [Earth Sciences NZ, 2025](https://www.earthsciences.nz/news/nationwide-study-reveals-escalating-flood-risk), and RBNZ assesses roughly 6% of properties (~120,000 homes) as high flood risk [RBNZ FSR special topic, May 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2). For that existing stock, the system offers no institutionalised way for a property that *reduces* its risk to have the reduction recognised — in its hazard designation, its LIM, its insurance premium, or its rates — and the owner who adapts anyway pays $130k+ for works [NIWA / WT Partnership, 2023](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf) plus consenting costs, while a declined consent inside Auckland's own mitigation scheme tips the property into buyout [OurAuckland, Apr 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/). The stream should test where in that chain (designation → disclosure → consent → incentive) the blockage binds hardest, and what a mitigation-recognition mechanism would look like.

This stream deliberately builds on, and does not repeat, stream #254 (flood-risk housing and insurance retreat): the market value paradox is covered by [`research/findings/other/flood-risk-value-paradox.md`](../research/findings/other/flood-risk-value-paradox.md) (#260), buyer/lender disclosure by [`research/findings/other/nz-flood-lending-buyer-disclosure.md`](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-lending-buyer-disclosure.md) (#264), and the managed-retreat policy baseline by #261 (in review). This root focuses on the consenting and designation machinery itself.

## Evidence

### H1 — the statutory frame: risk-based gateways, penalty-priced enablement

The RMA gateway is discretionary risk management, not prohibition: ss 106/106A let a consent authority refuse, *or grant subject to conditions*, where there is significant natural-hazard risk [MfE NPS-NH Implementation Guide, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/RMA/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-implementation-guide.pdf), and the s106A extension to land-use consents applies to applications from 21 August 2025 [Waikato Regional Council guidance, 2025](https://www.waikatoregion.govt.nz/assets/WRC/GuidanceRMAmendment-Act2025ConsentingImplications.pdf). The NPS-NH 2025 directs a "risk-based proportionate approach": avoidance is mandatory only for "very high" risk (Policy 3), decision-makers "must consider… existing and proposed mitigation measures" (cl 3.2(2)) and the cost-effectiveness of mitigation (cl 3.3(3)), and MfE's stated aim is to "maximise development while minimising losses" [MfE implementation guide, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/RMA/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-implementation-guide.pdf); [NZ Gazette, 2025](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045). The replacement Planning Bill and Natural Environment Bill (introduced 9 December 2025, submissions closed 13 February 2026) carry the same "proportionate and risk-based planning" goal [MfE Planning Bill page, accessed 2026-07-03](https://environment.govt.nz/acts-and-regulations/acts/planning-bill/); [Beehive, 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/planning-reform-bills-pass-first-reading).

The Building Act completes the picture: s71 requires refusal on hazard land unless "adequate provision" is made, but s72 *compels* grant where the work won't worsen the hazard — at the price of a s73/s74 entry on the title identifying the hazard. MBIE's guidance lists the consequences: purchaser notification, council liability exemption, "Toka Tū Ake EQC can legally decline to provide cover", private insurers may decline or exclude, banks may decline lending — and quotes the Court of Appeal calling the notice "a blot on the title" [MBIE, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf). The NHC confirms it may decline cover where damage matches the notified hazard, case by case, under s77 of the Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023 [NHC, section notices, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/). An entry *can* be removed under s74(3)–(4) where the authority determines it is no longer required — with evidence the hazard no longer exists or adequate provision has been made [MBIE, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf) — but no data exists on how often that ever happens (child issue below).

So H1 as stated ("systems favour don't-build-there") is too simple: the modern instruments credit mitigation on paper. What survives of H1 is real, though — the discretion is one-directional (an applicant has no right to consent where risk is mitigated), the enablement pathway carries title stigma and insurance loss, and Auckland's Plan Change 120 (rules effective 3 November 2025) moves high-risk-area development to non-complying status, i.e. the direction of travel for consenting is tightening [Auckland Council OurAuckland, Oct 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/10/auckland-council-toughens-up-on-building-in-flood-risk-areas/).

### H2 — granularity: polygons are partial-parcel; consequences are whole-parcel

Hazard layers themselves are mapped extents, not parcel tags — Auckland's floodplains live in council GIS and trigger Unitary Plan Chapter E36 rules wherever the mapped extent touches a site [Auckland Unitary Plan E36](https://unitaryplan.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/Images/Auckland%20Unitary%20Plan%20Operative/Chapter%20E%20Auckland-wide/5.%20Environmental%20Risk/E36%20Natural%20hazards%20and%20flooding.pdf); Whangārei's Plan Change 1 and Dunedin's 2GP map hazard extents the same way [Whangārei DC PC1](https://www.wdc.govt.nz/Services/Planning/District-Plan/District-Plan-changes/Current-plan-changes/PC1); [Dunedin 2GP natural hazards chapter](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/716560/11.-Natural-Hazards.pdf). But the operative unit for consequences is the parcel: Hamilton records flood information on the property record "when any portion of a property falls within a mapped flood hazard area" [Hamilton City Council](https://hamilton.govt.nz/property-rates-and-building/district-plan/flood-mapping/), and the LIM regime discloses hazards "affecting the land" as a whole. Waikato Regional Council's position statement is unusually candid: it is "not required to meet a specific level of accuracy or robustness" in LIM disclosure, may disclose draft data whose confidence "may be minimal or variable", and indicative regional information "will not have been designed to reflect site-specific conditions and may not even be applicable to a particular parcel of land" [WRC, 2025](https://www.waikatoregion.govt.nz/assets/WRC/RegionalHazardsPortalPositionStatementProvisionNaturalHazardInformationLIMs.pdf). Since market actors (buyers, insurers, lenders) read the LIM rather than the polygon, partial-parcel exposure converts to whole-property consequences.

### H3 — mitigation-blind by default; responsive only inside the recovery framework

Auckland Council states plainly that its flood maps are catchment-scale, "not site specific (based on individual property data)", "don't include flood mitigations to a building", rest ~95% on 2016 LiDAR, and that LIM flood content "does not include any mitigations that may have occurred at the property for a building or home" [OurAuckland, Mar 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2026/03/understanding-auckland-s-regional-flood-maps/). The 2025 LIM regulations require no risk assessment, further analysis, or inquiry beyond information already held [Regulations 2025 (SL 2025/68)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2025/0068/16.0/whole.html); [Simpson Grierson, 2025](https://www.simpsongrierson.com/insights-news/legal-updates/streamlining-natural-hazard-information-in-lims-what-new-requirements-mean-in-practice-for-both-councils-and-customers); [Rice Speir, 2025](https://ricespeir.co.nz/natural-hazard-information-whats-changing-for-lims-in-2025/). Correction routes are owner-funded and slow: a private site-specific flood assessment for consent purposes [Planning Plus, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.planningplus.co.nz/post/flooding-is-your-site-affected), Hamilton's published data-review practice note (the clearest such pathway found) [Hamilton City Council](https://hamilton.govt.nz/property-rates-and-building/district-plan/flood-mapping/), multi-year plan changes (Whangārei PC1: notified May 2023, still under appeal 3+ years on) [Whangārei DC](https://www.wdc.govt.nz/Services/Planning/District-Plan/District-Plan-changes/Current-plan-changes/PC1), the Ombudsman, or judicial review — *Weir v Kapiti Coast DC* [2013] NZHC 3516 established that councils *must* note hazard information on LIMs but can be pushed on presentation [Kāpiti Coast DC, 2013](https://www.kapiticoast.govt.nz/whats-on/news-archive/previous-years/2013/judge-rules-on-weir-judicial-review/).

The counter-precedent matters: under the post-Gabrielle categorisation, designations moved when mitigation was built. Forty-five Waiohiki properties went Category 2C→1 on completion of the rebuilt Tūtaekurī stopbank [Inside Government NZ, Apr 2025](https://insidegovernment.co.nz/cyclone-homes-recategorised-in-waiohiki/); eleven Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu properties had their category lifted as works neared completion [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/hawkes-bay-today/news/eleven-omahu-home-owners-get-green-light-to-stay-after-gabrielle-flooding/2U2RZTPCWBEPFOBOXFIHDO4I7M/); RNZ reports stopbank QA moved "many" 2C* areas to Category 1 [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/580182/the-end-of-category-3-cyclone-red-zones-to-be-phased-out). For property-level works, Auckland updates the LIM to record completed 2P mitigation and automatically revalues — an annotation, not a lifted designation [OurAuckland, Dec 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/12/category-2p-getting-on-with-your-work/). No case was found of a property formally recategorised after house-raising alone.

### H4 — the economics: six-figure costs, near-zero rewards, reactive co-funding

Costs: elevating a single-storey 106m² timber house up to 2 m was costed at $172,129 (and $131,874–$357,453 across construction types) by WT Partnership for NIWA in 2023, with concrete construction "might not be financially viable" [NIWA, 2023](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf); consenting adds building consent (statutory 20 working days, clock-stoppable) and, in flood overlays, resource consent — Christchurch requires consent where District Plan minimum floor levels (1-in-200-year) aren't met [Christchurch City Council, accessed 2026-07-03](https://ccc.govt.nz/services/water-and-drainage/stormwater-and-drainage/flooding/floor-level-requirements). Inside Auckland's own 2P scheme, "if the mitigations aren't feasible because a resource or building consent has been declined, the property will change to a Category 3" — buyout [OurAuckland, Apr 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/).

Rewards: Suncorp (Vero/AA brands) says home adaptations are "not currently reflected in our standard pricing"; ICNZ advises owners to tell their insurer because "the insurer won't necessarily be aware of any work done" [Consumer NZ, Nov 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding). Tower is the partial exception — address-level flood ratings using property characteristics, with an invitation to request re-rating after alterations, but no published re-rating criteria or worked example [Tower, risk-based pricing, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/); [interest.co.nz, 2021](https://www.interest.co.nz/insurance/113124/tower-customers-can-expect-premium-changes-insurer-uses-more-detailed-modelling). The NHC levy is flat (16c per $100 insured, capped) with no mitigation discount [AA Insurance, levies, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/levies). Rating valuations adjust *downward* for flood evidence [OurAuckland, Jun 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/06/valuing-storm-affected-properties/), but no council scheme was found that rewards mitigation through rates. An April 2026 IFSO ruling held an insurer rebuilding a flooded home need only meet the minimum consentable floor level, not fund resilience betterment [Insurance Business NZ, 2026](https://www.insurancebusinessmag.com/nz/news/property/nz-flood-rebuild-floor-ruling-clarifies-insurer-obligations-573008.aspx).

Co-funding exists only post-disaster: Auckland's 2P design-and-consent plus construction grants (initially 25% of capital value, lifted to 40% in February 2026 to avoid ~$14m of buyouts at ~$1.2m cost) [OurAuckland, Nov 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/11/auckland-council-confirms-mitigations-for-2p-properties-will-be-funded/); [RNZ, Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/585755/auckland-council-looks-at-rule-change-to-avoid-flood-buyout-costs); [1News, Feb 2026](https://www.1news.co.nz/2026/02/05/auckland-council-raises-flood-mitigation-limit-to-avoid-costly-buyouts/); Westport's $2.0m Adaptation Relief Fund for raising buildings/flood barriers, still not operative years after the 2021 flood [Buller District Council, accessed 2026-07-03](https://bullerdc.govt.nz/your-council/key-projects/resilient-westport-package/). Hawke's Bay 2P owners, by contrast, reported no funded pathway at all [NZ Herald / Hawke's Bay Today](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/hawkes-bay-today/news/cyclone-gabrielle-category-2p-property-owners-still-waiting-for-answers/CTEHTVYDNJEJBBBZ77IBQQWXOY/). Noy and Storey characterise the national pattern as choosing "the worst of all the trilemma policy options" — do nothing, then buy out after disaster [The Conversation, May 2025](https://theconversation.com/as-insurance-gets-harder-to-buy-nz-has-3-choices-for-disaster-recovery-and-we-keep-choosing-the-worst-one-255713).

### What's already being done, and the comparator menu

The National Adaptation Framework (October 2025) pledges a National Flood Map, clearer roles ("Individuals and private companies manage their risks"), investment shifting toward pre-event risk reduction, and market-reflected risk — but contains no property-level adaptation programme and no retreat framework, and commentators note "who pays" is unresolved [MfE NAF, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/climate-change/national-adaptation-framework-final-web.pdf); [Newsroom, Oct 2025](https://newsroom.co.nz/2025/10/16/long-awaited-climate-adaptation-plan-leaves-who-pays-unanswered/); [The Spinoff, Oct 2025](https://thespinoff.co.nz/politics/20-10-2025/whats-in-the-long-awaited-climate-adaptation-plan-and-what-was-left-out). The Independent Reference Group proposed ending buyouts after a 20-year transition, with adaptation on a beneficiary-pays basis [RNZ, Jul 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/566406/no-climate-change-buy-outs-in-future-expert-group-tells-government); [1News, Jul 2025](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/07/12/panel-recommends-end-to-weather-damage-buyouts-in-20-years/) — a world in which premium-visible mitigation becomes more valuable, not less.

Abroad, the reward machinery NZ lacks already runs at scale: US NFIP Risk Rating 2.0 credits elevation, elevated services and flood openings in premiums [FEMA, 2025](https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/documents/fema_rr-2.0_04-2025.pdf), the Community Rating System discounts premiums 5–45% for council-level floodplain management [FEMA CRS](https://www.fema.gov/floodplain-management/community-rating-system), and Alabama mandates insurer discounts up to 55% for FORTIFIED homes — a standard whose Hurricane Sally performance (loss frequency down 55–74%) was measured on 40,000+ properties [FORTIFIED incentives — Alabama](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives-alabama/); [IBHS/University of Alabama study](https://ibhs.org/ibhs-news-releases/study-shows-ibhss-fortified-program-reduced-hurricane-sally-damage/). The UK's Flood Re pays up to £10,000 per claim to "Build Back Better" (though only ~⅓ of eligible policyholders accept) and is prototyping Flood Performance Certificates to make installed resilience visible to insurers and lenders [Flood Re](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/); [DEFRA FloodReady, 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties). Queensland's $741m Resilient Homes Fund made retrofit (≤$50k) and raising the default post-flood offer, with buyback the minority pathway (~685 homes) [QRA](https://www.qra.qld.gov.au/resilient-homes-fund); [qld.gov.au](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/homeowners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund) — though NSW's equivalent shows the accommodation stream is operationally hardest (49 resilient-measure approvals vs 700+ buybacks by 2025) [NSW Reconstruction Authority](https://www.nsw.gov.au/departments-and-agencies/nsw-reconstruction-authority/our-work/resilient-homes-program/about-program); [Lismore App, 2025](https://lismoreapp.com.au/NewsStory/house-raises-and-retrofits-whats-the-latest/681c6959420092002f9b4d3c).

## Open follow-up questions

Proposed child research issues (each `Part of #370`); none crosses the G1 gate into ideation:

1. **Designation-correction pathways in practice.** Across 5–8 councils, what documented routes exist to update a flood map, hazard overlay or LIM notation after site works or better data — cost, duration, success cases? (Seed: Hamilton's review practice note, Auckland site-specific assessments, Whangārei PC1 latency, *Weir*, Ombudsman LIM cases.)
2. **The s73/s74 tagged-title population.** How many NZ titles carry Building Act natural-hazard entries, how are they distributed, and how many entries have ever been removed under s74(4)? (Likely OIA to LINZ/councils; would quantify whether the "enable with tag" pathway is a one-way door.)
3. **Consent economics of house-raising.** Real consent cost/time stacks (building + resource consent) for lifting an existing dwelling in 3–4 councils with flood overlays, plus any obtainable refusal/decline data — testing H4's "costly/difficult" limb against records rather than anecdote.
4. **Insurance recognition of mitigation.** Systematic survey of NZ insurers' published positions and IFSO rulings on premium recognition of property-level works; does any customer re-rating pathway actually function (Tower), and what would an NZ certification analogue of FORTIFIED / Flood Performance Certificates require?
5. **FOSAL as institutional precedent.** Document every post-Gabrielle recategorisation-after-mitigation and extract the administrative mechanism (evidence thresholds, QA, who decides) — could it become business-as-usual outside recovery frameworks, e.g. under the National Flood Map programme?
6. **Comparative instrument design.** What design features made NFIP RR2.0/CRS, Flood Re BBB/FPC, and the QLD/NSW resilient-homes funds work or fail (caps, certification, uptake, delivery capacity), and which are transplantable under the NAF's "incentivise risk reduction" pillar?

## What would change this conclusion

- Evidence that consent authorities routinely *grant* adaptation consents cheaply and quickly (question 3's data) would weaken H4's "difficult to consent" limb — currently supported more by structure than by refusal statistics.
- A documented, functioning insurer re-rating pathway after mitigation (question 4) would overturn the "unrewarded" conclusion for at least part of the market.
- Evidence that councils systematically ingest as-built floor levels or private mitigation into flood models (e.g. via the 2024 Auckland LiDAR rebuild or Christchurch's floor-level viewer) would weaken H3.
- The Environment Committee's reports on the Planning and Natural Environment Bills (due mid-2026; status unverified as at 2026-07-03) could change the statutory framing materially in either direction.
- Legal review by a resource-management practitioner could correct the statutory reading here — several provisions were verified through official secondary sources because legislation.govt.nz bot-blocked direct fetching.
