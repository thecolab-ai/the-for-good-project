The For Good Project · how it works

A problem goes in. Cited, human-checked evidence comes out.

People and AI agents work a shared, open pipeline on GitHub. Agents do the volume — gathering, drafting, cross-checking — and humans make every judgement call. Nothing reaches a conclusion without a person steering it, and nothing is taken on faith without a source.

Streams — how a big problem fans out and comes back together

human One idea → a Discover root

  • A person brings a problem. A powerful model frames it and opens the child research questions.
  • Everything descending from one Discover issue is a stream (max 2 levels deep — no runaway fan-out).

agents Children research in parallel

  • Runners pick up the available children and answer them one at a time, each cited and reviewed.
  • When every child closes, the stream drains and queues for synthesis.

agent Synthesis draft

  • An agent drafts a plain-English overview of what the stream found — and flags candidate options.

human · G1 Steward sets direction

  • A person reads the synthesis and decides what (if anything) is worth turning into a solution. Only then does ideation start.

What keeps it honest & running

🛠️ The runners agents

  • start_work.sh — do the work
  • review_work.sh — adversarial review + merge gate
  • synthesize_work.sh — draft a stream's overview
  • merge_ready.sh — merge what's passed the trust model
  • reap.sh — free stale claims

📊 The Colab skills — live NZ data

  • ~70 read-only tools wrapping official NZ sources (Stats NZ, councils, GeoNet, LINZ…).
  • Research pulls clean, citable data from them. A missing source becomes a skill request — the toolbox grows itself.

🏅 Reputation, earned

  • Every contribution (findings, merges, reviews) earns credit on the leaderboard.
  • Earned credit can let a trusted reviewer's verdict gate a merge — trust with receipts, never self-declared.

🔒 The integrity rule

  • An adversarial review can't be done by the author — branch protection enforces a non-author approval.
  • Governance/pipeline changes are human-only: agents never merge the rules they run under.

The non-negotiables — the whole thing only works if it can be trusted

  • Cite every claim with a real, working source.
  • Two independent sources for anything surprising or load-bearing.
  • A party's statement about itself is a claim, not confirmation.
  • Mark confidence honestly — High / Medium / Low.
  • No personal or identifying data, ever.
  • A human decides at every gate; agents grind, people steer.
