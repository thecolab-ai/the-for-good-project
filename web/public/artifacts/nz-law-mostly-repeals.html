Are most changes to NZ law really "repeals"?

A viral article called New Zealand "the fastest repealer in the West." So the community stopped guessing and counted. Here's what the numbers actually say — and, just as importantly, how sure we can be.

The short answer

No — repeals are a clear minority of law changes, under every way we counted.

Not one definition we tested — from the strictest to the most generous — got anywhere near half. But there's an honest catch, and it's near the bottom.

1 · It all hangs on how you define "repeal"

Medium confidence

In a fixed, reproducible sample of 120 recent amendment records: counting only true repeals gives ~11%. Even counting every "replace" as a repeal only reaches ~23%. Widen the definition as far as it honestly goes and it still doesn't cross the line.

2 · It's a minority no matter who's in power

Medium confidence

Repeal & revocation events as a share of all Act-amendment activity, by government term since 2008. It drifts between roughly 12% and 19% — never close to a majority, red or blue.

*Luxon I is a partial term. Source data is the Whiplash aggregate (see caveats).

3 · The headline numbers count different things

A lot of the confusion is that the big figures being quoted aren't measuring the same thing. They're reconcilable — but not interchangeable.

4 · When whole Acts are repealed, it's real policy — not tidying

Medium confidence

Of 71 whole-Act repeals (Jan 2023 – Jun 2026): none came via a classic "spent-law" clean-up bill. 83% were part of live legislative packages — repeal-and-replace reforms, treaty settlements, institutional change.

Examples of the "83%":

Fair Pay Agreements Act Repeal 2023 Resource Management repeal 2023 Water Services Acts Repeal 2024 Therapeutic Products Act Repeal 2024 Incorporated Societies Act 2022

The other 17% were routine annual turnover (Appropriation & Secondary Legislation Confirmation Acts).

5 · Read one Act line-by-line — and "amendment" mostly means adding

Low confidence · n=1 Act

The one Act from a seeded random sample that could be fully read from official text (Judicature (Timeliness) 2025). Of its 23 amending provisions, 15 inserted brand-new law and just one was a repeal. It proves the method works — it's far too small to generalise from.

How sure are we? (read this bit)

The verdict is consistent — but it isn't yet independent.

  • ⚠️ One source, many angles. Almost every number here traces back to one volunteer-built mirror of the official data — the same community project behind the original article. The findings agree with each other, but that's not the same as independent confirmation. No count has yet been replicated straight from the government's own bulk data.
  • 🚧 The hand-check stalled. Four of five randomly chosen Acts couldn't be read because the official legislation website's bot-protection blocked our tools — a tooling problem, not missing data — leaving the line-by-line sample at a single Act.
  • 🏷️ A known weak spot. The mirror always counts a legacy "omit" instruction as a repeal, when some were probably minor deletions — which could inflate historical repeal counts.

So: "mostly repeals" doesn't survive the count — but before anyone publishes a single hard number, it needs replication from the official source. That honesty is the finding.

Where this is heading

The stream is now at a human decision point (gate G1). The options on the table:

Get official access first — an API key or unblocked mirror so future counts don't hit the bot-protection wall. Small.
Finish the official-text sample — classify the 4 blocked Acts and reconcile against the mirror. Medium.
Full-corpus count with the author — audit the labels, publish a defensible whole-statute-book figure. Large.
Publish a plain-language explainer — the taxonomy, the three counting layers, and how the headline shifts (but never flips). Small.
