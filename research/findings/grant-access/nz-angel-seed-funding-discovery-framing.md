# NZ early-stage founders' angel/seed problem should be framed as a discovery-and-access gap to be tested, not assumed

> **Scope note:** this is the stream-framing output for Discover root [#110](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/110) — a Discover-stage framing memo that maps the landscape and fans out the stream's research children, following the same pattern as the merged stream #60 and #61 framings. It is **not** a leaf research finding. It verifies the issue's headline figures against primary sources, corrects two claims that turned out to be imprecise, and hands the causal and design questions to the research children ([#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141)–[#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145)). The core attribution the stream rests on — that *discovery/access*, not capital scarcity, is the binding constraint — is deliberately left as the first thing the children must test.

## Executive answer

- **The funding aggregates in the issue check out against primary sources, and they are striking.** 2025 was a record year for NZ startup investment — about $754m, up 61% — yet only 47 *genuinely new* companies received investment (46 in 2024, 51 in 2023), and expansion-stage rounds took 83% of the capital while being just 49% of rounds. So a record capital year coincided with a *narrowing* new-company pipeline [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/), [b2bnews / Young Company Finance](https://b2bnews.co.nz/news/nz-startup-investment-hits-754m-but-new-company-pipeline-shrinks/).
- **The micro-cheque segment the issue describes is real and measurable.** Catalist's FY25 New Zealand Angel Market Report puts total angel-group investment at $13,914,788 (up 2.7%) across 167 deals, with the *average* per-deal investment down to $12,446 and the *median* just $5,003 — exactly the $10k-and-under band the issue says has no marketplace [Catalist NZ Angel Market Report FY25](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf), [BusinessDesk](https://businessdesk.co.nz/article/startups/angel-investment-in-startups-is-up-but-spread-more-thinly). And that is only the money flowing through *tracked angel groups* — individual/solo angels outside networks are structurally uncounted, which is precisely why they're hard to find.
- **The "there's nowhere to look" framing needs one correction, not a retraction.** Angel Association NZ *does* run a member directory and a founder-facing "find a startup investor" function [Angel Association NZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/); NZGCP publishes a start-up investor list [NZGCP](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors). What the issue is really pointing at is the absence of a *maintained, machine-readable, stage/region/cheque-matchable* dataset — a data-quality and matchability gap, not a total void. That reframing matters for what gets built.
- **The government-side "map broke overnight" claim is directionally right but the precise date isn't confirmed.** Callaghan Innovation was disestablished during 2025 (closure announced 23 January 2025, function transfers detailed 14 February 2025), with its grants and *Startup and Founder Support* moving into MBIE [Wikipedia: Callaghan Innovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callaghan_Innovation), [Callaghan Innovation disestablishment updates](https://www.callaghaninnovation.govt.nz/about-us/disestablishment-updates/). I could not independently confirm the issue's specific "1 December 2025" cessation date — treat that date as unverified while the *fact* of the 2025 disestablishment and MBIE transfer is solid.
- **The official record already says the pipeline problem is real; it does not yet say discovery is the cause.** MBIE's 9 May 2025 briefing notes "regression in the seed funding market due to recent economic conditions" [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital), and the Startup Advisors Council's 2023 *Upstart Nation* report calls the ecosystem "noisy, fragmented, and disconnected" with government support "spread across multiple agencies… difficult for founders to navigate" [Upstart Nation, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/upstart-nation.pdf). Those support a *fragmentation* framing. They do **not** establish that access/discovery (rather than capital scarcity or investment-readiness) is the *binding constraint* for the sub-$250k founder — that is the stream's central unproven assumption and the job of [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141).
- **The stream should fan out before any ideation.** I opened five research children on: attrition/attribution ([#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141)), who writes the micro-cheques and how discoverable sub-$250k pathways are ([#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142)), what free/low-cost connection models work overseas ([#143](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/143)), whether NZ investors would participate in open formats / maintain a directory ([#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144)), and who is structurally locked out and what the distributional data shows ([#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145)).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the funding landscape and aggregate figures are High-confidence from official and primary sources, but the load-bearing causal claim (discovery/access is the binding constraint, and an open marketplace would move it) is currently **unevidenced** and rests partly on a single founder's lived experience. The stream is worth researching; it is not yet worth building against.

## Evidence

### Problem statement and who is affected

The Discover issue frames a five-part access failure: closed "who you know" networks; a brutally narrow visible funnel; no marketplace for the smallest ($10k–$200k) cheques; pay-to-play connection points (conferences, cohorts, accelerators); and an event format (curated "top-5" pitch nights) that showcases rather than connects [issue #110](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/110). The people it names as structurally locked out are founders without warm networks — first-timers, founders outside Auckland/Wellington, Māori and Pasifika founders, and women. That distributional claim is central to *who the stream is for* but is not yet backed by NZ data in this memo; it is assigned in full to [#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145).

### The funding aggregates are real, and the pipeline is narrowing despite record capital

2025 set a record for NZ startup investment at about $754m, up 61% year-on-year, per the Young Company Finance report (produced with NZGCP and Angel Association NZ) [b2bnews](https://b2bnews.co.nz/news/nz-startup-investment-hits-754m-but-new-company-pipeline-shrinks/), [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/). But the NZ Herald report of the same data records only **47 genuinely new companies** receiving investment in 2025 (against 46 in 2024 and 51 in 2023), and states that early-expansion and expansion-stage rounds were **49% of funded rounds but 83% of capital** [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/). In other words, a record dollar year is compatible with — and here coincides with — fewer new companies getting in the door and capital concentrating at the later stages. This is the strongest structural fact the stream has, and it does not depend on the access hypothesis being true.

### The micro-cheque segment is measurable — and mostly invisible

Catalist's *2025 Annual New Zealand Angel Market Report* records **$13,914,788 invested in 2025**, up **2.7%** on 2024, across **167 deals** (63 new deals, 104 follow-ons) — with **$6,139,309** in new deals and **$7,775,480** in follow-ons [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). The same report puts active angels at **455, up ~39% from 328**, with the **average per-deal investment falling to $12,446 and the median to $5,003** [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). Independent reporting of the same dataset frames it as "up, but spread more thinly" [BusinessDesk](https://businessdesk.co.nz/article/startups/angel-investment-in-startups-is-up-but-spread-more-thinly). Two things follow. First, the issue's "no marketplace for the $10k–$50k cheque" is pointing at a genuinely large, growing, low-ticket segment. Second — and critically for solution design — this is money that flows *through Catalist-tracked angel groups*; the report explicitly counts group activity that agrees to share anonymised data. Solo/individual angels writing cheques *outside* formal networks are not in this number at all, which is exactly the population the issue says is impossible to find. Quantifying that off-network segment is the core of [#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142).

### The current discovery infrastructure: a matchability gap, not a void

The issue's strongest claim — that AANZ "doesn't offer founder-facing, machine-readable discovery" — is *imprecise* and I am correcting it in the founders' favour of accuracy. AANZ's own site describes a searchable member database and a founder-facing route to "find pitch nights and connect with investors", and AANZ is the umbrella for formal angel networks whose memberships range roughly 20–200 individuals [Angel Association NZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/). NZGCP separately publishes a start-up investor list [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors). Enterprise Angels (Tauranga) runs **AngelEquity**, an online platform launched October 2016, open to wholesale investors, and reports facilitating over $50m into ~100 companies since 2008 [Enterprise Angels](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/), [AngelEquity](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/angelequity/) (note: one third-party company-data site lists AngelEquity as inactive — activity level should be verified before it is relied on, assigned to [#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142)). So discovery infrastructure exists. What appears *not* to exist is a maintained, open, machine-readable dataset a founder can filter by **stage × region × cheque size** — i.e. the difference between "a list of networks exists" and "I can see who invests at my stage, in my region, at my cheque size, and how to approach them." Whether that gap is the real bottleneck (versus distribution, trust, or investment-readiness) is the counter-hypothesis carried into [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141) and [#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144).

### The visible co-investment infrastructure starts above the founder's cheque size

NZGCP's Aspire NZ Seed Fund co-invests alongside private and angel investors on a matching basis, typically writing NZ$250K–NZ$1M at proof-of-concept to seed stage [NZGCP Aspire FAQ](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/NZGCP-Aspire-FAQ_Nov22.pdf) (the live Aspire page bot-blocks plain fetchers — details verified via NZGCP's own FAQ PDF and Google's cached description; see Sources). Two features matter for the framing. First, its floor sits at roughly the ceiling of the "get moving" band the issue describes ($10k–$200k), so it does not serve the sub-$250k founder. Second, by design it *matches* private investors the founder has already found — it presumes the access problem is solved. MBIE's 9 May 2025 briefing notes "regression in the seed funding market due to recent economic conditions" and flags it as informing a review of Aspire policy settings [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital) (dmsdocument bot-blocks plain fetchers; quote verified via search snippet — flagged in Sources). So the government's own record says the seed market has regressed; it does not attribute that to discovery.

### The fragmentation framing is on the record; the causal framing is not

The Startup Advisors Council's **2023 Upstart Nation** report (published via MBIE) states directly that "The UpStart ecosystem in New Zealand can be described as noisy, fragmented, and disconnected", that the ecosystem is "highly fragmented and difficult for founders to navigate", and that "Government support programmes are spread across multiple agencies" — and it proposes a national coordinating body (Accelerate Aotearoa) to reduce fragmentation [Upstart Nation, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/upstart-nation.pdf). This is strong, official corroboration that *fragmentation and navigability* are recognised problems. It is **not** evidence that a founder-investor *discovery marketplace* is the missing piece, nor that access (rather than capital or readiness) is what stops a given founder raising. Keeping that distinction honest is the whole point of the fan-out.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The whole stream's premise fails if [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141) finds capital scarcity or investment-readiness — not discovery/access — is what actually stops sub-$250k founders raising.** A founder who can't be found is a different problem from a founder who is found and declined, or one who isn't yet fundable. No source in this memo distinguishes those; the record-capital / narrowing-pipeline data is consistent with *any* of them.
- **The build case weakens to a distribution/trust problem, not an information gap, if [#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142)/[#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144) find existing infrastructure (AANZ directory, NGCP list, AngelEquity, equity-crowdfunding platforms) already covers discovery** and founders simply don't use or trust it. AANZ *does* offer a member directory — so "there's nowhere to look" is already partly false.
- **The supply side may not show up.** If NZ investors won't participate in open all-founders/all-investors formats or won't maintain directory entries (adverse selection, time, wholesale-investor marketing rules), an open marketplace is dead regardless of founder demand. This likely needs investor-side human validation, not desk research alone ([#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144)).
- **Unverified specifics I could not pin down:** the exact "1 December 2025" Callaghan cessation date; the current live/active status of AngelEquity; the issue's "~400 startups through the door for ~6 funded" figure for a named pathway (a founder-observed number I did not attempt to source — it belongs in [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141)).
- **Lived-experience dependency:** the distributional access claims (who is locked out) and the "even insiders can't articulate how a founder gets connected" observation come from the issue author's lived experience. These need corroboration from ecosystem data and from founders/investors directly — flagged as requiring a human with lived experience or domain authority before any solution is designed ([#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145), G1).
