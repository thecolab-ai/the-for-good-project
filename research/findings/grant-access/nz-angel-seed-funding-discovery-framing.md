## Executive answer

- **The funding aggregates in the issue check out against the Young Company Finance/AANZ/NZGCP dataset reported publicly, and they are striking, but not independently confirmed here.** Public reporting says 2025 startup investment was about $754m, up 61%, while only 47 *genuinely new* companies received investment (46 in 2024, 51 in 2023), and expansion-stage rounds took 83% of the capital while being just 49% of rounds [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/), [b2bnews / Young Company Finance](https://b2bnews.co.nz/news/nz-startup-investment-hits-754m-but-new-company-pipeline-shrinks/). b2bnews calls this a record year; NZ Herald corroborates the dollar, growth, new-company, and concentration figures but is not an independent underlying source.
- **The tracked angel-group market operates at relatively small average ticket sizes, but the per-cheque figures need care.** Catalist's FY25 New Zealand Angel Market Report puts tracked angel-group investment at $13,914,788 (up 2.7%) across 167 angel-group deals and 81 businesses [Catalist NZ Angel Market Report FY25](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf), [BusinessDesk](https://businessdesk.co.nz/article/startups/angel-investment-in-startups-is-up-but-spread-more-thinly). The report defines a "deal" as new money into a company by an angel group's nominee company, so two angel groups investing in one company round count as two deals. That means $13.9m / 167 is an *angel-group deal average* (~$83k), not a company-round cheque; $13.9m / 81 businesses implies ~$172k of tracked angel-group money per funded business on average, but the report does not give a company-level cheque distribution. The much-smaller $12,446 *average* and $5,003 *median* the report also gives are explicitly the **individual angel's ticket "per deal, per Angel" (excluding fund commitments)** — i.e. one angel's slice of a syndicated group deal, *not* the cheque a company receives — so they do not prove a $10k-and-under company-cheque segment. What they do show is a large, growing, low-per-angel-ticket participation base (455 active angels, up ~39%). The coverage caveat is narrower than "only group members": Catalist says the report is based on six active AANZ angel groups using Catalist and includes direct Catalist-platform investments by non-members where those investments are made alongside the groups. Angels entirely outside Catalist, or not investing alongside tracked groups, remain structurally uncounted.
- **The "there's nowhere to look" framing needs one correction, not a retraction.** Angel Association NZ *does* run a member directory and a founder-facing "find a startup investor" function [Angel Association NZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/); NZGCP publishes a start-up investor list [NZGCP](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors). What the issue is really pointing at is the absence of a *maintained, machine-readable, stage/region/cheque-matchable* dataset — a data-quality and matchability gap, not a total void. That reframing matters for what gets built.
- **The government-side "map broke overnight" claim is directionally right but the precise date isn't confirmed.** Callaghan Innovation was disestablished during 2025 (closure announced 23 January 2025, function transfers detailed 14 February 2025), with its grants and *Startup and Founder Support* moving into MBIE [Wikipedia: Callaghan Innovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callaghan_Innovation), [Callaghan Innovation disestablishment updates](https://www.callaghaninnovation.govt.nz/about-us/disestablishment-updates/). I could not independently confirm the issue's specific "1 December 2025" cessation date — treat that date as unverified while the *fact* of the 2025 disestablishment and MBIE transfer is solid.
- **The official record already says the pipeline problem is real; it does not yet say discovery is the cause.** These are two *distinct* on-the-record problems, and they should not be merged. MBIE's 9 May 2025 briefing notes "regression in the seed funding market due to recent economic conditions" [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital) — that is a **seed-market-regression** framing (the market has shrunk), *not* a fragmentation/navigability claim. Separately, the Startup Advisors Council's 2023 *Upstart Nation* report calls the ecosystem "noisy, fragmented, and disconnected" with government support "spread across multiple agencies… difficult for founders to navigate" [Upstart Nation, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/upstart-nation.pdf) — that is the **fragmentation** framing. So the fragmentation claim rests on Upstart Nation as a single primary source; MBIE is corroboration for regression, not an independent second source for fragmentation. Neither source establishes that access/discovery (rather than capital scarcity or investment-readiness) is the *binding constraint* for the sub-$250k founder — that is the stream's central unproven assumption and the job of [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141).
- **The stream should not proceed as normal while the G0 violation is unresolved.** The appropriate research shape, if a human clears G0, is five questions: attrition/attribution ([#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141)), who writes the micro-cheques and how discoverable sub-$250k pathways are ([#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142)), what free/low-cost connection models work overseas ([#143](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/143)), whether NZ investors would participate in open formats / maintain a directory ([#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144)), and who is structurally locked out and what the distributional data shows ([#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145)). However, [docs/STREAMS.md](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/STREAMS.md) says research fan-out is allowed only after the Discover root passes G0, represented by removing `needs-triage`; as of this access, [#110](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/110) still carries `needs-triage`. The existing child issues are therefore a workflow-gate **violation** needing explicit maintainer/steward remediation, not evidence that G0 has passed and not normal fan-out from this PR.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the funding landscape is well-documented, but the evidence is uneven and should not be marked uniformly High. The Catalist angel-market figures are High-confidence primary; the headline 2025 aggregates ($754m / +61% / 47 genuinely new companies) are only *public reporting* of a single underlying dataset (Young Company Finance/AANZ/NZGCP) — not the primary report itself — so they are **Medium, single-origin** (see the two-source table below); and the load-bearing causal claim (discovery/access is the binding constraint, and an open marketplace would move it) is currently **unevidenced** and rests partly on a single founder's lived experience. The stream is worth researching; it is not yet worth building against.

## Evidence

### Problem statement and who is affected

The Discover issue frames a five-part access failure: closed "who you know" networks; a brutally narrow visible funnel; no marketplace for the smallest ($10k–$200k) cheques; pay-to-play connection points (conferences, cohorts, accelerators); and an event format (curated "top-5" pitch nights) that showcases rather than connects [issue #110](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/110). The people it names as structurally locked out are founders without warm networks — first-timers, founders outside Auckland/Wellington, Māori and Pasifika founders, and women. That distributional claim is central to *who the stream is for* but is not yet backed by NZ data in this memo; it is assigned in full to [#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145).

### The funding aggregates are real, and the pipeline is narrowing despite concentrated capital

Public reporting of the Young Company Finance report (produced with NZGCP and Angel Association NZ) says NZ startup investment reached about $754m in 2025, up 61% year-on-year [b2bnews](https://b2bnews.co.nz/news/nz-startup-investment-hits-754m-but-new-company-pipeline-shrinks/), [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/). b2bnews describes that as a record; NZ Herald corroborates the $754m and 61% figures but, in the text available to me, does not independently use the "record" framing. The NZ Herald report of the same dataset records only **47 genuinely new companies** receiving investment in 2025 (against 46 in 2024 and 51 in 2023), and states that early-expansion and expansion-stage rounds were **49% of funded rounds but 83% of capital** [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/investment/start-up-company-investment-surged-to-754m-in-2025-after-deal-activity-jumped/5B4X67OA4FANLGYZIUCXHTDLGI/). In other words, a high-capital year is compatible with — and here coincides with — few new companies getting in the door and capital concentrating at later stages. This is the strongest structural fact the stream has, but because both public articles trace to the same YCF/AANZ/NZGCP dataset, it should be treated as a well-reported single-origin figure until the primary YCF report is cited directly.

### The tracked angel-group segment is measurable; the off-network segment is not

Catalist's *2025 Annual New Zealand Angel Market Report* records **$13,914,788 invested in 2025**, up **2.7%** on 2024, across **167 angel-group deals** (63 new deals, 104 follow-ons), **81 businesses**, and **455 active angels** [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). Its methodology matters: the report defines a "deal" as new money into a company by an angel group's nominee company and gives the example that two angel groups investing in one company round count as two deals [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). That works out to **~$83k of tracked angel-group money per counted group deal** ($13.9m / 167), **~$97k per new group deal** ($6.1m / 63), and **~$172k per funded business on average** ($13.9m / 81), but none of those is the same as a total company round size or a cheque-size distribution. Independent reporting of the same dataset frames angel investment as "up, but spread more thinly" [BusinessDesk](https://businessdesk.co.nz/article/startups/angel-investment-in-startups-is-up-but-spread-more-thinly).

**One figure needs care.** The report's headline "**Activity of the Average Angel Investor**" panel gives an **average investment of $12,446 and a median of $5,003**, both explicitly labelled "*per deal, per Angel*" and "*Excludes commitments to funds*" [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). These are **individual angels' slices of syndicated group deals** — not the total a company receives. My earlier draft conflated the two and read them as $10k-and-under company cheques; that is corrected here. What the per-angel figures *do* evidence is a broad, growing participation base (**active angels 455, up ~39% from 328**) writing small, portfolio-style tickets, which is consistent with the issue's picture but does not by itself demonstrate a $10k–$50k company-cheque marketplace gap.

Two narrower things still follow. First, the tracked angel-market averages are in or near the issue's sub-$200k band, especially when averaged by counted group deal; that supports the stream's focus on smaller early-stage capital, but only at **Medium** confidence because the report does not publish company-round cheque distributions. Second — and critically for solution design — the Catalist report covers six active AANZ angel groups using Catalist plus direct Catalist-platform investments by non-members where those investments were made alongside the groups [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf). It does **not** count angels entirely outside Catalist, or non-member direct investments that are not made alongside the tracked groups. Quantifying that off-platform/off-network segment, and establishing the actual company-level cheque-size distribution, is the core of [#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142).

### The current discovery infrastructure: a matchability gap, not a void

The issue's strongest claim — that AANZ "doesn't offer founder-facing, machine-readable discovery" — is *imprecise* and I am correcting it in the founders' favour of accuracy. AANZ's own site describes a searchable member database and a founder-facing route to "find pitch nights and connect with investors", and AANZ is the umbrella for formal angel networks [Angel Association NZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/). NZGCP separately publishes a start-up investor list [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors). Enterprise Angels (Tauranga) is another visible pathway: its homepage says it connects early-stage companies with angel investors and facilitates funding for companies, while its AngelEquity page says the AngelEquity site launched in October 2016, was consolidated into the Enterprise Angels brand after a three-year period, and saw about $3m invested via AngelEquity while Enterprise Angels investors invested $22m in 40 startups over the same period [Enterprise Angels](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/), [AngelEquity](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/angelequity/). So discovery infrastructure exists. What appears *not* to exist is a maintained, open, machine-readable dataset a founder can filter by **stage × region × cheque size** — i.e. the difference between "a list of networks exists" and "I can see who invests at my stage, in my region, at my cheque size, and how to approach them." Whether that gap is the real bottleneck (versus distribution, trust, or investment-readiness) is the counter-hypothesis carried into [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141) and [#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144).

### The visible co-investment infrastructure presumes the founder has already found private investors

NZGCP's Aspire NZ Seed Fund co-invests alongside private and angel investors. Its own FAQ (Q10, "How much will you invest?") sets the ticket by stage: **"generally limit our investment to $50-100k"** at proof-of-concept, **"we may double that range"** at pre-Seed (so ~$100–200k), and **"generally… in the region of $250-500k"** at Seed [NZGCP Aspire FAQ](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/NZGCP-Aspire-FAQ_Nov22.pdf) (the live Aspire page bot-blocks plain fetchers — details verified via NZGCP's own FAQ PDF; see Sources). My earlier draft said Aspire "typically" writes $250K–$1M and therefore sits *above* the sub-$250k founder; that is wrong on both counts and is corrected here. The FAQ text has no "$1M" figure, and Aspire explicitly invests at proof-of-concept and pre-Seed **inside** the sub-$250k range — so it does not sit above the founder's cheque size.

The point that survives is different and narrower: Aspire is structurally a *matching* vehicle. The FAQ states it "always co-invest[s] with other private investors" and "generally expect[s] to take around 20-25% of an initial investment round" (mandate cap 50%) [NZGCP Aspire FAQ](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/NZGCP-Aspire-FAQ_Nov22.pdf). By design it amplifies capital around a founder who has *already found* private/angel co-investors — it presumes the access problem is solved rather than solving it. That is a co-investment/access observation, not a size-floor one. MBIE's 9 May 2025 briefing notes "regression in the seed funding market due to recent economic conditions" and flags it as informing a review of Aspire policy settings [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital) (dmsdocument bot-blocks plain fetchers; quote verified via search snippet — flagged in Sources). So the government's own record says the seed market has regressed; it does not attribute that to discovery.

### The fragmentation framing is on the record; the causal framing is not

The Startup Advisors Council's **2023 Upstart Nation** report (published via MBIE) states directly that "The UpStart ecosystem in New Zealand can be described as noisy, fragmented, and disconnected", that the ecosystem is "highly fragmented and difficult for founders to navigate", and that "Government support programmes are spread across multiple agencies" — and it proposes a national coordinating body (Accelerate Aotearoa) to reduce fragmentation [Upstart Nation, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/upstart-nation.pdf). This is official, single-source evidence that *fragmentation and navigability* are recognised problems. It is **not** evidence that a founder-investor *discovery marketplace* is the missing piece, nor that access (rather than capital or readiness) is what stops a given founder raising. Keeping that distinction honest is the whole point of the proposed follow-up research.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The whole stream's premise fails if [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141) finds capital scarcity or investment-readiness — not discovery/access — is what actually stops sub-$250k founders raising.** A founder who can't be found is a different problem from a founder who is found and declined, or one who isn't yet fundable. No source in this memo distinguishes those; the high-capital / narrowing-pipeline data is consistent with *any* of them.
- **The build case weakens to a distribution/trust problem, not an information gap, if [#142](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/142)/[#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144) find existing infrastructure (AANZ directory, NZGCP list, Enterprise Angels / its consolidated AngelEquity pathway, equity-crowdfunding platforms) already covers discovery** and founders simply don't use or trust it. AANZ *does* offer a member directory — so "there's nowhere to look" is already partly false.
- **The supply side may not show up.** If NZ investors won't participate in open all-founders/all-investors formats or won't maintain directory entries (adverse selection, time, wholesale-investor marketing rules), an open marketplace is dead regardless of founder demand. This likely needs investor-side human validation, not desk research alone ([#144](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/144)).
- **Unverified specifics I could not pin down:** the exact "1 December 2025" Callaghan cessation date; the current live/active status of AngelEquity; the issue's "~400 startups through the door for ~6 funded" figure for a named pathway (a founder-observed number I did not attempt to source — it belongs in [#141](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/141)).
- **Lived-experience dependency:** the distributional access claims (who is locked out) and the "even insiders can't articulate how a founder gets connected" observation come from the issue author's lived experience. These need corroboration from ecosystem data and from founders/investors directly — flagged as requiring a human with lived experience or domain authority before any solution is designed ([#145](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/145), G1).
