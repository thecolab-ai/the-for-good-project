# Small NZ charities find grants through a patchwork of paid databases, council-mediated free access, funder lists, and volunteer labour; the strongest documented failures are matching, access, paperwork, and capacity

## Executive answer

- Small New Zealand charities and community groups are pointed to a mixed discovery system: paid or subscription databases such as givUS, Fundsorter, GEMS/GEM Local and GrantGuru; free access mediated by libraries or council-sponsored portals; public funder and peak-body link lists; and human support from grant writers, funder advisers, libraries and sector organisations [Philanthropy NZ](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker), [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us), [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/), [GEM Local](https://www.gemlocal.co.nz/), [Rangitikei District Council](https://www.rangitikei.govt.nz/news/2025/grant-guru-launches-for-rangitikei-district-residents).
- The best documented end-to-end seeker workflow is still manual: gather governance and finance documents, plan months ahead, obtain quotes, search/filter databases, build a spreadsheet of funders and deadlines, then tailor different paperwork for different funders [NZ Community Brass Bands Association](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).
- The most defensible failure points are not simply "there is no information"; they are that eligibility matching is split across non-open commercial tools, free access depends on local subscriptions, application paperwork varies by funder, and small/volunteer-led groups lack time and grant-writing skill [Hui E!](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/), [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/), [Stats NZ](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf).
- The affected population is large enough to matter: Charities Services reports 29,208 registered charities and 122,608 charity officers in its 2024/2025 sector snapshot, while Stats NZ's most recent non-profit satellite account report found that 89% of non-profit institutions did not employ staff in 2018 [Charities Services snapshot PDF](https://www.charities.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/102617/Charities-Sector-Snapshot-2024_2025.pdf), [Charities Services research page](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/research-into-charities), [Stats NZ report PDF](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf).
- The claim that small groups miss eligible grants because discovery is hard remains plausible, but not proven by public desk research alone; confirming it needs user interviews, library/database access mapping, and application-log or funder-intake evidence [Community Networks Aotearoa](https://www.communitynetworksaotearoa.org.nz/state-of-the-sector-survey-2024), [Hui E! / Fundsorter](https://www.huie.org.nz/funding-in-aotearoa-what-does-the-data-tell-us/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - channel inventory and friction points are supported by current public sources, but direct demand-side evidence from small charities' lived workflow is thin and mostly comes from practitioner guidance, support programmes, vendor positioning and sector surveys rather than a representative grant-seeker study.

## Evidence

### Scope and affected population

For this finding, "small charity" means the groups closest to Charities Services Tier 4: charities with annual operating payments under $140,000, no public accountability and cash-based accounting [Charities Services](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4), [Charities Services, choosing the right tier](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/which-tier-will-i-use). The broader affected population includes registered charities and unregistered community groups, because givUS explicitly targets communities, volunteer organisations, schools, groups, sports clubs and iwi, while COGS is open to voluntary and not-for-profit organisations working in local communities [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us), [Community Matters](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/community-organisations-grants-scheme).

Charities Services' 2024/2025 snapshot reports 29,208 registered charities and 122,608 charity officers, so the governance and administration burden of grant seeking falls across a large distributed sector [Charities Services snapshot PDF](https://www.charities.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/102617/Charities-Sector-Snapshot-2024_2025.pdf), [Charities Services research page](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/research-into-charities). Stats NZ's most recent non-profit satellite account report found that 89% of non-profit institutions did not employ staff in 2018; Volunteering NZ summarises the same Stats NZ release and notes that these figures exclude informal volunteering [Stats NZ report PDF](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf), [Volunteering NZ](https://www.volunteeringnz.org.nz/news-events/news-post/formal-volunteering-valued-at-4-billion).

### What groups currently use or are pointed to

Philanthropy New Zealand tells fundseekers that it is not a funding organisation and directs them to several discovery and support channels: Fundsorter, Generosity NZ/givUS, Strategic Grants/GEMS, Fundraising Institute Australasia, and Hui E!'s Hoa Putea grant-writing support [Philanthropy NZ](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker). Centre for Social Impact's funding guide similarly lists Fundsorter, Generosity NZ, GEMS/Strategic Grants, Public Trust, Perpetual Guardian and localcommunity.org.nz as places to look for funding information [Centre for Social Impact](https://www.centreforsocialimpact.org.nz/knowledge-base/funding-guide).

givUS is a paid Generosity NZ product for communities, volunteer organisations, schools, groups, sports clubs and iwi; Generosity NZ says it generates personalised quotes and that most council libraries subscribe on behalf of ratepayers, enabling free public access [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us). Generosity NZ also publishes "where to access" pages listing partner access points, including council and library entries; this supports a mediated-access model, but not a quantified measure of national coverage or service quality [Generosity NZ, where to find access](https://generosity.org.nz/where-to-find-access), [Generosity NZ, where to access](https://generosity.org.nz/where-to-access-).

Fundsorter positions itself for NZ charities and community groups that need matching and application drafting: its homepage says it helps users find grants and draft applications, its pricing page says plans start from $16/month, and its product copy targets both the experienced grants manager who wants broader coverage and the community leader who does not know where to start [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/), [Fundsorter pricing](https://www.fundsorter.com/pricing). Because Fundsorter is a vendor, I treat its claim that it surfaces opportunities users did not know to search for as corroborating product evidence of the search problem, not as independent proof that small groups miss grants [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/).

Strategic Grants' GEM Local is designed for small New Zealand charitable and community organisations, provides a customised grants calendar matched by activity, geography and legal status, tracks applications and report deadlines, and says its research team updates NZ grants daily [GEM Local](https://www.gemlocal.co.nz/). Council-sponsored portals create another free-but-local route: Ruapehu District Council provides eligible local community groups with free access to GEM Local, and Rangitikei District Council launched a free GrantGuru-powered grant finder for local residents, groups and businesses [Ruapehu GEM Local](https://ruapehudc.gemlocal.co.nz/), [Rangitikei District Council](https://www.rangitikei.govt.nz/news/2025/grant-guru-launches-for-rangitikei-district-residents).

Funder and sector link lists remain part of the workflow. The Tindall Foundation maintains an "other funding resources" page that points users to community trusts, government funds, corporate foundations, community foundations and funding databases, while DOC and Creative NZ each publish other-funder lists for their sectors [Tindall Foundation](https://tindall.org.nz/other-funding-resources/), [Department of Conservation](https://www.doc.govt.nz/get-involved/funding/other-funding-organisations/), [Creative New Zealand](https://creativenz.govt.nz/funding-and-support/all-opportunities/other-sources-of-funding-and-support).

### Documented workflow evidence

The clearest public seeker-side workflow I found is the NZ Community Brass Bands Association guide, which is a practitioner guide rather than a representative survey [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/). It advises groups to gather bank verification, financial statements, charity and incorporation documents, committee details and minutes; to obtain at least two competitive quotes because many funders require them; to use givUS via a local library where possible; to record funders, website links, deadlines and grant limits in a spreadsheet; and to tailor applications to different funders' criteria and paperwork [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).

That guide also identifies high-friction moments in plain workflow terms: finding the right place to apply is daunting because criteria vary, givUS may require hunting through a library catalogue, database filtering saves many hours of manual searching, and multiple applications require slightly different paperwork for each provider [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/). This is direct evidence of the process small groups may follow, but it is one sector-specific guide, so it should be treated as a strong lead rather than a population-level measure [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).

Hui E!'s Hoa Putea Grant Writing Support kaupapa is indirect but important workflow evidence because it exists to solve the application-capability gap for small grassroots or fully voluntary groups; Hui E! says the programme supports first applications of $5,000-$50,000 for groups under $2 million revenue or fully voluntary groups, and its review says grant writing is time consuming and requires skills that volunteer and small community organisations do not necessarily have [Hui E!](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/).

### Where the workflow breaks down

**1. Matching appears fragmented and non-open, but the seeker impact is an inference to test.** Fundsorter says it has 3,361 opportunities and 1,650 funders on its homepage, its separate data analysis describes almost 2,000 contestable opportunities, DOC says givUS has over 1,200 grants and schemes, and GEMS markets itself as a comprehensive grants database without publishing comparable counts [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/), [Hui E! / Fundsorter](https://www.huie.org.nz/funding-in-aotearoa-what-does-the-data-tell-us/), [Department of Conservation](https://www.doc.govt.nz/get-involved/funding/other-funding-organisations/), [Strategic Grants](https://www.strategicgrants.co.nz/gems/). These product counts are vendor or directory claims and are not directly comparable; they support a hypothesis that grantseekers face coverage and freshness uncertainty, but public desk research did not prove how often that uncertainty causes missed grants [Hui E! / Fundsorter](https://www.huie.org.nz/funding-in-aotearoa-what-does-the-data-tell-us/), [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).

**2. Free access depends on local intermediaries.** givUS is free to many users because council libraries subscribe, and Generosity NZ's access pages point users to partner councils and libraries rather than to an open national dataset [Generosity NZ](https://www.generosity.org.nz/giv-us), [Generosity NZ, where to find access](https://generosity.org.nz/where-to-find-access). Council-sponsored GrantGuru or GEM Local instances show the same pattern: free database access exists for Rangitikei or Ruapehu users because those councils bought or partnered for it, not because the data is universally open [Rangitikei District Council](https://www.rangitikei.govt.nz/news/2025/grant-guru-launches-for-rangitikei-district-residents), [Ruapehu GEM Local](https://ruapehudc.gemlocal.co.nz/).

**3. Application work remains manual after discovery.** NZCBA's guide tells groups to build their own spreadsheet, print or upload repeated documents, monitor expected response dates, and handle follow-through requirements after funds are granted [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/). GEM Local's features - application tracking, report-deadline tracking, deadline alerts, tip sheets, videos and writing samples - are also evidence that discovery alone does not complete the grant workflow [GEM Local](https://www.gemlocal.co.nz/).

**4. Volunteer capacity is a binding constraint.** Hui E! explicitly frames grant writing as time-consuming skilled work that small and volunteer organisations may lack, and Stats NZ found that most non-profit institutions had no paid staff in 2018 [Hui E!](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/), [Stats NZ report PDF](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf). Community Networks Aotearoa's 2024 State of the Sector report states that 77% of surveyed organisations had experienced increased demand for services or activities; NZ Navigator Trust's summary repeats the same report figure, so this is single-origin survey evidence rather than independent corroboration [Community Networks Aotearoa report PDF](https://irp.cdn-website.com/96a27c73/files/uploaded/State_of_The_Sector_Report_2024_-_Community_Networks_Aotearoa.pdf), [NZ Navigator Trust](https://nzntrust.org.nz/news/state-of-the-sector-2024-report).

**5. The missing evidence is behavioural, not just documentary.** Public sources show the tools and support ecosystem, but I did not find a representative NZ survey asking small charities which grant-discovery channels they use, how many eligible grants they miss, or where they abandon the process [Community Networks Aotearoa](https://www.communitynetworksaotearoa.org.nz/state-of-the-sector-survey-2024), [Community Research](https://communityresearch.org.nz/research/state-of-volunteering-in-aotearoa-new-zealand-report-2024/). That gap means the strongest conclusion is "these are the highest-friction steps to test", not "these steps have been quantified nationally" [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).

## What would change this conclusion

- A representative survey of small NZ charities and community groups asking which tools they use, how they heard about them, what they pay, and where they abandon applications would move the workflow findings from Medium to High confidence.
- A council-by-council access audit of givUS, GEM Local and GrantGuru would test whether local mediation is a major barrier or a minor edge case.
- Funder application logs showing many incomplete or abandoned applications at eligibility, document-gathering or submission stages would identify the highest-friction step more directly than vendor and practitioner descriptions.
- Interviews with Tier 4 charity officers, library staff, DIA Community Advisors, community trust staff and Hui E! grant writers are needed to confirm whether groups miss grants because they never discover them, misread eligibility, run out of time, or decide the likely grant is not worth the effort.
- I found Generosity NZ partner access pages, but could not verify an open coverage audit showing which councils provide givUS, GEM Local or GrantGuru access, whether access is remote or in-library only, and whether non-ratepayers can use it. I also could not verify a public NZ dataset that measures grant-discovery behaviour by small charities.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which councils and libraries provide free givUS access, what kind of access do they provide, and which councils sponsor GEM Local or GrantGuru portals?
- What is the median time cost of one grant application for a volunteer-led group, split by discovery, eligibility checking, document gathering, writing, submission and reporting?
- Which funder eligibility fields are hardest for small groups to interpret: geography, legal status, donee status, charitable purpose, project type, contribution limits, or quote/reporting rules?
- Do small groups under-apply because they do not discover grants, because they discover them too late, or because the application/reporting burden exceeds the expected value?
