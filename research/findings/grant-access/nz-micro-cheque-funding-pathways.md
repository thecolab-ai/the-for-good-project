# NZ's $10k-$200k startup cheques are real but only partly discoverable

## Executive answer

- The knowable part of the $10k-$200k NZ startup-cheque market is mainly formal angel-network syndication: Catalist's 2025 angel report says its dataset comes from six active Angel Association New Zealand member groups, with $13.9m invested across 167 group-level deals and 455 active angels, while AANZ says its members include angel networks, venture providers, equity-crowdfunding platforms, and investor-led tech incubators. [Catalist 2025 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf); [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/)
- The individual-angel segment outside formal groups is structurally undercounted: an MBIE baseline review found no legal requirement or standard process for collecting angel-investment statistics, said angel investment was difficult to measure because it mostly occurs outside the public arena, and said informal investment activity was unlikely to be captured because it is covert; an EU business-angel-market evaluation describes the same non-network segment as the "non-measured" market and says individual angel activity is private and extremely difficult to measure. [MBIE baseline review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/2264-baseline-review-angel-investment-report-pdf); [European Commission/CSES evaluation](https://ec.europa.eu/docsroom/documents/7862/attachments/1/translations/en/renditions/pdf)
- A first-time founder can find several doors, but few are good matches for "I need $10k-$200k in my region at my stage": AANZ has a founder-facing member directory, NZGCP has investor and funding lists, Enterprise Angels has a founder "Raise" pathway, Snowball publishes live/private-company offers and a raising-capital route, PledgeMe is a crowdfunding platform, and government pages list R&D, deep-tech, Māori, and Pacific business funding. [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/); [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors); [NZGCP funding](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/funding); [Enterprise Angels](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/); [Snowball](https://www.snowballeffect.co.nz/); [PledgeMe](https://pledgeme.co.nz/); [MBIE RDTI](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/r-d-tax-incentive); [Callaghan Deep Tech Incubators](https://www.callaghaninnovation.govt.nz/products/products/deep-tech-incubators/); [TPK Maori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)
- The main directory opportunity is not "make the first list"; it is "turn scattered public pages and opt-in funder data into a maintained, machine-readable match layer" with fields for cheque range, stage, region, sector, eligibility, investor type, founder-facing route, application status, and last verification. This conclusion follows from the current source shape: AANZ and NZGCP publish human-facing lists, Snowball and PledgeMe expose offers, FMA maintains licence/obligation data for crowdfunding providers, and NZBN/Companies Office APIs can keep organisation identity fields current. [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/); [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors); [FMA crowdfunding](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/); [NZBN API](https://www.nzbn.govt.nz/using-the-nzbn/nzbn-services/api/); [Companies Office APIs](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/using-our-data-through-apis/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium -- the formal-network and public-pathway map is well sourced, but the off-network individual/family-office/founder-angel segment is intentionally private and cannot be quantified from public desk research.

## Evidence

### Who writes the $10k-$200k cheques

Formal angel networks are the clearest visible source. AANZ says its member base includes angel networks, venture-capital providers, equity-crowdfunding platforms, and investor-led tech incubators, and its site offers a "Find a Startup Investor" member directory. [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/) NZGCP's investor resource says AANZ represents formal angel networks across the country with memberships ranging from 20 to 200 individuals, which implies many company cheques are syndicated from multiple small individual tickets rather than written by one named investor. [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors)

Catalist's 2025 annual angel report is the best current public measurement found for this formal-network channel, but its coverage is partial by design: the report says it is based on detailed transaction data from six active NZ angel investment groups that are AANZ members, plus investments made directly through Catalist alongside those groups, and that it does not cover venture-capital funds, other pooled investment groups, or individuals investing outside those angel groups. [Catalist 2025 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf) The same report gives $13.9m invested in 2025, 167 completed group-level deals, and 455 active angels. [Catalist 2025 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf) A rough group-level average is about $83k per deal ($13.9m / 167), which sits inside the issue's $10k-$200k band; this is only an average of angel-group deal events and does not reveal the distribution, company round size, or off-network capital. [Catalist 2025 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/2025+Annual+New+Zealand+Angel+Market+Report.pdf)

The same formal channel is usually investor-led and gated. Enterprise Angels describes itself as connecting startup business owners with angel investors, and its AngelEquity page says the online platform was created to attract wholesale investors and syndicate with other angel networks before being consolidated into Enterprise Angels; the same page reports about $3m invested via AngelEquity during its three-year standalone period and $22m invested by Enterprise Angels investors in 40 startups over that same period. [Enterprise Angels](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/); [Enterprise Angels AngelEquity](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/angelequity/) Angel HQ describes itself as Wellington's angel-investing network and "the meeting place for ambitious startups and passionate investors," which confirms a regional angel-network route but does not publish machine-readable cheque-size filters. [Angel HQ](https://www.angelhq.co.nz/)

Individual angels outside networks, founder-angels, family offices, corporates, and informal syndicates are known to exist but are not cleanly countable from public data. NZGCP's Aspire FAQ says Aspire co-invests with private investors including angel funds/networks, high-net-worth individuals, venture-capital funds, family offices, corporates, institutional investors, and increasingly offshore investors. [NZGCP Aspire FAQ](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/NZGCP-Aspire-FAQ_Nov22.pdf) The MBIE baseline review explains the NZ measurement problem: it says there was no legal requirement or standard process for collecting angel-investment statistics, angel investment predominantly occurred outside the public arena, informal investment was unlikely to be captured because it is covert, and the review's sample was assembled through a snowball approach from known angels. [MBIE baseline review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/2264-baseline-review-angel-investment-report-pdf) An official European Commission/CSES evaluation gives independent support for the general structural pattern: it says the business-angel market is fundamentally informal, many angels want anonymity, non-members of business-angel networks are a "non-measured" market, and the visible data collected from angel groups and networks represents only a fraction of the market. [European Commission/CSES evaluation](https://ec.europa.eu/docsroom/documents/7862/attachments/1/translations/en/renditions/pdf)

Equity-crowdfunding and wholesale-investment platforms are a second visible cheque path, but they are platforms rather than angels. Snowball says it provides simplified access to managed funds and private companies, publishes "Explore companies" and "Raising capital" routes, and says it has helped more than 100 high-growth Kiwi businesses raise more than $200m since 2014; on 2026-07-03 its homepage data exposed two featured company offers, one live offer with about $10.4m already raised and one preview offer with no raised amount shown, so this research does not treat Snowball as a $10k-$200k-specific seed directory. [Snowball](https://www.snowballeffect.co.nz/) PledgeMe describes itself as New Zealand's first equity-crowdfunding platform and says it helps Kiwis fund projects and equity-based campaigns. [PledgeMe](https://pledgeme.co.nz/) The FMA's crowdfunding page confirms licensed providers are regulated, says Equitise's crowdfunding licence was cancelled effective 3 April 2024, and sets obligations including annual regulatory returns and fair-dealing rules. [FMA crowdfunding](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/)

Public and quasi-public programmes can sit in or around the sub-$250k founder path, although many are not equity cheques. NZGCP Aspire invests at proof-of-concept, seed, and early-expansion stages, says it always looks to crowd in private capital, and its FAQ says it typically co-invests with private investors rather than replacing them. [NZGCP Aspire](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund); [NZGCP Aspire FAQ](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/NZGCP-Aspire-FAQ_Nov22.pdf) MBIE's R&D Tax Incentive is a 15% credit with a $50,000 minimum R&D expenditure threshold and $120m cap on eligible expenditure, which is discoverable and official but not an upfront investor cheque. [MBIE RDTI](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/r-d-tax-incentive) Callaghan's Deep Tech Incubator page says successful applicants get a minimum of $1m in funding, including a $750,000 repayable grant, so it is important for deep-tech founders but mostly above the sub-$250k pathway this issue asks about. [Callaghan Deep Tech Incubators](https://www.callaghaninnovation.govt.nz/products/products/deep-tech-incubators/)

Regional public support is more visible as advice and capability routing than as a dedicated equity-cheque path. WellingtonNZ's Regional Business Partner Network page says RBPN is funded by the New Zealand Government, made up of 14 regional growth agencies nationwide, and helps small and medium businesses connect to advice, people, resources, and access to funding/resources after sign-up. [WellingtonNZ RBPN](https://www.wellingtonnz.com/business/grow-and-innovate-your-business/regional-business-partner-network)

Māori and Pacific business funding is visible through official public pages, but it is not a simple angel-cheque substitute. Te Puni Kokiri's Māori Development Fund page says Budget 2025 allocated $40.21m per annum from 1 July 2025, that eligible legal entities can include limited-liability companies, and that proposals should contact a regional office first; it also says the fund excludes purchase of shares and business-as-usual costs. [TPK Maori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund) The Ministry for Pacific Peoples' Tauola Business Fund page says the fund supports Pacific businesses to foster growth, create employment, and build resilience, but the same page says applications are now closed. [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)

### What a machine-readable directory could use as source data

The starting dataset should be organisation-level, not person-level, because public investor identity data is incomplete and individual investors may not have consented to be listed. AANZ's member directory, NZGCP's investor/funding pages, FMA crowdfunding-provider information, Snowball/PledgeMe offer pages, Enterprise Angels/Angel HQ founder pages, TPK/MPP/MBIE/Callaghan programme pages, and NZBN/Companies Office organisation records are all legitimate public seeds for organisation entries. [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/); [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors); [FMA crowdfunding](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/); [Snowball](https://www.snowballeffect.co.nz/); [PledgeMe](https://pledgeme.co.nz/); [Enterprise Angels](https://www.enterpriseangels.co.nz/); [Angel HQ](https://www.angelhq.co.nz/); [TPK Maori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/); [MBIE RDTI](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/funding-information-and-opportunities/r-d-tax-incentive); [Callaghan Deep Tech Incubators](https://www.callaghaninnovation.govt.nz/products/products/deep-tech-incubators/); [NZBN API](https://www.nzbn.govt.nz/using-the-nzbn/nzbn-services/api/); [Companies Office APIs](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/using-our-data-through-apis/)

The minimum useful schema is: organisation name, URL, NZBN/company identifier where available, pathway type, cheque range, stage, region, sector focus, founder eligibility, investor eligibility, open/closed status, application/contact route, whether a warm introduction is required, whether the instrument is equity/grant/repayable grant/tax credit/debt, last-seen source URL, and last human verification date. This is an inference from the missing fields across the current public pages: NZGCP and AANZ provide lists and categories, government pages provide eligibility and status, platforms expose offers, and NZBN/Companies Office can maintain entity identity but none of those sources alone provides cheque-size matching. [AANZ members](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/members/); [NZGCP start-up investors](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/start-up-resources/start-up-investors); [NZBN API](https://www.nzbn.govt.nz/using-the-nzbn/nzbn-services/api/)

Freshness would need three mechanisms: scheduled source checks for public pages, opt-in funder self-service for non-public fields such as current cheque range and sector appetite, and visible stale-data labels when an entry has not been confirmed recently. NZBN's API supports watchlists and change notifications for business information, and the FMA page says licensed crowdfunding providers must submit annual regulatory returns, but neither source can keep qualitative founder-matching fields current without funder participation. [NZBN API](https://www.nzbn.govt.nz/using-the-nzbn/nzbn-services/api/); [FMA crowdfunding](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/)

## What would change this conclusion

- The off-network conclusion would change if AANZ, NZGCP, Catalist, a platform operator, or a credible researcher had a current anonymised dataset covering solo angels, family offices, founder-angels, and informal syndicates outside formal angel groups.
- The discoverability rating would improve if AANZ, NZGCP, Snowball, PledgeMe, incubators, or regional networks published structured fields for cheque range, stage, sector, region, application status, and founder route in a downloadable or API-accessible format.
- The directory case would weaken if founder interviews or conversion data showed that first-time founders can already identify and approach the right sub-$250k pathway but fail mainly because of investment-readiness, sector fit, or capital scarcity.
- I could not verify a public NZ-wide list of individual founder-angels, family offices, or solo angels willing to receive cold approaches; this likely needs investor-side human validation and consent.
- I could not verify a current, dedicated regional startup-equity fund directory below $250k beyond regional angel networks and Regional Business Partner-style advice/resource routing.
- I did not verify live conversion rates, response rates, founder outcomes, or the current activity level of every listed pathway; those require platform/network data that is not public.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which fields would NZ angel networks and platforms be willing to maintain publicly without creating spam, adverse selection, or regulatory risk?
- What proportion of founders using AANZ/NZGCP/platform pathways receive a useful referral or investor conversation?
- Which off-network investors would consent to organisation-level or pseudonymous listing by cheque range, sector, and region?
