# Small NZ charities find grants through a patchwork — a handful of commercial databases, funder link lists, and volunteer labour — and the process breaks down on cost, fragmented access, duplicated paperwork, and capacity

## Executive answer

- **There is no official or universal channel.** The discovery layer small charities are actually pointed to consists of commercial databases — givUS (Generosity New Zealand), Fundsorter, Strategic Grants' GEMS/GEM Local, and GrantsGuru — plus a handful of free partial directories and *curated link lists* hosted by funders, peak bodies, and government agencies. The sector's own peak body for funders (Philanthropy New Zealand) is explicit that it is "not a funding organisation" and refers fundseekers to exactly these tools [PNZ Fundseeker](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker).
- **The main free route is a library card.** givUS is subscription-only, but "most council libraries subscribe to givUS on behalf of ratepayers, which enables FREE public access" (vendor's claim, corroborated by library pages and practitioner guides) [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us). This makes free access to the incumbent funding database a function of which council you live under — and practitioners report the library route itself "can take some hunting to track it down" [NZCBA guide](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).
- **After discovery, the workflow is manual.** The most detailed practitioner account found (a volunteer-written guide for community bands) describes the standard path: assemble a reusable document pack, search givUS at the library, build your own spreadsheet of funders/deadlines/limits, then tailor paperwork per funder — with six-month lead times and two competitive quotes per item [NZCBA guide](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/).
- **The breakdown points are structural, not informational gaps a pamphlet can fix:** (1) paywalls and opaque pricing; (2) council-dependent free access; (3) no open-licensed dataset of NZ grants (data.govt.nz searches return only distribution-side data — §1), so even the databases disagree on how many opportunities exist (from "over 1,200" to 3,361); (4) grant-writing time and skill that volunteer-run groups don't have — the reason Hui E! runs a volunteer grant-writer matching programme; and (5) capacity: 89% of NZ's ~115,770 non-profits have **no paid staff at all** [Stats NZ NPISA 2018](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf).
- **The outcome is highly concentrated:** "Nine per cent of charities receive 91 per cent of philanthropic and grant-maker giving, meaning 91 per cent of charities get 9 per cent" [PNZ/JBWere fact sheet](https://primrose-ranunculus-tm9g.squarespace.com/s/PNZ_JB-Were-Fact-Sheet.pdf). Discovery friction is not the only cause, but it lands hardest on the volunteer-run majority.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the *channel inventory* is well-evidenced (vendor pages, peak-body and funder referral lists, government pages, all current). The *breakdown analysis* rests on a smaller demand-side evidence base: no NZ-wide survey directly measures how charities search for grants (the sector's longitudinal surveys measure funding adequacy, not discovery behaviour — itself a finding).

## Evidence

### 1. What charities are pointed to: the referral canon

The same short list of tools appears wherever a NZ community group asks "where do I find funding?":

**Philanthropy New Zealand (the funders' peak body)** maintains a "Fundseeker" page that refers grantseekers to: Fundsorter, Generosity NZ/givUS ("often available free through public libraries"), Strategic Grants (GEMS), the Fundraising Institute Australasia, and Hui E!'s Hoa Pūtea grant-writing support [PNZ Fundseeker](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker) (accessed 2 Jul 2026).

**Funders themselves host link lists.** The Tindall Foundation's "Other Funding" page is a representative map of the whole discovery landscape: 10 regional community trusts, 13 philanthropic family foundations, 12 government funds and ministries, 11 corporate foundations, 20 community foundations (counts scripted from page headings, 2 Jul 2026), and a closing "Funding Databases & Directories" section listing the DIA gaming-trusts directory (granted.govt.nz/societies), the Energy Trusts of NZ member list, Generosity NZ ("subscription-based, with free access through public libraries"), Fundsorter, the Local Community Trust Directory (localcommunity.org.nz), and Citizens Advice Bureau [Tindall Foundation](https://tindall.org.nz/other-funding-resources/) (accessed 2 Jul 2026). The Department of Conservation [DOC funding page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/get-involved/funding/other-funding-organisations/) and [Creative NZ](https://creativenz.govt.nz/funding-and-support/all-opportunities/other-sources-of-funding-and-support) publish equivalent lists for their sectors.

**Government's own front door is an application portal, not a search tool.** DIA's Community Matters is "a community funding hub for Lottery, Crown and Trust funding" where groups log in to *apply* for the funds DIA administers (Lottery, COGS) [Community Matters](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/); it does not search across the wider funder landscape. Nor does open data fill the gap: reproducible searches of the data.govt.nz catalogue (CKAN API, `package_search`, run 2 July 2026) return only grant *distribution* datasets — `grants` (264 results, all recipient/paid data such as COGS grant records and Class 4 grants paid), `grant eligibility` (7, none relevant), `grant funders` (1, a geology dataset) [data.govt.nz CKAN API](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=grants) — and Charities Services' open data covers charities' own filings, not funding opportunities [Charities Services, Open data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data/). (The companion finding for issue #5, PR #11 — under review, not yet merged — documents this landscape in full.)

### 2. The commercial databases

**givUS (Generosity New Zealand)** — the incumbent, formerly FundView. Vendor describes it as covering "grants and schemes for communities, volunteer organisations, schools, groups, sport clubs and Iwi"; pricing is quote-based ("we generate personalised quotes"), and the vendor states "Most council libraries subscribe to givUS on behalf of ratepayers, which enables FREE public access" [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us) (accessed 2 Jul 2026). DOC's referral page describes it as "over 1,200 grants and schemes" [DOC](https://www.doc.govt.nz/get-involved/funding/other-funding-organisations/). Library examples of the free channel: Marlborough District Libraries walk users through accessing givUS with a library login [Marlborough Libraries](https://www.marlboroughlibraries.govt.nz/about-the-library/news?item=id:2f8r17q2v1cxbyg85tk7); Selwyn Libraries lists Generosity NZ among its e-resources [Selwyn Libraries](https://www.selwynlibraries.co.nz/booksandresources/eresources/generosity-nz-funding).

**Fundsorter** — the newer entrant (flagged by a community member on issue #2 as "about a year old"). Its homepage stat block states **3,361 funding opportunities / 1,650 funders** (deterministic extraction from page HTML, 2 Jul 2026; same figures verified for the issue #5 finding) [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/). Entry pricing for the smallest organisations is $16/month billed annually ($192/year) (verified for issue #5, 1 Jul 2026). PNZ describes it as an algorithm that "analyses over 50 factors across nearly 3,000 funding opportunities" and then "with one click, they draft your application" [PNZ Fundseeker](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker). Fundsorter's positioning confirms the incumbent workflow's failure mode from the supply side: "Your current database tells you what you ask it. Fundsorter surfaces what you didn't know to search for" [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/).

**Strategic Grants (GEMS / GEM Local)** — a trans-Tasman grants consultancy whose Grants Expertise Management System provides customised grants calendars, plus workshops and application-writing services [PNZ Fundseeker](https://www.philanthropy.org.nz/fundseeker); GEMS is marketed as "New Zealand's largest and most current grants database" (no counts published) [Strategic Grants](https://www.strategicgrants.co.nz/gems/). Its small-organisation tier, GEM Local, is "designed specifically to help New Zealand community organisations and small non-profits find the right grants for them" [GEM Local](https://www.gemlocal.co.nz/).

**GrantsGuru and council-sponsored free portals** — GrantsGuru ($228/yr, "database focused on council portals" per Fundsorter's pricing comparison [Fundsorter pricing](https://www.fundsorter.com/pricing)) and GEM Local both run **council-sponsored instances free to local residents**: Rangitīkei District Council launched a GrantGuru-powered "Grant Finder" in March 2025 [Rangitīkei DC](https://www.rangitikei.govt.nz/news/2025/grant-guru-launches-for-rangitikei-district-residents), and Ruapehu District Council sponsors a GEM Local instance accessed with a free code [ruapehudc.gemlocal.co.nz](https://ruapehudc.gemlocal.co.nz/). This is a second postcode-dependent free channel alongside library givUS access: whether a small group gets a free grants database depends on what its council has bought. (Also in vendor comparisons: Go Gravy, a success-fee service that "finds and applies for NZ grants on your behalf" [Fundsorter pricing](https://www.fundsorter.com/pricing) — verified only as described by a competitor.)

Free partial alternatives: localcommunity.org.nz, "an online directory listing a wide range of community funders, trusts, and grantmakers across Aotearoa New Zealand" [Tindall Foundation](https://tindall.org.nz/other-funding-resources/); [localcommunity.org.nz](https://www.localcommunity.org.nz/view/communities/) (URL live, 2 July 2026); and the trust-company portals — Perpetual Guardian's funding hub and Public Trust's grantseeker portal, each listing "current and upcoming funding rounds" only for the trusts that company administers [Tindall Foundation](https://tindall.org.nz/other-funding-resources/); [Public Trust grants search](https://www.publictrust.co.nz/grants/?query=&type=organisation). Public Trust's portal is machine-readable in practice: its public search index returns 69 structured grant records including eligibility/exclusion text (queried directly, 2 July 2026; documented in PR #11).

### 3. The human-labour layer

Discovery tools only get a group to the paperwork. A visible ecosystem of human support exists precisely because the paperwork defeats volunteer-run groups:

- **Hui E! Hoa Pūtea Grant Writing Support** matches skilled volunteer grant writers with "small grassroots groups with a revenue of under $2 million dollars or [that] are fully voluntary run", supporting a group's *first* application of $5,000–$50,000. Its own programme review states the core problem plainly: "Grant writing is time consuming and requires a particular skillset that volunteer and small community organisations do not necessarily have" [Hui E! review](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/) ([review PDF](https://www.huie.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/Hui-E-Hoa-Putea-Review-2022.pdf)).
- **Paid grant writers and consultants**: e.g. FundingNZ advertises application-writing across the major gaming trusts and Lottery [FundingNZ](https://www.fundingnz.co.nz/communitygrants); FundingHQ sells a fundraising-capability platform aimed at organisations "looking to raise funds $50,000 or more" (as described by Creative NZ) [Creative NZ](https://creativenz.govt.nz/funding-and-support/all-opportunities/other-sources-of-funding-and-support), [FundingHQ](https://funding-hq.com/).
- **Training**: Exult runs sector workshops on "Sustainable Funding" for community organisations [Exult](https://www.exult.co.nz/workshops/sustainable-funding-2-hour/).
- **Generalist navigation**: Citizens Advice Bureau appears on funder referral lists as a free information service covering funding access [Tindall Foundation](https://tindall.org.nz/other-funding-resources/).

### 4. The practitioner workflow, end to end

The clearest end-to-end account found is the NZ Community Brass Bands Association's volunteer-written funding guide [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/) (accessed 2 Jul 2026). Its described workflow:

1. Build a reusable document pack (bank verification, financials "preferably audited", charity registration number, incorporation certificate, committee contacts, minutes).
2. Plan **at least six months ahead** ("grant organisations with larger limits generally meet less often").
3. Gather **at least two competitive quotes per item** ("a requirement for most grants").
4. Search givUS — "Unfortunately GivUs and GivMe are both pay-to-access, but there is a way around it. Many councils have subscriptions so you can access it via your local library… though it can take some hunting to track it down."
5. Build your own spreadsheet of funders, closing dates, and grant-size limits.
6. Tailor each application to each funder's criteria — "the tedious part, simply from the perspective of organising slightly different sets of paperwork for each funding provider."

The guide's summary of what the database saves you — "many, many hours of googling and perusing funding criteria" — is the clearest available statement of what discovery costs without one.

### 5. Where it breaks down

**(a) Cost and opaque pricing.** Both national databases are paid products: Fundsorter from $192/year for the smallest organisations; givUS by "personalised quote" with no published price [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us). For fully-volunteer groups, any subscription competes with programme spending.

**(b) Free access is a postcode lottery.** The free route to givUS depends on the local council's library subscribing. The vendor says "most" do [Generosity NZ](https://generosity.org.nz/giv-us); no public list of subscribing councils was found, and the practitioner guide notes the resource is hard to even locate in library catalogues [NZCBA](https://nzcba.org/resources/funding/). The same pattern repeats with the newer council-sponsored GrantGuru/GEM Local portals (Rangitīkei, Ruapehu — section 2): free database access exists only where a council has bought it. Which councils' residents get free access, through either channel, is unverified — flagged below as a follow-up.

**(c) No ground truth on coverage or freshness.** Because there is no open-licensed dataset of NZ grants (data.govt.nz CKAN searches return only distribution-side data — §1; documented in full in PR #11), the commercial databases cannot be audited and their own counts diverge: DOC describes givUS as "over 1,200 grants and schemes" [DOC](https://www.doc.govt.nz/get-involved/funding/other-funding-organisations/); Fundsorter's analysis published via Hui E! covered "almost 2,000 funding opportunities" [Hui E!](https://www.huie.org.nz/funding-in-aotearoa-what-does-the-data-tell-us/); Fundsorter's live homepage now claims 3,361 [Fundsorter](https://www.fundsorter.com/). Hui E!'s framing of that analysis concedes the baseline: "Up until now, very little public data has been available on what funding is available in New Zealand, and without good data, we can't make good decisions" [Hui E!](https://www.huie.org.nz/funding-in-aotearoa-what-does-the-data-tell-us/).

**(d) Time and skill burden on volunteers.** Hui E!'s programme review: grant writing "is time consuming and requires a particular skillset that volunteer and small community organisations do not necessarily have"; supported applicants said external writers freed "so many extra hours" [Hui E! review](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/). This sits inside a worsening macro picture: the 2024 State of the Sector survey (biennial, run by Community Networks Aotearoa/ComVoices) reports "a more competitive funding environment", demand up for 77% of organisations, and respondents saying "Funding is the hardest issue we are facing. Always begging for every cent" [SOTS 2024](https://www.communitynetworksaotearoa.org.nz/state-of-the-sector-survey-2024) ([report PDF](https://irp.cdn-website.com/96a27c73/files/uploaded/State_of_The_Sector_Report_2024_09.10.24_Master_Export.pdf)).
Notably, the survey instrument does not ask *how* organisations find funding: a deterministic search of the published 2024 questionnaire ([SSS24 Questions PDF](https://irp.cdn-website.com/96a27c73/files/uploaded/SSS24_Questions.pdf)) finds funding-*adequacy* questions (Q18–22, Q34) but zero questions on discovery methods, databases, or search tools. The sector's main longitudinal dataset measures funding adequacy, not discovery. No NZ survey of grant-discovery behaviour was found.

**(e) Most of the sector has nobody to do this work.** Stats NZ's Non-Profit Institutions Satellite Account (the most recent edition, 2018) counted 115,770 non-profit institutions and found **89% did not employ staff** — entirely volunteer-run ("in 2018, 89 percent of NPIs did not employ staff. This was 1 percentage point lower than in 2013 (90 percent)"; Table 6: 103,272 NPIs with zero paid employees vs 12,508 employing) [Stats NZ NPISA 2018, Employment numbers + Table 6](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Reports/Non-profit-institutions-satellite-account-2018/non-profit-institution-satellite-account-2018.pdf). (An earlier draft of this finding quoted 73% from the report's *Health activity group* section — corrected after adversarial review; the whole-sector figure is 89%.) Eligibility thresholds in the support ecosystem confirm "small" is the norm, not the exception: COGS requires under $2M annual operating expenditure [Community Matters COGS](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/community-organisations-grants-scheme); Hoa Pūtea targets under $2M revenue or fully-voluntary groups [Hui E! review](https://www.huie.org.nz/review-of-the-hoa-putea-grant-writing-support-kaupapa/).

**(f) The observable outcome is extreme concentration.** "Nine per cent of charities receive 91 per cent of philanthropic and grant-maker giving, meaning 91 per cent of charities get 9 per cent" — Philanthropy NZ's fact sheet summarising the JBWere NZ Support Report [PNZ/JBWere fact sheet](https://primrose-ranunculus-tm9g.squarespace.com/s/PNZ_JB-Were-Fact-Sheet.pdf). The report itself states "Fundraising is dominated by the largest organisations with the largest 9% of charities receiving 91% of all donations" — JBWere's live URLs have since rotted (404, 2 July 2026), so the report is cited via its Internet Archive snapshot [JBWere NZ Support Report, Feb 2020 (archived PDF)](https://web.archive.org/web/20230406140035/https://www.jbwere.co.nz/media/1qudxw3q/jbwere-nz-support-report-digital.pdf) (stat verified in the archived PDF text). Note this is **one underlying analysis**, not two independent sources — flagged in the claims table. FundingHQ's marketing makes the same point ("the vast majority of available funding goes to less than 10% of charities leaving most under-resourced and at risk" [FundingHQ](https://funding-hq.com/)) — though this likely derives from the same JBWere analysis, so it corroborates currency of the claim, not independence. Concentration has many causes (grant size, capability, relationships); discovery friction is one contributor, not proven to be the dominant one.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A demand-side survey.** The biggest gap in this finding is direct usage data: everything here shows what charities are *pointed to* and what practitioners *describe*, not measured behaviour (e.g. what % of small charities have used givUS or Fundsorter, or rely on word of mouth). A survey by Charities Services, Hui E!, or a funder would materially strengthen or overturn the breakdown analysis.
- **givUS subscriber and coverage data.** If Generosity NZ published its scheme count, update cadence, and the list of subscribing libraries/councils, points (b) and (c) could be verified or refuted.
- **A newer Non-Profit Institutions Satellite Account.** The 89%-volunteer-run figure is from 2018; the whole-sector trend 2013→2018 moved only one percentage point (90%→89%), so it is likely still broadly right — but a newer edition would confirm.
- **Evidence that concentration is driven by something other than discovery/capability** (e.g. deliberate funder strategy toward fewer, larger grants) would weaken the implicit link between discovery friction and the 9/91 outcome. The JBWere concentration figure also uses 2020-era data.
- **A human with sector lived experience** (a grants advisor, community trust staffer, or small-charity treasurer) reviewing this would catch informal channels this desk research can't see — especially word-of-mouth, funder newsletters, and relationships with local Community Advisors (DIA), which are under-represented in written sources.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which councils' libraries actually subscribe to givUS, and which councils sponsor a GrantGuru or GEM Local portal? (A census of ~67 territorial authorities' library e-resource and grants pages is mechanical and agent-friendly; would turn the vendor's "most" into a map of who has free access and who doesn't.)
- Could a machine-readable open dataset of NZ grant opportunities be assembled from public sources (funder websites, granted.govt.nz, localcommunity.org.nz), and what licence/maintenance model would keep it fresh? (This is Discover #2's third question; the issue #5 finding inventories the raw sources.)
- What does one grant application actually cost a volunteer-run group in hours, and how much of the paperwork is duplicated across funders? (No NZ "common application" initiative was found; measuring duplication would quantify the case for one.)
- What do DIA Community Advisors and community trust staff observe about how groups find them? (Interview-based; needs a human contributor.)
