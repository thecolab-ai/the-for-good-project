# The warm-network / first-timer barrier can be quantified in NZ — not from existing published data, but with a new founder-funnel survey that adapts fielded overseas finance and informal-investment items

## Executive answer

- **From existing NZ data: no.** Of the NZ sources I could check — NZGCP/Dealroom, Catalist/AANZ, and the accelerators Creative HQ, Ministry of Awesome and Kōkiri — none publishes founder **repeat-status** (first-time vs serial) or **referral source / warm-intro dependence** in a form that can be aggregated (Electrify and university incubators were not individually verified; see §1). NZGCP's Young Company Finance is deal-level and built on "voluntary collaboration among investors," not founder-level [NZGCP YCF Autumn 2026](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2026.pdf); Catalist's angel report reached the same wall on even basic diversity ("no meaningful data could be obtained") [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). This confirms and extends #145's finding that first-timer status is unmeasured.
- **From new collection: yes — but the access measures must be adapted and tested for NZ angel/seed.** The barrier is quantifiable with a founder survey (or coordinated accelerator/angel-network intake) that adapts two established, internationally-fielded survey constructs plus one simple flag (these are already fielded in large recurring surveys, not claimed to carry dedicated validation studies for angel/seed use): (1) the **"discouraged borrower"** split from the US Federal Reserve Small Business Credit Survey — a fielded construct that separates firms that *applied and were denied* from firms that *did not apply because they expected to be turned down* [Fed SBCS 2022 Report on Employer Firms](https://www.fedsmallbusiness.org/-/media/project/smallbizcredittenant/fedsmallbusinesssite/fedsmallbusiness/files/2021/2022-sbcs-employer-firms-report). That gives a template for splitting "reached investors but got a no" from "needed capital but didn't go to investors" — but note the Fed item locates non-application in *expected rejection*, not in an inability to *find or reach* investors, so the specific "couldn't find/reach investors / couldn't get an introduction" access branch is an NZ adaptation that would need cognitive testing (see §2); (2) GEM's **informal-investor relationship module**, fielded internationally, which records the *relationship of the funder to the founder* (close family, friend/neighbour, work colleague, stranger) [GEM 29-nation informal-investing study (Bygrave et al.)](https://digitalcollections.babson.edu/digital/api/collection/ferpapers/id/2377/download) — a fielded measure of *who provided* informal capital, adjacent to but not identical with warm-network *access* to formal angels/VCs; and (3) a **warm-introduction flag** (was the investor reached via a mutual contact or cold) — a single survey item whose *predictive salience* the UK diversity work established, rather than a proven reusable instrument in its own right [Sifted, on the Diversity VC / British Business Bank 2019 report](https://sifted.eu/articles/warm-introductions-are-bad-for-diversity). The GEM relationship item and the warm-intro flag are kept as **two distinct questions** precisely because "who funded me" and "how I reached an investor" are different things.
- **The minimum viable instrument is ~6 questions** (see below): sought funding y/n → reached investors y/n (and if not, why — the discouraged/access branch) → pitched-but-declined vs closed → how the investor was reached (warm intro / cold / open programme) → first-time vs repeat founder → relationship to any funder secured. That set would separate "couldn't find/reach" from "reached them but got a no," tag warm-intro pathway, and tag repeat-status — the three axes #160, #145 and #141 all flagged as absent — but the custom "couldn't find/reach / couldn't get intro" answer options need founder cognitive testing before use.
- **Overseas, warm-intro dependence is well documented and points one way.** The peer-reviewed anchor is Shane & Cable (2002): across 50 ventures and 202 seed-stage investors, both **direct and indirect network ties** made an investor more likely to fund a venture, via information transfer [Shane & Cable, *Management Science* 2002](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:inm:ormnsc:v:48:y:2002:i:3:p:364-381). The widely-quoted "**warm introductions are 13× more likely to be funded** than cold approaches" figure (UK, Diversity VC / British Business Bank, 2019) is real but rests on a single study — treat the *number* as Medium and the *direction* as well-supported [Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/warm-introductions-are-bad-for-diversity).
- **Transferable to NZ in mechanism, not in magnitude.** The causal mechanism — information asymmetry at seed stage forces investors onto trust/network signals, and closed networks then favour the already-connected — is generic and appears in NZ's own official diagnosis (MBIE names "information problems between entrepreneurs, lenders and investors") [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital). NZ's pre-seed/seed markets depend heavily on high-net-worth angel investors rather than institutional VC [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital), and NZ historically ranked among the highest informal-investment economies in the GEM comparison (2000-round data) [GEM 29-nation informal-investing study (Bygrave et al.)](https://digitalcollections.babson.edu/digital/api/collection/ferpapers/id/2377/download) — both factors that plausibly keep warm-network access salient here. I have no current-year measurement establishing NZ is *more* informal-dependent than comparable ecosystems today, so this is a heavy-angel-dependence point, not a "than most" claim. Either way, the specific UK/US magnitudes (13×; 6% female first-time-deal value) are not NZ measurements and should not be quoted as NZ facts.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — High that the checked NZ public sources do not publish repeat-status or warm-intro dependence; Medium that overseas fielded items provide useful building blocks (Fed covers expected-rejection non-application, GEM covers funder-to-founder relationship, and neither directly measures "couldn't reach investors" in NZ angel/seed); Medium that the mechanism transfers to NZ (strong theory + NZ's own official language, but no NZ-specific measurement yet); Medium on the exact overseas magnitudes (single underlying study for the 13× figure).

## Evidence

### 1. Can existing NZ data be aggregated to measure this? The checked public sources do not capture it

**NZGCP / Young Company Finance / Dealroom.** The main NZ deal dataset is deal-level, not founder-level, and explicitly a partial view: NZGCP cautions Young Company Finance data comes from "voluntary collaboration among investors" and may miss deals led by non-participating investors [NZGCP YCF Autumn 2026](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2026.pdf). Its diversity dashboard tracks gender and a combined Māori/Pasifika bucket but not founder repeat-status or how the founder reached the investor [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). Dealroom hosts founder-claimable profiles and an investor-matching tool but exposes no public aggregate of repeat-status or referral source [NZGCP Dealroom](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/about-us/news-and-media/resources/dealroom). NZGCP itself has stated "significant gaps in availability of data in relation to start-up companies and their fund-raising needs" [NZGCP SPE 2024/25](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2024-25-FINAL-30-June24.pdf) — a measurement gap acknowledged at source (see sibling finding #141).

**Angel layer (Catalist / Angel Association NZ).** NZGCP relays that "the Catalist Q2 2025 report indicated that there are 110 active angel investors in New Zealand [and] no meaningful data could be obtained for diversity" [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). If even basic gender/ethnicity data could not be obtained at the angel layer, it is very unlikely that finer fields such as first-timer status or warm-intro source are being published — though this is an inference from the diversity-data gap, not a statement in the NZGCP report, and it does not establish that Catalist/AANZ fail to *hold* such fields internally (only that no aggregate is published).

**Accelerators & incubators (checked here: Creative HQ, Ministry of Awesome, Kōkiri).** These *do* run structured intake and publish impact reporting, but on **outcomes and reach**, not on repeat-status or investor-referral pathway. Creative HQ publishes an annual impact report focused on economic impact, capital raised and jobs [Creative HQ 2025 impact report](https://creativehq.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Creative-HQ_2025_impact_report.pdf); Ministry of Awesome's Founder Catalyst runs structured seasonal cohort intake (expression of interest plus an expert interview) and publishes headline outcome metrics — startups supported, share still operating, private capital raised, revenue generated — but not founder repeat-status or how founders reached investors [Ministry of Awesome — Founder Catalyst](https://ministryofawesome.com/founders_founder-catalyst/); Kōkiri is a kaupapa-Māori accelerator delivered through Poutama Trust / partners [Poutama Trust — Kōkiri](https://poutama.co.nz/apply-for-the-kokiri-accelerator-programme/). Across these three, I found **no** public report that breaks founders down by first-time vs repeat, or by how they reached investors. Electrify Aotearoa and the university incubators named in #160 were **not** individually verified for this finding, so this negative result covers the sources checked, not the full NZ accelerator/incubator layer. **Important distinction:** referral *to a programme* is not the same as warm-intro dependence *to an investor*; conflating the two would mismeasure the barrier. Programme intake may be a plausible collection point, but I have not verified that any accelerator currently captures these fields, could publish them, or would have capacity to add them.

*Net:* the barrier cannot be quantified from the existing NZ published data I could check. This is a genuine data absence across NZGCP/Dealroom, Catalist/AANZ, and the accelerators checked (Creative HQ, Ministry of Awesome, Kōkiri) — consistent with #145 (first-timer status unmeasured) and #141 (no seeker/failure denominator). It is a "checked sources" absence, not a proof that every NZ programme lacks the fields.

### 2. The minimum viable instrument to separate "couldn't reach investors" from "reached them but got a no"

A closely related distinction is a solved measurement problem in credit research: the **discouraged borrower**. The US Federal Reserve's Small Business Credit Survey defines discouraged firms as "those that did not apply for financing because they believed they would be turned down," and separates them from firms that applied and were denied — weighting responses to the population by firm age, size, industry, geography, and owner race/ethnicity and gender [Fed SBCS 2022 Report on Employer Firms](https://www.fedsmallbusiness.org/-/media/project/smallbizcredittenant/fedsmallbusinesssite/fedsmallbusiness/files/2021/2022-sbcs-employer-firms-report). This gives a proven template for the *applied-and-declined vs did-not-apply* split #141 flagged as needed. But note the boundary carefully: the Fed construct locates non-application in **expected rejection**, not in an **inability to find or reach** investors — these are different failure modes. Porting it to seed/angel therefore means splitting the "didn't apply / didn't pitch" stream into its own reasons — expected rejection, couldn't find who invests at my stage/region, couldn't get a warm introduction, couldn't afford paid access — and it is that access/discovery layer (the stream #110 is about) that the Fed item does **not** already measure. Treat the "couldn't find/reach / couldn't get an intro" branch as a new adaptation to be cognitively tested with founders, not as something the discouraged-borrower construct ships with.

For the **warm-network** dimension there is a related fielded instrument, though it measures a narrower thing than investor-introduction access. GEM's informal-investment module records the **relationship of the funder to the founder** — in the 29-nation GEM informal-investing study, ~48% of informal investment went to a relative's business, 28.9% to a friend/neighbour, 10.6% to a work colleague, and 8.4% to a stranger [GEM 29-nation informal-investing study (Bygrave et al.)](https://digitalcollections.babson.edu/digital/api/collection/ferpapers/id/2377/download). That variable captures *who provided* informal capital (family / friend / colleague / stranger), already fielded internationally (including, historically, in New Zealand, which GEM found to be among the highest informal-investment economies). It is **not** the same as measuring whether a formal angel or VC was reached through a warm introduction — a founder can raise from a stranger via a warm intro, or from a relative with no intro at all. The instrument below therefore keeps the GEM relationship item (question 6) and a separate **warm-introduction flag** (question 5) as two distinct measures rather than treating the GEM module as the warm-network measure.

Combining these, a **minimum viable ~6-question founder instrument** would be:

1. In the last 12 months, did you seek external angel/seed investment? (y/n → screens the denominator)
2. Did you actually reach / pitch any investors? (y/n)
3. **If no:** why not? *forced choice:* couldn't find who invests at my stage/region/cheque · couldn't get an introduction / no warm contact · couldn't afford paid programmes/events where investors are · didn't feel ready · chose not to · other. *(the discouraged/access branch — isolates "couldn't find/reach")*
4. **If yes:** outcome — closed / still open / declined; and if declined, primary stated reason. *(isolates "reached them but got a no")*
5. For investors you reached, how were they reached? *forced choice:* warm introduction via a mutual contact · existing personal/professional relationship · cold outreach · open programme/pitch event/accelerator · online matching platform. *(the warm-intro flag — the UK studies' key variable)*
6. Is this your first company/raise, or have you founded/raised before? + relationship to any funder secured (close family / other relative / friend / colleague / stranger). *(repeat-status + GEM relationship module)*

Add the SBCS-style weighting demographics (founder gender, ethnicity, region, stage, cheque size) and the instrument would yield the first NZ read on *what share of unsuccessful raises fail at "couldn't reach" vs "reached and declined," and how both correlate with warm-intro pathway, funder relationship, and first-timer status*. At ~6 questions it may be proportionate to append to a future NZ founder survey rather than run as a standalone study — but this is only a design suggestion: I have not verified that any such survey is currently running, that it has spare questionnaire space, what it would cost, or that its owners would agree to add the module.

### 3. What overseas research says about warm-intro dependence, and whether it transfers

**Peer-reviewed anchor.** Shane & Cable (2002) studied 50 high-technology ventures and 202 seed-stage investors and found investors "more likely to invest in new ventures when they have a previously established **direct tie** to the entrepreneur" and, separately, when they have an **indirect tie** — the effect running through information transfer, not just social obligation [Shane & Cable, *Management Science* 2002](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:inm:ormnsc:v:48:y:2002:i:3:p:364-381); [INFORMS listing](https://ideas.repec.org/a/inm/ormnsc/v48y2002i3p364-381.html). This is the strongest, least-hype evidence that ties causally raise funding odds at seed stage.

**The headline magnitude.** The most-quoted figure is that startups reaching VCs through "warm introductions" are **~13× more likely to be funded** than those arriving via cold pitch submissions, from a February 2019 Diversity VC / British Business Bank report; the same work found all-female founding teams benefit *less* from warm intros, partly because ~87% of UK VC decision-makers are male [Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/warm-introductions-are-bad-for-diversity). The British Business Bank's later synthesis restates the mechanism plainly: because of information asymmetry, early-stage "investments tend to occur within a (closed) network," with "the tendency to invest through 'warm' introductions," and "founders from underserved communities may not have access to the same … networks" [British Business Bank, *Finding what works*, §1.2 (Wayback snapshot)](https://web.archive.org/web/2023id_/https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/about/research-and-publications/finding-what-works-pathways-to-improve-diversity-in-venture-capital-investment/chapter-1/section-1-2).

**Transferability to NZ — mechanism yes, magnitude no.** The mechanism is generic (information asymmetry → reliance on trust/network signals → advantage to the connected) and is echoed in NZ's own official diagnosis: MBIE attributes capital-market failure partly to "information problems between entrepreneurs, lenders and investors" [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital), and Startup Genome found NZ founders "receive less help from local investors and experts" than peer ecosystems (see #141). NZ's pre-seed/seed markets depend heavily on high-net-worth angel investors rather than institutional VC [MBIE, Access to growth capital](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31135-access-growth-capital), which plausibly keeps warm-network access salient here — but that is a heavy-angel-dependence observation, not a measured finding that NZ is *more* network-dependent than peer ecosystems (the strongest NZ informal-investment evidence, from the GEM 2000 round, is historical), and the specific UK/US numbers are not NZ measurements. The right transfer is the *instrument and the hypothesis*, not the figure.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A private data pull.** I can only see *published* data. If NZGCP/Dealroom, Angel Association NZ member networks, or accelerators hold founder-level repeat-status / referral fields internally, the "not aggregable" conclusion flips to "aggregable but unpublished." A single admin export (repeat-status × how-investor-was-reached, de-identified) would settle it. This is the highest-value next step and needs an org insider or a data-sharing agreement — a human with authority, not an agent.
- **A fielded pilot.** The proposed instrument is untested in NZ. A pilot (even n≈150–200, the order of the Startup Genome NZ founder survey) would show whether founders can reliably self-classify "couldn't reach" vs "declined," and whether the warm-intro flag has enough variance to be informative. Cognitive-testing the forced-choice categories with real founders (lived experience) is required before fielding.
- **Selection bias is the main threat.** Any founder survey reaches founders who are *findable* — disproportionately those already in networks/programmes. The founders most locked out (never entered any programme, no warm contacts) are the hardest to sample, so a naïve survey would *understate* the barrier. A representative frame (e.g. Companies Office / IRD-linked, or a Stats NZ IDI approach) would be needed to fix this, and that needs institutional access.
- **What I could not verify:** the primary Diversity VC / British Business Bank 2019 report and the BBB "Finding what works" live page were tooling-blocked (HTTP 403) to my fetch ladder — classified BLOCKED, not dead; I relied on a Wayback snapshot and Sifted's reporting, so the exact 13× figure is not confirmed against the primary. I could not confirm New Zealand's *current-year* GEM participation (the GEM consortium site blocked automated fetches); NZ's strong informal-investment showing is from historical GEM rounds. I did not obtain any NZ accelerator's raw application schema, so I cannot state definitively that none capture repeat-status internally — only that none *publish* it.
