# NZ distributional data confirms women and Māori/Pasifika founders are severely underrepresented among angel/seed-funded startups; region shows heavy concentration but first-timer status is not measured at all

## Executive answer

- **Gender — well evidenced.** Women are about a quarter of NZ founders but get a tiny share of the money. Two independent measures put women's founder share near a quarter — Startup Genome says **26% of NZ founders are women** [UoA](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/02/20/why-aren-t-more-women-launching-startups--.html), and NZGCP independently records **27% of angel/VC-funded founding teams as female** in FY25 (up from 21% in FY24) [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). Yet the dollar share is a sliver: the Gender Investment Gap study reports **female-only startups received just 2.9% of VC dollars in 2024** — $4,590,144 of $159,949,403 across 20 funds — and only about **1-in-10 female-only startups got any investment at all** [The Gender Investment Gap](https://www.thegenderinvestmentgap.co.nz/); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/573260/appallingly-low-female-startups-getting-less-than-3-percent-of-venture-capital). Treat that **exact 2.9% figure as Medium confidence**: it rests on a single underlying study whose coverage is stated (the 2024 report covers 20 VC funds — up from 2 in 2022 and 14 in 2023) but whose full methodology — how individual deals were classified by founder gender, and how the dollar figures were compiled — is not published, and RNZ/NZ Herald report that same study rather than corroborate it independently. The *direction* — women badly underfunded — is separately corroborated by an older Startup Genome figure of ~7% of VC to solely-women-founded startups [UoA](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/02/20/why-aren-t-more-women-launching-startups--.html).
- **Ethnicity — one dataset, and it shows severe underrepresentation.** The only recurring public disaggregation is NZGCP's own dashboard: **4% of funded founding teams were Māori/Pasifika in FY25** (3% FY24), against a combined population share of roughly **26.7%** (Māori 17.8%, Pacific peoples 8.9%, 2023 Census) [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); [Stats NZ 2023 Census](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-counts-by-ethnic-group-age-and-maori-descent-and-dwelling-counts/). On the *investor* side the gap is starker — of NZGCP's ~54 GPs, **19% were female and 0% Māori**; of ~39 investment professionals, 46% female and 0% Māori [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf).
- **Region — concentration is real, "lock-out" is inferred not proven.** Angel/VC deal activity is overwhelmingly in the main centres: **Auckland 56%, Wellington 20%, Canterbury 9%** of FY2024 deals, with the report stating the three main regions account for ~81% of deals [NZGCP Young Company Finance Autumn 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2025.pdf); Dealroom confirms "Auckland maintains its position as the most funded city" [NZGCP/Dealroom Tech Ecosystem Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/New-Zealand-2025-report.pdf). But this measures where funded companies are *headquartered*, not whether founders outside the centres are structurally shut out versus there simply being fewer startups there — the data cannot separate those.
- **First-timer vs repeat — no data exists.** I found **no public NZ source** that disaggregates angel/seed recipients by first-time versus repeat founder, or by whether a founder had a warm investor network. Since "founders without warm networks / first-timers" is a central plank of stream #110's claim, this is a genuine, load-bearing data absence — not merely unsearched.
- **Bottom line for the stream:** the equity-of-access framing is **strongly supported for women** and **supported for Māori/Pasifika** by distributional data; it is **plausible but unproven for region** (concentration ≠ access barrier) and **unmeasured for first-timers**. Almost all disaggregation is gender-only or a single combined Māori/Pasifika bucket; there is no public breakdown by dollars-per-group beyond gender, no Māori-vs-Pasifika split, no intersectional cut (e.g. wāhine Māori), and no first-timer status.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — High that women are underfunded *as a direction* (two independent founder-share measures, plus two independent dollar-share studies pointing the same way: GIG's 2.9% and Startup Genome's ~7%), but Medium for the *exact* 2.9% dollar figure (a single underlying study; coverage stated at 20 VC funds but the full deal-classification methodology unpublished; RNZ/Herald non-independent); Medium for Māori/Pasifika (one recurring source, a combined bucket, headcount not dollars); Low for region-as-barrier and for the (absent) first-timer dimension.

## Evidence

### Gender: ~a quarter of founders, a sliver of the funding

Two independent measures corroborate that women are roughly a quarter of NZ founders. The University of Auckland, citing Startup Genome, states "just 26 percent of founders in New Zealand are women" — described as one of the highest rates globally [UoA](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/02/20/why-aren-t-more-women-launching-startups--.html). Independently, NZGCP's Annual Report 2025 records the diversity of the 423 startups funded by angels and VCs in FY25 as **27% female, 4% Māori/Pacific, 2% other under-represented group**, up from **21% female, 3% Māori or Pasifika** the prior year [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf).

The funding gap is far wider than the founder gap. The Gender Investment Gap research (Jenny Rudd and Theresa Gattung) found female-only-founded startups received **2.9%** of venture capital invested in 2024 — **$4,590,144 of a total $159,949,403** — meaning they were "shut out of 97 cents in every dollar" [The Gender Investment Gap](https://www.thegenderinvestmentgap.co.nz/). Reporting the same study, RNZ adds that this came "from a total pool of $160m invested by 20 venture capital funds last year" and that "only one-in-10 [female-only startups] are getting any investment at all" [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/573260/appallingly-low-female-startups-getting-less-than-3-percent-of-venture-capital); the NZ Herald covered the same 2.9% finding [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/economy/employment/female-led-startups-get-just-29-of-venture-capital-funding-in-2024/XIPEDLLIBRFTXFFQIQSC3AZ5AY/). A separate, earlier Startup Genome figure cited by UoA puts venture capital to solely-women-founded startups at "about 7 percent in New Zealand" — higher than the GIG number but measuring a different year/definition, and pointing in the same direction [UoA](https://www.auckland.ac.nz/en/news/2025/02/20/why-aren-t-more-women-launching-startups--.html).

Note that RNZ/Herald both trace to the single GIG study, so they are one source reported twice; the GIG study and Startup Genome's ~7% are the two genuinely independent lines of evidence on the funding gap. Both are VC-focused; neither isolates the pure angel/seed layer, though NZGCP's founding-team figure spans "startups funded by angels and VCs."

### Ethnicity: a single combined bucket, and it is small

The only recurring public disaggregation by ethnicity I found is NZGCP's own performance dashboard, which reports the Māori/Pasifika share of funded founding teams at **4% (FY25)** and **3% (FY24)** [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). For scale, the 2023 Census records Māori at **17.8%** and Pacific peoples at **8.9%** of the population — a combined ~26.7% [Stats NZ 2023 Census](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-counts-by-ethnic-group-age-and-maori-descent-and-dwelling-counts/). So Māori and Pasifika founders appear among funded founding teams at roughly one-sixth of their population share.

On the capital-allocation side, NZGCP reports its fund managers as **~54 GPs of which 19% identified as female and none as Māori**, and **~39 investment professionals of which 46% identified as female and none as Māori** [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). This is directly relevant to stream #110's "closed circles / who-you-know" framing: the people deciding where early-stage capital goes are themselves non-diverse on ethnicity.

The data thins out sharply beyond this. NZGCP notes that for the angel layer, "the Catalist Q2 2025 report indicated that there are 110 active angel investors in New Zealand [and] no meaningful data could be obtained for diversity" [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). No public source I found splits Māori from Pasifika, reports their share of *dollars* rather than count of teams, or covers intersectional identity. As an illustration that deliberate mandates move the number, the VC fund Hillfarrance has publicly stated 50% of its NZ portfolio is women-founded and 44% Māori-run — but this is one fund's self-reported portfolio, not ecosystem data, and I treat it as context only [The Spinoff](https://thespinoff.co.nz/business/25-06-2021/local-venture-capital-fund-becomes-first-in-nz-to-adopt-global-diversity-pledge).

### Region: heavily concentrated, but concentration is not the same as lock-out

NZGCP's Young Company Finance Autumn 2025 report gives a direct regional breakdown of FY2024 deals: **Auckland 56%, Wellington 20%, Canterbury 9%**, then Bay of Plenty 4%, Manawatū-Whanganui 4%, Otago 3%, Waikato 2%, and Hawke's Bay / Taranaki / Nelson / Marlborough at 1% each [NZGCP Young Company Finance Autumn 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2025.pdf). The same report states the "three main regions, Auckland, Wellington and Christchurch [account] for 81% of total deals in FY 2024" (the chart figures sum to ~85%; I flag this minor internal inconsistency in the source) [NZGCP Young Company Finance Autumn 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2025.pdf). Dealroom's 2025 ecosystem report reinforces that "Auckland maintains its position as the most funded city in New Zealand" [NZGCP/Dealroom Tech Ecosystem Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/New-Zealand-2025-report.pdf).

The important caveat: these figures record where funded companies are *headquartered*, not whether founders based outside the centres face an access barrier. Auckland alone holds roughly a third of NZ's population — about 1.8 million of ~5.34 million [Stats NZ subnational population estimates 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2024-2018-base/) — and hosts the largest concentration of startups by HQ location [NZGCP/Dealroom Tech Ecosystem Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/New-Zealand-2025-report.pdf) (which charts "startups by HQ location" and notes Auckland "maintains its position as the most funded city in New Zealand"), so some concentration is expected. The public data cannot separate "fewer startups exist outside the centres" from "startups outside the centres try and are locked out" — proving the latter would need denominator data (startups seeking funding by region), which does not exist (see #141, which found NZ does not publicly count active fundraisers or failed raises).

### First-timer vs repeat founder: not measured

Despite this being explicit in stream #110's framing ("first-timers ... are structurally locked out"), I found no public NZ dataset — NZGCP, Catalist, Angel Association NZ, Startup Genome/MBIE, or Stats NZ — that disaggregates angel/seed recipients by first-time versus repeat founder, or by prior investor relationship / warm-intro status. NZGCP's dashboard tracks gender and a Māori/Pasifika bucket but not founder repeat-status [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf). The absence itself is the finding for this dimension; I have split it out as a dedicated research question (#160).

### The meta-finding: disaggregation is shallow and uneven

The disaggregated data that exists is: (a) NZGCP's dashboard — gender, one combined Māori/Pasifika bucket, "other underrepresented," estimated from Young Company Finance and NZ Private Capital publications plus self-disclosure (95% of Aspire *applicants* provided demographic data, but the 423-company ecosystem figure is estimated, not a census) [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); (b) the Gender Investment Gap study — gender and dollars, VC only [The Gender Investment Gap](https://www.thegenderinvestmentgap.co.nz/); and (c) YCF's regional deal split [NZGCP Young Company Finance Autumn 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-YCF-Autumn-2025.pdf). There is no public source combining any two of these axes, none reporting dollars-per-group for ethnicity, none splitting Māori from Pasifika, none intersectional, and none by first-timer status. Catalist's explicit "no meaningful data could be obtained for diversity" at the angel layer is a direct statement of the gap [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A dollars-by-group breakdown for ethnicity.** NZGCP reports Māori/Pasifika as 4% of funded *founding teams* (headcount), not their share of *capital*. If Māori/Pasifika founders raise systematically smaller rounds, their dollar share could be well below 4%; if concentrated in a few large raises, higher. A Dealroom/NZGCP export by ethnicity × amount would settle this.
- **A Māori-vs-Pasifika split and intersectional data.** The combined bucket hides potentially very different realities for Māori, Pacific peoples, and wāhine Māori. This needs its own investigation (opened as #159) and likely a human with lived experience / kaupapa Māori authority to frame it well.
- **Regional denominator data.** The regional figures are deal *location*, not access. Data on how many startups per region *sought* funding (and failed) would convert "concentration" into an actual access-barrier claim — but #141 found NZ does not publicly count active fundraisers or failed raises, so this cannot currently be resolved.
- **Any first-timer / warm-network measure.** No source measures this today (opened as #160). Founder-level survey data, or accelerator/angel-network funnel data tagged by repeat-status and referral source, would be needed.
- **What I could not verify:** the full methodology of the Gender Investment Gap study — its 2024 report does state the coverage (20 VC funds) and the topline dollar splits, but not how individual deals were classified by founder gender or how the dollar totals were compiled, and it has not been independently replicated; MBIE's Startup Genome assessment PDF was behind an Incapsula bot-wall to my fetch ladder this run (it is cited successfully in sibling finding #141, so treated as blocked-not-dead); and I could not independently extract Catalist's angel-market report (consistent with #141's experience) — I rely on NZGCP's quotation of it.
