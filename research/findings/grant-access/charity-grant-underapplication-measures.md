# Missed grant funding for small NZ charities is only partially measurable from public data

## Executive answer

- A defensible public-data measure exists for **known unmet applicant demand**: some funders publish applications considered, requested amounts, approval status, approved amounts, and sometimes decline reasons; for example, the Lottery Grants Board reported 5,648 applications requesting $572 million in 2024/25, while DIA's Class 4 workbook schema includes status, amount requested, amount granted, and decline reason fields ([Lottery Grants Board annual report 2024/25](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/103852/Lottery-Grants-Board-Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf); [DIA Class 4 Grants Data](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-statistics-class-4-grants-data); [DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx)).
- A defensible public-data measure does **not** exist for the full "eligible but did not apply" pool, because public charity returns report income received, public funder registers mostly report submitted or approved requests, and eligibility rules are not consistently linked to every charity that could have applied ([Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data); [Community Matters results reporting guidance](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/results-reports); [Fundsorter funding data notes](https://www.fundsorter.com/data-1)).
- The strongest proxy is a **funder-by-funder measurement frame**: define small charities as Tier 4 or lower Tier 3 organisations, join public charity annual-return fields to funder application/outcome files where names or registration numbers can be matched, and report applicant participation, approval rate, partial-funding gap, and declined-request gap by region and purpose ([Charities Services Tier 4 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4); [Charities Services Tier 3 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-3); [Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data)).
- The proxy should be labelled **partial**, not "missed funding", unless it is supplemented by private funder CRM data, fund-seeker discovery logs, or a sampled charity survey that records eligible opportunities seen, eligibility matches, applications started, applications submitted, and reasons for not applying ([Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together); [Fundsorter funding data notes](https://www.fundsorter.com/data-1)).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the conclusion is well supported for the measurement limits of public data, but the size of non-application cannot be estimated without private or newly collected behavioural data.

## Evidence

### What "small charity" can mean in public data

Charities Services describes Tier 4 reporting as for small charities with simple financial activity, and says Tier 4 can be used when a charity has annual operating payments under $140,000, no public accountability, and cash-based accounting ([Charities Services Tier 4 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4)). Charities Services describes Tier 3 as for small to medium charities with more complex financial activity, and says Tier 3 can be used when a charity has annual operating payments of $140,000 or more but less than $5 million, no public accountability, and accrual-based accounting ([Charities Services Tier 3 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-3)).

Charities Services' 2024/25 sector snapshot reports 29,208 registered charities, total sector income of $29.70 billion, and $4.28 billion of "grants and other funding" income in 2024/25, so grants are visible as a sector-level income category but not as missed opportunities ([Charities Services sector snapshot 2024/25](https://www.charities.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/102617/Charities-Sector-Snapshot-2024_2025.pdf)). Charities Services' open-data page says the OData service includes published data about charities, officers, and annual returns, is free and unauthenticated, and can return data as JSON or CSV, so annual-return grant-income fields are accessible for aggregate analysis ([Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data)).

Public charity-return data can identify charities with low or zero grants revenue, and the current OData latest-return endpoint exposes fields such as `GovtGrantsContracts`, `AllOtherGrantsAndSponsorship`, `GeneralGrantsReceived`, and `GrantsRevenueFromOtherSources` in annual-return records, based on an endpoint check run on 2026-07-02 ([Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data); [Charities Services OData endpoint](https://www.odata.charities.govt.nz/GrpOrgLatestReturns?$top=1&$format=json)). That is a **recipient-side proxy** only, because a charity with zero reported grant income may have been ineligible, unaware, capacity-constrained, recently unsuccessful, voluntarily not seeking grants, or funded through a different category ([Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data)).

### Public funder data can measure known unmet applicant demand

The Lottery Grants Board annual report says the Board allocated $398,029,957 of lottery profits in 2024/25, and it says 5,648 applications requested $572 million in funding during the same year ([Lottery Grants Board annual report 2024/25](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/103852/Lottery-Grants-Board-Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf)). The same annual report publishes committee-level fields including total amount available, total amount requested, total amount approved, number of requests considered, number of requests approved, and percentage approved for multiple committees, so it supports oversubscription and applicant success-rate measures among applicants who reached the Lottery system ([Lottery Grants Board annual report 2024/25](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/103852/Lottery-Grants-Board-Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf)).

DIA says Class 4 grants data is published to improve understanding of pokie-gambling grant distribution, and DIA links the dataset from its Class 4 Grants Data page ([DIA Class 4 Grants Data](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-statistics-class-4-grants-data)). The official DIA Jan-Jun 2022 workbook includes columns for final organisation name, organisation type, application purpose, decline reason, territorial authority, region, amount granted, amount requested, society name, status, and accept/decline date, so this version supports application-level analysis of declined, partially approved, and approved requests ([DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx)).

In the Jan-Jun 2022 DIA Class 4 workbook, a local calculation found 14,649 rows, 10,294 accepted applications, 4,355 declined applications, $277,950,744 requested, $151,150,246 granted, and $70,247,114 requested in declined applications; this calculation is reproducible from the official workbook but is limited to that workbook's period and societies ([DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx)). The same local calculation found 5,091 accepted applications where the amount granted was below the amount requested, so partial funding is measurable in that workbook but still applies only to applicants who submitted requests ([DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx)).

Some individual gaming funders publish application-level outcome files: TTCF's 2024/25 publication report includes organisation, region, general category, published purpose, amount applied, amount approved, published status, published decision, and funding round fields ([TTCF applications report 2024/25](https://ttcfltd.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Publication-Report-1st-April-24-to-31st-March-25.pdf)). NZCT's public grants file includes approved grants and a section for applications declined since 1 October 2023, which supports similar applicant-outcome measurement but still excludes non-applicants ([NZCT grants file to 30 April 2026](https://www.nzct.org.nz/s/Grants-for-website-since-2023-24-to-30-April-2026.pdf)).

### Public funder data cannot measure eligible non-application

Fundsorter says its funding landscape data covers almost 2,000 contestable funding opportunities across 37 categories, but its technical notes say the data measures opportunity counts rather than dollar amounts available or granted, excludes non-public opportunities such as proactive grantmakers, and can count one opportunity in multiple categories ([Fundsorter funding data notes](https://www.fundsorter.com/data-1)). Fundsorter therefore helps map the opportunity landscape, but it cannot by itself quantify dollars missed by eligible charities because it lacks amounts available, applicant behaviour, and outcome data for each eligible charity ([Fundsorter funding data notes](https://www.fundsorter.com/data-1)).

Community Matters guidance says grant recipients may need to complete results reports, and those reports can include original grant request information, budget, agreed reporting, payment information, spending, outcomes, and supporting financial statements ([Community Matters results reporting guidance](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/results-reports)). That guidance shows DIA holds richer post-award and application-management data than is visible in public reports, but it does not show a public dataset that records every eligible non-applicant or every application started but not submitted ([Community Matters results reporting guidance](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/results-reports)).

Foundation North reported that more than sixty organisations gave feedback through surveys, interviews, and focus groups on applicant friction, barriers, or confusion in accessing funding, and its 2025 applicant-experience work included funding discovery, supporting documents, application-form completion, and decision/payment communication improvements ([Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together)). This is independent evidence that applicant barriers are observable through funder-user research, but it is not a national quantitative missed-dollar estimate ([Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together)).

### Recommended measurement frame

The valid public-data headline should be "known unmet requested demand among applicants", calculated as requested amount minus approved amount for funders that publish request and approval fields, with separate counts for fully declined and partially approved applications ([Lottery Grants Board annual report 2024/25](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/103852/Lottery-Grants-Board-Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf); [DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx); [TTCF applications report 2024/25](https://ttcfltd.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Publication-Report-1st-April-24-to-31st-March-25.pdf)). The valid charity-side proxy should be "small charities with low or zero grant income", segmented by reporting tier, purpose, region, and income mix, because the Charities Register exposes annual returns but does not record unsubmitted applications ([Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data); [Charities Services Tier 4 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4); [Charities Services Tier 3 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-3)).

The invalid headline is "eligible small charities missed $X in grants" from public data alone, because eligibility requires fund-specific rules, timing, geography, activity fit, documentation status, prior-grant restrictions, and organisational intent that are not all present in public charity returns or public award registers ([Fundsorter funding data notes](https://www.fundsorter.com/data-1); [Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together); [Community Matters results reporting guidance](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/results-reports)).

## What would change this conclusion

- A national public dataset that links fund opportunities, eligibility criteria, submitted applications, started-but-not-submitted applications, declined applications, partial approvals, approved amounts, requested amounts, and charity registration numbers would change the conclusion from "partial proxy" to "direct measurement possible" ([DIA Class 4 Grants Data](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-statistics-class-4-grants-data); [Charities Services Open Data](https://www.charities.govt.nz/charities-in-new-zealand/the-charities-register/open-data)).
- A data-sharing agreement with major funders that exposes de-identified eligibility-screening, abandoned-application, decline, partial-approval, and applicant-support records would allow stronger estimates of under-application than public award registers allow ([Community Matters results reporting guidance](https://www.communitymatters.govt.nz/results-reports); [Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together)).
- A representative survey or panel of Tier 4 and lower Tier 3 charities that records eligible opportunities discovered, opportunities not discovered until after closing, decisions not to apply, applications started, applications submitted, and reasons for stopping would allow a defensible behavioural estimate of eligible non-application ([Charities Services Tier 4 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4); [Charities Services Tier 3 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-3); [Foundation North applicant-experience project](https://www.foundationnorth.org.nz/news-and-reports/article/improving-the-applicant-experience-together)).
- I could not verify a current national dollar value for "eligible small charities that did not apply" from public sources, and I could not verify that the protected data.govt.nz catalogue page for Class 4 data was accessible to plain HTTP or agent-browser fetches on 2026-07-02, although the DIA landing page and official DIA workbook URL were accessible ([DIA Class 4 Grants Data](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-statistics-class-4-grants-data); [DIA Class 4 Jan-Jun 2022 workbook](https://www.dia.govt.nz/diawebsite.nsf/Files/Gambling-Class-4-Grants-Data-Review/%24file/Class-4-Grants-Dataset-Jan-June-2022-CSV.xlsx)).

## Open follow-up questions

- Which major NZ funders can export application-start, submitted, declined, partial-approval, and approved data with charity registration numbers or NZBNs, and under what data-sharing conditions?
- How many Tier 4 and lower Tier 3 charities report zero or low grant income by region and purpose in the latest Charities Services OData extract?
- Can Fundsorter-style opportunity data be combined with a sampled charity panel to estimate the proportion of eligible opportunities not discovered before deadline?
- Do small charities that receive applicant support from major funders have higher submission or success rates than similar charities that do not receive support?
