# Public NZ data maps Māori and Pacific enterprise support better than Māori/Pasifika access to angel and seed capital

## Executive answer

- **The public Māori/Pasifika startup-capital dataset is still thin.** NZGCP's 2025 Annual Report reports Aspire applicants as **13% Māori/Pacific** and **48% female-founded**, but it combines Māori and Pacific founders, reports applicants rather than dollars deployed, and does not publish Māori/Pacific founder outcomes by stage, cheque size, gender, region, or intersectional identity. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)
- **Māori enterprise data is materially stronger than Māori founder-capital data.** MBIE/BERL report the Māori economy at **$126b in assets**, **$32b in economic contribution**, and nearly **24,000 Māori-owned businesses** in 2023, while Stats NZ says its formal Tatauranga umanga Māori population still under-covers the potential Māori business population. [MBIE, Te Ōhanga Māori reports](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports); [Stats NZ, Tatauranga umanga Māori 2023 technical report](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Methods/Tatauranga-umanga-Maori-2023-purongo-matatini-Statistics-on-Maori-businesses-2023-technical-report/Download-document/Tatauranga-umanga-Maori-2023-purongo-matatini-Statistics-on-Maori-businesses-2023-technical-report.pdf)
- **Pacific enterprise data exists but is newer and less connected to startup-capital reporting.** Auckland Council's 2025 research paper uses Stats NZ and Inland Revenue data to count **5,271 economically significant Pacific businesses and sole traders** in 2023, and Pacific Business Trust says it supports more than **2,000 active Pacific-owned businesses**, but neither source connects those businesses to angel/seed fundraising outcomes. [Auckland Council, Quantifying the Pacific Economy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media/zz0f0oec/quantifying-the-pacific-economy-research-paper.pdf); [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/)
- **The ecosystem is a mix of capability builders, grants, investor-readiness programmes, mainstream co-investment, and emerging kaupapa Māori venture vehicles.** The visible map includes Te Puni Kōkiri Māori Enterprise and Māori Development Fund, Poutama Trust, Māori Women's Development Inc, Tupu/Kōkiri-style accelerators, Tapuwae Roa's Te Ara Takatū playbook, NZTE/Invest New Zealand Māori investment support, Pacific Business Trust, the Ministry for Pacific Peoples' Tauola Business Fund, NZGCP Aspire, and Māui Toa VC. [TPK Māori Enterprise](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-enterprise); [TPK Māori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund); [Poutama Trust](https://poutama.co.nz/); [MWDI](https://mwdi.co.nz/); [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/); [Tapuwae Roa, Te Ara Takatū](https://www.tapuwaeroa.org/te-ara-takatu/); [Invest New Zealand, Our Kaupapa](https://www.nzte.govt.nz/page/our-kaupapa); [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/); [NZGCP Aspire](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund); [Māui Toa](https://www.mauitoa.vc/)
- **Bottom line:** public sources support the existence of an emerging kaupapa Māori/Pacific enterprise-support and investment-readiness ecosystem, but they do **not** yet support a quantitative answer to "what share of Māori founders or Pacific founders get angel/seed capital, and on what terms?" [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); [NZGCP SPE 2026/27](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2627-FINAL.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the ecosystem map is well supported by public organisation and government pages, but the central capital-access conclusion is an absence-of-public-data finding that could change if NZGCP, Catalist, angel groups, accelerators, Dealroom, iwi funds, or Pacific providers shared aggregated private funnel data.

## Evidence

### What data exists on Māori/Pasifika access to angel and seed capital

NZGCP's 2025 Annual Report is the strongest public source I found for current Māori/Pasifika startup-capital participation: it says 95% of potentially new Aspire applicants were willing to provide demographic information, 80 applications were received, 48% of respondents had female founders, and 13% identified as Pacific peoples or Māori. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)

That NZGCP metric is useful but limited for this issue because it combines Māori and Pacific peoples, covers applicants rather than funded companies or dollars, excludes existing Aspire portfolio companies seeking follow-on funding, and does not publish outcomes by ethnicity, gender, region, or round size. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)

NZGCP's same Annual Report says 53 general partners across venture firms included 10 identifying as female and none identifying as Māori; it also reports Catalist Q2 2025 as indicating 110 active angel investors and says no meaningful diversity data could be obtained for those angel investors. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)

NZGCP's 2026/27 Statement of Performance Expectations says sourcing may place additional emphasis on Māori-, Pasifika-, and women-led startups while all investment decisions remain commercial, and it says NZGCP will work with MBIE during FY27 to improve definitions, data sources, baselines, and narrative reporting for the early-stage investment ecosystem. [NZGCP SPE 2026/27](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2627-FINAL.pdf)

The 2025 NZGCP evidence therefore supports a "known data gap" conclusion more strongly than a "measured funding gap" conclusion: the public data can say Māori/Pacific representation among new Aspire applicants was 13%, but it cannot say what share of Māori founders or Pacific founders sought, received, or were refused angel/seed capital. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); [NZGCP SPE 2026/27](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2627-FINAL.pdf)

For population context, Stats NZ's 2023 Census national data reports Māori at 887,493 people, or 17.8% of the census usually resident population, and Pacific peoples at 442,632 people, or 8.9%. [Stats NZ, 2023 Census national and regional data](https://www.stats.govt.nz/infographics/2023-census-national-and-regional-data/)

### Enterprise datasets: useful denominator context, not angel/seed access data

MBIE's Te Ōhanga Māori 2023 summary says the Māori economic contribution grew from $17b in 2018 to $32b in 2023, the Māori asset base grew from $69b to $126b, Māori self-employment rose 49%, Māori employers rose 31%, and there were nearly 24,000 Māori-owned businesses in 2023. [MBIE, Te Ōhanga Māori reports](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports)

Stats NZ's Tatauranga umanga Māori 2023 technical report says its formal TUM 2023 population included 1,338 Māori authorities, 3,849 other Māori enterprises, and 372 Māori tourism businesses, while Te Matapaeroa 2021 used IDI data to identify 20,499 Māori-owned businesses and indicated TUM covers only a small proportion of enterprises owned by Māori. [Stats NZ, Tatauranga umanga Māori 2023 technical report](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Methods/Tatauranga-umanga-Maori-2023-purongo-matatini-Statistics-on-Maori-businesses-2023-technical-report/Download-document/Tatauranga-umanga-Maori-2023-purongo-matatini-Statistics-on-Maori-businesses-2023-technical-report.pdf)

Auckland Council's 2025 Quantifying the Pacific Economy paper, using Stats NZ and Inland Revenue data, counts 3,555 businesses with Pacific owners and 1,716 Pacific sole traders in 2023, giving 5,271 economically significant private-for-profit Pacific businesses and sole traders. [Auckland Council, Quantifying the Pacific Economy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media/zz0f0oec/quantifying-the-pacific-economy-research-paper.pdf)

The same Auckland Council paper says Pacific-owned businesses nationally increased from 2,205 in 2013 to 3,555 in 2023, and that Auckland had 53% of New Zealand's Pacific businesses with owners while having 63% of the Pacific population aged 30-64. [Auckland Council, Quantifying the Pacific Economy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media/zz0f0oec/quantifying-the-pacific-economy-research-paper.pdf)

Pacific Business Trust says it is Aotearoa New Zealand's Pacific economic development agency, supports more than 2,000 active Pacific-owned businesses, and provides programmes to empower and accelerate Pacific-owned businesses. [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/)

These Māori and Pacific enterprise datasets are important for denominator design, but none of the public versions I found links enterprise records to angel applications, investor meetings, term sheets, seed rounds, deal sizes, valuations, or rejection reasons. [MBIE, Te Ōhanga Māori reports](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy/te-ohanga-maori-the-maori-economy-reports); [Auckland Council, Quantifying the Pacific Economy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media/zz0f0oec/quantifying-the-pacific-economy-research-paper.pdf); [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)

### Māori ecosystem map

Te Puni Kōkiri's Māori Enterprise page says its Māori Enterprise team supports Māori enterprises to thrive, helps connect people with regional Māori business networks, lists partnership agencies, and identifies Poutama Trust as providing advisory support and investment for business investigation, training, and growth. [TPK Māori Enterprise](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-enterprise)

Te Puni Kōkiri's Māori Development Fund page says the fund is administered by TPK, makes grants also called investments to eligible applicants, supports iwi and Māori businesses and organisations, and had $40.21m per annum allocated from 1 July 2025. [TPK Māori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund)

The Māori Development Fund is not a general angel/seed substitute because its FY2025/2026 priorities are productivity of iwi and Māori assets, co-investment in infrastructure, increased Māori export revenues, and community resilience initiatives; its eligible examples include advice, feasibility studies, and business cases that can make access to capital easier. [TPK Māori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund)

Poutama Trust publicly describes Te Pūtea Pakihi Māori / Māori Business Funding as investment assistance for investigation, growth, and training, which makes it relevant to capability and growth support rather than a disclosed angel/seed equity dataset. [Poutama Trust](https://poutama.co.nz/)

Māori Women's Development Inc says it provides micro-loans, business coaching, and financial capability programmes for wāhine Māori and their whānau, and its business-criteria page says its business loans are for Māori women and their whānau who want to start or expand a business. [MWDI](https://mwdi.co.nz/); [MWDI business criteria](https://mwdi.co.nz/business-criteria/)

Kōkiri was publicly described by Poutama Trust in 2022 as a 10-week kaupapa Māori programme supporting early-stage impact-positive startups into investment-ready ventures, with up to 10 teams, mentors, coaches, investor networks, and a $10,000 startup grant. [Poutama Trust, Kōkiri Accelerator](https://poutama.co.nz/apply-for-the-kokiri-accelerator-programme/)

Tupu currently describes itself as an eight-week accelerator for Māori founders building high-growth startups, says each year it supports 10 founder teams, and positions the programme around becoming investment-ready and scaling nationally or globally. [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/)

Tapuwae Roa's Te Ara Takatū page says the report explores Aotearoa New Zealand's venture-capital ecosystem and gives advice and insights to Rakahinonga Māori to become investment ready, navigate their pathways, and grow in the early stages of startup growth. [Tapuwae Roa, Te Ara Takatū](https://www.tapuwaeroa.org/te-ara-takatu/)

Māui Toa says it is a venture-capital investment management company focused on early-stage investment into high-growth impact-led technology and innovation companies in Aotearoa, and says its purpose is to accelerate Māori investment into early-stage high-growth technology and innovation companies. [Māui Toa](https://www.mauitoa.vc/)

Invest New Zealand says it supports ambitious businesses to raise growth capital, matches investors with investment opportunities, works with iwi holding and operating companies, Māori land trusts and incorporations, whānau-owned companies and other Māori-linked entities, and can support early-stage capital raises as well as larger projects. [Invest New Zealand, Our Kaupapa](https://www.nzte.govt.nz/page/our-kaupapa)

Reserve Bank's Māori Access to Capital consultation page says its issues paper identified factors that may act as barriers between Māori and specific forms of capital in the financial system, and says the Reserve Bank wanted to work with the private sector, iwi Māori, business, communities, and government organisations on options to improve access to capital for Māori. [Reserve Bank, Improving Māori Access to Capital](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/have-your-say/improving-maori-access-to-capital)

### Pacific ecosystem map

Pacific Business Trust says it is the Pacific economic development agency of Aotearoa New Zealand and provides business health checks, workshops, an online community, customised support, procurement support, and Hatch Pacific for young entrepreneurs. [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/)

The Ministry for Pacific Peoples' Tauola Business Fund page says the fund is now closed, supported Pacific businesses to foster growth, create employment, and build resilience, and had four funding pools ranging from under $40,000 to $100,001-$400,000. [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)

Tauola is relevant to Pacific business growth but is not a current angel/seed capital channel because the public page says applications are closed and frames the support as business funding for productivity, jobs, resilience, and economic development rather than private equity investment. [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)

The Pacific Business Trust and Ministry for Pacific Peoples sources show Pacific business support and grant pathways, but I did not find a public Pacific-specific angel network, Pacific founder seed-fund dataset, or Pacific founder deal-funnel report equivalent to NZGCP's Aspire diversity applicant metric. [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/); [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf)

### What the ecosystem map implies

The public landscape has three different kinds of pathway that should not be collapsed into one "investment" bucket: grants and public funding, capability and investment-readiness support, and private equity/VC/angel investment. [TPK Māori Development Fund](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-development-fund); [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/); [Māui Toa](https://www.mauitoa.vc/); [NZGCP Aspire](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund)

The kaupapa Māori side has the clearest visible startup-investment readiness layer through Tupu/Kōkiri and Te Ara Takatū, and the clearest visible kaupapa Māori early-stage VC vehicle through Māui Toa, but public pages do not publish aggregate Māori founder applications, funded deals, dollars invested, or follow-on outcomes for those pathways. [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/); [Poutama Trust, Kōkiri Accelerator](https://poutama.co.nz/apply-for-the-kokiri-accelerator-programme/); [Tapuwae Roa, Te Ara Takatū](https://www.tapuwaeroa.org/te-ara-takatu/); [Māui Toa](https://www.mauitoa.vc/)

The Pacific side has visible business-development infrastructure through Pacific Business Trust and Tauola, but I found less public evidence of Pacific-specific early-stage equity infrastructure than for kaupapa Māori pathways. [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)

The next useful data model would treat Māori and Pacific separately, use total-response ethnicity where people identify with more than one group, and include intersectional fields such as wāhine Māori, Pacific women founders, region, stage, sector, amount sought, amount committed, valuation, instrument, investor type, and reason a raise did not close. [Stats NZ, 2023 Census national and regional data](https://www.stats.govt.nz/infographics/2023-census-national-and-regional-data/); [Auckland Council, Quantifying the Pacific Economy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/media/zz0f0oec/quantifying-the-pacific-economy-research-paper.pdf); [NZGCP SPE 2026/27](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2627-FINAL.pdf)

Because this research touches Māori data and Pacific community data, any future dataset design should be reviewed by people with Māori data-governance authority, Pacific data and community expertise, and founder lived experience before collection or publication decisions are made. [data.govt.nz, Māori Data and AI guidance](https://data.govt.nz/leadership/centre-for-data-ethics-and-innovation/guidance/maori-data-and-ai-guidance-for-business); [TPK Māori Enterprise](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/maori-enterprise); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)

## What would change this conclusion

- A public or permissioned NZGCP/Dealroom/Aspire dataset that reports applicants, funded companies, dollars invested, stage, region, gender, and outcomes separately for Māori founders and Pacific founders would change the central conclusion from "not publicly measurable" to a measured access gap. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); [NZGCP SPE 2026/27](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-SPE-2627-FINAL.pdf)
- Aggregated funnel data from angel groups, Catalist, accelerators, Māui Toa, Tupu/Kōkiri, Tapuwae Roa, Pacific Business Trust, Tauola, iwi investment entities, and mainstream VC funds would change the ecosystem map from a pathway inventory into an evidence base about conversion rates and capital terms. [Māui Toa](https://www.mauitoa.vc/); [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/); [Pacific Business Trust](https://pacificbusiness.co.nz/); [MPP Tauola Business Fund](https://www.mpp.govt.nz/programmes-and-funding/economic-growth/tauola-business-fund/)
- A Māori data-governance review or Pacific data-governance review could change what should be collected, who may hold it, and what can safely be published; this desk review cannot make that authority call. [data.govt.nz, Māori Data and AI guidance](https://data.govt.nz/leadership/centre-for-data-ethics-and-innovation/guidance/maori-data-and-ai-guidance-for-business)
- I could not verify a distinct public "Punakā" early-stage investment vehicle from the issue text; searches mostly surfaced unrelated results or Punakaiki/Poutama/Tupu-type pathways, so that name should be checked by a human with ecosystem context before synthesis. [Poutama Trust](https://poutama.co.nz/); [Tupu Accelerator](https://tupu.org.nz/)
- I could not extract the Catalist FY25 Angel Market Report PDF through the local fetch ladder: `curl` returned HTTP 403 and `node scripts/fetch.mjs` classified the Catalist PDF URL as **BLOCKED** by tooling/IP, not dead. I therefore rely on NZGCP's Annual Report for the "no meaningful angel diversity data" statement because NZGCP quotes that limitation in its own reporting. [NZGCP Annual Report 2025](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/assets/Media/NZGCP-Annual-Report-2025-FINAL.pdf); [Catalist FY25 PDF, blocked in local fetch](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/Annual%2B-%2BFY25%2BCatalist%2BNZ%2BAngel%2BMarket%2BReport.pdf)
- I did not use or publish any individual founder or investor data, and I did not attempt to infer ethnicity from names, organisations, portfolio pages, or media coverage. [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md)

## Open follow-up questions

- What aggregate fields can NZGCP, Catalist, AANZ member groups, Māui Toa, Tupu/Kōkiri, Tapuwae Roa, Pacific Business Trust, and MPP publish without exposing commercially sensitive or culturally sensitive information?
