# Free/low-cost founder–investor connection overseas: what works, and what transfers to NZ

> **Scope.** This is a depth-1 research child of stream [#110](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/110) ("Early-stage NZ founders can't reach angel & seed funding"). It surveys the free/low-cost founder–investor *connection* formats the framing memo named — open demo days, investor office hours, AngelList/Metal/Cherub-style matching platforms, university angel matching, government registries, and open pitch/crowdfunding marketplaces — and asks, for each: **what it costs founders, how it is funded, what outcomes are documented, and what would or would not transfer to New Zealand's small, relationship-driven market.** It deliberately distinguishes independently-evidenced claims from vendor marketing. It does **not** re-answer sibling stream questions (who writes NZ's $10k–$200k cheques; whether NZ angels would attend open-networking formats) — those need their own research and, in places, a human with lived experience.

## Executive answer

- **"Free to the founder" almost always means someone else pays.** The sustainable overseas models fund the connection in one of three ways: **(a) equity** (accelerators — YC, Techstars — take a stake and charge no cash; demo day is a benefit of that deal) [YC](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal), [Techstars](https://www.techstars.com/newsroom/investment-terms); **(b) the investor side** (matching platforms monetise carry/fees on investors, not founders — AngelList since 2015, Gust, F6S) [AngelList/Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AngelList); or **(c) the public purse / a university** (government-subsidised angel networks and co-investment funds) [Scottish Enterprise evaluation](https://www.gov.scot/binaries/content/documents/govscot/publications/factsheet/2017/09/esif-evaluation-reports-2007-2013/documents/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/govscot:document/Evaluation+-+Scottish+Enterprise_Scottish+Co-Investment+Fund+II.pdf). A "free open directory" with no one paying for curation is the one model for which this survey found **no** documented success — a *bounded* negative (see the registry search in §4: absence of evidence across the jurisdictions checked, not proof none exists anywhere).
- **The strongest independent evidence says an open listing to "the crowd" underperforms; capital flows when a credible intermediary vouches.** The leading academic study of online matching (AngelList) found the platform only worked once it added professional *curation* (syndicates), which raised capital flows to new regions by ~33% — and even that gain depended on intermediaries connected back to the funding hub [Catalini & Hui, NBER w24777](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24777). Online platforms cut *economic* distance but not *social* distance; funding still concentrates where capital already is [Agrawal, Catalini & Goldfarb, NBER w16820](https://www.nber.org/papers/w16820).
- **The clearest transferable "open" format is equity crowdfunding — and NZ already has it.** US Reg CF raised ~US$343m in 2024 across licensed portals [Kingscrowd/CCA data](https://kingscrowd.com/2024-investment-crowdfunding-trends-stats-and-platform-rankings/); NZ's FMCA-licensed platforms (Snowball Effect, PledgeMe) run the same model under a NZ$2m/12-month cap [FMA](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/), [Snowball licence](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/licensed-providers/the-snowball-effect-limited/). It is success-fee funded (Snowball's published schedule: an NZD$5,000 upfront fee plus a 7.5% success fee, subject to a NZD$25,000 minimum [Snowball disclosure statement, archived 2025-06-17](https://web.archive.org/web/20250617081607/https://www.snowballeffect.com.au/legal/disclosure-statement); PledgeMe a 7.5% success fee [PledgeMe FAQ](https://guide.pledgeme.co.nz/faq-equity)), issuer-disclosure-gated rather than all-comers, and **not costless to sustain** — Equitise's NZ licence was cancelled in April 2024 and the firm entered administration [FMA cancellation](https://www.fma.govt.nz/news/all-releases/media-releases/fma-cancels-crowdfunding-services-licence-for-equitise/), [Crowdfund Insider](https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2024/11/231972-investment-crowdfunding-platform-equitise-enters-administration-after-failing-to-secure-funding/).
- **For a small, relationship-driven market like NZ, the best-evidenced overseas template is Scotland/Ireland's: public money subsidising a *curated angel network*, paired with co-investment and investor tax incentives — not an open registry.** Scotland's independently-evaluated Co-Investment Fund plus its angel association (LINC) together accounted for ~35% of all small-business private equity in Scotland [Scottish Enterprise evaluation](https://www.gov.scot/binaries/content/documents/govscot/publications/factsheet/2017/09/esif-evaluation-reports-2007-2013/documents/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/govscot:document/Evaluation+-+Scottish+Enterprise_Scottish+Co-Investment+Fund+II.pdf). NZ already runs the co-investment half of this (NZGCP Aspire) [NZGCP](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund); what it appears to lack is a *free, open front door* — defined here as a single free public entry point that routes any founder to the right curated angel group without first needing wholesale-investor contacts or a pre-existing lead. AANZ does publish a searchable directory of member groups [AANZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/) and Aspire takes online applications [NZGCP](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund), so this is an **inferred product/design gap, not a claim that no infrastructure exists**.
- **Almost none of the headline "outcome" numbers are independently audited.** Techstars' "74% raise within three years" [Techstars blog](https://www.techstars.com/blog/innovation-in-action/how-techstars-helps-pre-seed-founders-raise-capital-and-scale-faster), the AI-matching tools' traction figures (Metal, Cherub) [YC: Metal](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/metal-2), [TechCrunch: Cherub](https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/17/cherub-the-raya-of-angel-investing-is-reinventing-angel-rounds-with-a-950-subscription/), and the NZ Investment Network's global-franchise marketing [NZ Investment Network](https://www.newzealandinvestmentnetwork.co.nz/) are all self-reported by the vendor or its partners; treat them as marketing until independently verified. (I removed an earlier "AngelList — 12,000 startups funded" figure from this bullet: I could not source it, so the specific number is not asserted here.) Where independent evaluation exists (Scotland, and the academic literature), it is more sober.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the *shapes* of the models and their funding/cost structures are well-sourced (much of it primary), and the academic caution about open matching is peer-reviewed; but most *outcome magnitudes* are vendor-reported, and there is **no NZ-specific study** directly testing what would work here.

## Evidence

### 1. Open demo days and investor office hours (accelerators & universities)

**Cost to founders: cash-free, equity-priced, and gated.** Y Combinator invests US$500,000 (US$125k for a fixed 7% plus US$375k on an uncapped MFN SAFE) and states plainly, "we don't charge any fees to the companies to be part of YC" — Demo Day is a benefit of that deal, not a paid event [YC deal](https://www.ycombinator.com/deal). Techstars is the same shape: US$220,000 in (a US$20k CEA for ~5% common plus a US$200k uncapped MFN SAFE), and "there is no fee (i.e., monetary cost) to join a Techstars accelerator program" [Techstars terms](https://www.techstars.com/newsroom/investment-terms). Both are **strongly gated** — competitive batch entry, and (for YC) an invite-only, "software generated" audience of ~1,000 investors on Demo Day [YC Demo Day FAQ](https://www.ycombinator.com/demoday/faq). *Confidence: High (primary sources, both fetched).*

**"Office hours"** at YC are the core mentorship format — group partners run group office hours roughly fortnightly and one-on-ones on demand, available to portfolio founders (and alumni) as part of the equity-funded deal, not sold separately [YC library](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/carousel/Office%20Hours). There is a "YC Open Office Hours" concept aimed beyond the batch, but its current status was not verified for this finding — flagged as a lead, not a fact.

**University demo days that are genuinely free and non-dilutive exist, but are affiliation-gated.** Stanford's **StartX** "takes no equity and charges no fees" and is a 501(c)(3) funded by grants/donations/corporate sponsorship — but at least one founder per company must have a Stanford connection [StartX](https://web.startx.com/), [Wikipedia: StartX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/StartX). **MIT delta v** gives "$75K equity-free funding" per team and runs a public-facing Demo Day, but it is MIT's student capstone accelerator, and its separate *Investor Days* are explicitly invite-only [MIT delta v](https://entrepreneurship.mit.edu/accelerator/program/), [MIT Investor Days](https://entrepreneurship.mit.edu/accelerator/investor-days-2025/). So the "free/open" university format is real but restricted to that university's community. *Confidence: High on cost/eligibility. (MIT delta v publishes its own cumulative cohort-outcome figures, but I could not independently verify them, so no such number is asserted or relied on here.)*

**Outcomes are mostly self-reported, and the independent evidence is mixed.** Techstars' widely-quoted "74% of accelerator companies raise capital within three years" and ">$1M average first raise" appear on its own blog with no cited source; a third-party newsletter attributes the figure to PitchBook (2023) but the underlying report was not located, and Techstars' own total-capital figure varies across its pages ($29B/$30.4B/$32.1B) [Techstars blog](https://www.techstars.com/blog/innovation-in-action/how-techstars-helps-pre-seed-founders-raise-capital-and-scale-faster). The peer-reviewed literature is genuinely split: accelerators are defined as cohort programs "that culminate in a public pitch event... a 'demo-day'", and studies of their effect on fundraising find "conflicting" results — some positive, some "muted or even negative", with effects varying by program design and founder pre-entry knowledge [Journal of Innovation and Entrepreneurship, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13731-025-00586-6), [The design of startup accelerators, Research Policy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048733319300939). *Confidence: Medium — the "mixed effect" conclusion is well-supported; specific vendor percentages are not.*

### 2. Online founder–investor matching platforms (AngelList/Wellfound, Gust, F6S, Metal, Cherub)

**The founder side is usually free or cheap; the money comes from investors or subscriptions.** AngelList began in 2010 as a free "online introduction board", and since 2015 has let startups raise from angels free of charge; it monetises the **investor** side via a 5% cut on syndicates (launched 2013), fund administration, and rolling-fund subscriptions [AngelList/Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AngelList), [TechCrunch on syndicates](https://techcrunch.com/2013/07/27/angellist-syndicates-lets-angels-get-carry-for-helping-a-startup-raise-money/). Its jobs product spun out as Wellfound in 2022. **Gust** (rebranded from AngelSoft in 2011) is fundamentally B2B software sold to angel groups/VCs, with a free community tier and paid founder subscriptions [TechCrunch on Gust](https://techcrunch.com/2011/09/13/angelsoft-relaunches-as-gust-com-now-connects-startups-to-investors/). **F6S** is free to founders and monetised through service-provider deals and accelerator/government program administration [TechCrunch on F6S](https://techcrunch.com/2013/10/17/f6s-becomes-a-global-clearing-house-for-startup-freebies-100m-worth/). *Confidence: High on the cost/funding structure (multiple independent journalistic sources).*

**The newer "AI matching" tools charge founders directly and have essentially no independent outcome evidence.** **Metal** (metal.so) is a founder-paid subscription (a free tier plus paid plans; pricing is demo-gated and has shifted over time) whose traction ("100+ YC founders paying", a Techstars partnership) is vendor/partner-reported [YC company page](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/metal-2), [Metal–Techstars partnership](https://www.techstars.com/blog/blog/program-news/fundraising-support-for-techstars-portfolio-companies-with-metal). **Cherub** (investwithcherub.com) is a dating-app-style marketplace: investors free, founders on a freemium tier plus a paid plan (its pricing moved from ~$480/$950-a-year to a free + $79/month structure) [TechCrunch on Cherub](https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/17/cherub-the-raya-of-angel-investing-is-reinventing-angel-rounds-with-a-950-subscription/), [LA Business Journal](https://labusinessjournal.com/featured/cherub-connects-startups-with-funding/). Cherub's outcome figures ("~20% of alpha intros funded, ~$1M raised"; "$3M+ facilitated") are company-reported metrics relayed by two outlets — more credible than raw marketing, but not audited. Both are US-focused. *Confidence: Medium on structure; Low on outcomes.*

**Critically, the best independent evidence says open matching alone doesn't work — curation does.** Catalini & Hui's study of AngelList (the leading US platform) found the "wisdom of the crowd" breaks down for equity because of information asymmetry; the platform only produced results once it added **professional curation** (syndicates led by reputationally-credible investors), which increased capital flows to new regions by ~33% and made curated startups 37% more likely to generate above-median returns — but the democratisation "relies on the presence of intermediaries with professional networks" bridging those regions back to the hub [Catalini & Hui, NBER w24777](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24777). Practitioner accounts corroborate directionally: a bare public listing historically meant cold-messaging hundreds of investors, while the good deals migrated to curated/syndicate channels [Outlander VC](https://outlander.vc/fieldguide/the-truth-about-raising-money-on-angellist/), and warm-referral deal flow is pre-filtered and far higher-converting than inbound from directories [Angel Investors Network guide](https://angelinvestorsnetwork.com/angel-investing/finding-angel-investors-deal-flow-guide). *Confidence: Medium-High for the academic finding; the practitioner corroboration is weaker (blogs).*

### 3. Equity crowdfunding as open matching (US Reg CF; NZ Snowball/PledgeMe/Equitise)

**This is the one genuinely "open" format with a documented, regulated track record — but it is success-fee funded and geographically bounded.** In the US, Regulation Crowdfunding raised ~US$422.7m in 2023 and ~US$343.6m in 2024 across licensed portals, dominated by Wefunder (#1) and StartEngine (#2) [Kingscrowd 2024](https://kingscrowd.com/2024-investment-crowdfunding-trends-stats-and-platform-rankings/), [SEC Reg CF offering data](https://www.sec.gov/data-research/statistics-data-visualizations/regulation-crowdfunding-cf-offerings). Founder costs are mostly success-fee-based, but **not uniformly upfront-free** — the model splits: Wefunder and Republic charge only on a successful raise (Wefunder ~7.5–7.9%, waived if the founder brings a US$25k+ lead investor; Republic 6% cash + 2% securities), whereas **StartEngine charges 7% + 2% *plus* a US$10,000 upfront compliance/due-diligence fee** [CrowdSpace: Wefunder](https://thecrowdspace.com/platform/wefunder/), [platform fee comparison](https://growthturbine.com/blogs/wefunder-vs-republic-vs-start-engine-vs-dalmore-vs-issuance-com-vs-dealmaker). The transferability catch: **Reg CF requires a US-organised issuer** — 17 CFR 227.100(b) makes an issuer ineligible if it "is not organized under, and subject to, the laws of a State or territory of the United States or the District of Columbia" [17 CFR 227.100(b) (Cornell LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/17/227.100) — so a NZ founder would have to reincorporate into a US entity to use it [crowdfunding-law explainer](https://crowdfundingattorney.com/2021/07/30/using-reg-cf-to-raise-money-for-a-non-u-s-business/). Reg-CF completion rates are commonly cited around 67–79% of closed offerings, but from commercial data firms, not the primary SEC read — treat as approximate. *Confidence: High on the model and US-incorporation constraint; Medium on completion rates.*

**New Zealand already runs this model, natively.** Licensed equity crowdfunding operates under the Financial Markets Conduct Act 2013, with each issuer capped at NZ$2m from the public per 12 months without a full product disclosure statement [FMA crowdfunding](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/crowdfunding/). **Snowball Effect** (FMA-licensed 30 July 2014 [FMA licence](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/licensed-providers/the-snowball-effect-limited/)) publishes a fee schedule of an NZD$5,000 upfront fee plus a success fee on funds raised — headline option 7.5% of the capital amount (subject to an NZD$25,000 minimum), with two lower-headline alternatives that defer part of the fee or take warrants — and runs the licence-mandated "Mandatory Checks" on a company before an offer (compliance/background checks, explicitly *not* a view on commercial merit) [Snowball disclosure statement, archived 2025-06-17](https://web.archive.org/web/20250617081607/https://www.snowballeffect.com.au/legal/disclosure-statement). Its self-reported cumulative total was >NZ$75m as of November 2020 [Wikipedia: Snowball Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snowball_Effect_(equity_crowdfunding)) — treat as self-reported; I could not confirm a current figure. **PledgeMe** (also licensed 2014) charges a 7.5% success fee, payable only if the goal is met by deadline [PledgeMe — FAQ (equity)](https://guide.pledgeme.co.nz/faq-equity), and reports 31 campaigns raising ~NZ$18m in its first four licensed years [PledgeMe blog](https://blog.pledgeme.co.nz/equity-crowdfunding-ten-years-on/) (self-reported). These platform totals are self-reported and should be flagged as such. **The sustainability warning is concrete:** the FMA cancelled **Equitise's** NZ crowdfunding licence effective 3 April 2024 for non-compliance, and the firm entered voluntary administration months later [FMA cancellation](https://www.fma.govt.nz/news/all-releases/media-releases/fma-cancels-crowdfunding-services-licence-for-equitise/), [Crowdfund Insider](https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2024/11/231972-investment-crowdfunding-platform-equitise-enters-administration-after-failing-to-secure-funding/). So NZ's "open" rail exists, but it is issuer-disclosure-gated (not all-comers), success-fee/upfront-fee funded, and not costless to keep running. *Confidence: High on the regime, the fee schedules (now sourced to each platform's own disclosure/FAQ), and the Equitise closure; Low on the cumulative-raise marketing totals.*

### 4. Government-backed matching / registries and university angel matching

**The evidenced small-market model is public money subsidising a *curated angel network* + co-investment + investor tax relief — not an open directory.** Scotland is the strongest case because it has an **independent evaluation** (Malcolm Watson Consulting for Scottish Enterprise): the Scottish Co-Investment Fund invested £45.5m into 139 businesses (2009–13), levering £74.6m of private partner money (1.64:1), and — the load-bearing point for a small market — angel syndicates plus matching SCF represented ~35% of all private equity into small businesses in Scotland; crucially the model "depends on successful operation of angel syndicates" and on national (SEIS/EIS) tax incentives, with LINC Scotland developing the syndicates [Scottish Enterprise evaluation](https://www.gov.scot/binaries/content/documents/govscot/publications/factsheet/2017/09/esif-evaluation-reports-2007-2013/documents/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/evaluation-scottish-enterprise_scottish-co-investment-fund-ii-pdf/govscot:document/Evaluation+-+Scottish+Enterprise_Scottish+Co-Investment+Fund+II.pdf). *Confidence: Medium (single but independent, high-quality evaluation).*

Ireland runs a state-backed version: **HBAN**, jointly funded by Enterprise Ireland and InterTradeIreland and (since 2024) operated by Dogpatch Labs, whose stated function is "matching investors with high quality investment opportunities" delivered through five regional hubs; the programme reported a record €33m invested in start-ups in its most recent year [Enterprise Ireland/HBAN 2.0](https://www.enterprise-ireland.com/en/news/enterprise-ireland-and-intertradeireland-launch-next-generation-halo-business-angel-network-hban-2-0-under-new-operator-dogpatch-labs). (An earlier draft cited a "€177m into 730+ companies since inception" cumulative figure and "free-to-pitch showcases / iHPSU co-investment / investor-readiness grants" to an RTÉ report; the Enterprise Ireland page does not carry those specifics and the RTÉ page was Cloudflare-blocked to every fetch rung available here, so I have dropped the unverifiable detail and kept only what the primary funder page states.) The UK's **Angel CoFund** (£100m, government-funded) invests £100k–£1m alongside angel syndicates — again co-investment through networks, not an open registry [GOV.UK](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/100-million-fund-to-support-business-growth-goes-national). Singapore's **Startup SG Equity** co-invests on a 7:3-then-1:1 basis, and Israel's Innovation Authority now *funds the operating costs of angel clubs* — both subsidise the intermediary, not an open board [EnterpriseSG SEEDS Capital](https://www.seedscapital.sg/about-us/our-co-investment-model/), [Israel Innovation Authority](https://innovationisrael.org.il/en/press_release/winners-to-establish-angel-clubs/). *Confidence: Medium — funding structures are official/primary; outcome figures are mostly program-reported (except Scotland).*

**University angel matching follows the same "curated, affiliation-gated, light-touch-for-founders" pattern.** MIT Alumni Angels and Cambridge Angels are alumni/experienced-investor networks with a deliberately light process — MIT decides "whether to invite a company to present after 1 or 2 conversations" and says it doesn't "burden startups or investors with excess process and requirements", while Cambridge runs an open application with "no-obligation" investor office hours — but they gate on university affiliation and publish little outcome data [MIT Alumni Angels](http://www.mitalumniangels.com/about), [Cambridge](https://ie.cam.ac.uk/support_article/cambridge-angels/). (Neither page states an application fee either way; I earlier asserted "no application fee" and have removed that as unsupported — the sourced point is only the light, open process, not a stated zero fee.) **On open registries: across the jurisdictions surveyed for this finding (UK, Ireland, Scotland, Singapore, Israel, US, NZ), I found no government-run, open, free, searchable investor registry with an independent evaluation showing it works.** Method: for each jurisdiction I checked the government/agency angel-support pages cited in this section and the directories they point to; the searchable directories that surfaced were commercial (Crunchbase, AngelList, Angel Match), and the "official" ones were membership-gated intermediary lists (e.g. UKBAA's member directory sits behind paid membership) [UKBAA](https://ukbaa.org.uk/about/). This is a **bounded negative** — absence of evidence in the places checked, not proof none exists anywhere. *Confidence: Medium for the negative finding — consistent across the jurisdictions checked, but the search was not exhaustive.*

### 5. What this means for New Zealand

**NZ's connection channels are already curated and wholesale-gated — matching the overseas "what works" pattern, but (arguably) without a free public matching front door.** The Angel Association NZ is a network/directory of more than a dozen member angel groups and funds [AANZ](https://www.angelassociation.co.nz/); access to those groups runs through their own pitch-and-due-diligence processes, and investors generally have to qualify as wholesale/eligible investors under the FMCA [Catalist FY25 report](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/Catalist+NZ+Angel+Market+Report+FY25.pdf). (I could not source the stronger claim that "an investment committee handpicks who presents" for the network as a whole — it varies by group — so I've narrowed it to the pitch/due-diligence workflow the Catalist report describes.) The government's matching instrument, NZGCP **Aspire**, co-invests *alongside* an angel/VC lead you must already have found [NZGCP Aspire](https://www.nzgcp.co.nz/funding/aspire-seed-fund). The one NZ platform closest to "open matching", the **NZ Investment Network** (arm of a global franchise), lets any founder post free but publishes only vague global-franchise marketing (its site claims "over the past 15 years we've raised funding for thousands of businesses") and no NZ-specific, independently-verified outcomes [NZ Investment Network](https://www.newzealandinvestmentnetwork.co.nz/). *Confidence: High on the structure; the open-board's outcomes are unverified marketing.*

**The market is genuinely small and concentrated, which is exactly the condition under which open matching struggles and curated networks do relatively well.** Catalist's FY25 Angel Market Report (year to 31 March 2025) records NZ$15.06m invested across 125 deals into 74 businesses by 341 active angels, with the top 20 angels contributing ~44% of all capital [Catalist FY25](https://cdn.catalist.co.nz/Documents/Reports/Catalist+NZ+Angel+Market+Report+FY25.pdf). (The separate *Young Company Finance* / Angel Association calendar-2025 figures report NZ$13.9m of angel capital — up just 2.7% — across 167 deals, up ~34% from 125, by 455 active angels, up from 328 — a different series and period; the two should not be blended, but both show a market in the low tens of millions, highly concentrated [RNZ, "Early-stage angel investment in start-up businesses grows for first time since 2021"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/586313/early-stage-angel-investment-in-start-up-businesses-grows-for-first-time-since-2021).) In a market this thin, the academic caution bites hardest: online platforms remove economic distance but not social frictions, and funding concentrates where relationships already exist [Agrawal, Catalini & Goldfarb, NBER w16820](https://www.nber.org/papers/w16820).

**So, transfer verdict:**

| Overseas model | Founder cost | Sustained by | Transfers to NZ? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Equity accelerator + demo day (YC/Techstars) | Equity, no cash | The accelerator's fund | **Partly** — NZ already has an equity-taking accelerator (Startmate, whose ANZ programme is joinable from New Zealand and invests on set terms — $120k via a SAFE or priced round, at a $1.5m post-money valuation for founders who haven't previously raised [Startmate](https://www.startmate.com/accelerator/program)) plus an active angel/seed ecosystem (Icehouse Ventures' Ice Angels network and its First Cut fund for under-30 founders — *funds*, not accelerators [Icehouse Ventures](https://www.icehouseventures.co.nz/about)); all competitive-entry, so not a *broad* front door |
| University free/non-dilutive demo day (StartX/MIT) | Free (equity-free) | University + donors | **Partly** — needs a university/philanthropic sponsor; would be affiliation-gated |
| Open online matching board (bare listing) | Free | (nothing sustainable) | **No** — the model with the weakest evidence; adverse selection in a tiny market |
| Curated online matching (AngelList syndicates) | Free to founder | Investor-side carry/fees | **Cautiously** — works *because of* curation; needs credible NZ syndicate leads, not just software |
| Equity crowdfunding (Reg CF / Snowball / PledgeMe) | Success fee (~5–8%), + upfront on some platforms (Snowball NZD$5k; StartEngine US$10k) | Fees on successful raises | **Yes — already here**; open on the investor side, issuer-vetted; sustainability is real (Equitise folded) |
| Govt-subsidised curated angel network + co-investment + tax relief (Scotland/Ireland) | Not verified; public subsidy lowers network/intermediary cost | Government + investor tax incentives | **Best fit** — NZ has the co-investment half (Aspire); the gap is *inferred* — no *free public matching front door* routing founders into the curated networks (AANZ offers a member-group directory, not matching) — plus no SEIS/EIS-style angel tax relief |
| Open government investor registry | — | — | **None found in the jurisdictions surveyed** (UK, Ireland, Scotland, Singapore, Israel, US, NZ) — a bounded negative, not proof none exists |

## Surprising or load-bearing claims

## What would change this conclusion

- **A rigorous, independent evaluation of an *open* matching platform showing real raises for early-stage founders in a small market.** The strongest evidence here (Catalini & Hui; Agrawal et al.) is US-based and pre-dates the current AI-matching tools (Metal, Cherub). If Metal or Cherub published audited match-to-raise data — or if a small-market registry demonstrably worked — the "open boards don't work" conclusion would weaken. I could not find any such evaluation.
- **NZ-specific evidence on whether angels would engage with an open/free format.** This finding infers from market structure and overseas analogues; it does **not** test NZ investor behaviour directly. That question (a sibling in stream #110) needs primary input from NZ angels and network operators — a **human with lived experience/authority** is required; an agent cannot settle it.
- **Better outcome data behind the vendor claims.** Most magnitudes cited by platforms (Techstars 74%, AngelList's own platform totals, Snowball's cumulative $, NZ Investment Network's franchise-wide marketing) are self-reported. If primary/audited figures diverged materially, the relative ranking of models could shift — though the *cost/funding structure* conclusions would hold.
- **Changes I could not verify:** Snowball's **current cumulative raise total** (the >NZ$75m figure is self-reported and dates to November 2020), the current open/closed status of the UK Angel CoFund and Regional Angels Programme (British Business Bank pages returned 403), the precise current FMA-licensed-provider count, and NZ Investment Network's actual NZ deal outcomes (only global-franchise marketing is public). The Snowball and PledgeMe **fee schedules are now sourced** to each platform's own disclosure statement / FAQ (Snowball: NZD$5,000 upfront + 7.5%, min NZD$25k; PledgeMe: 7.5% success fee). None of the open items change the headline; they are noted for the next contributor.

## Open follow-up questions

- Would NZ angels and network conveners actually engage with a *free, open front door* into the existing curated pitch-night networks — and what specifically stops them today? (Needs primary NZ interviews; sibling to this in #110.)
- Is there a case for a NZ SEIS/EIS-style **angel tax incentive**, given Scotland's model leaned heavily on it? (Policy research; MBIE-facing.)
- What would a *NZ-appropriate* curated matching layer look like — a lightweight directory of *which angel group covers which stage/region/cheque size* (a curated signpost, not an open all-to-all board)?
- Do NZ's equity-crowdfunding platforms (Snowball, PledgeMe) actually serve the $10k–$200k *angel* gap, or only larger public offers? (Complements the sibling cheque-size question.)
