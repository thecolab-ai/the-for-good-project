# NZ's broiler / poultry-meat supply is materially less exposed to an H5N1 incursion than its egg supply

> **Scope.** This finding answers the **broiler / poultry-meat** half deferred from #227 (the egg/layer half is answered in [`nz-poultry-egg-supply-h5n1-exposure.md`](nz-poultry-egg-supply-h5n1-exposure.md)). Per the #285 guardrail, it does **not** reuse the egg conclusion: the whole point is that meat-chicken systems differ from layer systems in housing, turnover, restock speed, geography and import settings, and those differences change the exposure picture. Overseas figures are used as **comparators**, not NZ forecasts.

## Executive answer

- **NZ chicken meat is domestically supplied and, like eggs, has no fresh-meat import buffer.** NZ produces ~220,000 tonnes of chicken meat a year (~120 million meat chickens; ~40 kg per capita) and consumes almost all of it domestically ([PIANZ production/consumption](https://www.pianz.org.nz/news/nz-chicken-meat-and-poultry-production-and-consumption-show-continuing-increases/); [AkoVet NZ poultry](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/)). MPI is explicit that **"you cannot import fresh chicken meat into New Zealand"** — only cooked/processed product from approved countries, with stricter rules again for duck and turkey ([MPI steps to importing poultry](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/food/poultry/steps-to-importing/); [MPI importing poultry](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/importing-food-and-beverages/poultry)). So a domestic shortfall can't be quickly backfilled with fresh imports — the same structural feature that makes eggs exposed.

- **But three biological/structural features make broiler-meat supply much more resilient to H5N1 than eggs.** (1) **Housing** — most NZ meat chickens are raised indoors in barns (only ~30% of broiler farms are free-range, and even those house the birds), and a NZ national survey found the housed broiler sector had *little* wild-bird avian-influenza exposure, whereas free-range layers were the at-risk group — which is exactly why NZ's only-ever HPAI detection (H7N6, Dec 2024) was in a **free-range layer**, not a broiler ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report), citing Rawdon et al. 2010; [AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/)). (2) **Turnover** — broilers are slaughtered at 35–42 days, so exposure windows are short and a depopulated broiler shed can be **restocked in weeks, not the ~year a layer flock needs** ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/); [Stuff, brief life of a meat chicken](https://www.stuff.co.nz/business/farming/105860625/explainer-the-rules-for-raising-chickens)). (3) **Geography** — broiler farms cluster in Waikato, Taranaki, Canterbury and Auckland; Otago (the H7N6 site) has **no broiler properties at all**, and the nearest broiler was ~150 km from the infected farm ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)).

- **The overseas comparator points the same way: under H5N1, meat holds up while eggs crash.** In the US 2024–25 outbreak, egg-layers lost ~50.7 million birds (Oct 2024–Mar 2025) and wholesale/retail egg prices "surg[ed] to all-time highs by February," yet **broiler production rose ~3%** and wholesale broiler prices *fell* ~2%, because broilers "are not as susceptible to the virus due to their shorter grow-out cycle" and sit away from the worst migratory flyways ([Univ. of Arkansas AES yearend 2025](https://aaes.uada.edu/news/yearend-poultry-2025/); [American Farm Bureau — Avian Influenza Hits Turkeys and Eggs Hardest](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/avian-influenza-hits-turkeys-and-eggs-hardest)). This is a comparator, not a NZ forecast, but it is a consistent, well-evidenced direction.

- **So the H7N6 "contained in five months" story tells us even less about meat chickens than about eggs.** It was a free-range **layer** event, in a region with **zero** broiler farms, ~150 km from the nearest broiler property; it exercised none of the broiler sector's real chokepoints. Reading it as evidence that NZ's *chicken-meat* supply is H5N1-ready would be a category error ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)).

- **Where meat supply IS exposed is concentration and chokepoints, not grow-out flocks.** The sector is extremely concentrated and vertically integrated: **Tegel alone is ~50% of the market, and four companies (Tegel, Inghams, Brinks/PH van den Brink, Turks) supply ~99%** of NZ poultry meat, each running its own breeding, hatchery, feed and processing chain ([Te Ara — industry structure](https://teara.govt.nz/en/poultry-industry/page-6); [AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/); [WATTPoultry — Tegel](https://www.wattagnet.com/top-poultry-companies/company/tegel-foods)). The supply-critical failure isn't a grow-out shed (fast to replace) — it's a **processing plant or a breeding/grandparent flock** at one of a handful of operators, plus the sector's dependence on **imported pedigree genetic eggs** (Cobb/Aviagen) that a global H5N1 disruption could choke ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/)). And an incursion instantly threatens NZ's small export trade and disease-free status — as Brazil's first commercial H5N1 case did in May 2025, triggering 60-day bans from China, the EU and others ([Reuters via Oklahoma Farm Report](https://www.oklahomafarmreport.com/2025/05/19/bird-flu-in-top-chicken-exporter-brazil-triggers-trade-bans/); [MercoPress](https://en.mercopress.com/2025/05/17/brazil-halts-chicken-exports-due-to-bird-flu-outbreak)).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the structural facts (self-sufficiency, no fresh-meat imports, ~99% four-firm concentration, housed/short-cycle birds) and the US broiler-vs-layer comparator are well-sourced and consistent; the *conclusion* that NZ chicken-meat supply would ride out an H5N1 incursion better than eggs is a robust inference, but there is **no NZ-specific modelling** of a broiler H5N1 shock, the ~30% free-range broiler segment is a growing exposure pathway, and the concentration/chokepoint risk is under-quantified.

## Evidence

### NZ chicken meat is home-grown and has no fresh-import relief valve

New Zealand is effectively self-sufficient in chicken meat and consumes almost all of what it produces. PIANZ reports total chicken-meat production "just over 220,000 metric tonnes" in 2021 with per-capita consumption of ~43 kg (2021)/~41 kg (2022), and says "most poultry and meat chicken production is consumed in New Zealand," with only a small export sector (~$27 million in 2017) ([PIANZ](https://www.pianz.org.nz/news/nz-chicken-meat-and-poultry-production-and-consumption-show-continuing-increases/)). AkoVet puts throughput at ~120 million meat chickens a year across ~165 broiler farms ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/)); MPI's H7N6 report, using PIANZ/EPF data at December 2024, counts **230 broiler properties** (and 170 layer properties) — the "farms" vs "properties" and dataset differences explain the gap, but the order of magnitude is consistent ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)).

Crucially, the supply is closed at the border for the product consumers actually buy: MPI states plainly that **"you cannot import fresh chicken meat into New Zealand"** ([MPI steps to importing poultry](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/food/poultry/steps-to-importing/)). Imported poultry meat must be cooked/processed and sourced only from countries listed in the Import Health Standard, and duck and turkey can be imported only from MPI-approved production systems in disease-free zones/compartments ([MPI steps to importing poultry](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/food/poultry/steps-to-importing/); [MPI importing poultry](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/importing-food-and-beverages/poultry)). This means chicken meat shares the eggs' core vulnerability — no quick fresh-import backfill — with one partial difference: **cooked/processed chicken can be imported** from approved sources, giving a slow, thin buffer for processed product that table eggs do not have.

### Why an H5N1 incursion is less likely to hit meat chickens hard — housing

The most important difference from eggs is the housing pathway. NZ meat chickens are raised in barns — "the main housing types for broilers are barns where birds are raised exclusively indoors or free-range where chickens are still kept in barns but able to access outdoor areas during the day," with about **30% of farms free-range** ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/); [PIANZ NZ Chicken](https://www.pianz.org.nz/nz-chicken/)). Housed birds have far less contact with wild birds, which are the H5N1 reservoir.

MPI's own H7N6 epidemiology report makes the exposure gradient explicit, citing a NZ national survey (Rawdon et al. 2010): free-range layer properties "are at risk of exposure to avian influenza viruses from wild birds," while "the chicken broiler, caged/barn layer, turkey broiler and pullet-rearer sectors, which are housed, had little exposure. It is perhaps not surprising then that New Zealand's first detection of HPAI was in a free-range layer property" ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)). In other words, the exact incursion mechanism that produced NZ's only HPAI event — free-range hens foraging outdoors and picking up virus from wild birds — largely bypasses the housed broiler sector.

The caveat, which I hold open rather than resolve: Rawdon's survey is from 2010, and the ~30% free-range **broiler** segment (barns with daytime outdoor access) shares some of the same wild-bird pathway that hit the free-range layer in 2024. So "housed = protected" is a strong tendency, not an absolute — a free-range broiler flock is a plausible incursion point, just a much smaller and shorter-lived target than the free-range layer population.

### Why an H5N1 incursion is less likely to hit meat chickens hard — turnover and restock

Broilers live 35–42 days ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/); [Stuff](https://www.stuff.co.nz/business/farming/105860625/explainer-the-rules-for-raising-chickens)). Two consequences follow. First, the **exposure window per bird is tiny** compared with a layer hen that produces for a year or more, so at any moment far fewer bird-days are at risk. Second, if a broiler flock is lost to depopulation, the shed can be **cleaned and restocked within weeks** from the hatchery pipeline — whereas a depopulated layer flock takes roughly a year to rear replacement pullets back into lay. A one-off broiler depopulation is therefore a short supply interruption, not a multi-month hole.

The US 2024–25 experience is the clearest quantified comparator (used as a comparator, not a NZ forecast). Over Oct 2024–Mar 2025, US egg-layers lost ~50.7 million birds and wholesale/retail egg prices "surg[ed] to all-time highs by February"; over the same window **broiler production rose ~3%** and the wholesale broiler composite *fell* ~2% to ~US$1.26/lb, with analysts attributing broiler resilience to the "shorter grow-out cycle" and to broilers sitting away from the Central/Pacific flyways where HPAI concentrates ([Univ. of Arkansas AES yearend 2025](https://aaes.uada.edu/news/yearend-poultry-2025/); [American Farm Bureau](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/avian-influenza-hits-turkeys-and-eggs-hardest)). Broilers are not immune — the US did see individual commercial broiler flocks culled (e.g. >1.5 million birds in two California flocks) — but the sector-level signal is that meat supply and price held while eggs crashed ([American Farm Bureau](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/avian-influenza-hits-turkeys-and-eggs-hardest)).

### Why an H5N1 incursion is less likely to hit meat chickens hard — geography

Broiler production is geographically spread and clustered away from the one place H5N1's proxy (H7N6) actually struck. MPI's report tabulates broiler properties by region: Waikato 79, Taranaki 58, Canterbury 48, Auckland 31, Manawatū-Whanganui 7 — the main clusters — while **Otago, where the H7N6 farm was, has 14 commercial layer properties and no broiler properties**, and the "closest broiler property [was] around 150 km away" from the infected farm ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)). Geographic dispersion across both islands means a localised incursion is unlikely to take out a large share of national meat capacity at once — unlike egg supply, where a single operator (~a third of layers) concentrates the risk.

### What the H7N6 event does — and does not — tell us about meat supply

The Dec-2024 H7N6 response was genuinely well-run (single property, ~160,000 layer hens culled, eradicated in ~5 months — controls lifted and eradication declared 28 April 2025), but it is close to **uninformative about broiler exposure**: it was a free-range *layer* farm, in a region with zero broiler properties, ~150 km from the nearest broiler, and it arose from a domestic LPAI→HPAI mutation in outdoor-foraging hens rather than from the global H5N1 clade ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [RNZ, ~160k culled](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm); [MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report) for the region/distance/property-type detail). It exercised the layer sector's incursion pathway and none of the broiler sector's actual failure modes (see below). So while it should not be read as proof of *egg* readiness (per the #227 finding), it should even less be read as evidence that *chicken-meat* supply is H5N1-ready.

One transferable lesson does carry over: the report notes almost all populated sheds on the index property were already infected by the time depopulation began, and sets the operational bar as "entire shed killing methods and rapid entire property depopulation implemented swiftly after diagnosis" ([MPI H7N6 epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)). Broiler barns are high-density, low-genetic-diversity populations — good conditions for rapid within-shed spread once virus enters — so the *speed* lesson applies to broilers even though the *pathway* mostly does not.

### Where the real meat-supply exposure sits: concentration, chokepoints and status

If grow-out flocks are the resilient part of the system, the fragile parts are the chokepoints above them:

- **Extreme concentration.** Tegel is ~50% of the NZ poultry market, and the four largest producers — Tegel (Auckland, New Plymouth, Christchurch), Inghams (Waikato), PH van den Brink/"Brinks" (Christchurch, South Auckland) and Turks (Manawatū) — supply **over 99%** of NZ poultry meat ([Te Ara](https://teara.govt.nz/en/poultry-industry/page-6); [AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/); [WATTPoultry — Tegel company profile](https://www.wattagnet.com/top-poultry-companies/company/tegel-foods)). Tegel alone processes "over 40 million birds or 70,000 tons of dressed poultry annually" across its three regions — Auckland, New Plymouth and Christchurch ([WATTPoultry — Tegel feed conversion](https://www.wattagnet.com/articles/13751-new-zealand-s-tegel-poultry-achieves-world-s-best-feed-conversion)).
- **Vertical integration = single points of failure.** Each major operator runs its own breeder flocks, hatcheries, feed mills and processing plants ([Te Ara](https://teara.govt.nz/en/poultry-industry/page-6); [WATTPoultry](https://www.wattagnet.com/top-poultry-companies/company/tegel-foods)). An H5N1 hit (or a precautionary depopulation / movement-control order) on a **processing plant** or a **grandparent/parent breeding flock** would bottleneck far more supply, and for far longer, than losing interchangeable grow-out sheds — because breeding stock, not meat birds, is the slow-to-replace layer of the pyramid.
- **Imported genetic-stock dependency.** The breeding pyramid ultimately rests on **imported pedigree genetic eggs** (Cobb/Aviagen), quarantined before joining NZ great-grandparent/grandparent flocks ([AkoVet](https://akovet.org/for-researchers/nz-poultry-home/)). A global H5N1 disruption to those overseas breeding supply chains could impair NZ's ability to rebuild breeding stock after a domestic event — a second-order exposure eggs share but that is especially load-bearing for a fast-turnover meat sector.
- **Trade and status risk.** NZ's poultry-meat exports are small (~$27m, 2017), but an incursion instantly jeopardises the HPAI-free status that underpins any export access. Brazil — the world's largest chicken exporter (~35% of global trade, ~US$10bn in 2024) — saw its **first commercial H5N1 case in May 2025 trigger 60-day import bans from China, the EU, South Korea and others** ([Reuters via Oklahoma Farm Report](https://www.oklahomafarmreport.com/2025/05/19/bird-flu-in-top-chicken-exporter-brazil-triggers-trade-bans/); [MercoPress](https://en.mercopress.com/2025/05/17/brazil-halts-chicken-exports-due-to-bird-flu-outbreak); [The Poultry Site](https://www.thepoultrysite.com/news/2025/08/brazil-chicken-exports-fall-on-bird-flu-trade-bans)). For NZ this is mainly a trade/status risk rather than a domestic-supply risk, but it is the mechanism by which a small incursion becomes an economic event.

### Readiness (shared with the egg finding)

Broilers sit under the same Government–industry readiness architecture as eggs: a Government–industry operational agreement with the Poultry Industry Association (PIANZ) and Egg Producers Federation (EPF) took effect **1 September 2025**, covering Newcastle disease, infectious bursal disease and all HPAI strains including H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b, with industry contributing 45% of readiness costs and leading on-farm response with MPI support ([Beehive — Agreement signed on poultry biosecurity](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)); and MPI's 2025 consultation on mandatory on-farm biosecurity programmes, whose summary of submissions was released 15 April 2026 with decisions still pending ([MPI H5N1 consultation](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)). The March-2026 targeted consultation explicitly included **turkey and duck meat producers**, indicating the regime reaches the meat sector, not just eggs ([MPI H5N1 consultation](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)). The same open caveat applies: the agreement limits cost-shareable activities specifically for an H5N1 outbreak, and no published NZ modelling of a poultry-meat supply/price shock could be found (see the egg finding for further agreement detail — not re-litigated here).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published NZ H5N1 supply/price scenario for poultry meat.** No NZ-specific modelling of a broiler H5N1 shock was found; if MPI or industry has modelled processing-plant or breeding-flock loss, it could show the concentration risk is larger (or smaller) than this qualitative read implies, and would replace the US comparator with a domestic figure.
- **Evidence that H5N1 behaves unlike the housed-broiler expectation in NZ.** If surveillance showed H5N1 readily entering *housed* broiler barns (via staff, fomites, rodents, or the growing free-range broiler segment), the "housed = low exposure" premise — which leans on a 2010 survey — would weaken. Establishment/pathway risk is the subject of sibling issues in stream #225.
- **A processing-plant or breeding-flock incident.** The whole "meat is resilient" case assumes grow-out flocks are the exposed layer. A real H5N1 hit on a Tegel/Inghams processing plant or a grandparent flock — or a movement-control order that idles one — would test the chokepoint risk this finding can only flag qualitatively.
- **The final H5N1 regulations.** As with eggs, whether mandatory biosecurity programmes are enacted (and how they treat free-range broilers and the four integrators) would materially change the readiness assessment ([MPI H5N1 consultation](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)).

**Could not verify:** any NZ-specific quantified poultry-meat H5N1 economic model; a second independent source for the imported-genetic-egg dependency beyond AkoVet; a precise current split of housed vs free-range *broiler* production volume (only the ~30%-of-farms figure); whether NZ processing capacity has any redundancy to absorb the loss of one major plant; the exact current national broiler flock/tonnage (dated/mixed-unit sources).

## Open follow-up questions

- Is NZ's poultry-meat supply resilience actually bottlenecked at **processing plants and breeding flocks** rather than grow-out sheds — and is there any redundancy if one of the four integrators loses a plant to H5N1 or a movement-control order? (Good candidate for a dedicated resilience/redundancy research issue.)
- How exposed is the ~30% free-range **broiler** segment to the same wild-bird pathway that caused H7N6, and has that segment grown enough since the 2010 survey to change the "housed sector has little exposure" conclusion?
- What is NZ's dependency on **imported pedigree genetic stock** (Cobb/Aviagen), and how would a global H5N1 disruption to overseas great-grandparent supply chains affect NZ's ability to rebuild after a domestic event?
- Does the answer differ for **duck and turkey meat** (smaller, differently housed, differently traded) — the sectors MPI specifically consulted in March 2026?
