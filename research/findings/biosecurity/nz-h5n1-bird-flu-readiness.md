# NZ is still free of H5N1 bird flu and has a plausible One Health plan, but public evidence leaves surveillance intensity and wildlife coverage open

> **Scope note (stream root, depth 0).** This is the framing baseline for Stream #225.
> It answers the core question — *what H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b is, how it could reach NZ,
> NZ's current status, and the architecture of who is responsible for what* — to the full
> method standard, and gives an overview pass on wildlife, poultry, human risk and gaps.
> The deep dives on each are split into four research sub-issues: wildlife exposure
> ([#226](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/226)), poultry & egg
> supply ([#227](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/227)),
> human/zoonotic readiness
> ([#228](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/228)), and where the
> gaps sit ([#229](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/229)).

## Executive answer

- **NZ has never had a case of H5 bird flu and remains free of the H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b panzootic** that has killed wildlife, poultry and some mammals worldwide. The dead giant petrel found on Farewell Spit on 2 July 2026 that prompted this issue most likely died in storm conditions and is being tested as a precaution; every dead/sick-wildlife report to MPI so far has tested negative ([RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/environment/660641/giant-petrel-found-dead-on-farewell-spit-to-be-tested-for-bird-flu); [Wildlife Health Australia, 26 Jun 2026](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Incidents/Incident-Information/alert-h5n1-h5-bird-flu-global-outbreak)).
- **The threat is closer than it was.** Australia recorded its *first* H5 high-pathogenicity avian influenza detections in wild birds in June 2026; WOAH says the first notified case was HPAI H5N1 related to clade 2.3.4.4b in a migratory brown skua at Cape Le Grand, WA, and Australian DAFF reported five confirmed wild-bird cases by 9am AEST on 3 July 2026 ([WOAH, 30 Jun 2026](https://www.woah.org/en/australia-notifies-first-case-of-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-a-wild-bird/); [Australian DAFF, accessed 3 Jul 2026](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/campaigns/birdflu)). The most likely route into NZ is a migratory wild bird, which cannot be stopped at the border ([Te Niwha Q&A](https://www.teniwha.com/news/q-and-a-on-avian-influenza); [RNZ, 20 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/607927/biosecurity-minister-says-nz-prepared-for-deadly-bird-flu-outbreak)).
- **Do not confuse this with the contained 2024 event.** NZ's only HPAI outbreak was H7N6 in a single Otago poultry farm in December 2024 — a *locally evolved* strain, not the global H5N1 clade; it was eradicated (culling and decontamination concluded 27 Dec 2024) with no human cases ([MoH briefing, 17 Jun 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)).
- **A coordinated plan exists.** MPI/Biosecurity NZ (incursion), DOC (wildlife) and the Ministry of Health/Health NZ (human risk) share a joint "One Health Approach to H5N1" guide; the poultry sector signed an operational agreement in Aug 2025 and mandatory farm-biosecurity regulations were consulted on in Sep–Nov 2025; DOC has completed a world-first vaccine trial protecting five critically endangered species; and Health NZ has an antiviral stockpile and ICU H5 subtyping ([MPI](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/); [DOC, 20 Aug 2025](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2025-media-releases/docs-bird-flu-vaccine-trial-a-success/); [MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)).
- **The two visible soft spots:** the public record lists reporting, annual waterfowl sampling, wildlife-hospital testing, DOC coordination and academic sampling, but it does not publish enough sample-size or flyway-coverage detail to judge whether NZ would detect a wild-bird incursion early; and the wildlife vaccine work so far reaches only five managed threatened bird species — a completed trial, with a broader vaccination plan still in development — not the broader seabird colonies or marine mammals that H5N1 has affected overseas ([MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/); [MPI, protecting native species](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/protecting-our-native-species-from-avian-influenza); [DOC, 20 Aug 2025](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2025-media-releases/docs-bird-flu-vaccine-trial-a-success/); [Wildlife Health Australia, 26 Jun 2026](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Incidents/Incident-Information/alert-h5n1-h5-bird-flu-global-outbreak)).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the current status, the agencies' roles and the documented plans are **High** confidence (multiple official, current sources agree). The judgement of *how adequate* that preparedness is, is inherently **Medium**: it rests partly on documents that redact key figures, and the gap analysis was deliberately kept at overview level and handed to sub-issues #226–#229.

## Evidence

### What H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b is, and the scale of the global panzootic

The strain of concern is high-pathogenicity avian influenza (HPAI) **H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b** — the lineage that has spread globally through wild birds and into other animals, "resulting in some instances of mammal-to-human transmission," with "no sustained human-to-human transmission" reported to date ([MoH briefing, 17 Jun 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)). WHO says currently circulating zoonotic influenza viruses have not demonstrated sustained human-to-human transmission, and CDC says the current A(H5) bird-flu public-health risk is low while sporadic human cases continue to be monitored ([WHO fact sheet, 2026](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/influenza-%28avian-and-other-zoonotic%29); [CDC current situation, 6 Mar 2026](https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/situation-summary/index.html)). The Ministry of Health's briefing records the scale: in the United States alone "more than 70 humans, 170 million birds and over 1,000 dairy herds have been infected," and from 2003 to 2 June 2025 "a total of 978 human cases of H5N1 infection have been reported globally to the World Health Organisation from 25 countries," most resulting in severe illness ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)). This is the same subtype "predominantly circulating in poultry, wild birds and mammals globally" that WOAH names in the Australian notification ([WOAH, 30 Jun 2026](https://www.woah.org/en/australia-notifies-first-case-of-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-a-wild-bird/)).

### Current status: NZ free; Australia no longer free

New Zealand "has never had a case of H5 bird flu," and MPI says it is "closely monitoring the movement of the virus and was prepared for its arrival" ([RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/environment/660641/giant-petrel-found-dead-on-farewell-spit-to-be-tested-for-bird-flu)). A March 2026 OFFLU overview described Oceania (Australia, New Zealand and Pacific Islands) as the only continental region still free from HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b; that regional context changed when WOAH was notified on 20 June 2026 of Australia's **first** HPAI H5N1 detection — a migratory brown skua (*Stercorarius antarcticus*), "a wild bird, not mainland poultry," at Cape Le Grand National Park ~700 km southeast of Perth, with a virus "related to H5N1 HPAI clade 2.3.4.4b" ([OFFLU, Mar 2026](https://offlu.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/2026_Final-OFFLU-statement-III.pdf); [WOAH, 30 Jun 2026](https://www.woah.org/en/australia-notifies-first-case-of-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-a-wild-bird/)). Australian DAFF says Australia had five confirmed H5 high-pathogenicity avian influenza cases in wild birds by 9am AEST on 3 July 2026, with no evidence of infection in poultry or the wider agriculture industry ([Australian DAFF](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/campaigns/birdflu)). MPI's chief veterinary officer Dr Mary van Andel said there had been "five detections of H5 in migratory seabirds in Australia but no reports of transmission," and assessed "a low risk of infected birds migrating to New Zealand"; she also noted "an uptick in reports of dead and sick wildlife to MPI" since the Australian detections, "all [of which] had tested negative" ([RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/environment/660641/giant-petrel-found-dead-on-farewell-spit-to-be-tested-for-bird-flu)). The Farewell Spit giant petrel that triggered this issue "most likely died after being battered by storm conditions" and was found by Pest Free Onetahua staff ([RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/environment/660641/giant-petrel-found-dead-on-farewell-spit-to-be-tested-for-bird-flu)).

### The pathway into NZ: migratory birds you cannot stop at the border

The dominant route is wild birds, not trade. "Across the world this virus has spread through wildlife, primarily wild birds," and "it is very hard to stop it arriving because we can't shut our borders to migratory birds" (Prof. Jemma Geoghegan, University of Otago; Dr Rachel Stanyer, DOC veterinarian) ([Te Niwha Q&A](https://www.teniwha.com/news/q-and-a-on-avian-influenza)). Biosecurity Minister Andrew Hoggard frames NZ's advantage as time, not immunity: "Our geographical isolation has protected us and given us time to prepare," and "if it arrives here, it will almost certainly establish itself in wild birds" ([RNZ, 20 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/607927/biosecurity-minister-says-nz-prepared-for-deadly-bird-flu-outbreak); [Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)).

### Distinguishing the contained 2024 H7N6 event from the H5N1 clade of concern

NZ's "first (and only) HPAI outbreak occurred in December 2024 where H7N6 (another strain of HPAI) was detected in a free-range poultry farm in Otago." Critically, "the H7N6 virus likely originated from a LPAI [low-pathogenicity avian influenza] within a poultry flock on the farm, which then mutated" — i.e. it evolved *locally*, and is **not** the global 2.3.4.4b clade. "Culling of chickens and extensive decontamination of the site concluded on 27 December 2024," "more than 100 exposed workers were monitored [with] no human cases … detected," and "internationally, no human cases of H7N6 have been reported" ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)). The affected property was Mainland Poultry's Hillgrove farm in Otago, where about 160,000 hens were culled ([RNZ, "Nearly 160k hens culled at Otago farm," 9 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm)). MPI's 4 December 2024 situation update — which does not itself name Hillgrove or give the cull total — recorded that culling began on a "farm managed by Mainland Poultry" and that a separate, smaller property had "around 6,000 birds" ([MPI situation update, 4 Dec 2024](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/news/media-releases/situation-update-h7n6-bird-flu-detection-otago-4-december-2024)). The takeaway: NZ has proven it can contain a poultry HPAI incursion, but that was a self-limiting local strain — not the wildlife-adapted panzootic strain.

### The preparedness architecture: who does what

**Lead agencies and the joint plan.** NZ's readiness "takes a One Health approach"; a national guide, the "One Health Approach to H5N1," was "jointly developed by MPI, [the] Department of Conservation, [the] Ministry of Health and Health New Zealand," recognising the connection between people, animals and the environment ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf); [MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/)). MPI's planning "aims to" do four things: "reduce the impact on native species"; "maintain supply of poultry meat and eggs to the domestic market and maintain access to overseas markets where possible"; "reduce the impact on the commercial poultry industry"; and "protect human health" ([MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/)).

**Poultry & food supply.** In August 2025 "the Poultry Industry Association of New Zealand and the Egg Producers Federation signed an operational agreement with the government on how [to] jointly prepare for and respond to exotic poultry diseases" ([MPI](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/)). On 17 September 2025 the government opened a six-week consultation (to 2 November 2025) on regulations that would require commercial operators to run audited "avian biosecurity control programmes," with standards for depopulation, disposal, cleaning and disinfection, plus offences and fines for non-compliance ([Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)). *Supply and price consequences are not quantified here — split to [#227](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/227).*

**Wildlife (DOC).** DOC completed what it calls a world-first vaccine trial, giving two doses of Poulvac Flufend (a killed, low-pathogenicity vaccine) to "small numbers of 5 native bird species" — **kākāpō, takahē, kakī (black stilt), kākāriki karaka (orange-fronted parakeet) and tūturuatu (shore plover)**. No bird showed an adverse reaction; all five showed a strong initial antibody response, lasting ≥6 months in four species, though "in kakī the levels dropped at 3 months." MPI "approved the vaccine trial, which was conducted by DOC veterinarians under strictly controlled conditions"; the trial is not yet an operational vaccination programme — "MPI and DOC are working on a broader vaccination plan for the 5 species and potentially others that might also benefit from vaccination if H5N1 reaches Aotearoa" ([DOC media release, 20 Aug 2025](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2025-media-releases/docs-bird-flu-vaccine-trial-a-success/); [MPI, protecting native species](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/protecting-our-native-species-from-avian-influenza)). DOC is also "leading work to mitigate risks to threatened species on public conservation land … developing site-specific response plans and species protection plans," alongside predator control and population-resilience work ([DOC wildlife health](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/)). *Which colonies are actually exposed — split to [#226](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/226).*

**Surveillance.** The system combines an "annual sampling and testing programme at non-migratory waterfowl sites with Fish & Game New Zealand"; contracts with wildlife hospitals to test wild birds (added as "enhanced surveillance" after the H7N6 detection); monitoring by poultry groups, bird owners, vets and rehabilitation centres; and DOC coordination on suspected exotic disease in wild birds. The public is asked to report "three or more sick or dead wild birds in a group" to MPI's hotline (0800 80 99 66). MPI representatives visited the UK in August 2024 to study HPAI response ([MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/); [MPI, protecting native species](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/protecting-our-native-species-from-avian-influenza)). A dedicated academic surveillance study (Te Niwha / University of Otago) swabs Australasian gannets at Farewell Spit and runs a virome study ([RNZ, Our Changing World](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/ourchangingworld/564326/our-changing-world-getting-ready-for-h5n1-bird-flu)).

**Human/zoonotic readiness.** MoH "assesses the overall public health risk of H5N1 to New Zealand as … low," citing "the very low likelihood of importation of a case, very low likelihood of human-to-human transmission and the high impact of disease." WHO similarly says currently circulating zoonotic influenza viruses have not demonstrated sustained human-to-human transmission, and CDC assesses the current A(H5) public-health risk as low while continuing surveillance ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf); [WHO fact sheet](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/influenza-%28avian-and-other-zoonotic%29); [CDC current situation](https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/situation-summary/index.html)). Readiness measures include: oseltamivir (Tamiflu) plus "two alternative antivirals" recommended, with Pharmac engaged to supply cases and exposed people via hospital pharmacies; a national supply of influenza antivirals "including Tamiflu … [for] treatment or prophylaxis … in the event of an influenza pandemic," which "the WHO and infectious diseases experts" expect to be effective against current strains; H5 subtyping of "all intensive care unit (ICU) patients" who test positive for influenza A, to catch early zoonotic transmission; and the "New Zealand Pandemic Plan [as] the operative document in the event of sustained human-to-human transmission" ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)). *Depth of that readiness — split to [#228](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/228).*

### Where the gaps sit (overview)

Three soft spots are visible even at baseline level, though a full assessment is handed to [#229](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/229):

1. **Surveillance intensity is not visible enough to judge early-detection adequacy.** Public sources show several channels — public reports of ≥3 dead birds, annual waterfowl sampling, wildlife-hospital testing, DOC coordination, poultry/vet monitoring and an academic gannet study — but they do not publish wild-bird sample numbers, target species, geographic spread or migratory-flyway coverage. Experts also flag genuine "knowledge gaps about how wild birds in particular transmit viruses" and how migratory and non-migratory birds interact (Prof. Jemma Geoghegan) ([MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/); [Te Niwha Q&A](https://www.teniwha.com/news/q-and-a-on-avian-influenza); [RNZ, Our Changing World](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/ourchangingworld/564326/our-changing-world-getting-ready-for-h5n1-bird-flu)).
2. **The visible vaccination defence reaches few species.** The completed vaccine trial — and the broader vaccination plan MPI and DOC are now developing from it — covers five critically endangered species that can be captured and dosed; that is a narrow, managed-species defence rather than population-level protection for the colonial seabirds and marine mammals that OFFLU and Wildlife Health Australia identify as vulnerable to H5N1 impacts overseas ([DOC media release, 20 Aug 2025](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2025-media-releases/docs-bird-flu-vaccine-trial-a-success/); [MPI, protecting native species](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/protecting-our-native-species-from-avian-influenza); [OFFLU, Mar 2026](https://offlu.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/2026_Final-OFFLU-statement-III.pdf); [Wildlife Health Australia, 26 Jun 2026](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Incidents/Incident-Information/alert-h5n1-h5-bird-flu-global-outbreak)).
3. **Human-side detail is thin in public documents.** The June 2025 MoH briefing redacts figures under s 9(2)(c) of the Official Information Act and does not detail human-vaccine procurement, so the depth of pandemic-tail readiness cannot be judged from the public record alone ([MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)).

None of this is cause for alarm about *current* status — NZ is free of H5N1 and the confirmed facts stay firmly separate from the scenario. The point is that readiness is real but uneven: agencies have documented One Health, poultry, wildlife and human-health measures, while public documents still leave surveillance intensity, wildlife reach and some human-side quantities unresolved ([MPI surveillance & planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza/); [DOC media release, 20 Aug 2025](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2025-media-releases/docs-bird-flu-vaccine-trial-a-success/); [MoH briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf)).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A confirmed H5N1 detection in or near NZ** (e.g. a positive test on one of the current dead-wildlife reports, or spread of the Australian wild-bird cases toward NZ's migratory routes) would flip the status section from "free" to "incursion risk imminent" and change every downstream judgement. The Farewell Spit petrel result is pending as of 3 Jul 2026.
- **The redacted portions of the MoH briefing (s 9(2)(c))**, or a released copy of the "One Health Approach to H5N1" guide and MPI's response briefing [ref B25-0334], could materially raise or lower confidence in human-side readiness — I could not verify antiviral stockpile *volumes*, human-vaccine procurement, or the actual surveillance sampling *intensity*.
- **Independent evidence on surveillance coverage** — e.g. how many wild-bird samples per year, in which species and places, over what flyway coverage, versus an explicit early-detection benchmark — would confirm or refute the surveillance-intensity gap. This is the crux of [#229](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/229).
- **A post-incident review of the 2024 H7N6 response** would test the assumption that NZ's containment capability transfers to a wildlife-adapted H5N1 strain (a very different problem, since H5N1 would establish in wild birds).
- I did **not** use Heard Island / sub-Antarctic elephant-seal detections as evidence for NZ's current status. Wildlife Health Australia reports H5 bird flu detections and elephant-seal pup mortality there, but the NZ status conclusion relies on WOAH's June 2026 Australian notification, Australian DAFF's current wild-bird case count and MPI's statement that NZ reports had tested negative as of 3 July 2026.
