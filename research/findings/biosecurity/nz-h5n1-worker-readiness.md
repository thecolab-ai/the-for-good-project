# NZ has credible H5N1 worker-protection guidance, but public evidence of delivery readiness is thin

## Executive answer

- New Zealand's public occupational-health setup for an H5N1 arrival is strongest at the *guidance and legal-duty* layer: Health NZ has a detailed April 2025 workplace guide covering poultry, dairy, wildlife/conservation, veterinary, animal-lab, healthcare and medical-lab workers; WorkSafe's HSWA guidance makes PCBUs responsible for managing work-related illness risks; and MPI's dairy/livestock and poultry sector pages both point workplace managers back to the Health NZ workplace guidance [Health NZ workplace guidance, Apr 2025](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf), [WorkSafe HSWA guide](https://www.worksafe.govt.nz/managing-health-and-safety/getting-started/introduction-hswa-special-guide/), [MPI dairy/livestock page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/avian-influenza-dairy-cattle-and-other-livestock), [MPI poultry page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/commercial-poultry-and-avian-influenza).
- The weak point is *operational proof*: I found no public NZ source giving the size or allocation of PPE reserves for rural, farm, wildlife or laboratory workforces; no public audit of fit-testing or PPE uptake in those groups; and, although the Health NZ CDC Manual does set out a public channel for post-exposure oseltamivir (hospital-pharmacy dispensing, with local public-health teams or couriers taking it to exposed people off-site), no public evidence that this channel can meet the Health NZ "within one day" contact-management target for dispersed rural, wildlife or lab workers [Health NZ CDC Manual](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/conditions/communicable-disease-control-manual/avian-influenza), [Ministry of Health National Reserve Supplies](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/emergency-management/national-reserve-supplies).
- The best NZ stress test is the December 2024 Otago H7N6 poultry response, where more than 100 exposed workers were monitored and no human cases were detected, but that is only weak evidence for H5N1 readiness because the same Ministry of Health briefing says no human H7N6 cases have been reported internationally while H5N1 has caused human infections overseas [MoH HPAI briefing, paras 18, 22-23](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).
- The US dairy outbreak shows why guidance is not enough: CDC recorded 71 US human A(H5) cases since February 2024, mostly detected through human monitoring, and a Colorado study found respirator use and use of all recommended PPE remained low among exposed dairy workers even after outbreak detection [CDC current situation, 6 Mar 2026](https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/situation-summary/index.html), [CDC MMWR, 7 Nov 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/wr/mm7344a2.htm).
- ACC probably has a route for an occupationally acquired H5N1 infection, because work-related infections can be covered through WRGPDI settings, including single exposure to infections from animals or carcasses, but I found no H5N1-specific ACC guidance or pre-decision statement for farm, wildlife or lab workers [MBIE WRGPDI explainer, 30 Oct 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/employment-and-skills/employment-legislation-reviews/review-framework-for-list-of-occupational-diseases/discussion-document-seeking-feedback-on-the-proposed-additions-to-schedule-2-of-the-accident-compensation-act-2001/background-on-work-related-gradual-process-disease-or-infection-injuries).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high confidence that the written guidance and legal duties exist; medium confidence that delivery readiness is publicly unproven, because an absence in public sources does not prove agencies lack internal logistics, procurement or enforcement plans.

## Evidence

### What NZ already has on paper

Health NZ's April 2025 *Avian Influenza Health and Safety Guidance for Workplaces* is the clearest public occupational-health document for H5N1 readiness: it says it is for workplace managers and people who work with birds or other animals at risk of avian influenza, and it names at-risk groups including poultry and dairy farmers/workers, cull/disposal/disinfection workers, outbreak responders, veterinary staff, zoo and sanctuary staff, commercial fishers, conservation workers, animal laboratory staff, healthcare and medical-laboratory workers, wildlife researchers/volunteers, roadside carcass workers and workers exposed to unpasteurised milk if dairy cattle become infected [Health NZ workplace guidance, pp2-3](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf).

The same guidance is operationally detailed at the workplace level: before detection, employers must identify avian-influenza exposure risk and take reasonably practicable steps to protect workers; after HPAI detection, employers should provide well-fitted PPE and PPE training, signage, clean/dirty areas for donning and doffing, handwashing, PPE disposal, laundry and equipment-disinfection facilities, higher-risk worker health monitoring, and isolation/medical-advice support for symptomatic workers [Health NZ workplace guidance, pp4-5](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf).

The PPE recommendations are specific enough to be usable but demanding enough to expose a delivery risk: recommended items include nitrile gloves, tight-fitting goggles where aerosols or splashes may occur, designated waterproof footwear, coveralls, head/hair cover, and P2/N95 respiratory protection; the guide says all negative-pressure filtering respirators, including disposable and reusable respirators, must be fit-tested before use [Health NZ workplace guidance, pp5-7](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf). WorkSafe's respiratory-protection guidance independently says PCBUs must ensure RPE fits each worker by fit testing, that one size/model is unlikely to fit everyone, and that fit testing should occur when RPE is first provided and be repeated at least yearly or after material facial changes [WorkSafe RPE advice](https://www.worksafe.govt.nz/topic-and-industry/personal-protective-equipment-ppe/respiratory-protective-equipment/advice-for-businesses/).

WorkSafe's general HSWA guidance gives the enforcement frame rather than an H5N1-specific plan: HSWA covers nearly all work and workplaces, requires risks to health and safety from work to be managed, includes work-related health conditions as well as injuries, and gives WorkSafe functions to monitor and enforce compliance, provide guidance and investigate incidents [WorkSafe HSWA guide](https://www.worksafe.govt.nz/managing-health-and-safety/getting-started/introduction-hswa-special-guide/). This means WorkSafe has a statutory lane if farms, laboratories or response contractors fail to manage biological exposure risks, but I found no public WorkSafe H5N1 inspection or enforcement plan specific to dairy, poultry, wildlife or laboratory work.

MPI's sector pages reinforce the same framework rather than adding a separate worker-health delivery plan. Its dairy/livestock page says NZ has never had H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b, explains US dairy spillover and says infected US dairy workers were in close contact with infected cattle and not wearing appropriate PPE, then points workplace managers to Health NZ workplace guidance [MPI dairy/livestock page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/avian-influenza-dairy-cattle-and-other-livestock). Its poultry page says H5N1 is likely to become widespread in wild birds and impossible to eradicate if it arrives, says high biosecurity is the most effective containment measure, and points poultry operators to the same Health NZ workplace guidance [MPI poultry page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/commercial-poultry-and-avian-influenza).

### Post-exposure prophylaxis has a documented delivery channel, but rural timeliness is unproven

Health NZ's CDC Manual says high-risk contacts and exposed people should be identified and, within one day, assessed, offered post-exposure prophylaxis when appropriate and actively monitored; it also says a medical officer of health can prescribe oseltamivir after infectious-disease consultation [Health NZ CDC Manual](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/conditions/communicable-disease-control-manual/avian-influenza). The workplace guidance is slightly more operational: if lower-level PPE, no PPE, or a PPE breach occurred, the public health service may advise Tamiflu and "arrange for it to be dispensed" [Health NZ workplace guidance, p9](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf).

A public last-mile channel does exist, and I should be precise about it. The CDC Manual's "Guidance for accessing antiviral post-exposure prophylaxis" says post-exposure oseltamivir (approved for this use on 1 December 2024 and funded by the National Public Health Service) can only be dispensed for contacts or exposed persons from hospital pharmacies, not community pharmacies; local public health services are advised to use their usual local processes for accessing medication from the closest hospital pharmacy to where the contact resides; and in the event of a large number of contacts or exposed persons, antivirals should be collected from the hospital pharmacy by a local public health team member (or similar, e.g. courier service) and provided to all contacts/exposed persons off the hospital site [Health NZ CDC Manual](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/conditions/communicable-disease-control-manual/avian-influenza). That is a defined channel and last-mile handoff, and it sits alongside the National Reserve Supply, from which the Ministry of Health says antivirals will be released to hospitals and public health units if required, with the stockpile stored across multiple NZ locations; the 2025 HPAI briefing adds that the Ministry and Health NZ have engaged Pharmac to enable antiviral treatment for cases and exposed people through hospital pharmacies [MoH National Reserve Supplies](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/emergency-management/national-reserve-supplies), [MoH HPAI briefing, paras 33-35](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).

What the public record does not evidence is capacity or timeliness for dispersed rural delivery. The CDC Manual sets a "within one day" target for assessing high-risk contacts, offering prophylaxis and starting active monitoring, but I found no public source showing whether this hospital-pharmacy-plus-courier model can meet that target for a milker, culler, DOC field worker, contractor or laboratory worker who may be hours from the closest hospital pharmacy, nor how it scales if a single outbreak generates many simultaneously exposed workers [Health NZ CDC Manual](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/conditions/communicable-disease-control-manual/avian-influenza). So the fair conclusion is narrower than "no protocol": the channel is documented, but its rural capacity and timeliness are not publicly evidenced.

### PPE reserve and uptake are the largest public gap

The Ministry of Health says the National Reserve Supply stockpile is continually reviewed, located across NZ, and operated under a usage policy covering logistics for moving supplies from bulk stores, but the current public page does not publish item counts for PPE or earmark PPE for non-health farm/wildlife/lab workers [MoH National Reserve Supplies](https://www.health.govt.nz/strategies-initiatives/programmes-and-initiatives/emergency-management/national-reserve-supplies). MPI says it is working with DOC, the Ministry of Health, Health NZ and industry partners to prepare for H5N1, and Health NZ says employers should provide PPE and training once HPAI is detected, but I found no public NZ evidence of how many P2/N95 respirators, goggles, coveralls, gloves, boots, PAPRs or fit-test slots would be available for dispersed rural and wildlife workforces [MPI dairy/livestock page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/avian-influenza-dairy-cattle-and-other-livestock), [Health NZ workplace guidance, pp4-7](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf).

The US dairy evidence makes this a material gap rather than a paperwork concern. CDC's Colorado survey found that among 83 dairy workers on three infected farms, use of PPE increased after detection, but reported N95 or other respirator use among workers exposed to ill cows was still only 26% after detection and use of all CDC-recommended PPE was 5% after detection [CDC MMWR, 7 Nov 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/wr/mm7344a2.htm). The same study found 72 of 83 interviewed workers were interviewed in Spanish, illustrating that worker communication, trust and language are part of PPE delivery, not just stock availability [CDC MMWR, 7 Nov 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/wr/mm7344a2.htm). This transfers only partly to NZ, but it is the best real-world evidence that dairy PPE guidance can fail at the point of use.

### Dairy pathways are not just "bird contact"

NZ's dairy context matters because the exposed worker pathway in cattle is plausibly milk and milking-equipment exposure. MPI says NZ's farming practices differ from the US and assesses bird-to-cattle or livestock transmission risk as low if H5N1 arrives, but it also tells farmers to maintain milking hygiene, clean and disinfect borrowed milk-feeding equipment, avoid cross-site staffing, and monitor for cattle signs such as reduced milk production and thickened colostrum-like milk [MPI dairy/livestock page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/avian-influenza-dairy-cattle-and-other-livestock). Stats NZ counted 5.7 million dairy cattle at 30 June 2025, and DairyNZ's latest dairy statistics report says 4.68 million cows were in milk in the 2024/25 season, so a cattle spillover would involve a large, geographically dispersed sector [Stats NZ agricultural production, year to Jun 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/agricultural-production-statistics-year-to-june-2025/), [DairyNZ 2024/25 Dairy Statistics](https://www.dairynz.co.nz/media/oglesqfm/nz-dairy-statistics-24-25.pdf).

The milking-equipment pathway is supported by two independent sources. A CDC *Emerging Infectious Diseases* study found H5N1 in unpasteurised milk remained infectious on rubber inflation liners and stainless steel after one hour, and concluded that contaminated milking-unit surfaces create H5N1 transmission risk to workers during milking [Le Sage et al., EID, 2024](https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/eid/article/30/8/24-0775_article). A 2025 *Nature Medicine* study found infectious H5N1 can persist in raw milk and raw-milk cheese under refrigeration and says human infections have been linked to exposure to and/or ingestion of contaminated raw milk from affected cows [Nature Medicine, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-025-04010-0). These are US-based and experimental/epidemiological sources, not evidence of NZ cattle infection; their relevance is that NZ's worker guidance correctly includes raw milk and dairy cattle, but the public delivery evidence for milking-parlour PPE is still missing.

### Poultry and wildlife/conservation workers have clearer response precedent, but H7N6 is a weak H5N1 proxy

The December 2024 Otago H7N6 outbreak tested some NZ occupational-health machinery: MPI reported culling, secure carcass/litter disposal, movement restrictions, an enhanced surveillance zone and a regional control centre, and the later Ministry of Health HPAI briefing says more than 100 exposed workers were monitored with no human cases detected [MPI H7N6 situation update, 4 Dec 2024](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/news/media-releases/situation-update-h7n6-bird-flu-detection-otago-4-december-2024), [MoH HPAI briefing, para 23](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf). This is a positive sign for public-health monitoring during a poultry response, but it cannot prove H5N1 readiness because the same briefing says almost all global H5N1 human infections have been linked to close animal or contaminated-environment contact, while no H7N6 human cases have been reported internationally [MoH HPAI briefing, paras 18, 23](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).

For DOC and wildlife workers, the public guidance again names the risk but does not publish resourcing. DOC says MPI would lead any HPAI response in partnership with DOC, the Ministry of Health and Health NZ, and says DOC is leading work to mitigate risks to threatened species on public conservation land [DOC avian influenza page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/). Health NZ's workplace guide explicitly includes conservation workers, commercial fishers and wildlife staff/volunteers/researchers, and its PPE matrix includes higher-risk activities such as contact with sick or dead birds or contaminated fomites [Health NZ workplace guidance, pp3, 10-15](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf). I found no public inventory of DOC or contractor PPE, fit-testing coverage, training completion, or protocols for deploying clean/dirty areas, laundry and contaminated-waste handling at remote colony or carcass sites.

### Laboratory workers are recognised, but NZ public lab-worker detail is sparse

Health NZ's workplace guidance includes animal laboratory staff, medical laboratory workers and staff handling clinical waste in the at-risk occupational groups [Health NZ workplace guidance, p3](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/general-public/topics/diseases-conditions/avian-influenza-health-safety-guidance.pdf). The CDC Manual states that suspected avian-influenza cases require appropriate respiratory/eye specimens and laboratory testing through public-health processes, while the 2025 HPAI briefing says all ICU patients nationwide who test positive for influenza A are subtyped to detect H5N1 [Health NZ CDC Manual](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/guidance-standards/topic/conditions/communicable-disease-control-manual/avian-influenza), [MoH HPAI briefing, paras 29-32](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).

What is not public is a current NZ H5N1 laboratory occupational-health protocol equivalent to the CDC's laboratory biosafety page, which specifies clinical-laboratory and support-staff precautions for specimens potentially containing novel influenza A/H5N1 viruses [CDC laboratory biosafety guidance](https://www.cdc.gov/bird-flu/php/severe-potential/guidelines-for-laboratory-biosafety.html). This is not evidence that NZ labs are unprepared; it means the public record is too thin to assess lab-worker training, biosafety cabinet/centrifuge requirements, respiratory protection, specimen-routing cutoffs, or staffing resilience from open sources alone.

### ACC cover is plausible, not pre-confirmed for H5N1

ACC cover is not absent, but it is not pre-clear either. MBIE explains that work-related gradual process, disease or infection injuries can be covered where an injury is caused by a work-related task or environment; examples include single exposure to harmful agents such as certain infections from animals or carcasses; and there are two routes, either the section 30(2) test or a Schedule 2 occupational-disease entry [MBIE WRGPDI explainer](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/employment-and-skills/employment-legislation-reviews/review-framework-for-list-of-occupational-diseases/discussion-document-seeking-feedback-on-the-proposed-additions-to-schedule-2-of-the-accident-compensation-act-2001/background-on-work-related-gradual-process-disease-or-infection-injuries). Because I found no ACC or MBIE source naming H5N1 specifically, the defensible conclusion is that an occupational H5N1 infection would likely be assessed under general work-related infection settings, not that cover is guaranteed for every exposed worker.

## What would change this conclusion

- **PPE readiness:** A current Health NZ/MPI/DOC/industry inventory showing quantities, locations, trigger thresholds, and allocation rules for respirators, goggles, coveralls, gloves, boots, face shields/PAPRs and fit-testing capacity for dairy, poultry, wildlife/conservation and laboratory workers would move confidence upward.
- **Delivery readiness:** A public operational protocol for dispensing oseltamivir to exposed workers already exists (hospital-pharmacy dispensing with public-health-team/courier handoff off-site, per the CDC Manual); what would move confidence is evidence that this channel can actually meet the "within one day" target at rural scale — a tabletop exercise, after-action report, or timing data for dispersed farm, wildlife or lab workers.
- **Compliance evidence:** Fit-testing records, PPE-training completion, exercises, after-action reports, or industry audits for farms, DOC contractors, wildlife responders and diagnostic labs would test whether the guidance is implementable.
- **WorkSafe/ACC specificity:** A WorkSafe H5N1 enforcement or inspection plan, or an ACC decision tree/OIA response on occupational zoonotic influenza claims, would reduce the current uncertainty.
- **What I could not verify:** I could not verify any PPE reserve earmarked for non-health rural or wildlife workers, current P2/N95 fit-test coverage, employer compliance among small farms/contractors, DOC remote-site occupational-health resourcing, lab-worker biosafety implementation, or an H5N1-specific ACC position. These may exist internally; they are not visible in the public sources I checked.
