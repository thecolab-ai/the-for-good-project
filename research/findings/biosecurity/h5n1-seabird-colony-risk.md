# NZ's largest H5N1 wildlife exposure sits in colonial seabirds and sea lions, mostly outside DOC's vaccination reach

## Executive answer

- The highest-exposure native wildlife groups are dense, mobile colonies: tākapu/Australasian gannet colonies at Cape Kidnappers, Muriwai and Farewell Spit; tara/white-fronted tern and gull colonies; Hutton's shearwater colonies above Kaikōura; the single Westland petrel/tāiko breeding range near Punakaiki; northern royal albatross colonies in the Chatham Islands and Taiaroa Head; and New Zealand sea lion breeding colonies in the Auckland/Campbell subantarctics, Rakiura/Stewart Island, Otago and the Catlins [NZ Birds Online, Australasian gannet](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/australasian-gannet), [NZ Birds Online, white-fronted tern](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/white-fronted-tern), [NZ Birds Online, black-billed gull](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/black-billed-gull), [NZ Birds Online, Hutton's shearwater](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/huttons-shearwater), [DOC, Westland petrel](https://www.doc.govt.nz/nature/native-animals/birds/birds-a-z/westland-petrel-taiko/), [NZ Birds Online, northern royal albatross](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/northern-royal-albatross), [DOC, New Zealand sea lion](https://www.doc.govt.nz/nature/native-animals/marine-mammals/seals/new-zealand-sea-lion/).
- DOC's vaccination response reaches a narrow extinction-insurance layer: about 300 core breeding birds from five intensively managed species, namely kākāpō, takahē, tchūriwat'/tūturuatu/shore plover, kakī/black stilt and kākāriki karaka/orange-fronted parakeet [DOC media release, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/). It does not materially cover free-living seabird or marine-mammal colony mortality, because DOC says it is not possible to vaccinate all birds and is focusing only where two vaccine doses can be safely given [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/).
- The strongest species-level concern is for gannets and terns/gulls, because MPI selected Australasian gannet, white-fronted tern and black-billed gull as representative NZ spread-pathway species with colonial/flocking traits, and overseas H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b has already caused high mortality in closely related northern gannets and colony-breeding seabirds [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct), [Lane et al., 2023](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ibi.13275), [Pohlmann et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12016834/).
- The strongest place-based conservation concern is for range-restricted colonies: Westland petrel breeds only along an 8 km coastal-forest stretch near Punakaiki, Hutton's shearwater is restricted to two alpine colonies plus a managed Kaikōura Peninsula colony, and northern royal albatross breeding is concentrated in the Chatham Islands with a tiny Taiaroa Head colony [DOC, Westland petrel](https://www.doc.govt.nz/nature/native-animals/birds/birds-a-z/westland-petrel-taiko/), [NZ Birds Online, Hutton's shearwater](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/huttons-shearwater), [NZ Birds Online, northern royal albatross](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/northern-royal-albatross).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high confidence on the response boundary and colony locations; medium confidence on which NZ colonies would take the biggest hit, because NZ has no confirmed H5N1 outbreak in native colonies and the risk ranking is inferred from overseas outcomes, colony density, movement ecology and conservation concentration.

## Evidence

### Current status and response boundary

New Zealand remained free of HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b in MPI's public planning page reviewed 26 September 2025, while Australia confirmed five H5 bird-flu cases in wild migratory seabirds by 9am AEST on 3 July 2026 [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza) ([archived 12 Apr 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260412171304/https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza)), [Australian Government DAFF, accessed 3 Jul 2026](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/campaigns/birdflu) ([archived 20 Jun 2026; before the latest count](https://web.archive.org/web/20260620031755/https://www.agriculture.gov.au/campaigns/birdflu)). DOC's 2 July 2026 trigger for vaccination was that H5N1 had recently been detected in migratory seabirds in Australia, not that New Zealand had a confirmed detection [DOC media release, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/) ([archived 3 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703093722/https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/)).

MPI's national planning assumption is that wild-bird arrival cannot be stopped and that early detection plus biosecurity can reduce impact, while its technical appendix concludes that, if H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b is brought to New Zealand by migratory birds, establishment and widespread spread in free-living birds is likely [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza), [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct). MPI explicitly says its free-living-birds assessment did not evaluate which geographic locations, seasons or bird species are most likely to be involved, so the colony ranking below is a scenario judgement rather than an official MPI ranking [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct).

DOC's vaccine trial and programme are deliberately narrow: the trial covered five native species selected for critical conservation status and reliance on captive breeding or intensive conservation, and the 2026 rollout covers about 300 core breeding birds from those same five species [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/), [DOC media release, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/). DOC says vaccination may protect a few core breeding populations from extinction, but it is not possible to vaccinate all birds and the focus is species where the full two-dose vaccine can be safely given [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/).

### Which NZ colonies look most exposed

**1. Tākapu/Australasian gannet colonies: high exposure, lower extinction risk.** Australasian gannets breed in dense colonies; most NZ gannetries are off the North Island; the largest mainland gannetry is Cape Kidnappers at around 5,000 breeding pairs; and other mainland sites include Muriwai and Farewell Spit [NZ Birds Online, Australasian gannet](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/australasian-gannet). MPI selected Australasian gannet as a migrating seabird pathway species and recorded it as a likely HPAI host in its 21-species NZ spread model [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct). The overseas analogue is strong: a northern gannet study found unusually high mortality at 40 colonies, equal to 75% of global colonies, during the 2022 H5N1 outbreak [Lane et al., 2023](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ibi.13275). Farewell Spit is already a practical surveillance site: a Birds New Zealand project reported dark-eyed Australasian gannets there in 2024-25, swabbing and banding, and no HPAI detection from swabs as of the project note [Birds New Zealand, 2025](https://www.birdsnz.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/2025-PAF-Gannet-Surveillance-Overview-Article-2510.pdf).

**2. Tara/white-fronted tern and gull colonies: high exposure and uneven conservation risk.** White-fronted terns occur around NZ coasts, some disperse to south-eastern Australia, the NZ population has declined markedly over 40 years, and breeding usually occurs in large dense colonies on river beds, sand dunes, stacks and cliffs [NZ Birds Online, white-fronted tern](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/white-fronted-tern). Black-billed gulls mostly breed on inland river gravels, with a 2016-17 survey identifying 60,256 nests and occasional "super colonies" of more than 10,000 birds [NZ Birds Online, black-billed gull](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/black-billed-gull). Red-billed gulls breed in dense mainland colonies and have suffered large declines at Three Kings Islands, Mokohinau Islands and Kaikōura Peninsula [NZ Birds Online, red-billed gull](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/red-billed-gull). MPI selected white-fronted tern and black-billed gull as NZ spread-pathway species, and a German Wadden Sea outbreak paper found HPAI H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b caused mass mortality among colony-breeding seabirds, with mortality in some tern colonies reaching 40% [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct), [Pohlmann et al., 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12016834/).

**3. Range-restricted petrels and shearwaters: lower direct H5N1 evidence, high consequence if a colony is hit.** Westland petrel is endemic, breeds only on the West Coast of the South Island, and its only breeding location is an 8 km stretch of coastal forest near Punakaiki with around 4,000 breeding pairs annually [DOC, Westland petrel](https://www.doc.govt.nz/nature/native-animals/birds/birds-a-z/westland-petrel-taiko/). Hutton's shearwater is endemic, Nationally Vulnerable, and restricted to two alpine colonies in the Seaward Kaikōura mountains plus a managed Kaikōura Peninsula colony, with more than 100,000 breeding pairs estimated despite its endangered status [NZ Birds Online, Hutton's shearwater](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/huttons-shearwater). MPI selected Westland petrel and Hutton's shearwater as migrating pelagic seabird pathway species, but its technical appendix does not provide colony-specific mortality estimates for either species [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct).

**4. Toroa/northern royal albatross: lower colony-density exposure than gannets or terns, high conservation and public-profile consequence.** Northern royal albatross breeds only in New Zealand, primarily on The Sisters and The Forty-Fours in the Chatham Islands, with a small Taiaroa Head colony near Dunedin [NZ Birds Online, northern royal albatross](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/northern-royal-albatross). NZ Birds Online estimates 6,500-7,000 breeding pairs in the Chatham Islands, 5,200-5,800 pairs breeding each year, and nearly 30 pairs annually at Taiaroa Head [NZ Birds Online, northern royal albatross](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/northern-royal-albatross). MPI selected northern royal albatross as a migrating pelagic seabird pathway species, and DOC links ACAP HPAI guidance for albatrosses and petrels from its HPAI page, but I found no public DOC colony-specific H5N1 mitigation plan for Taiaroa Head or the Chathams [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct), [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/).

**5. New Zealand sea lions and fur seals: outside the bird-vaccine programme, with overseas mammal mortality as the warning signal.** DOC says New Zealand sea lions are endemic, about 10,000 animals, Nationally Endangered, and mostly breed at the Auckland and Campbell subantarctic islands with smaller breeding populations at Rakiura/Stewart Island, Otago and the Catlins [DOC, New Zealand sea lion](https://www.doc.govt.nz/nature/native-animals/marine-mammals/seals/new-zealand-sea-lion/). Overseas mammal outbreaks make this a real spillover concern: a Nature Communications paper states that more than 30,000 South American sea lions died as H5N1 spread along Peru and Chile in 2022-23, and an Emerging Infectious Diseases report recorded more than 5,000 sea-lion deaths in Peru by April 2023 [Pérez et al., 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-53766-5), [Leguia et al., 2023](https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/eid/article/29/12/23-0192_article). DOC's public HPAI instructions include reporting mass deaths of fur seals or sea lions, but the vaccination programme is avian and does not protect marine mammals [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/), [DOC media release, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/).

### How far DOC's response reaches

DOC's response reaches four layers: vaccination for five intensively managed bird species, public reporting instructions for three or more sick/dead birds or marine mammals, wildlife-health guidance on not handling carcasses, and participation in a One Health readiness approach with MPI, Health NZ and the Ministry of Health [DOC media release, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.doc.govt.nz/news/media-releases/2026-media-releases/doc-to-vaccinate-at-risk-birds-against-bird-flu/), [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/), [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza). MPI's wider detection system includes the exotic pest and disease hotline, annual waterfowl sampling with Fish & Game, monitoring through wildlife hospitals and rehabilitation centres, remote sampling kits, and HPAI testing contracts with wildlife hospitals after the Otago H7N6 detection [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza).

That reach is useful for early detection, triage and extinction insurance in a few managed species, but it does not amount to population-level protection for free-living colonial seabirds or marine mammals [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/), [MPI free-living birds technical appendix](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/69642/direct). The practical gap is not that DOC ignored seabirds; it is that the response tools visible in public sources are surveillance, reporting, biosecurity hygiene and a small vaccination programme, while the biggest exposed biomass is in dense colonies that cannot be handled twice at scale without major disturbance, logistics and welfare risk [DOC wildlife-health page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/), [NZ Birds Online, Australasian gannet](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/australasian-gannet), [NZ Birds Online, white-fronted tern](https://www.nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/white-fronted-tern).

## What would change this conclusion

- A DOC or MPI colony-level response plan that names priority seabird and marine-mammal sites, defines deployable actions beyond reporting/hygiene, and states resourcing for those actions would change the conclusion that public response reach is mostly detection rather than colony protection.
- A confirmed H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b detection in New Zealand, or updated Australian spread evidence after 3 July 2026, would change the exposure ranking because the first NZ pathway and season would matter.
- Species-specific susceptibility or serology for Australasian gannet, white-fronted tern, black-billed gull, Hutton's shearwater, Westland petrel, northern royal albatross, New Zealand fur seal and New Zealand sea lion would change the ranking; current species ranking relies heavily on overseas analogues and ecology.
- I could not verify any public DOC plan that maps HPAI actions to individual seabird colonies such as Cape Kidnappers, Muriwai, Farewell Spit, Kaikōura, Punakaiki, Taiaroa Head or the Chatham Islands.
- A human with DOC, MPI, iwi/hapū authority for affected rohe, wildlife-hospital operations knowledge, or seabird colony-management authority should check whether unpublished operational plans exist and whether naming priority colonies creates any operational or biosecurity risk.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which NZ seabird colonies have current baseline counts, carcass-removal feasibility, access constraints and iwi/hapū governance contacts ready for an H5N1 response?
- What would a practical "do not spread it between colonies" operating protocol look like for tourism, researchers, fishers and volunteers at gannet, tern, gull and albatross sites?
- Are wildlife hospitals and rehabilitation centres funded and equipped to test, isolate or decline admissions during a suspected HPAI wildlife outbreak without becoming amplifiers?
- Which marine-mammal breeding sites have realistic monitoring and carcass-management options if sea lion or fur-seal mortality begins?
