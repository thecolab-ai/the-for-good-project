# NZ's published H5N1 wild-bird surveillance is not transparently sized for early incursion detection

## Executive answer

- New Zealand has real H5N1 preparedness machinery: MPI, DOC, Health NZ and the Ministry of Health run a One Health approach. MPI lists hotline reporting, annual non-migratory waterfowl sampling with Fish & Game, monitoring through wildlife hospitals and rehabilitation centres, DOC coordination, human-case coordination, international collaboration and border checks as early-detection measures [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza); [Ministry of Health briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).
- The government's flagship strategy — MPI's *One Health approach for HPAI H5N1* (August 2025), written for senior ODESC officials — asserts that "New Zealand has robust surveillance systems to detect the arrival of HPAI H5N1" but is explicitly a "high-level outline" and publishes **no** sampling target, site list, seasonal frame, detection probability, or trigger logic; it defers operational detail to other (non-public) response documents [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1).
- The quantified public surveillance base is modest and concentrated: in 2024 MPI sampled 1,280 healthy resident mallards at four North Island sites, screened 78 sick wild-bird samples through a wildlife-hospital stream, received 453 avian notifications and opened 90 AI investigations; a separate Te Niwha study sampled 700 healthy shorebirds/seabirds across 33 species in 2023/24 [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930); [ESR/PHF Science record of Waller et al. 2025](https://research.esr.cri.nz/articles/journal_contribution/Avian_Influenza_Virus_Surveillance_Across_New_Zealand_and_Its_Subantarctic_Islands_Detects_H1N9_in_Migratory_Shorebirds_but_Not_2_3_4_4b_HPAI_H5N1/29825780).
- The gap is not "no surveillance"; it is that the public record — including the top-level One Health strategy — does not publish a current H5N1 design target, site list, seasonal sampling frame, detection probability, Southern Ocean/seabird trigger logic, or sustained funding line that would let a reviewer judge whether wild-bird surveillance is sized to catch an incursion early enough to change management [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1); [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930); [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus).
- Independent experts and public-health authors are asking for exactly that stronger One Health detection layer: Te Niwha describes the role of wild aquatic birds in NZ transmission as unclear and says limited surveillance/resources leave NZ unprepared; PHCC says NZ needs effective surveillance and early detection across wildlife, poultry, livestock and humans [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus); [PHCC, 2024](https://www.phcc.org.nz/briefing/potential-avian-influenza-pandemic-time-nz-ramp-preparedness).
- Australia is the clearest nearby comparator and is more transparent about scale and escalation: Wildlife Health Australia reports risk-based targeted sampling plus general mortality surveillance, twice-yearly public reporting, and more than 164,000 wild-bird samples tested from July 2005 to December 2025; DAFF reports more than A$113 million committed for surveillance, preparedness and response, including a national healthy-wild-bird program, AviFluMap, One Health Surveillance Initiative activity and Indigenous ranger early-detection work [Wildlife Health Australia wild-bird surveillance](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Our-Work/Surveillance/Wild-Bird-Surveillance); [DAFF preparedness page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/pests-diseases-weeds/animal/avian-influenza/government-action).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the published evidence consistently shows a transparency and sizing gap, and having now read MPI's top-level One Health strategy (previously unavailable) *strengthens* the finding: even the flagship document that would naturally state a design instead asserts "robust surveillance" without publishing one. It remains Medium (not High) only because that strategy explicitly defers to other operational response documents (e.g. the NZ Government Biosecurity Response Guide) that are not public and could contain a sized design.

## Evidence

### What NZ is currently doing

MPI says early detection is crucial to managing H5N1's impact on biodiversity, food supply and trade, and says NZ is closely monitoring global spread, particularly near the Ross Sea region in Antarctica [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza). MPI's listed early-detection measures are the exotic pest and disease hotline, annual sampling at non-migratory waterfowl sites with Fish & Game NZ, monitoring and awareness with wildlife hospitals/poultry/bird-owner/veterinary/wildlife-rehabilitation groups, DOC coordination on suspected wild-bird disease, Ministry of Health coordination on suspect human cases, international collaboration and border checks [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza). MPI and DOC ask the public to report three or more sick or dead wild birds in a group to the exotic pest and disease hotline, which makes the public mortality signal a formal part of detection [MPI reporting guidance](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/about-avian-influenza-and-the-risk-to-nz); [DOC avian influenza page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/).

The 2025 MPI Surveillance Annual Report gives the clearest public sizing data. In 2024, the annual active programme sampled 1,280 healthy resident mallards from four central North Island sites; 665 were influenza A RT-qPCR positive, all detected viruses were low pathogenicity, and none tested positive for H5 or H7 by subtype RT-qPCR [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). The same report says this annual mallard programme has run since 2004, initially focused on migratory birds from 2004 to 2009 and then focused on resident waterfowl from 2010 onward [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). The 2004-2020 peer-reviewed analysis behind that programme sampled 18,693 birds, including migratory shorebirds, coastal species and resident waterfowl, and concluded that the findings suggested a low probability of HPAI introduction via long-distance bird migration under the then-observed epidemiology [Stanislawek et al., PLOS One, 2024](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0303756).

MPI added a wildlife-hospital stream in 2024 because H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b changed the epidemiology overseas and could arrive through the Southern Ocean pathway [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). That stream screened 59 wild-bird samples at Dunedin Wildlife Hospital from January to September 2024 and 19 further samples from September to December 2024, all negative; MPI planned expansion in 2025 to South Island Wildlife Hospital, The Nest at Wellington Zoo, Auckland Zoo's New Zealand Centre for Conservation Medicine and Birdcare Aotearoa [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). General surveillance produced 453 avian disease or mortality hotline notifications in 2024, 90 investigations that included AI as a differential diagnosis, and one poultry HPAI H7N6 detection; the same report says there has never been an HPAI wildlife case in NZ [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930).

Te Niwha funded a complementary genomics study rather than a routine government surveillance programme. Te Niwha lists the project as completed, 2024-2026, with funding of $1,229,012, led by Prof Jemma Geoghegan and Dr David Winter, and describes goals including transmission dynamics, environmental monitoring, and detection in water/sediment as a more accessible alternative to direct bird sampling [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus). The resulting Waller et al. 2025 study sampled 700 wild aquatic birds across 33 species from NZ, offshore islands and subantarctic territories during the 2023/24 migration season, detected no H5N1, and found one H1N9 low-pathogenicity detection in red knots [ESR/PHF Science record of Waller et al. 2025](https://research.esr.cri.nz/articles/journal_contribution/Avian_Influenza_Virus_Surveillance_Across_New_Zealand_and_Its_Subantarctic_Islands_Detects_H1N9_in_Migratory_Shorebirds_but_Not_2_3_4_4b_HPAI_H5N1/29825780). PHF Science says the same Te Niwha work was developing portable in-field eDNA detection tools and establishing sites for ongoing virus surveillance, but the public project page does not show a post-project operating budget, routine cadence, or national service owner for that ongoing network [PHF Science, 2024](https://www.phfscience.nz/news-publications/genomics-research-to-boost-aotearoa-new-zealand-s-defences-against-highly-pathogenic-bird-flu/); [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus).

### What the top-level One Health strategy does — and does not — publish

MPI's *One Health approach for HPAI H5N1* is the government's flagship, cross-agency (MPI, DOC, Ministry of Health, Health NZ) strategy document, published August 2025 (ISBN 978-1-991380-85-2) and explicitly written "for senior officials who are part of the National Resilience System" and the ODESC crisis-coordination system [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). It is the natural home for a national surveillance design, and it makes a strong assurance claim — "New Zealand has robust surveillance systems to detect the arrival of HPAI H5N1 and we have plans in place for when it arrives" — but it presents itself as "a high-level outline" and contains **no** sampling target, site list, seasonal frame, detection-probability estimate, or escalation trigger for wild-bird surveillance [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). Its "Getting ready" section describes readiness as monitoring international developments, strengthening biosecurity (especially on-farm poultry biosecurity), and stakeholder engagement — not a quantified wild-bird detection design [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). The guide explicitly defers operational detail to a set of other documents — the NZ Government Biosecurity Response Guide, the National CDEM Plan Order 2015, the NZ Pandemic Plan, the Communicable Disease Control Manual and the National Resilience System Handbook — none of which is the wild-bird surveillance design either, and the Biosecurity Response Guide is not published in full [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1).

The strategy is candid about the two facts that make early detection matter: arrival is "expected to be via migratory birds," and once here H5N1 is "expected to be become widespread in New Zealand's wild bird population" with eradication "unlikely to be feasible" and not attempted for wild birds [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). It confirms that DOC "will not actively manage infected populations on public conservation land other than to reduce disturbance," reserving additional protection for threatened species, and that vaccination of threatened native birds "may be carried out where feasible, based on the vaccination trial conducted during 2024" [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). On the human side it says that during a response the proportion of influenza A-positive samples that are subtyped will be increased, matching the Ministry of Health briefing's account of ICU subtyping [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1); [Ministry of Health briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf). Reading this document — which the earlier version of this finding could not, because raw fetches returned an Incapsula block — removes the possibility that the flagship strategy quietly contained the missing design: it does not, and it is the assurance-level document, not the operational one.

### Where the preparedness gaps sit

The main wild-bird gap is public sizing and design assurance. MPI publishes enough to show surveillance exists, but not enough to test whether it is sized for early H5N1 incursion detection: neither the operational MPI surveillance pages nor the top-level One Health strategy gives a current H5N1-specific sampling target by pathway, seasonal sensitivity target, Southern Ocean/seabird sentinel-site list, threshold for switching from routine to enhanced active sampling, or expected detection delay under plausible low-prevalence incursions [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza); [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1); [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). This matters because the long-running active design is still centred on resident mallards, while MPI itself says the newer H5N1 lineage has a wider host range including seabirds and has expanded geographically into subantarctic regions and the Antarctic Peninsula [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930); [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1).

The second gap is that early wild-bird detection appears heavily dependent on conspicuous disease, care-presentation, or one-off research coverage outside the annual mallard programme. Hotline reporting requires people to notice and report clusters; wildlife-hospital screening only samples birds that are found, rescued and admitted; Te Niwha's 700-bird work provides a valuable 2023/24 baseline but is not presented as a permanent nationwide active-surveillance service [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza); [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930); [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus). The One Health strategy is frank that if HPAI arrives and spreads in wild birds, eradication will not be attempted and One Health partners "will not seek to intervene or apply controls" for free-living wild birds, so the value of detection depends on how early it occurs and whether it can still trigger threatened-species protection, poultry biosecurity and human-risk actions [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1); [DOC avian influenza page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/).

The third gap is knowledge and environmental-surveillance translation. Te Niwha says wild aquatic birds are central to biosecurity but their role in NZ transmission and human/animal-health impact is unclear; PHF Science quotes Geoghegan saying that knowledge gap leaves NZ vulnerable, and Te Niwha says environmental methods in water and sediment are a research focus rather than a fully described routine system [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus); [PHF Science, 2024](https://www.phfscience.nz/news-publications/genomics-research-to-boost-aotearoa-new-zealand-s-defences-against-highly-pathogenic-bird-flu/). Health-side surveillance is better specified for severe human disease than for environmental spillover: the Ministry of Health says influenza A requires subtyping to distinguish H5 from seasonal H1/H3, suspected HPAI is notifiable, and all ICU patients across NZ hospitals who test positive for influenza A now undergo subtyping to detect H5N1 [Ministry of Health briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf).

The fourth gap is funding transparency. The Te Niwha research project discloses $1.229 million for 2024-2026, but I found no equivalent NZ public line item that commits ongoing H5N1 wild-bird surveillance funding at a national operating scale; the One Health strategy names the agencies and their roles but attaches no surveillance budget figure [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus); [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1). By contrast, DAFF says Australia has committed more than A$113 million for H5 bird flu surveillance, preparedness and response, including A$43.9 million for coordination, surveillance and biosecurity capacity, A$47.1 million for threatened-species and biodiversity protective action, and A$22.1 million for pandemic flu vaccines and antivirals [DAFF preparedness page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/pests-diseases-weeds/animal/avian-influenza/government-action).

### What independent recommendations imply

WOAH says early detection is the first line of defence against avian influenza and that accurate warning systems, early detection and timely reporting are essential because the disease can spread rapidly across regions [WOAH avian influenza page](https://www.woah.org/en/disease/avian-influenza/). PHCC says effective zoonotic surveillance should support early detection of H5N1 in wildlife, poultry and livestock, and that NZ should strengthen One Health approaches and early detection/response systems across poultry, livestock, wildlife and humans [PHCC, 2024](https://www.phcc.org.nz/briefing/potential-avian-influenza-pandemic-time-nz-ramp-preparedness). Wildlife Health Australia's HPAI toolbox for wildlife managers says preparedness should enhance early detection and that enhanced surveillance can include regular population observation, samples from dead animals even where HPAI is not suspected, healthy-animal sampling and environmental samples from soil, faeces and water [WHA HPAI wildlife-manager toolbox, 2026](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Portals/0/ResourceCentre/H5BirdFlu/WHA_HPAI_Risk_mitigation_toolbox.pdf).

Against those recommendations, NZ's public system looks adequate for confirming endemic LPAI patterns in resident mallards, investigating visible mortality events, and detecting severe human H5N1 disease if it appears in ICU, but not transparently adequate for low-prevalence early detection in migratory shorebird, seabird, subantarctic, or environmental pathways before visible morbidity or mortality [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930); [Ministry of Health briefing](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/H2025068297-Briefing-Introduction-to-High-Pathogenic-Avian-Influenza-HPAI.pdf); [WHA HPAI wildlife-manager toolbox, 2026](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Portals/0/ResourceCentre/H5BirdFlu/WHA_HPAI_Risk_mitigation_toolbox.pdf). This is a Medium-confidence gap assessment because the One Health strategy defers to non-public operational response documents that could contain the missing design, but the public-facing evidence — now including the top-level strategy itself — is not sufficient for an external decision-maker to verify early-detection adequacy [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1); [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus).

### Comparator: Australia's escalating posture

Australia is not identical to NZ, but it is a useful comparator because it faces H5N1 risk through wild-bird movement and is now responding to confirmed wild-bird detections. DAFF reported that Australia had five confirmed H5 bird flu cases in wild birds as of 9am AEST on 3 July 2026, four in Western Australia and one in South Australia, with no evidence of poultry infection or mass mortality at that time [DAFF bird flu situation page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/campaigns/birdflu). DAFF also says Australia works with state and territory governments, industry and experts; WHA runs a national programme to test healthy wild birds; WHA and Deakin created AviFluMap to track bird movements and overseas H5 outbreaks; WHA delivers additional activity through the One Health Surveillance Initiative; and Indigenous ranger groups support early-detection surveillance in northern Australia [DAFF preparedness page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/pests-diseases-weeds/animal/avian-influenza/government-action).

Wildlife Health Australia describes the National Avian Influenza Wild Bird Surveillance Program as a national programme with targeted surveillance of healthy wild birds, especially waterfowl, shorebirds and seabirds, at key locations and times, plus general surveillance through significant or unusual sickness/death investigations into eWHIS [Wildlife Health Australia wild-bird surveillance](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Our-Work/Surveillance/Wild-Bird-Surveillance). WHA reports public data twice a year through Wild Bird News and says more than 164,000 wild-bird samples were tested from July 2005 to December 2025 [Wildlife Health Australia wild-bird surveillance](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Our-Work/Surveillance/Wild-Bird-Surveillance). A WOAH regional review similarly describes Australia's wild-bird work as risk-based active surveillance plus enhanced passive surveillance, federally funded and implemented by jurisdictions, targeting waterfowl where there is mixing with shorebirds and proximity to poultry/humans [WOAH regional avian-disease surveillance review](https://rr-asia.woah.org/app/uploads/2023/08/avian_disease_surveillance_asia_pacific_final_clean.pdf).

The comparison is not that NZ should copy Australia's scale exactly. NZ has fewer major northern-hemisphere migratory shorebird species and resident waterfowl generally do not migrate internationally, which supports a lower introduction probability than many continental settings [Stanislawek et al., PLOS One, 2024](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0303756); [MPI Surveillance Annual Report 52(3), 2025](https://sciquest.org.nz/search/results-2/downloadfulltext/175930). The comparison does show what transparent preparedness can look like: explicit targeted and general surveillance streams, public cumulative sampling scale, regular public reporting, risk-map tooling, dedicated funding, and named early-detection partners [Wildlife Health Australia wild-bird surveillance](https://wildlifehealthaustralia.com.au/Our-Work/Surveillance/Wild-Bird-Surveillance); [DAFF preparedness page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/pests-diseases-weeds/animal/avian-influenza/government-action).

## What would change this conclusion

- A published MPI/DOC H5N1 surveillance design with target species, sites, sampling seasons, sample sizes, environmental-sampling plan, expected detection probability and response triggers would change the central conclusion from "not transparently sized" to an assessment of whether that design is technically adequate. The One Health strategy points to the (non-public) NZ Government Biosecurity Response Guide and related handbooks as where operational detail lives; releasing or summarising the wild-bird surveillance design from those would be decisive [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1).
- Evidence that Te Niwha's eDNA/environmental network has become an ongoing funded operational service, rather than a completed research project, would reduce the environmental-surveillance gap [Te Niwha project](https://www.teniwha.com/research-projects/genomics-informed-detection-surveillance-and-capacity-building-to-prepare-aotearoa-for-the-existential-threat-of-highly-pathogenic-avian-influenza-virus); [PHF Science, 2024](https://www.phfscience.nz/news-publications/genomics-research-to-boost-aotearoa-new-zealand-s-defences-against-highly-pathogenic-bird-flu/).
- Internal or OIA-released budget papers showing a sustained national H5N1 wildlife surveillance appropriation, comparable in purpose if not scale to Australia's published funding, would change the funding-transparency conclusion [DAFF preparedness page](https://www.agriculture.gov.au/biosecurity-trade/pests-diseases-weeds/animal/avian-influenza/government-action).
- I read MPI's *One Health approach for HPAI H5N1* in full (22 pages, August 2025) via the ADR-0006 fetch ladder after earlier raw fetches were Incapsula-blocked; it confirmed the guide is a high-level assurance/response strategy and does not contain a sized wild-bird surveillance design. It nonetheless names other operational documents I could not read (the Biosecurity Response Guide is not published in full); those, plus any internal MPI/DOC operational sampling plan, remain the key unverified source of a possible published design [MPI One Health approach for HPAI H5N1, August 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/70513-One-Health-approach-for-HPAI-H5N1).
- I did not contact MPI, DOC, wildlife hospitals, iwi, poultry operators, or public-health officials; a human domain review is needed before turning this into operational recommendations because site selection, iwi/community reporting arrangements, threatened-species handling and poultry trade-offs require authority and relationships this desk review does not have [DOC avian influenza page](https://www.doc.govt.nz/our-work/wildlife-health/avian-influenza/); [MPI surveillance and planning](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/pest-and-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/animal-disease-threats-to-new-zealand/high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza/surveillance-and-planning-for-avian-influenza).

## Open follow-up questions

- What exact sample sizes, sites, target species and detection-probability assumptions are in MPI/DOC's operational H5N1 wildlife surveillance plan (as referenced but not published in the One Health strategy), and how do they differ between northern flyway, Southern Ocean/subantarctic and urban/wildlife-hospital pathways?
- What would it cost to run a sustained NZ environmental H5N1 surveillance network at priority flyway, seabird colony, wetland and wildlife-care sites, and which agency would own operations after Te Niwha research funding?
- Which threatened native bird species and colony sites would benefit from earlier detection by days or weeks, and which management actions would actually be available after a positive environmental or wild-bird signal, given the One Health strategy's stance that wild-bird populations will not be actively managed?
- How should iwi, hapū, DOC rangers, Fish & Game, wildlife hospitals, birders, hunters and coastal communities be supported to report safely without turning passive surveillance into unmanaged public exposure?
