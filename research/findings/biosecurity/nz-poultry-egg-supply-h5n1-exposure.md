# NZ's egg (table-egg) supply is highly exposed to an H5N1 incursion, and the contained 2024 H7N6 event is a weak proxy for a persistent H5N1 outbreak

> **Scope.** Issue #227 asks how exposed "NZ's poultry/egg supply" is. This finding answers the **egg / table-egg** half in full — self-sufficiency, layer-flock concentration, import buffer, prices, and an overseas comparator. **Broiler / poultry-meat supply is explicitly out of scope**: the finding gives only high-level sector context for it (economic size, shared readiness/agreement) and flags a dedicated poultry-meat exposure assessment as an open follow-up (see below). A reader should not take the egg conclusion as an assessment of chicken-meat supply exposure.

## Executive answer

- **NZ eggs are domestically supplied and concentrated.** Land Use NZ describes New Zealand egg production and consumption as domestic, and EPF says table-egg imports are not allowed under government quarantine regulations; MPI's egg import pathway is for egg products and ingredients, not shell/table eggs ([Land Use NZ / poultry](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); [EPF farming standards](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/nz-egg-farming/nz-farming-standards/); [MPI importing eggs](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/importing-food-and-beverages/eggs/steps-to-importing)). One operator, Mainland Poultry (Zeagold), is NZ's largest egg producer — an estimated one-third to ~40% of national supply — and it was the exact farm hit by H7N6 in 2024 ([WATTPoultry / Navis acquisition, 2017](https://www.wattagnet.com/egg/egg-production/article/15521392/navis-capital-acquires-new-zealands-mainland-poultry-wattagnet); [DealStreetAsia, 29 May 2026 — ~40% of domestic egg consumption](https://www.dealstreetasia.com/stories/navis-pep-gateway-mainland-poultry-483935)). Concentration + no routine table-egg import channel = real systemic exposure.

- **Supply was already tight before any bird flu.** The 2023 battery-cage phase-out left the national laying flock roughly 10% (~400,000 hens) short of consumption for months, with supermarket rationing ([USDA FAS](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/new-zealand-new-zealand-experiences-egg-supply-shortage); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/483179/egg-shortage-free-range-egg-farmers-dealing-with-huge-demand)). A mass-depopulation event lands on an already-thin buffer.

- **H7N6 (Dec 2024) was contained — but it is a poor model for H5N1.** ~160,000 hens on Mainland's Hillgrove farm were culled; the virus arose *domestically* when free-range hens picked up low-path virus from wild birds and it mutated to high-path, with no persistent wild reservoir, so it was stamped out in ~5 months ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago), [Wayback](http://web.archive.org/web/20260602045400/https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm); [MPI H7N6 response epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)). H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b would arrive via wild birds and, per the responsible minister, "almost [certainly]" establish in wild birds; MPI separately says that if H5N1 arrives and becomes widespread in wild birds, eradication is unlikely, meaning **repeated** spillover risk rather than a one-off farm event ([Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan); [MPI H5N1 consultation page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)).

- **The overseas price shock shows the plausible direction, not a NZ forecast.** In the US, H5N1 has repeatedly reduced layer flocks and egg production, and USDA ERS links those reductions to egg-price increases in 2022, 2024 and 2025; retail prices peaked in March 2025 before falling in April ([USDA APHIS](https://www.aphis.usda.gov/livestock-poultry-disease/avian/avian-influenza/hpai-detections/commercial-backyard-flocks); [USDA ERS food price outlook](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings); [USDA ERS chart of note](https://www-tx.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/112677)). Innovate Animal Ag estimates a 7–10% supply deficit and US$14.5b extra consumer spend, but I treat those as single-source estimates rather than official statistics ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025)). NZ egg prices have already climbed from ~$4.46/dozen (2020) to ~$8.77 (Feb 2026), so the sector is price-sensitive and has no routine table-egg import relief valve ([Stats NZ food price index](https://www.stats.govt.nz/topics/food-price-index/); [Figure.NZ retail egg price series](https://figure.nz/chart/WNZOpEoBKRyz4hBh-a5aHFhuTKVojTjod)).

- **The sector is more ready than in 2024 — but readiness for H5N1 specifically is still unfinished and partly self-limited.** A Government–industry operational agreement took effect on 1 September 2025, MPI consulted from 17 September to 2 November 2025 on mandatory on-farm biosecurity programmes, and MPI released a submissions summary on 15 April 2026 after further targeted industry consultation in March 2026; final decisions had not been posted on the MPI consultation page when checked for this finding ([Beehive, agreement](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity); [MPI consultation page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)). The agreement explicitly limits cost-shareable activities for an H5N1 outbreak, and no published NZ modelling of the supply/price impact of a mass H5N1 depopulation could be found.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the structural exposure facts (self-sufficiency, concentration, prior tightness) and the H7N6 timeline are well-sourced and current; the *scale* of an H5N1 price/supply shock for NZ is an informed inference from overseas comparators, not a NZ-specific modelled figure.

## Evidence

### NZ's egg supply has no routine table-egg import buffer and is concentrated in a few hands

New Zealand is effectively self-sufficient in shell/table eggs: Land Use NZ describes local laying-hen production and national consumption, EPF says government quarantine rules mean table eggs cannot be imported into New Zealand, and MPI's import steps refer to "egg products and ingredients" rather than a table-egg import pathway ([Land Use NZ](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); [EPF farming standards](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/nz-egg-farming/nz-farming-standards/); [MPI importing eggs](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/import/importing-food-and-beverages/eggs/steps-to-importing)). EPF's import/export statistics page also separates the poultry industry's import/export statistics from domestic egg production and consumption, but I could not independently verify an official zero-import trade series for table eggs in the time available ([EPF key statistics](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/nz-egg-industry-key-statistics/)). The national flock is roughly 3 million commercial layer hens producing on the order of 1.1–1.2 billion eggs a year (Land Use NZ cites ~3 million layers and ~93.8 million dozen consumed in 2016; EPF cites ~229 eggs per person per year, which across ~5.3 million people implies ~1.2 billion eggs) ([Land Use NZ](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); [EPF fast facts](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/fast-facts-about-eggs-and-egg-farming-in-new-zealand-2/egg-farming/)).

Production is concentrated. Mainland Poultry (trading as Zeagold) is the country's largest egg producer. The "around a third" share commonly quoted traces to dated coverage — a 2017 WATTPoultry/Navis acquisition story and a 2022 NZ Herald piece — alongside roughly 1.2–1.3 million hens ([WATTPoultry, 2017](https://www.wattagnet.com/egg/egg-production/article/15521392/navis-capital-acquires-new-zealands-mainland-poultry-wattagnet); [NZ Herald, 2022](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/the-country/news/huge-changes-for-nzs-largest-egg-business-as-it-moves-to-free-range/MLZOJCQPX4RIA7J4DXH5VOZW2M/)). More recent 2026 coverage of the company's sale to Pacific Equity Partners Gateway (with Navis) describes it as NZ's largest egg supplier accounting for **around 40% of domestic egg consumption**, so the current figure may be higher than "a third" ([DealStreetAsia, 29 May 2026](https://www.dealstreetasia.com/stories/navis-pep-gateway-mainland-poultry-483935); [Rural News Group, 29 May 2026](https://www.ruralnewsgroup.co.nz/rural-news/rural-general-news/mainland-poultry-new-ownership-pep-gateway)). Either way the concentration point strengthens: a single operator supplies roughly a third to ~40% of national egg supply. Historically the largest ~20 companies have accounted for over 75% of production ([Te Ara](https://teara.govt.nz/en/poultry-industry/page-4)). Because there is no routine table-egg import channel, a large depopulation at a top producer would directly remove national supply unless emergency settings changed. The 2024 H7N6 event hit precisely this operator.

The broader poultry sector (meat + eggs) is economically significant: ~$2.2 billion in domestic value and ~$200 million in exports annually, per the Government ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).

### Supply was already tight before bird flu, because of the cage phase-out

Battery cages were banned from 1 January 2023, removing a large slice of colony/cage capacity faster than barn and free-range capacity could replace it. USDA's Foreign Agricultural Service and NZ reporting recorded a national laying flock roughly 10% (~400,000 hens) short of consumption, with supermarket purchase limits and shortages persisting for months ([USDA FAS](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/new-zealand-new-zealand-experiences-egg-supply-shortage); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/483179/egg-shortage-free-range-egg-farmers-dealing-with-huge-demand); [Stuff](https://i.stuff.co.nz/business/130867413/egg-shortage-hits-supermarkets-as-caged-eggs-phased-out)). Egg prices reflect this: Stats NZ food-price data shows fresh eggs averaging ~$4.46/dozen (2020), ~$8.71 (2023) and ~$8.77 (Feb 2026), with a ~18.5% annual jump recorded in October 2025 ([Stats NZ food price index](https://www.stats.govt.nz/topics/food-price-index/); [Figure.NZ retail egg price series](https://figure.nz/chart/WNZOpEoBKRyz4hBh-a5aHFhuTKVojTjod)). A biosecurity depopulation event lands on top of this already-thin, already-expensive baseline.

There is also a structural tension: welfare-driven moves away from cages push more hens into **free-range and barn** systems (Foodstuffs and Woolworths committed to dropping colony-cage eggs), and free-range birds foraging outdoors have the *most* contact with wild birds — the exact pathway that caused the 2024 H7N6 outbreak ([Stuff](https://i.stuff.co.nz/business/130867413/egg-shortage-hits-supermarkets-as-caged-eggs-phased-out); [MPI H7N6 page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago)).

### What the 2024 H7N6 event actually was — and why it does not predict H5N1

On 2 December 2024, MPI placed movement controls on Mainland Poultry's Hillgrove farm near Dunedin after confirming H7N6 high-pathogenicity avian influenza — the **first-ever detection of HPAI in NZ**. Around 160,000 hens (across four sheds) were culled; more than 5,600 tests were run across 36 flocks on 5 linked farms and HPAI was found nowhere else; controls were lifted and the disease declared eradicated on 28 April 2025 ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago), [Wayback snapshot](http://web.archive.org/web/20260602045400/https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [RNZ, controls lifted](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/559292/controls-lifted-at-otago-poultry-farm-after-high-pathogenic-bird-flu-eradicated); [RNZ, ~160k culled](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm)). MPI also published an H7N6 response epidemiology report on 26 February 2025; I treat it as epidemiological evidence, not a full after-action review. A later MPI-authored self-declaration to WOAH gives the clearest accessible detail: tracing identified 11 medium- to high-risk properties, all 1,566 dead birds tested and 4,280 premise-freedom samples from at-risk properties were negative, and 79 suspect-disease avian investigations between 1 January and 30 June 2025 ruled out HPAI ([MPI H7N6 response epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report); [MPI/WOAH self-declaration](https://www.woah.org/app/uploads/2025/09/2025-09-new-zealand-hpai-selfd.pdf)).

Reading the epidemiology report itself surfaces three lessons that bear directly on how transferable this "contained in five months" outcome is to an H5N1 incursion. **(1) The property was large and clustered internally:** it held ~161,900 laying birds (eight flocks across four buildings) plus a rearing site of ~60,700 pullets, and epidemiological analysis found almost all populated sheds were already infected by the time depopulation started — so even a "single-property" event is a six-figure cull. **(2) Containment was partly a matter of geography, not just response quality:** the infected farm was coastal and isolated (nearest commercial layer property 10 km away, nearest broiler ~150 km), and the report warns that the ~11 days between diagnosis and completion of whole-property depopulation "in an area more densely populated with poultry properties … could have resulted in a larger outbreak." **(3) The report sets an operational bar NZ has not yet had to meet at scale:** "entire shed killing methods and rapid entire property depopulation implemented swiftly after diagnosis of the first shed should be the goal for any future response" ([MPI H7N6 response epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report)). None of this contradicts the "successfully eradicated" narrative, but it shows the H7N6 response drew on a favourable set of circumstances (one isolated farm, no wild reservoir) that a persistent H5N1 incursion would not repeat.

Critically, MPI's own finding is that H7N6 **arose in New Zealand**: free-range hens foraging outside were exposed to a low-pathogenicity virus that is endemic in NZ wild birds, which then mutated inside the chickens into a high-path strain ([MPI H7N6 page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago)). Because there was no persistent high-path reservoir in wildlife, once the affected flock was removed the outbreak was over. The government explicitly framed H7N6 as the *"less virulent"* strain and said H5N1 preparation helped the response ([RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/559292/controls-lifted-at-otago-poultry-farm-after-high-pathogenic-bird-flu-eradicated)).

H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b is a different problem: it is the global panzootic circulating in wild birds and would arrive via migratory/wild birds. The responsible minister's own framing is that if it arrives it will "almost [certainly]" establish in wild birds, potentially causing food shortages, and MPI's H5N1 consultation page says eradication is unlikely if the virus arrives and becomes widespread in wild birds ([Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan); [MPI H5N1 consultation page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)). A persistent wild reservoir means **repeated** reintroduction to farms — so "we stamped out H7N6 in five months" is genuine but does not demonstrate readiness for an endemic-in-wildlife H5N1 situation.

### What an H5N1 outbreak could do to supply and prices — the overseas comparator

The clearest quantified evidence is the United States, where H5N1 2.3.4.4b has been circulating in wild birds and repeatedly hitting poultry:

- APHIS confirmed HPAI in US commercial flocks from 8 February 2022 and says the virus continues to ebb and flow with wild-bird movement; the same APHIS page notes 378.5 million egg-laying chickens as context for the size of the US flock ([USDA APHIS](https://www.aphis.usda.gov/livestock-poultry-disease/avian/avian-influenza/hpai-detections/commercial-backyard-flocks)).
- USDA ERS states that the HPAI outbreak that began in 2022 reduced egg-layer flocks and egg production, contributing to retail egg-price increases of 32.2% in 2022, 8.5% in 2024 and 21.9% in 2025 ([USDA ERS food price outlook](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings)).
- USDA ERS reported that retail egg prices fell to US$5.12/dozen in April 2025, more than a dollar below their March peak; that supports the timing and existence of a major 2025 price spike without relying on an advocacy estimate for the precise peak ([USDA ERS chart of note](https://www-tx.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/112677)).
- Innovate Animal Ag estimates 150m+ birds culled, a 7–10% egg-supply deficit, a US$6.22/dozen March 2025 price peak and US$14.5b extra consumer spend from May 2024 to April 2025; because I did not find independent official corroboration for the 7–10% and US$14.5b figures, those remain **single-source, Low-confidence scale estimates** ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025)).

NZ is not identical — it is a smaller market with different flock structure and regulatory settings. The defensible lesson is narrower: in a large market with some import flexibility, repeated H5N1 losses were still associated with material production reductions and large egg-price increases, so NZ should not treat the contained H7N6 event as evidence that a persistent H5N1 outbreak would have only localised supply effects. I did not find a NZ elasticity model that would justify a numeric price multiplier.

### How ready is the sector now?

Post-H7N6, the Government and industry moved to formalise readiness:

- A **Government–industry operational agreement** with the Poultry Industry Association (PIANZ) and Egg Producers Federation (EPF) took effect **1 September 2025**. It covers Newcastle disease, infectious bursal disease and all HPAI strains including H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b; industry contributes 45% of readiness costs and (per the release) 40% of HPAI response costs; industry leads on-farm response with MPI support ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).
- A **six-week consultation (17 Sept – 2 Nov 2025)** proposed **mandatory avian biosecurity control programmes**: commercial operators would need audited biosecurity plans covering depopulation, disposal, cleaning and record-keeping, with offences and penalties for non-compliance; some rules could reach semi-commercial and backyard owners ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)). MPI updated the consultation page on **15 April 2026** to release a summary of submissions, noted targeted consultation with PIANZ, EPF, and turkey and duck meat producers in **March 2026**, and said it would update the page again when decisions are made ([MPI consultation page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)).

Two caveats temper the "we're ready" story. First, the agreement's own terms state cost-shareable activities *for an H5N1 outbreak* "should be limited" — i.e. the funded/cost-shared response for H5N1 is deliberately narrower than for other diseases, which raises an unresolved question of who bears the cost of a large H5N1 event ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)). Second, the direct H7N6 response cost is reported inconsistently — Biosecurity NZ expected total spend to stay within the **$20 million** it set aside, while the Government elsewhere characterised the 2024 outbreak as costing **~$25 million** — so even a single-farm event is a multi-tens-of-millions problem ([RNZ, restock](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/596999/chooks-back-on-the-lay-as-hillgrove-farm-restocks-poultry-sheds-after-bird-flu-outbreak); [Beehive, agreement](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published NZ H5N1 supply/price scenario.** If MPI or industry has modelled the domestic supply loss and price impact of an H5N1 mass-depopulation event, that would replace my overseas-comparator inference with a NZ-specific figure — and could show NZ is more (or less) buffered than the US analogy implies. I could not find such modelling.
- **Evidence that H5N1 would behave like H7N6 in NZ.** If wild-bird surveillance showed NZ's migratory-bird pathways make sustained H5N1 establishment unlikely, the "repeated spillover" premise weakens. (Pathway/establishment risk is the subject of sibling issues #226 and #229 in this stream.)
- **The final form of the H5N1 regulations.** MPI released a summary of submissions on 15 April 2026 and says it will update the consultation page when decisions are made; actual decisions on whether mandatory biosecurity programmes are enacted, and with what scope for backyard/free-range flocks, would materially change the readiness assessment ([MPI consultation page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/consultations/proposed-regulations-for-managing-high-pathogenicity-avian-influenza-h5n1-in-poultry)).
- **A broader H7N6 after-action review.** MPI has published an H7N6 response epidemiology report, and this finding now uses it alongside New Zealand's MPI-authored WOAH self-declaration, but I did not find a broader independent after-action review covering operational performance, compensation, resourcing, welfare, or industry impacts. Such a review could confirm or undercut the "contained successfully" narrative beyond the epidemiological containment evidence ([MPI H7N6 response epidemiology report](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/67965-H7N6-response-epidemiology-report); [MPI/WOAH self-declaration](https://www.woah.org/app/uploads/2025/09/2025-09-new-zealand-hpai-selfd.pdf)).

**Could not verify:** exact up-to-date national flock and production totals (behind EPF files I could not parse cleanly); an official table-egg import trade series showing zero imports by tariff line; any NZ-specific quantified H5N1 economic model; the precise reconciliation of the $20m vs ~$25m H7N6 cost figures; whether the operational agreement's "limited" H5N1 cost-sharing has been quantified.

## Open follow-up questions

- Is there (or should there be) a NZ-specific model of egg/poultry-meat supply loss and consumer price impact under an H5N1 mass-depopulation scenario, given the lack of a routine table-egg import channel? (Good candidate for a new Research issue.)
- How exposed are **broiler/poultry-meat** supply chains (as distinct from eggs) to H5N1, given different housing and turnover? This finding centres on eggs.
- Does the concentration risk (one operator ≈ a third of supply) warrant a specific resilience/redundancy policy — and how do backyard/semi-commercial flocks fit the mandatory-programme regime?
- What did the H7N6 response actually cost end-to-end (including compensation), and is that funding model adequate for a multi-farm H5N1 event where cost-sharing is "limited"?
