# NZ's poultry/egg supply is highly exposed to an H5N1 incursion, and the contained 2024 H7N6 event is a weak proxy for a persistent H5N1 outbreak

## Executive answer

- **NZ eggs are 100% domestic and concentrated.** New Zealand imports no table eggs — supply is entirely home-grown behind strict biosecurity, so any production gap *cannot* be plugged by imports the way it can overseas ([Land Use NZ / poultry](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); [Egg Producers Federation](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/)). One operator, Mainland Poultry (Zeagold), produces around a third of national supply — and it was the exact farm hit by H7N6 in 2024 ([WATTPoultry / Navis acquisition](https://www.wattagnet.com/egg/egg-production/article/15521392/navis-capital-acquires-new-zealands-mainland-poultry-wattagnet); [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/the-country/news/huge-changes-for-nzs-largest-egg-business-as-it-moves-to-free-range/MLZOJCQPX4RIA7J4DXH5VOZW2M/)). Concentration + no import buffer = real systemic exposure.

- **Supply was already tight before any bird flu.** The 2023 battery-cage phase-out left the national laying flock roughly 10% (~400,000 hens) short of consumption for months, with supermarket rationing ([USDA FAS](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/new-zealand-new-zealand-experiences-egg-supply-shortage); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/483179/egg-shortage-free-range-egg-farmers-dealing-with-huge-demand)). A mass-depopulation event lands on an already-thin buffer.

- **H7N6 (Dec 2024) was contained — but it is a poor model for H5N1.** ~160,000 hens on Mainland's Hillgrove farm were culled; the virus arose *domestically* when free-range hens picked up low-path virus from wild birds and it mutated to high-path, with no persistent wild reservoir, so it was stamped out in ~5 months ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago), [Wayback](http://web.archive.org/web/20260602045400/https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm)). H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b would arrive via wild birds and, per the responsible minister, "almost [certainly]" establish in wild birds — meaning **repeated** spillover onto farms, not a one-off ([Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)).

- **The overseas price shock shows the ceiling.** In the US, H5N1 has killed 150m+ birds since 2022, held egg supply ~7–10% below normal, drove wholesale prices to a record ~US$6.22/dozen (March 2025), and cost consumers an extra ~US$14.5 billion in a year ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025); [CBS](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/eggs-prices-shortages-bird-flu-2025/)). NZ egg prices have already climbed from ~$4.46/dozen (2020) to ~$8.77 (Feb 2026), so the sector is price-sensitive and has no import relief valve.

- **The sector is more ready than in 2024 — but readiness for H5N1 specifically is unproven and partly self-limited.** A Government–industry operational agreement (effective 1 Sep 2025) and a Sept–Nov 2025 consultation on mandatory on-farm biosecurity programmes are real steps, but the agreement explicitly limits cost-shareable activities for an H5N1 outbreak, and no published NZ modelling of the supply/price impact of a mass H5N1 depopulation could be found ([Beehive, agreement](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity); [Beehive, consultation](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the structural exposure facts (self-sufficiency, concentration, prior tightness) and the H7N6 timeline are well-sourced and current; the *scale* of an H5N1 price/supply shock for NZ is an informed inference from overseas comparators, not a NZ-specific modelled figure.

## Evidence

### NZ's egg supply has no import buffer and is concentrated in a few hands

New Zealand is effectively self-sufficient in eggs: all table eggs are produced domestically, and strict biosecurity controls mean table eggs are not imported ([Land Use NZ](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); the Egg Producers Federation states "all of the eggs produced in New Zealand come from local farmers," [EPF](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/)). The national flock is roughly 3 million commercial layer hens producing on the order of 1.1–1.2 billion eggs a year (Land Use NZ cites ~3 million layers and ~93.8 million dozen consumed in 2016; EPF cites ~229 eggs per person per year, which across ~5.3 million people implies ~1.2 billion eggs) ([Land Use NZ](https://landusenz.org.nz/poultry/); [EPF fast facts](https://eggfarmers.org.nz/fast-facts-about-eggs-and-egg-farming-in-new-zealand-2/egg-farming/)).

Production is concentrated. Mainland Poultry (trading as Zeagold) is the country's largest egg producer with around a one-third share of national supply and roughly 1.2–1.3 million hens ([WATTPoultry](https://www.wattagnet.com/egg/egg-production/article/15521392/navis-capital-acquires-new-zealands-mainland-poultry-wattagnet); [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/the-country/news/huge-changes-for-nzs-largest-egg-business-as-it-moves-to-free-range/MLZOJCQPX4RIA7J4DXH5VOZW2M/)). Historically the largest ~20 companies have accounted for over 75% of production ([Te Ara](https://teara.govt.nz/en/poultry-industry/page-4)). Because there is no import channel, a large depopulation at a top producer directly removes national supply with no external backfill. The 2024 H7N6 event hit precisely this operator.

The broader poultry sector (meat + eggs) is economically significant: ~$2.2 billion in domestic value and ~$200 million in exports annually, per the Government ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).

### Supply was already tight before bird flu, because of the cage phase-out

Battery cages were banned from 1 January 2023, removing a large slice of colony/cage capacity faster than barn and free-range capacity could replace it. USDA's Foreign Agricultural Service and NZ reporting recorded a national laying flock roughly 10% (~400,000 hens) short of consumption, with supermarket purchase limits and shortages persisting for months ([USDA FAS](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/new-zealand-new-zealand-experiences-egg-supply-shortage); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/483179/egg-shortage-free-range-egg-farmers-dealing-with-huge-demand); [Stuff](https://i.stuff.co.nz/business/130867413/egg-shortage-hits-supermarkets-as-caged-eggs-phased-out)). Egg prices reflect this: Stats NZ food-price data shows fresh eggs averaging ~$4.46/dozen (2020), ~$8.71 (2023) and ~$8.77 (Feb 2026), with a ~18.5% annual jump recorded in October 2025 ([Stats NZ food price index](https://www.stats.govt.nz/topics/food-price-index/); [Figure.NZ retail egg price series](https://figure.nz/chart/WNZOpEoBKRyz4hBh-a5aHFhuTKVojTjod)). A biosecurity depopulation event lands on top of this already-thin, already-expensive baseline.

There is also a structural tension: welfare-driven moves away from cages push more hens into **free-range and barn** systems (Foodstuffs and Woolworths committed to dropping colony-cage eggs), and free-range birds foraging outdoors have the *most* contact with wild birds — the exact pathway that caused the 2024 H7N6 outbreak ([Stuff](https://i.stuff.co.nz/business/130867413/egg-shortage-hits-supermarkets-as-caged-eggs-phased-out); [MPI H7N6 page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago)).

### What the 2024 H7N6 event actually was — and why it does not predict H5N1

On 2 December 2024, MPI placed movement controls on Mainland Poultry's Hillgrove farm near Dunedin after confirming H7N6 high-pathogenicity avian influenza — the **first-ever detection of HPAI in NZ**. Around 160,000 hens (across four sheds) were culled; more than 5,600 tests were run across 36 flocks on 5 linked farms and HPAI was found nowhere else; controls were lifted and the disease declared eradicated on 28 April 2025 ([MPI, situation update 28 Apr 2025](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago), [Wayback snapshot](http://web.archive.org/web/20260602045400/https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago); [RNZ, controls lifted](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/559292/controls-lifted-at-otago-poultry-farm-after-high-pathogenic-bird-flu-eradicated); [RNZ, ~160k culled](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/536048/bird-flu-outbreak-nearly-160k-hens-culled-at-otago-farm)).

Critically, MPI's own finding is that H7N6 **arose in New Zealand**: free-range hens foraging outside were exposed to a low-pathogenicity virus that is endemic in NZ wild birds, which then mutated inside the chickens into a high-path strain ([MPI H7N6 page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/biosecurity/exotic-pests-and-diseases-in-new-zealand/active-biosecurity-responses-to-pests-and-diseases/a-strain-of-bird-flu-h7n6-in-otago)). Because there was no persistent high-path reservoir in wildlife, once the affected flock was removed the outbreak was over. The government explicitly framed H7N6 as the *"less virulent"* strain and said H5N1 preparation helped the response ([RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/559292/controls-lifted-at-otago-poultry-farm-after-high-pathogenic-bird-flu-eradicated)).

H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b is a different problem: it is the global panzootic circulating in wild birds and would arrive via migratory/wild birds. The responsible minister's own framing is that if it arrives it will "almost [certainly]" establish in wild birds, potentially causing food shortages ([Beehive, 17 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)). A persistent wild reservoir means **repeated** reintroduction to farms — so "we stamped out H7N6 in five months" is genuine but does not demonstrate readiness for an endemic-in-wildlife H5N1 situation.

### What an H5N1 outbreak could do to supply and prices — the overseas comparator

The clearest quantified evidence is the United States, where H5N1 2.3.4.4b has been circulating in wild birds and repeatedly hitting poultry:

- 150 million+ commercial birds culled since Feb 2022; ~44 million table-egg layers culled Oct 2024–Feb 2025 alone ([CBS](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/eggs-prices-shortages-bird-flu-2025/); [Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025)).
- Egg supply held roughly **7–10% below** normal ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025)).
- Average wholesale/retail prices spiked to a record ~US$6.22/dozen in March 2025, with some retail over US$8–9 ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025); [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2025/02/26/bird-flu-egg-prices-growing-shortages-eggs)).
- Consumers spent an estimated extra **US$14.5 billion** on eggs between May 2024 and April 2025 ([Innovate Animal Ag](https://innovateanimalag.org/hpai-costs-2025)).

NZ is not identical — a smaller market, different flock structure — but the transmissible lesson is that a ~7–10% supply loss can double prices when demand is inelastic. NZ's *no-imports* structure removes the safety valve the US partially had (the US could and did seek imports), so a comparable proportional loss could produce at least as sharp a domestic price response.

### How ready is the sector now?

Post-H7N6, the Government and industry moved to formalise readiness:

- A **Government–industry operational agreement** with the Poultry Industry Association (PIANZ) and Egg Producers Federation (EPF) took effect **1 September 2025**. It covers Newcastle disease, infectious bursal disease and all HPAI strains including H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b; industry contributes 45% of readiness costs and (per the release) 40% of HPAI response costs; industry leads on-farm response with MPI support ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).
- A **six-week consultation (17 Sept – 2 Nov 2025)** proposed **mandatory avian biosecurity control programmes**: commercial operators would need audited biosecurity plans covering depopulation, disposal, cleaning and record-keeping, with offences and penalties for non-compliance; some rules could reach semi-commercial and backyard owners ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-takes-action-bird-flu-preparedness-plan)).

Two caveats temper the "we're ready" story. First, the agreement's own terms state cost-shareable activities *for an H5N1 outbreak* "should be limited" — i.e. the funded/cost-shared response for H5N1 is deliberately narrower than for other diseases, which raises an unresolved question of who bears the cost of a large H5N1 event ([Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)). Second, the direct H7N6 response cost is reported inconsistently — Biosecurity NZ expected total spend to stay within the **$20 million** it set aside, while the Government elsewhere characterised the 2024 outbreak as costing **~$25 million** — so even a single-farm event is a multi-tens-of-millions problem ([RNZ, restock](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/596999/chooks-back-on-the-lay-as-hillgrove-farm-restocks-poultry-sheds-after-bird-flu-outbreak); [Beehive, agreement](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/agreement-signed-poultry-biosecurity)).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published NZ H5N1 supply/price scenario.** If MPI or industry has modelled the domestic supply loss and price impact of an H5N1 mass-depopulation event, that would replace my overseas-comparator inference with a NZ-specific figure — and could show NZ is more (or less) buffered than the US analogy implies. I could not find such modelling.
- **Evidence that H5N1 would behave like H7N6 in NZ.** If wild-bird surveillance showed NZ's migratory-bird pathways make sustained H5N1 establishment unlikely, the "repeated spillover" premise weakens. (Pathway/establishment risk is the subject of sibling issues #226 and #229 in this stream.)
- **The final form of the 2025 regulations.** The consultation outcome (are mandatory biosecurity programmes actually enacted, with what scope for backyard/free-range flocks?) would materially change the readiness assessment. This finding predates the consultation's conclusion.
- **A formal H7N6 post-incident review.** The Government referenced "lessons" and areas to strengthen, but I found no *published* independent post-incident review document — only media/ministerial summaries. A real review could confirm or undercut the "contained successfully" narrative.

**Could not verify:** exact up-to-date national flock and production totals (behind EPF PDFs I could not parse); any NZ-specific quantified H5N1 economic model; the precise reconciliation of the $20m vs ~$25m H7N6 cost figures; whether the operational agreement's "limited" H5N1 cost-sharing has been quantified.

## Open follow-up questions

- Is there (or should there be) a NZ-specific model of egg/poultry-meat supply loss and consumer price impact under an H5N1 mass-depopulation scenario, given the no-imports structure? (Good candidate for a new Research issue.)
- How exposed are **broiler/poultry-meat** supply chains (as distinct from eggs) to H5N1, given different housing and turnover? This finding centres on eggs.
- Does the concentration risk (one operator ≈ a third of supply) warrant a specific resilience/redundancy policy — and how do backyard/semi-commercial flocks fit the mandatory-programme regime?
- What did the H7N6 response actually cost end-to-end (including compensation), and is that funding model adequate for a multi-farm H5N1 event where cost-sharing is "limited"?
