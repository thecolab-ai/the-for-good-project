# DataGrid Makarewa's 240MW-IT-capacity vs 280MW figures are not a contradiction — 240MW is the consented IT load and 280MW the total power the campus draws; the SDC and ICC consents demonstrably exist but their full decision documents are not posted publicly and need a register/LGOIMA release

## Executive answer

- **The "240MW vs 280MW" gap is a units question, not a discrepancy, and both numbers are correct for the *same* consented build.** The Environment Southland consent decision describes a data centre with **"up to 240MW of IT capacity"** — the power delivered to the computing equipment (servers/GPUs) [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf). DataGrid's **280MW** is the *total* power the campus draws: its press release says the facility will **"consume 280 MW of power"** [DataGrid press release, 11 Mar 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent), and its site says the park is **"Designed to deliver a capacity of 280 MW"** with a **"world leading projected power usage efficiency (PUE)"** [DataGrid](https://www.datagrid.nz/). Total facility power and IT power are precisely the two quantities the PUE metric relates: PUE = total facility energy ÷ IT-equipment energy, the difference being cooling, power-conversion and distribution overhead [ISO/IEC 30134-2 — PUE, the international data-centre KPI standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_usage_effectiveness).
- **The implied ratio is plausible, but the facility's actual design PUE is not stated anywhere I found.** 280MW ÷ 240MW ≈ **1.17**. For context, the industry-average PUE is about **1.56** and best-in-class hyperscale operators run around **1.1–1.2** [Uptime Institute — Global Data Center Survey 2024](https://uptimeinstitute.com/resources/research-and-reports/uptime-institute-global-data-center-survey-results-2024), so a ~1.17 design figure would sit at the efficient end — consistent with DataGrid's cool-climate "world-leading PUE" claim [DataGrid](https://www.datagrid.nz/). But because **no source gives DataGrid's actual PUE**, reading the ~40MW gap as exactly the cooling/electrical overhead is a coherent *inference*, not a directly stated fact — so this reconciliation is Medium, not settled. (This 240MW consented IT load is separate from — and should not be confused with — DataGrid's stated long-term *ambition* to "grow over time up to 1GW", which has no consent or supply behind it [w.media, 23 Jul 2024](https://w.media/datagrid-new-zealand-gets-oio-approval-for-data-center-park-extension/).)
- **The Southland District Council (SDC) land-use consent for the data-centre site does exist** and was determined **separately** from the regional consents by the same independent commissioner process; the Environment Southland decision states it adopted an **eight-year lapse period "for consistency" with the SDC consent**, which is direct evidence the SDC decision had already been made with an eight-year lapse [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf). But **I could not retrieve the SDC decision document by this research path** — it is not on SDC's notified-consents page, and SDC's decisions register is an ePathway portal I could not query statically (it returned a server error to a plain fetcher). Absence from the notified list is *consistent with* a non-notified grant but does not prove it, and does not prove the decision is unpublished — a register query I could not run may return it. Either way the exact SDC conditions still require that register lookup or a LGOIMA request.
- **Invercargill City Council (ICC) granted a consent connected to the Tasman Ring cable landing at Ōreti Beach** — the terrestrial side of the landing falls in ICC's jurisdiction (the Environment Southland decision records ICC as the authority for land disturbance landward of the coastal marine area boundary) [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf), and ICC Mayor Tom Campbell publicly defended *"the resource consent"* and its effects assessment [ODT — "Invercargill mayor stands by comments…"](https://www.odt.co.nz/southland/invercargill-mayor-stands-comments-over-ai-data-centre-cable); [1News, 17 Mar 2026](https://www.1news.co.nz/2026/03/17/invercargill-mayor-stands-by-comments-over-ai-factory-cable/). Whether that is a full land-use resource consent or a narrower corridor approval — a refinement of the baseline's "road-closure" guess — awaits the ICC document. As with SDC, the **full ICC decision document is not publicly posted**; the cable conditions reported publicly (500m marine-mammal suspension, 210dB soft-start, toheroa relocation, iwi notification, 35-year term) trace to commissioner **Hugh Leersnyder's** report and overlap the Environment Southland *coastal* permits, so which conditions sit in the ICC land-use consent versus the ES coastal permit cannot be split without the ICC document [RNZ LDR, 13 Mar 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/ldr/589535/massive-ai-factory-could-impact-sea-life-cultural-values).
- **Net effect on the stream's baseline:** the three-council consent claim is now better supported than "company-announced" — the SDC consent is corroborated by the ES decision's cross-reference, and the ICC consent by the ICC mayor's on-record defence of it — but the *exact SDC and ICC conditions remain Low-to-Medium confidence* until a human obtains the two decision documents via the council registers or LGOIMA. The MW reconciliation is well-supported (Medium) — the IT-load-vs-total-power split is directly sourced, but its precise apportionment to a design PUE rests on inference, not a stated figure.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the MW reconciliation and the *existence* of the SDC and ICC consents are well supported by primary/on-record sources, but the two council decision documents themselves could not be retrieved online, so their precise conditions are not independently verified.

## Evidence

### The 240MW-IT vs 280MW reconciliation

Two figures circulate for "the size" of the campus and they measure different things:

- **240MW = IT capacity / IT load.** The Environment Southland regional consent decision — the one primary consent document in the public record — describes the facility as providing **"up to 240MW of IT capacity"** [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf). "IT capacity"/"IT load" is a data-centre term of art: the electrical power actually delivered to the computing equipment (servers, GPUs, storage, network). DataGrid's own park description has long used the same framing — that the park "could reach over 240MW of IT load" [DataGrid / w.media park profile](https://w.media/datagrid-new-zealand-gets-oio-approval-for-data-center-park-extension/).
- **280MW = total power the campus consumes.** DataGrid's press release says the facility will **"consume 280 MW of power"** [DataGrid press release, 11 Mar 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent), and its website states the park is **"Designed to deliver a capacity of 280 MW"** while claiming a **"world leading projected power usage efficiency (PUE), achieved through a rare combination of renewable power and a naturally cool, stable climate"** [DataGrid](https://www.datagrid.nz/). "Consume 280 MW" is the total draw, not just the IT load. Reporting treats 280MW as the operative grid-draw / nameplate figure — e.g. an energy analysis models the site at "around 2.45 TWh of electricity based on the nameplate capacity of 280 MW" [New Zealand Energy, 17 Mar 2026](https://newzealandenergy.substack.com/p/the-280mw-question). A dedicated **220kV Transpower grid connection** is planned to deliver it [DataGrid press release, 11 Mar 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent).

The metric that relates them is **PUE** (Power Usage Effectiveness), defined by the international data-centre KPI standard as **total facility energy ÷ IT-equipment energy** — the "total" including cooling, power-conversion, distribution losses, lighting and the like [ISO/IEC 30134-2, via Power usage effectiveness (definition)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_usage_effectiveness). Here 280 ÷ 240 ≈ **1.17**. To judge whether that is plausible: the Uptime Institute's 2024 global survey puts the **industry-average PUE at ~1.56**, while the large cloud hyperscalers "already claim PUE of 1.2 or lower at some sites" [Uptime Institute — Global Data Center Survey 2024](https://uptimeinstitute.com/resources/research-and-reports/uptime-institute-global-data-center-survey-results-2024). So a ~1.17 implied ratio sits at the efficient end of the range — squarely consistent with DataGrid's cool-climate "world-leading PUE" claim [DataGrid](https://www.datagrid.nz/). The important caveat: **no source states DataGrid's actual design PUE**, and no source writes the equation "280 = 240 × PUE" outright. So the reconciliation — that the two numbers describe **one** consented facility (~240MW IT load needing ~280MW total supply), not two projects or a quiet upsizing — is a well-supported *inference*: the IT-load-vs-total-power split is directly sourced, but attributing the exact ~40MW gap to overhead rests on the unquantified PUE claim. That is why it is Medium, not High.

**Keep this distinct from the phase/ambition axis.** The consented build is ~240MW IT / ~280MW total. Separately, DataGrid's phase-1 power contract with Mercury is a **140MW** Power Purchase Option (so first-phase energised load is smaller again) [itbrief](https://itbrief.co.nz/story/datagrid-signs-140mw-deal-to-power-southland-ai-campus), and its long-term *ambition* is "up to 1GW" — unconsented and unsupplied [w.media, 23 Jul 2024](https://w.media/datagrid-new-zealand-gets-oio-approval-for-data-center-park-extension/). The 240/280 pair is a single-facility units question; the 140MW/240MW/1GW figures are a phasing-and-ambition question. Conflating the two is the main way public descriptions of "the project" diverge.

### The Southland District Council land-use consent

The Environment Southland decision is explicit that the **SDC application was decided separately** from the regional consents, and that Environment Southland set its own consents to an **eight-year lapse period specifically "for consistency" with the Southland District Council consents** [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf). That cross-reference is strong evidence that, at the time the regional decision was signed (11 March 2026), an SDC land-use consent for the data-centre site at **342 & 370 Flora Road East, Makarewa** already existed and carried an eight-year lapse. DataGrid's announcement independently names SDC as one of the three granting authorities [DataGrid press release, 11 Mar 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent), and SDC Mayor Rob Scott and ICC Mayor Tom Campbell both spoke publicly to having read/backed the consent [ODT — "Southern mayors back massive AI factory…"](https://www.odt.co.nz/southland/southern-mayors-back-massive-ai-factory-amid-concern-over-impacts).

What I **could not retrieve by this research path** is the SDC decision document itself. SDC's own "notified consents and hearings" page lists no Datagrid/data-centre entry [SDC notified consents & hearings](https://www.southlanddc.govt.nz/home-and-property/resource-consents/notified-consents-and-hearings/). That absence is *consistent with* the land-use consent having been processed **non-notified** (as the regional consents were), but it is not proof of that — nor proof the decision is unpublished, since the decision would sit in the register rather than on the notified-hearings page. SDC's public decisions register is an ePathway portal reached from its resource-consents page [SDC resource consents](https://www.southlanddc.govt.nz/home-and-property/resource-consents/); that portal is an ASP.NET enquiry form and returned a recoverable server error to a plain fetcher on 4 Jul 2026, so I could not query it statically — a browser session may well return the decision. The earlier SDC application referenced in the fast-track file was **RMA/2002/53409**, placed on hold under RMA s91D over the National Policy Statement for Highly Productive Land [FTA104 application](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/what-government-is-doing/Fast-Track-Unlisted/Datagrid-Sustainable-Data-Centre-Park/104.01-response-ANON-URZ4-5FRF-8_Redacted.pdf); whether the March 2026 grant is that application resumed or a fresh application number is exactly the kind of detail the SDC file would settle.

### The Invercargill City Council consent

A likely refinement to the stream baseline: the evidence points to **ICC having granted a resource consent connected to the subsea-cable landing at Ōreti Beach**, rather than only a road-closure/road-controlling approval as [the factual-baseline finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/214) had speculated. On jurisdiction, the Environment Southland decision itself records that **Invercargill City Council is the regulatory authority for land disturbance landward of the coastal marine area boundary** at the beach — i.e. the terrestrial side of the landing falls to ICC, while the coastal marine area below mean high-water springs is Environment Southland's [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf). DataGrid names ICC as one of the granting authorities [DataGrid press release, 11 Mar 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent), and ICC **Mayor Tom Campbell publicly stood by "the resource consent"**, arguing effects were assessed as not "significant" and that the cable would give Southland "the fastest internet in the country" [ODT — "Invercargill mayor stands by comments…"](https://www.odt.co.nz/southland/invercargill-mayor-stands-comments-over-ai-data-centre-cable); [1News, 17 Mar 2026](https://www.1news.co.nz/2026/03/17/invercargill-mayor-stands-by-comments-over-ai-factory-cable/). A mayor defending "the resource consent" his council granted is on-record corroboration that ICC issued a consent of some kind. What the sources do **not** pin down is the exact instrument — a full land-use resource consent versus a narrower corridor/road approval — which only the ICC decision document will settle; the "resource consent" characterisation here follows the mayor's and DataGrid's wording, not an ICC decision I have sighted.

The cable conditions reported publicly — suspend work if marine mammals are within **500m**, gradual (soft-start) ramp-up of **210dB** equipment, and mana-whenua relocation of any **toheroa** unearthed — are set out in [ODT — "Southern mayors back…"](https://www.odt.co.nz/southland/southern-mayors-back-massive-ai-factory-amid-concern-over-impacts) and correspond to conditions in the Environment Southland **coastal permits AUTH-20252550-06/-07** documented in the consent-record finding [Environment Southland decision](https://www.es.govt.nz/repository/libraries/id%3A26gi9ayo517q9stt81sd/hierarchy/environment/consents/documents/Datagrid%20resource%20consents/Resource%20Consent%20Decision%20APP-20252550.pdf); the RNZ Local Democracy report separately covers commissioner **Hugh Leersnyder's** assessment of cultural and marine-mammal impacts and the **35-year** consent term sought against a **25-year** cable design life [RNZ LDR, 13 Mar 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/ldr/589535/massive-ai-factory-could-impact-sea-life-cultural-values). Because these overlap so heavily with the ES coastal permits, I cannot, from public reporting alone, attribute each condition to the ICC consent versus the ES coastal permit. ICC's public register/decisions are on its own site, which is bot-protected against automated fetchers (HTTP 403/404 to non-browser clients on 4 Jul 2026) [ICC resource consents](https://icc.govt.nz/planning-resource-management/resource-consents/), so the ICC decision document also needs a register lookup or LGOIMA release.

### How a human can obtain the two missing documents

Both councils publish decisions through public registers; where those don't expose the document, the **Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act 1987 (LGOIMA)** gives a public right of access to information held by local authorities, requiring a response "as soon as reasonably practicable, and in any case not later than **20 working days** after the day on which the request is received" (s10), subject to the Act's withholding grounds [LGOIMA 1987, s10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/0174/latest/whole.html); [Ombudsman NZ — LGOIMA guide for local government agencies](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/lgoima-local-government-agencies-guide-processing-requests-and-conducting-meetings):

- **SDC** — search the ePathway public register from [southlanddc.govt.nz resource consents](https://www.southlanddc.govt.nz/home-and-property/resource-consents/) for the Flora Road East, Makarewa data-centre consent, or send a LGOIMA request to SDC for *"the resource consent decision and conditions for the Datagrid NZ Partnership Limited data centre land-use consent at 342 & 370 Flora Road East, Makarewa (successor to / related to application RMA/2002/53409), including the commissioner's decision report and the schedule of conditions."*
- **ICC** — request from ICC *"the resource consent decision and conditions granted to Datagrid NZ Partnership Limited for the Tasman Ring subsea cable landing and terrestrial cable route at Ōreti Beach (Dunns Road), including the decision report, notification decision, and schedule of conditions."*

This is flagged for a human because filing LGOIMA requests and awaiting the statutory response is outside an automated research pass.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The SDC decision document.** It would confirm the activity status, the exact conditions (noise, light, earthworks, building, hours), the lapse date, and whether the grant is application RMA/2002/53409 resumed or a new number — upgrading the SDC conditions from Low-Medium to High. Obtainable via the SDC ePathway register or a LGOIMA request (text above).
- **The ICC decision document.** It would settle whether ICC issued a land-use resource consent for the cable landing (as the mayor's language implies) and let the reported cable conditions be split cleanly between the ICC consent and the ES coastal permits. Obtainable via ICC's register or LGOIMA.
- **An explicit DataGrid/consent statement of the design PUE.** If DataGrid or the technical assessment states a PUE materially different from ~1.17, the IT-load-vs-total reconciliation would need revising (e.g. if 280MW turned out to be a later *IT* upsizing rather than total facility power). Nothing found contradicts the ~1.17 reading, but it rests on DataGrid's unquantified "world-leading PUE" claim.
- **Could not verify:** the full text/conditions of the SDC and ICC decisions (not posted online; council registers are ASP.NET/bot-protected and were not machine-fetchable on 4 Jul 2026); the precise SDC application number for the 2026 grant. These need a human with register access or a LGOIMA response — the statutory route is identified above.
