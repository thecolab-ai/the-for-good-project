# New Zealand's addiction policy landscape is active but fragmented across health, justice, gambling regulation, councils, and community providers

## Executive answer

- **Current government policy is not blank, but it is split across several systems.** The National-led Government has a 2026-2029 health-system substance-harm action plan, five mental health and addiction targets, a methamphetamine ministerial action plan, a 2026 alcohol-regulation amendment bill, a 2025/26-2027/28 gambling harm strategy, and staged implementation of the Online Casino Gambling Act 2026. [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Health NZ, mental health and addiction targets](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/mental-health-and-addiction-targets); [Ministry of Justice, actions to combat methamphetamine](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [Ministry of Justice, improving alcohol regulation](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/alcohol-reform/); [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728); [DIA, online gambling provider guidance](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers)
- **Delivery responsibilities are distributed, not centralised.** The Ministry of Health is the health system's lead policy, regulation and monitoring advisor; Health NZ funds and delivers mental health, addiction and Hauora Māori services; Justice leads alcohol-policy and meth action-plan machinery; Police and Customs sit on supply/enforcement and wastewater intelligence; DIA is the primary gambling regulator; and councils are statutory decision points for alcohol licensing and class 4/TAB venue policies. [Ministry of Health, our role](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/our-role); [Health NZ, role and priorities](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/our-role-and-priorities); [Ministry of Justice, meth action](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/); [NZ Police, Q1 2026 wastewater programme](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026); [New Zealand Customs, drug interceptions](https://www.customs.govt.nz/information-releases-and-legislation/statistics/drug-interceptions); [DIA, regulatory agency roles](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-regulatory-agency-roles); [Ministry of Justice, local councils and alcohol](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/key-initiatives-archive/sale-and-supply-of-alcohol/community-involvement/role-of-local-councils/)
- **Labour's clearest previous reform in this stream is the 2023 community-participation alcohol law; current opposition/minor-party positions are issue-level rather than one complete alternative package.** Labour's current site has a methamphetamine criticism and a current policy page focused on wider health/cost issues; the Greens have a standing harm-minimisation drug policy and a live overdose-assistance member's bill; ACT has a verified alcohol-licensing fairness/red-tape position and supported the overdose-assistance bill at first reading; Te Pāti Māori has Māori-led health/justice policy and supports decriminalising drug use; the most concrete New Zealand First-specific source I verified was its vote against the overdose-assistance bill at first reading. [Beehive, 2023 alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Labour, meth release](https://www.labour.org.nz/news/release-govt-must-tackle-meth-use-crisis/); [Labour, current policies](https://www.labour.org.nz/our-policies/); [Green Party, drug policy](https://www.greens.org.nz/drug_policy); [Hansard, 29 April 2026 overdose bill vote](https://hansard.parliament.nz/hansard-transcript/2026-04-29/bills-drug-overdose-assistance-protection-legislation); [ACT, alcohol law proposals](https://www.act.org.nz/news/act-delivers-results-parmjeet-parmar%E2%80%99s-alcohol-law-proposals-adopted-in-government-reforms); [Te Pāti Māori, health policy](https://www.maoriparty.org.nz/health); [Te Pāti Māori, justice statement](https://www.maoriparty.org.nz/if_you_re_not_trying_to_abolish_prisons_in_aotearoa_what_are_you_doing_in_parliament)
- **A small volunteer project should aim at navigation, evidence packaging, service-directory quality, and civic-participation tooling, not clinical advice, enforcement, licensing, or Treaty-partnership decisions.** Public sources point to low-authority gaps around helping people find free gambling/AOD support, drug-checking providers, council licensing channels and submission opportunities, while clinical treatment, drug checking operations, alcohol/gambling law settings, enforcement powers, and kaupapa Māori service design require regulated providers, statutory authority, clinical governance, lived-experience co-design, or Crown/Māori partnership. [Health NZ, gambling harm programmes](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/gambling-harm-programmes); [Ministry of Health, drug checking](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/drug-checking); [Healthpoint, kaupapa Māori gambling harm directory](https://www.healthpoint.co.nz/mental-health-addictions/gambling-harm/kaupapa-maori-problem-gambling/); [Ministry of Health, Te Tiriti framework](https://www.health.govt.nz/maori-health/te-tiriti-o-waitangi-framework)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the machinery and role map are well supported by official sources, but current party positions are unevenly published and the finding did not include lived-experience, provider, iwi, council or clinical interviews.

## Evidence

### Current government policy settings

The Action Plan to Prevent and Reduce Substance Harm 2026-2029, published on 18 March 2026, frames the health-system response around prevention and early intervention, access to services, workforce growth, and system effectiveness. [Ministry of Health, substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029)

Health NZ's mental health and addiction targets were announced by the Government in July 2024, aim to be achieved by 2030, and include faster access to specialist services, faster access to primary mental health and addiction services, shorter mental-health/addiction ED stays, training 500 mental health and addiction professionals each year, and allocating 25% of mental health and addiction investment to prevention and early intervention. [Health NZ, mental health and addiction targets](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/mental-health-and-addiction-targets)

The Ministry of Justice says the methamphetamine response is being advanced through a Methamphetamine Ministerial Group led by the Minister of Justice, with actions covering international supply disruption, maritime/border security, police enforcement powers, proceeds-of-crime recovery, a public-awareness campaign, increased services for communities hit hardest by meth, and $23.1 million in October 2025 for offshore liaison, money-laundering disruption and Resilience to Organised Crime in Communities work. [Ministry of Justice, meth action](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/actions-to-combat-meth/)

The Government's Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Improving Alcohol Regulation) Amendment Bill is a 2026 Government bill at select committee stage, and the Ministry of Justice says it aims to make alcohol licensing more practical while maintaining public safety, including District Licensing Committee changes, rapid-delivery controls, digital age verification, zero/no-alcohol drink rules, cellar-door changes and special-licence streamlining. [Ministry of Justice, improving alcohol regulation](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/alcohol-reform/); [Parliament bills API via vendored `nz-parliament` CLI, bill 262-1](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/b98f4d35-d0bd-47ab-a326-08de82fd2ebd)

The Ministry of Health confirmed the 2026/27 alcohol levy at $17.135 million, says the funding is ring-fenced and reinvested in alcohol-harm services, and lists supported activities including community programmes, screening and brief interventions, FASD prevention/support, impaired-driving prevention, interlock pilots and targeted health-promotion campaigns. [Ministry of Health, alcohol levy 2026/27](https://www.health.govt.nz/news/alcohol-levy-confirmed-to-support-harm-reduction-across-communities)

For gambling, the Ministry of Health's 2025/26-2027/28 strategy took effect on 1 July 2025 and includes a three-year service plan, levy rates for non-casino gaming machine operators, casinos, TAB NZ and Lotto NZ, and the problem-gambling levy regulations for the same period. [Ministry of Health, gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728)

For online casino gambling, DIA says the Online Casino Gambling Act 2026 is in force, the full licensed regime is not expected to be operational until 2027, EOI submissions are expected in July 2026, an auction is expected in September 2026, applications are expected in October 2026, and a prohibition phase starts on 1 December 2026 before exemptions end on 1 June 2027. [DIA, online gambling provider guidance](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers); [legislation.govt.nz via vendored `legislation-nz` CLI, Online Casino Gambling Act 2026](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2026/14/en/latest/)

### Delivery roles and statutory levers

The Ministry of Health's role is system stewardship: it says it is the lead advisor to government on health and works through policy, regulation and monitoring, including monitoring Health NZ and other Crown health entities. [Ministry of Health, our role](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/our-role)

Health NZ's role is delivery and commissioning: it says it leads day-to-day planning and delivery of publicly funded hospital, primary and community health services, including mental health and addiction services and Hauora Māori health services. [Health NZ, role and priorities](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/our-role-and-priorities)

Health NZ's alcohol and other drug programme page says it provides information, advice, research and resources for preventing AOD-related harm, delivers alcohol health-promotion programmes, and funds nationwide Impaired Driving Rehabilitation programmes through 10 local providers. [Health NZ, alcohol and other drug programmes](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/alcohol-and-other-drug-programmes)

Health NZ's gambling harm programme page says it manages Safer Gambling Aotearoa, supports sector messaging and host-responsibility work with DIA, the Ministry and gambling venues, funds free confidential counselling through contracted services, contracts public-health services to prevent and minimise gambling harm, and leads national education and awareness work. [Health NZ, gambling harm programmes](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/gambling-harm-programmes)

The Ministry of Health regulates drug checking: it licenses providers, renews/suspends/cancels licences, sets licence conditions, manages complaints and compliance, and lists the current licensed providers as KnowYourStuffNZ, PHF Science, the NZ Drug Foundation, the University of Auckland School of Pharmacy research project, and Drug Injecting Services Canterbury Trust. [Ministry of Health, drug checking](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/drug-checking)

Police provide wastewater intelligence and enforcement capacity; the Q1 2026 wastewater report covers almost 80% of New Zealand's population and focuses on methamphetamine, MDMA and cocaine because they are routinely detected. [NZ Police, Q1 2026 wastewater programme](https://www.police.govt.nz/about-us/publication/national-drugs-wastewater-programme-quarter-1-2026)

Customs is a border supply-disruption actor; its drug-interception statistics are Customs seizure statistics from 2010 onwards, and the page notes 2025 data were preliminary as at 7 April 2026. [New Zealand Customs, drug interceptions](https://www.customs.govt.nz/information-releases-and-legislation/statistics/drug-interceptions)

DIA is the primary gambling regulator and says it undertakes gambling licensing, compliance and enforcement under the Gambling Act 2003 while balancing harm minimisation against community benefit from gambling proceeds. [DIA, regulatory agency roles](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-regulatory-agency-roles)

Councils have two separate statutory surfaces: territorial authorities run District Licensing Committees for alcohol licence and manager-certificate decisions, and local/unitary councils develop and review class 4 venue and TAB venue policies that can limit gambling opportunities and manage local gambling impacts. [Ministry of Justice, local councils and alcohol](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/key-initiatives-archive/sale-and-supply-of-alcohol/community-involvement/role-of-local-councils/); [DIA, gambling regulatory agency roles](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Gambling-regulatory-agency-roles)

### Previous Labour-led alcohol reforms and current party positions

The clearest previous Labour-led alcohol reform for this issue is the Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Community Participation) Amendment Bill, passed in August 2023, which removed appeals against provisional local alcohol policies, removed cross-examination at alcohol licensing hearings, allowed any person or group to object to a licence application except trade competitors, and extended objection timeframes. [Beehive, alcohol community-participation reforms](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws)

Labour's current verified opposition material is issue-specific rather than a complete alternative addiction policy package: Labour's website carries a 2025 methamphetamine release criticising the Government's response and citing Police wastewater data, while Labour's current policy page foregrounds wider affordability, health and cost-of-living commitments rather than a standalone alcohol, gambling or drug-harm platform. [Labour, meth release](https://www.labour.org.nz/news/release-govt-must-tackle-meth-use-crisis/); [Labour, current policies](https://www.labour.org.nz/our-policies/)

Labour MP Kieran McAnulty's Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Sales on Anzac Day Morning, Good Friday, Easter Sunday, and Christmas Day) Amendment Bill passed first reading ("first hurdle") in Labour messaging and then received Royal Assent on 1 April 2026, repealing public-holiday alcohol-sale restrictions for licensed businesses already permitted to open on those days. [Parliament bills API via vendored `nz-parliament` CLI, bill 123-3](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/4d9ebb89-9a83-4c10-5318-08dd514abaa2); [Labour, trading laws bill release](https://www.labour.org.nz/news/release-bill-to-make-trading-laws-fairer-passes-first-hurdle/)

The Green Party has a current official drug policy based on evidence-based harm minimisation, accessible services for substance-use disorders, regulated cannabis, harm-reduction education, and Te Tiriti obligations in drug policy. [Green Party, drug policy](https://www.greens.org.nz/drug_policy)

The Green Party's Drug Overdose (Assistance Protection) Legislation Bill (which the Green Party described as the "Drug Overdose (Assistance Protection) Amendment Bill") is at select committee stage and is intended to protect people who call for help during an overdose or adverse reaction from being charged for low-level drug offences under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1975. [Parliament bills API via vendored `nz-parliament` CLI, bill 204-1](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/9a67a135-1a72-4fd5-7a12-08ddf0d773dc); [Green Party, bill naming](https://www.greens.org.nz/green_party_bill_to_protect_overdose_emergency_callers_from_legal_risks_receives_cross_party_support)

Hansard records the overdose-assistance bill's first reading on 29 April 2026 as passing with Ayes from Labour, Greens, ACT, Te Pāti Māori and one independent, and Noes from National and New Zealand First. [Hansard, 29 April 2026 overdose bill vote](https://hansard.parliament.nz/hansard-transcript/2026-04-29/bills-drug-overdose-assistance-protection-legislation)

ACT's verified party-specific position in this issue set is deregulatory/practical alcohol licensing: ACT MP Parmjeet Parmar says her alcohol-law proposals were adopted in the Government's 2026 reforms, including limiting objections to people who live or work locally and giving applicants a right of reply. [ACT, alcohol law proposals](https://www.act.org.nz/news/act-delivers-results-parmjeet-parmar%E2%80%99s-alcohol-law-proposals-adopted-in-government-reforms)

Te Pāti Māori's current health policy calls for rebuilding and strengthening Māori-led health authority and solutions, shifting toward prevention and whānau wellbeing, and making services culturally safe and accountable to Māori. [Te Pāti Māori, health policy](https://www.maoriparty.org.nz/health)

Te Pāti Māori's January 2026 justice statement says immediate steps toward its long-term justice goal include decriminalising drug use, moving resources from prisons to prevention, rehabilitation and community-led solutions, and focusing on healing rather than punitive incarceration. [Te Pāti Māori, justice statement](https://www.maoriparty.org.nz/if_you_re_not_trying_to_abolish_prisons_in_aotearoa_what_are_you_doing_in_parliament)

I did not verify a current New Zealand First alcohol, gambling or drug-treatment policy page equivalent to the Green and Te Pāti Māori issue pages; the most concrete current New Zealand First-specific source in this review is Hansard's record that New Zealand First voted against the overdose-assistance bill at first reading. [Hansard, 29 April 2026 overdose bill vote](https://hansard.parliament.nz/hansard-transcript/2026-04-29/bills-drug-overdose-assistance-protection-legislation)

### Iwi/Māori and community-provider landscape

The Ministry of Health's Te Tiriti framework says the Crown must provide for and properly resource kaupapa Māori health and disability services, and that Māori must be co-designers with the Crown in governance, design, delivery and monitoring of the primary health system for Māori. [Ministry of Health, Te Tiriti framework](https://www.health.govt.nz/maori-health/te-tiriti-o-waitangi-framework)

Healthpoint's public directory lists 13 kaupapa Māori gambling-harm providers and describes kaupapa Māori gambling harm as gambling and other addiction programmes provided in a kaupapa Māori framework. [Healthpoint, kaupapa Māori gambling harm directory](https://www.healthpoint.co.nz/mental-health-addictions/gambling-harm/kaupapa-maori-problem-gambling/)

Hāpai Te Hauora describes its kaupapa as including Māori public-health leadership, alcohol and other drugs, mental health and wellbeing, and prevention and minimisation of gambling harm, while Te Rau Ora's provider directory describes Hāpai as coordinating, workforce-development and advocacy services across gambling-harm minimisation and tobacco control with a focus on high-need populations. [Hāpai Te Hauora, kaupapa](https://hapai.co.nz/); [Te Rau Ora, Hāpai provider entry](https://terauora.com/directory-maori_provid/provider/hapai-te-hauora/)

Established non-government advocacy and information organisations in the landscape include Health Coalition Aotearoa on alcohol policy, Amohia te Waiora/Alcohol.org.nz on alcohol-harm information and resources, and the NZ Drug Foundation on drug-harm information, safer drug laws and harm reduction. [Health Coalition Aotearoa, alcohol](https://www.healthcoalition.org.nz/alcohol/); [Alcohol.org.nz, Amohia te Waiora](https://www.alcohol.org.nz/home); [NZ Drug Foundation](https://drugfoundation.org.nz/home)
