# Alternative source countries could absorb a small China-origin student shock over time, but not a fast 50% shock at comparable certainty or revenue quality

Part of #438. Split from #502. This finding answers whether India, Southeast Asia, and other source countries have enough structural capacity to replace a 25-50% fall in China-origin international student enrolments, not whether current diversification policy is well designed.

## Executive answer

- **A 25% China shock is plausible only as a multi-market, multi-year replacement.** China supplied 34% of 92,580 international enrolments in 2025, or about 31,500 students, so a 25% shock is roughly 7,900 enrolments and a 50% shock is roughly 15,800 enrolments [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). India at 14% is the only alternative market near the required scale, while the Philippines, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Vietnam, Thailand, and other Southeast Asian markets are individually small [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [ICEF Monitor, 2025](https://monitor.icef.com/2025/12/new-zealands-international-student-numbers-climbing-amid-strong-public-support/).
- **India has volume but not China-like conversion certainty.** In 2025 offshore student visa decisions, China had 9,959 applications and a 97% approval rate, while India had 7,235 applications and a 59% approval rate; Nepal was 52%, Sri Lanka 80%, the Philippines 93%, and Vietnam 84% [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).
- **Revenue replacement is not assured.** ENZ's annual report says China generated $1.43b of education-related travel exports in the year ended June 2025, equal to 33.2% of all education-related travel exports, which is close to China's 34% enrolment share; I could not verify equivalent India or Southeast Asia fee-per-student figures because the official Export Education Levy workbook is discoverable but Cloudflare-blocked from this environment [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf); [Education Counts](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand); [data.govt.nz CKAN API](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_show?id=international-students-studying-in-new-zealand).
- **Aggregate provider headroom exists, but it is not frictionless capacity.** The sector had 92,580 enrolments in 2025, still 80% of the pre-COVID peak, and the government target is 105,000 enrolments in 2027 and 119,000 by 2034; that implies numerical headroom for a low shock, but ENZ says recovery is uneven across subsectors and its own growth planning still depends on understanding provider capacity and barriers [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [International Education Going for Growth Plan, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/International-Education-Going-for-Growth-Plan-2025.pdf); [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf).
- **The global pipeline is real but contested.** OECD data shows China and India remained the two largest origins for internationally mobile tertiary students in OECD countries in 2022, with Vietnam, Nepal, and India also among the top ten; however, more than two-thirds of Chinese and Indian students abroad were concentrated in Australia, Canada, Japan, the UK, and the US, which means New Zealand competes against much larger destinations for the same demand [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/03/what-are-the-key-trends-in-international-student-mobility_495dcfac/2a423a76-en.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - enrolment, visa, policy, and agent-channel claims are supported by current official sources; the revenue-comparability claim is Medium-Low because the source-country fee workbook could not be fetched and the available public evidence is China-specific.

## Evidence

### Scale and product-market fit

The replacement problem is large relative to every non-China source market: ENZ's 2025 full-year data reports 92,580 international students, with China at 34%, India at 14%, Japan at 9%, South Korea and Sri Lanka at 4% each, and Germany, the United States, Nepal, and the Philippines at 3% each [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). Applying those shares to the 2025 total gives about 31,500 China enrolments, about 13,000 India enrolments, about 8,300 Japan enrolments, about 3,700 each for South Korea and Sri Lanka, and about 2,800 each for Germany, the United States, Nepal, and the Philippines [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

That means replacing a 25% fall in China enrolments would require growth roughly equal to another 60% of India's current New Zealand enrolment base, and replacing a 50% fall would require more than doubling India's current base if India alone did the work [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). A mixed-market replacement is more plausible than an India-only replacement, because ENZ says strong growth has recently come from Sri Lanka and Nepal, and ICEF's summary of ENZ's Jan-Aug 2025 data says Nepal rose from about 250 students in 2022 to close to 2,500 in 2025, while Sri Lanka moved into New Zealand's fourth-largest source position [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [ICEF Monitor, 2025](https://monitor.icef.com/2025/12/new-zealands-international-student-numbers-climbing-amid-strong-public-support/).

Southeast Asia is attractive for product-market fit but too small to carry the shock by itself: the Philippines was only 3% of 2025 enrolments, Vietnam appears in ENZ/ICEF's top ten but below the 3% markets, and INZ's 2025 offshore visa decision table shows only 1,239 Philippines and 842 Vietnam student-visa applications, compared with 9,959 from China and 7,235 from India [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [ICEF Monitor, 2025](https://monitor.icef.com/2025/12/new-zealands-international-student-numbers-climbing-amid-strong-public-support/); [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).

Latin America does not look like a near-term China-shock absorber at current New Zealand visa scale: Brazil had 666 offshore student visa applications in 2025 with an 85% approval rate, Colombia had 464 applications with a 63% approval rate, Chile had 280 applications with an 83% approval rate, Mexico had 140 applications with a 92% approval rate, and Peru had 67 applications with a 55% approval rate [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/). Latin America may be useful for diversification at the margin, but the 2025 public visa volumes are one to two orders of magnitude below the 7,900-15,800 China-shock range [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).

### Pricing and revenue comparability

The best public China-specific revenue evidence suggests China is not merely high-volume but revenue-aligned with the sector average: ENZ's 2024/25 annual report states that New Zealand exported $1.43b of education-related travel to China in the year ended June 2025, equal to 33.2% of all education-related travel exports, while China was around 34% of enrolments [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf); [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). This is a useful directional check, but education-related travel includes more than tuition fees, and it does not prove that India, Nepal, Sri Lanka, the Philippines, or Vietnam pay the same per-student tuition or living-spend profile [Stats NZ June 2025 international trade workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-June-2025-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-june-2025-quarter.xlsx).

The source that should settle source-country tuition comparison is the Export Education Levy full-year statistics workbook, because Education Counts says it includes tuition fees and international student enrolments by provider type, region, and selected country of citizenship [Education Counts](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand). The data.govt.nz CKAN API confirms the workbook resource and stable URL, but direct `curl` and the repo's browser fetch ladder returned a Cloudflare 403/BLOCKED classification from this environment, so I did not use inaccessible workbook values for India or Southeast Asia [data.govt.nz CKAN API](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_show?id=international-students-studying-in-new-zealand); [Education Counts workbook URL](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/export-education-levy-full-year-statistics.xlsx).

There is a visible sector-mix risk even without the blocked fee table: the New Zealand China Council's statistics page, citing Ministry of Education data, reports 18,010 of China's 31,545 students were in universities and 7,495 in schools in 2025, while ICEF's summary of ENZ data says most Chinese students study in universities and Indian students are more evenly split across vocational and higher-education subsectors [New Zealand China Council](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/); [ICEF Monitor, 2025](https://monitor.icef.com/2025/12/new-zealands-international-student-numbers-climbing-amid-strong-public-support/). If replacement growth is concentrated in lower-fee or shorter-duration pathways, a 1:1 enrolment replacement would not be a 1:1 revenue replacement; this remains Medium-Low confidence until the Export Education Levy country-by-provider fee table is accessible [Education Counts](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand).

### Visa and regulatory barriers

New Zealand's headline student settings became more recruitment-friendly in late 2025: INZ increased eligible in-study work rights from 20 to 25 hours a week from 3 November 2025, extended work rights to approved exchange and Study Abroad tertiary students, and said these changes were part of the International Education Going for Growth Plan [Immigration NZ, 2025](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/upcoming-changes-to-student-visa-work-rights/). The Fee Paying Student Visa still requires an offer from an approved provider, payment of full course costs, and funds for living costs, including NZD $20,000 per year for tertiary, English-language, or other non-compulsory study of one year or more [Immigration NZ Fee Paying Student Visa](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/visas/fee-paying-student-visa/).

The country-specific barrier is not a formally published "risk category" in the sources I found; it is visible in outcomes. INZ's 2025 offshore student visa decision table reports China at 9,959 applications, 9,704 approved, and a 97% approval rate; India at 7,235 applications, 4,276 approved, and a 59% approval rate; Nepal at 1,957 applications, 1,024 approved, and a 52% approval rate; Sri Lanka at 1,867 applications, 1,491 approved, and an 80% approval rate; the Philippines at 1,239 applications, 1,151 approved, and a 93% approval rate; and Vietnam at 842 applications, 711 approved, and an 84% approval rate [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).

Those approval rates make the structural replacement task steeper than enrolment shares imply: at India's 2025 offshore approval rate, producing 7,900 additional approved student visas would require roughly 13,400 additional offshore decisions if application quality and approval rates did not change, and producing 15,800 would require roughly 26,800 additional decisions [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/). The Philippines and Vietnam have stronger approval rates, but their 2025 offshore application volumes are too small to absorb a China shock without very large growth in awareness, agent conversion, and provider recruitment activity [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).

Competitor policy cuts both ways. Australia is more expensive and more managed after its July 2026 student visa charge increase to AUD $2,500, but ASEAN passport holders, including the Philippines and Vietnam, are exempt from that increase [Study Australia, 2026](https://www.studyaustralia.gov.au/en/tools-and-resources/news/student-visa-application-charge-increase). Australia also processes offshore student visas under Ministerial Directions 111/115 and says the direction is not a visa cap, but its November 2025 ministerial release says lodgements were down more than 26% and commencements down 16% under managed growth [Australian Department of Home Affairs](https://immi.homeaffairs.gov.au/visas/getting-a-visa/visa-processing-times/visa-processing-priorities/student-visa); [Australian Ministers' Media Centre, 2025](https://ministers.education.gov.au/hill/new-ministerial-direction-balance-international-student-distribution).

Canada is less open than it was: IRCC planned to issue 437,000 study permits in 2025, a 10% decrease from the 2024 cap, and extended provincial/territorial attestation requirements to master's and doctoral students and most in-Canada applicants [IRCC, 2025](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/news/notices/2025-provincial-territorial-allocations-under-international-student-cap.html). The UK also tightened family migration for most students starting courses from 1 January 2024, with dependants limited to government-sponsored students or qualifying postgraduate/research routes [GOV.UK Student Visa dependants](https://www.gov.uk/student-visa/family-members). These competitor restrictions may redirect some demand to New Zealand, but they also reflect the same integrity, housing, and migration pressures that could constrain New Zealand if growth accelerates too quickly [OECD, 2025 higher-education report](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/international-students-in-higher-education_005ff28d-en/full-report/overview_61547f95.html).

### Provider, pathway, and logistics capacity

New Zealand has aggregate numerical headroom because 2025 enrolments were 80% of the pre-COVID peak, and the Going for Growth Plan targets 105,000 enrolments in 2027 and 119,000 by 2034, compared with 92,580 in 2025 [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [International Education Going for Growth Plan, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/International-Education-Going-for-Growth-Plan-2025.pdf). That headroom is enough in arithmetic terms for a 25% China shock and close to enough for a 50% shock by 2034, but it is not evidence that the right providers, regions, programmes, housing, English pathways, and agent channels can absorb the shock quickly [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf).

The subsector pattern matters because ENZ's 2025 release says universities grew 14% to 38,025 and schools grew 10% to 20,155, while recovery outside universities and schools was more uneven across institutes of technology, polytechnics, and other subsectors [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). ENZ's OIA-released growth-planning material says the agency was engaging through peak bodies to understand provider capacity for growth and barriers to that growth, and that its market assessments would be regularly refreshed to inform planning [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf).

English-language and pathway capacity is part of the growth model rather than a solved constraint: the Going for Growth Plan includes pathway and English Language Schools in the agent course content and calls for a targeted route to market, in-market events, and tailored content for diverse student audiences [International Education Going for Growth Plan, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/International-Education-Going-for-Growth-Plan-2025.pdf); [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf). The plan's existence supports the conclusion that the government sees route-to-market and pathway capability as necessary enablers, not that they are already at China-replacement scale [International Education Going for Growth Plan, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/International-Education-Going-for-Growth-Plan-2025.pdf).

### Agent networks and buyer relationships

China is a mature relationship market for New Zealand education. ENZ's 2024/25 annual report says the New Zealand-China High-level Education Forum convened all eight New Zealand universities and China's top ten universities, around 20 new arrangements were signed, seven vocational partnerships had been established under a Te Pūkenga/New Zealand Institute of Skills and Technology memorandum, and the June 2025 Prime Minister's trade mission to China included ten provider partnership agreements with combined education partnership revenue over NZ$43.8m [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf).

India and Vietnam are now "grow" markets, not equally mature substitutes. ENZ's annual report says Prime Ministerial trade missions went to Vietnam, India, and China, and that those three countries are considered "grow" markets in the Going for Growth Plan [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf). ENZ's OIA-released agent paper says agents have historically been used by more than 50% of international students enrolling in New Zealand, schools are anecdotally at least 80% agent-reliant, and 2024 Studymove research found 44% of international university students in New Zealand received agent assistance compared with 86% for Australia [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf).

The agent channel is being strengthened, but the evidence points to a build-out phase: ENZ launched the New Zealand Education Agent Course with ICEF Academy in June 2024, had 2,006 agent enrolments and 685 completions by June 2025, and its OIA-released agent paper says a 2024 IDP campaign across Australia, the Philippines, India, and Vietnam lifted New Zealand leads by 28% versus the previous period [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf); [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf). That is positive for medium-term absorption but not proof of immediate China-scale replacement.

### Alternative-market pipeline and competitor capacity

The global outbound-student pipeline supports diversification in principle: OECD reports that international students in OECD countries rose from 3.0m in 2014 to more than 4.6m in 2022, China and India remained the two biggest sources, Vietnam ranked third by 2022, and Nepal was also in the top ten [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/03/what-are-the-key-trends-in-international-student-mobility_495dcfac/2a423a76-en.pdf). The same OECD report says Australia, Canada, France, Germany, New Zealand, the UK, and the US hosted nearly two-thirds of international students in OECD countries in 2022, and more than two-thirds of Chinese and Indian students abroad studied in just Australia, Canada, Japan, the UK, and the US [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/03/what-are-the-key-trends-in-international-student-mobility_495dcfac/2a423a76-en.pdf).

This means the alternative markets are not supply-constrained in the abstract, but New Zealand is competing for a small share of very large flows. OECD's 2026 higher-education report says Australia, Canada, and the UK rely materially on international fees and are shifting toward more managed or sustainable recruitment, while pathways to temporary stay after graduation are important in recruitment efforts across comparator countries [OECD, 2026](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/international-students-in-higher-education_005ff28d-en/full-report/overview_61547f95.html). New Zealand can benefit if competitor restrictions push students to look elsewhere, but Australia, Canada, the UK, and the US retain stronger scale, brand familiarity, alumni networks, and agent coverage than New Zealand [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/03/what-are-the-key-trends-in-international-student-mobility_495dcfac/2a423a76-en.pdf); [OECD, 2026](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/international-students-in-higher-education_005ff28d-en/full-report/overview_61547f95.html).

## What would change this conclusion

- Access to the 2025 Export Education Levy workbook in a normal browser or through non-blocked egress would materially improve the pricing conclusion, because it should allow source-country tuition-fee-per-enrolment comparisons for China, India, Nepal, Sri Lanka, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Thailand [Education Counts](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand). **Update — 2026-07-08 re-attempt (see source note below): this workbook is now reachable.** The upgraded ADR-0006 fetch ladder's new Jina reader rung (r.jina.ai, fetching from its own reputable egress) cleared the 2026-07-05 Cloudflare / govt-bot-protection wall, so a dedicated follow-up finding can now extract the levy figures directly. Two caveats for whoever does that work: the workbook publishes *average tuition fee per EFTS by provider sector* (a single sector-level row, not by country of citizenship) and *student counts by selected citizenship by sector* on separate sheets, so a per-source-country tuition-per-student figure must be reconstructed by combining those two sheets rather than read from one published column; and the recovered values were not re-derived into this finding under the narrow re-attempt task, so the pricing conclusion below is now *recoverable* but not yet *recovered*.
- Provider-level capacity data by subsector, city, housing availability, and programme type could change the logistics conclusion; the public evidence confirms aggregate headroom and uneven recovery, but not whether specific universities, schools, PTEs, English-language providers, or regions can absorb the right students quickly [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).
- A post-2026 time series showing India, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Vietnam, or the Philippines sustaining high application growth while maintaining approval rates would strengthen the absorption case; falling approval rates would weaken it [Immigration NZ, 2025 decisions](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/study/for-education-providers/data-and-processing-times-for-international-student-visas/offshore-student-visa-application-decision-data/overseas-student-visa-application-decisions-for-2025/).
- Evidence that competitor restrictions in Australia, Canada, the UK, or the US are redirecting applicants to New Zealand rather than to other destinations would raise confidence; the sources reviewed show competitor constraints but do not measure New Zealand's capture rate [IRCC, 2025](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/news/notices/2025-provincial-territorial-allocations-under-international-student-cap.html); [Study Australia, 2026](https://www.studyaustralia.gov.au/en/tools-and-resources/news/student-visa-application-charge-increase); [GOV.UK Student Visa dependants](https://www.gov.uk/student-visa/family-members).
- Human or provider input would be needed to verify agent-network maturity and operational constraints in specific cities and subsectors, because public ENZ documents describe strategy and examples rather than a complete agent-by-market capacity map [ENZ OIA release, 2025](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/2526-041-OIA-Request-Documents-related-to-the-International-Education-Growth-Plan.pdf).

I could not verify source-country tuition/full-fee rates for India and Southeast Asia, exact provider-level spare capacity, a current public NZ country-specific visa risk categorisation beyond approval-rate outcomes, or a complete country-by-country education-agent network map. **The first of these — the Export Education Levy workbook block — was re-tested on 2026-07-08 and is now clearable (see the source note below); it no longer stands as an environment limitation. The core conclusion (alternative source countries could absorb a small China-origin shock over time, but not a fast 50% shock at comparable certainty or revenue quality) does not depend on that workbook: it rests on ENZ enrolment shares, INZ offshore-visa approval rates, and the ENZ agent-channel/growth-plan documents, all fetched directly. Only the Medium-Low revenue-comparability sub-claim was gated on the blocked table, and the overall Medium confidence is left unchanged pending the follow-up extraction rather than raised on this note alone.**
