# What can NZ learn from how Australia, the US and the UK have regulated peptides, and which levers are realistically transplantable?

## Executive answer

- **NZ is not "behind" Australia on BPC-157 specifically — it moved first.** Medsafe's Medicines Classification Committee (MCC) recommended BPC-157 be scheduled as a prescription medicine at its **70th meeting, 25 May 2023** [Medsafe, *Minutes of the 70th meeting of the Medicines Classification Committee*, 25 May 2023](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/mccMin25May2023.htm). The TGA's equivalent final decision — Schedule 4 plus an Appendix D "possession without authority" offence — wasn't published until **22 May 2024**, almost exactly a year later, triggered by 48 Australian Border Force import referrals since 1 July 2022, with the schedule change taking effect **1 June 2024** [TGA, *Notice of final decisions to amend (or not amend) the current Poisons Standard*, 22 May 2024, p.10](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/publication/scheduling-decisions-final/notice-final-decision-amend-or-not-amend-current-poisons-standard-acms-43-accs-37-joint-acms-accs-35). The comparator that actually matters isn't "catch up to Australia on individual substances" — NZ already schedules faster, one substance at a time. It's **finishing the group-classification proposal** Medsafe put to the MCC in June 2025, which has stalled since the 74th meeting deferred it on 23 July 2025 (per the stream's [#712 finding-in-progress](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/pull/722), not re-verified here).
- **The one Australian lever that is genuinely, immediately transplantable without new legislation:** in June 2026 the TGA formally added "unapproved peptide products" to its published compliance priorities — a resourcing/administrative decision, not a scheduling one — backed by a joint TGA–Australian Border Force–Victoria Police operation that seized over AU$2m of peptides, performance/image-enhancing drugs and steroids [TGA, *TGA strengthens compliance focus on unapproved peptide products as part of evolving risk response*, 10 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/tga-strengthens-compliance-focus-unapproved-peptide-products-part-evolving-risk-response). Medsafe and NZ Police/Customs could adopt an equivalent joint compliance-priority declaration today; it doesn't require MCC action.
- **Australia's other lever — banning an entire drug class from compounding by regulation, rather than scheduling each analogue — is structurally interesting but not confirmed as available to NZ.** The TGA closed compounded GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide, dulaglutide) by amending the Therapeutic Goods Regulations 1990 to remove that whole analogue class from the pharmacist extemporaneous-compounding exemption, effective for all compounding **on or after 1 October 2024**, with no dosage-form exception and no grandfathering [TGA, *Update on the glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) pharmacy compounding changes*, 18 June 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/update-glucagon-peptide-1-receptor-agonists-glp-1-ras-pharmacy-compounding-changes). Whether Medsafe/the Ministry of Health has an equivalent regulatory power to strip a compounding/extemporaneous-dispensing exemption for a peptide class under the Medicines Regulations 1984 — as opposed to routing every new substance through full MCC classification — is **not verified here** and is the most concrete follow-up question this finding raises.
- **The US FDA's mechanism targets a different point in the supply chain and is largely not transplantable to NZ's documented problem.** FDA's "Category 2" list governs what *licensed compounding pharmacies* may make from bulk substances under Section 503A/503B — it has nothing to do with personal importation, which is how peptides actually enter NZ [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md, #713]. Its 2026 episode is also a cautionary tale, not a model: BPC-157 and eleven other peptides were pulled from Category 2 in April 2026 **because the original nominators withdrew their submissions**, not because FDA reassessed the safety concerns it had identified — the substances remain in regulatory limbo pending a Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC) meeting on 23–24 July 2026 that will only produce a non-binding recommendation, with formal rulemaking "likely... more than a year" beyond that [FDA, *Category 2 of the Bulk Substances Nominated Under Sections 503A or 503B*, accessed via Wayback 22 June 2026 snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260622152257/https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/certain-bulk-drug-substances-use-compounding-may-present-significant-safety-risks); [Federal Register, *Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee; Notice of Meeting*, Doc. 2026-07361, 16 April 2026](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/16/2026-07361/pharmacy-compounding-advisory-committee-notice-of-meeting-establishment-of-a-public-docket-request); [Orrick, *FDA Announces Removal of 12 Peptides from Category 2*, April 2026](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2026/04/FDA-Announces-Removal-of-12-Peptides-from-Category-2-and-Schedules-PCAC-Meetings). This is a lesson about process fragility (a submission-driven bulk-list can be emptied by withdrawal, not by evidence), not a lever to copy.
- **The UK's problem is a different problem, and its lesson is about enforcement scale, not scheduling law.** MHRA is not fighting unscheduled "research chemical" peptides at the border — GLP-1 medicines are already prescription-only, approved medicines in the UK; the crisis is organised criminal *counterfeiting and diversion* of that approved class. In 2025 the MHRA's Criminal Enforcement Unit seized close to 20 million doses of illegal medicines worth ~£45m, of which roughly 5,680 were GLP-1 pens/units, disrupted over 1,500 websites/social accounts and denied criminals £2.1m in assets under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, and in October 2025 dismantled what it called the largest illicit weight-loss-drug manufacturing operation ever found in the UK [GOV.UK/MHRA, *MHRA seizes illegal medicines worth almost £45m in 2025*, 26 January 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/mhra-seizes-illegal-medicines-worth-almost-45m-in-2025-disrupting-major-criminal-networks); [Pharmaceutical Journal, *Weight-loss drugs among £45m of illegally traded medicines seized in 2025*](https://pharmaceutical-journal.com/article/news/weight-loss-drugs-among-45m-of-illegally-traded-medicines-seized-in-2025). Legally, the UK's operative test — whether a product is marketed or intended for human therapeutic use, regardless of a "research chemical" label — is the same test Medsafe already applies to peptides [#713]; the gap isn't the law, it's that NZ has nothing resembling a dedicated criminal enforcement unit, asset-denial regime, or platform-takedown partnership operating at anything like that scale.
- **None of the three comparators has actually solved NZ's core documented gap:** the inability to seize an unscheduled-but-clearly-human-use peptide at the border before classification (Medsafe currently issues a "high-risk medicine letter" and releases the parcel — [#713](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/pull/719)). Australia solves it only *after* scheduling (via the Appendix D clause 5 possession offence created alongside BPC-157's Schedule 4 entry); the US mechanism doesn't touch imports at all; the UK's approved-medicine framework doesn't apply to a substance NZ hasn't scheduled yet. Closing that specific gap looks like a genuine NZ-specific design problem, not an import.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — every individual regulatory fact below is High confidence, drawn from primary regulator documents (TGA notices/PDFs, FDA.gov, Federal Register, Medsafe MCC minutes, GOV.UK/MHRA releases) that I fetched and read directly. The overall confidence is pulled down to Medium by the *synthesis* — judgements about what is "transplantable" to NZ involve legal questions (e.g., whether Medsafe has an equivalent compounding-exemption power) that I could not verify and are flagged explicitly below.

## Evidence

### Australia (TGA) — individual scheduling is slow but working; enforcement-priority declaration is fast and precedented

**BPC-157 → Schedule 4.** The TGA delegate's proposal was a direct response to 48 referrals for BPC-157 importation received from the Australian Border Force since 1 July 2022, seeking to align BPC-157 with other performance- and image-enhancing substances. An interim decision was published 3 April 2024; the **final decision confirming Schedule 4 (prescription-only) status plus a new Appendix D, clause 5 entry** (making possession without authorisation an offence) was published **22 May 2024**, with an implementation date of **1 June 2024** [TGA, *Notice of final decisions to amend (or not amend) the current Poisons Standard (November 2023)*, 22 May 2024, pp.10, PDF pp.10](https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/publication/scheduling-decisions-final/notice-final-decision-amend-or-not-amend-current-poisons-standard-acms-43-accs-37-joint-acms-accs-35) (fetched via a Wayback snapshot after the live TGA page and PDF both timed out on direct fetch — see fetch notes). 22 public submissions were received in the pre-meeting consultation, 21 in full support. From first referral data (mid-2022) to legal effect (1 June 2024), the process took roughly **two years**.

For comparison, Medsafe's own MCC independently reached the same substantive conclusion — prescription-medicine status for BPC-157 — **almost a year earlier**: the 70th MCC meeting (25 May 2023) recorded that "Medsafe has encountered instances of importation of BPC-157 for personal use, and receives enquiries regarding the legality of importation... The Committee recommended that BPC-157 should be added to the New Zealand schedule as a prescription medicine" [Medsafe, *Minutes of the 70th meeting of the Medicines Classification Committee*, 25 May 2023](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/mccMin25May2023.htm). Medsafe's own later submission independently confirms this took effect and cross-references Australia's status: "BPC-157 was classified at the 70th MCC meeting held on 25 May 2023 as a prescription medicine, following several instances of importation for personal use. It is classified as a prescription medicine in Australia" [Medsafe, *Classification of Unscheduled Peptides*, submission to the 74th MCC, June 2025, p.5](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). **This is the clearest evidence in this research that NZ's individual-substance scheduling process is not the bottleneck** — it moved on this substance faster than Australia's.

**GLP-1 compounding ban — a different, class-wide lever.** Rather than scheduling each GLP-1 analogue individually, the TGA amended the Therapeutic Goods Regulations 1990 (Schedule 5, item 6) to remove **all medicines containing GLP-1 receptor agonist analogues, regardless of dosage form**, from the pharmacist extemporaneous-compounding exemption. The change was published 18 June 2024 and applies to **all compounding on or after 1 October 2024** — meaning compounded semaglutide, tirzepatide and dulaglutide products can no longer be lawfully compounded and supplied by an Australian pharmacist at all, with no shortage exemption or grandfathered stock [TGA, *Update on the glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) pharmacy compounding changes*, 18 June 2024](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/update-glucagon-peptide-1-receptor-agonists-glp-1-ras-pharmacy-compounding-changes). This is a single-sourced claim (the TGA's own media release is the only primary source I located describing the mechanism and date; I could not independently verify the regulation text itself beyond the release's description — flagged in the surprising-claims table below).

**Peptides as a declared compliance priority (June 2026).** Separately from scheduling, the TGA in June 2026 "expanded its priority focus areas to include unapproved peptide products, following increased detection of unlawful importation, supply and advertising." The release names a recent joint TGA–Australian Border Force–Victoria Police operation that seized peptides, performance/image-enhancing drugs and illicit steroids worth "an estimated street value in excess of $2 million," and states the TGA's regulatory response "may include infringement notices, product seizures, import interventions and, where appropriate, civil or criminal penalties" [TGA, *TGA strengthens compliance focus on unapproved peptide products as part of evolving risk response*, 10 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/tga-strengthens-compliance-focus-unapproved-peptide-products-part-evolving-risk-response). This is an administrative prioritisation decision, not new law — the transplantable part is the *decision to prioritise and joint-agency operationally resource it*, not any legal instrument.

### United States (FDA) — a compounding-pharmacy gate, not a border gate

The FDA's relevant machinery sits entirely inside Section 503A/503B of the Federal Food, Drug & Cosmetic Act, which governs what a **licensed pharmacist or outsourcing facility** may compound from a bulk drug substance — not what an individual may import for personal use. Substances nominated for the "503A Bulks List" are sorted into three categories under FDA's interim policy: **Category 1** (may be compounded, no FDA action intended, subject to conditions), **Category 2** (FDA has identified significant safety risks and will not extend the Category 1 policy — effectively "do not compound"), and **Category 3** (insufficient information to evaluate) [FDA, *Bulk Drug Substances Used in Compounding Under Section 503A of the FD&C Act*](https://web.archive.org/web/20260505203745/https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/bulk-drug-substances-used-compounding-under-section-503a-fdc-act) (fetched via Wayback after the live FDA page 403'd a direct fetch; content current as of 26 September 2024 per the page's own footer).

Peptides including BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295 and others were placed in Category 2 in 2023 over concerns about immunogenicity, peptide-related impurities and limited human safety data. In **April 2026**, FDA published an updated Category 2 list moving twelve of these peptides (BPC-157, TB-500, KPV, MOTs-C, Semax, Epitalon, Emideltide/DSIP, Dihexa acetate, injectable GHK-Cu, Melanotan II, Cathelicidin LL-37 and PEG-MGF) into a "nominated but withdrawn" table — confirmed directly on FDA's own page, which still records the original safety-risk rationale for each substance immediately below the "withdrawn" heading [FDA, *Certain Bulk Drug Substances for Use in Compounding That May Present Significant Safety Risks*](https://web.archive.org/web/20260622152257/https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/certain-bulk-drug-substances-use-compounding-may-present-significant-safety-risks) (Wayback snapshot dated 22 June 2026; page content current as of 22 April 2026). Independent legal commentary confirms the mechanism was nomination withdrawal, not a safety re-review: "these substances are being removed from Category 2 because their original nominations were withdrawn by the nominators — not because the FDA has affirmatively found them safe" [Orrick, *FDA Announces Removal of 12 Peptides from Category 2*, April 2026](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2026/04/FDA-Announces-Removal-of-12-Peptides-from-Category-2-and-Schedules-PCAC-Meetings). A Federal Register notice (Docket FDA-2025-N-6895, published 16 April 2026) schedules a Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting for **23–24 July 2026** to consider whether BPC-157, KPV, TB-500 and MOTs-C (23 July) and Emideltide/DSIP, Semax and Epitalon (24 July) should be formally added to the 503A Bulks List — a non-binding recommendation to FDA, not a decision [Federal Register, Doc. 2026-07361, 16 April 2026, fetched as PDF from public-inspection.federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/16/2026-07361/pharmacy-compounding-advisory-committee-notice-of-meeting-establishment-of-a-public-docket-request).

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are handled through an entirely separate track: they are FDA-approved drugs, so compounding them is governed by drug-shortage rules, not the bulk-substance category system. FDA's enforcement-discretion period for compounding shortage versions ended once courts denied preliminary injunctions in *Outsourcing Facilities Association v. FDA* (semaglutide, N.D. Tex. 4:25-cv-00174, decided 24 April 2025; tirzepatide, 4:24-cv-00953, decided 5 March 2025), and both drugs remain off FDA's 503B Bulks List (i.e., no clinical-need finding for outsourcing-facility compounding) [FDA, *FDA clarifies policies for compounders as national GLP-1 supply begins to stabilize*](https://web.archive.org/web/20260702062153/https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-alerts-and-statements/fda-clarifies-policies-compounders-national-glp-1-supply-begins-stabilize) (Wayback snapshot, 2 July 2026).

**None of this reaches personal importation.** NZ's documented peptide problem is overwhelmingly individuals importing parcels for personal use, not NZ-licensed compounding pharmacies sourcing bulk peptide powder [#713]. The FDA framework is relevant to the separate NZ question of wellness-clinic/compounding-pharmacy supply (already the subject of [issue #714](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/714) in this stream) but does not address the border/import gap this stream's framing highlighted.

### United Kingdom (MHRA) — same legal test as NZ, vastly larger enforcement operation, and a different underlying problem

Peptides like BPC-157 and TB-500 sit in the UK's system the same way they do in NZ's: lawful to sell as a labelled "research chemical," unlawful once marketed, supplied or administered for human therapeutic use, because that use triggers the requirement for a marketing authorisation under the Human Medicines Regulations 2012 (SI 2012/1916), regulation 46 [Legislation.gov.uk, *The Human Medicines Regulations 2012*](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2012/1916/contents). Breach is a criminal offence carrying an unlimited fine and up to two years' imprisonment on conviction, though the MHRA more often uses warning letters and formal cautions before prosecuting [Lexology, *Enforcement of pharmaceutical product and medical device regulations in United Kingdom*]. This is structurally the same test Medsafe applies — "for research purposes only" has no legal effect once the buyer's therapeutic intent is clear [#713] — so there is no scheduling-law lesson to transplant here.

What *is* different is the shape of the UK's actual peptide problem and the scale of its response. GLP-1 medicines (Ozempic, Mounjaro, Wegovy) are already licensed, prescription-only medicines in the UK; the crisis MHRA is fighting is organised-crime counterfeiting and diversion of that approved class, not classification of novel unscheduled peptides. In 2025 the MHRA's Criminal Enforcement Unit:

- seized close to **20 million doses** of illegally traded medicines (up from 17 million in 2024) worth an estimated **£45 million** street value, of which **~5,680 units were GLP-1 weight-loss pens/products** [GOV.UK/MHRA, *MHRA seizes illegal medicines worth almost £45m in 2025*, 26 January 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/mhra-seizes-illegal-medicines-worth-almost-45m-in-2025-disrupting-major-criminal-networks); corroborated independently by [Pharmaceutical Journal, *Weight-loss drugs among £45m of illegally traded medicines seized in 2025*](https://pharmaceutical-journal.com/article/news/weight-loss-drugs-among-45m-of-illegally-traded-medicines-seized-in-2025);
- disrupted more than **1,500 websites and social media accounts** and removed over **1,200 social media posts** promoting illegal products [GOV.UK/MHRA, 26 January 2026, as above];
- denied criminals **£2.1 million** in assets using powers under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 [GOV.UK/MHRA, 26 January 2026, as above];
- in October 2025, dismantled what it described as the **largest illicit weight-loss-medicine manufacturing operation ever found in the UK**, in Northampton, seizing more than 2,000 unlicensed tirzepatide/retatrutide pens and raw chemical ingredients (Operation Dunlin), with a follow-up disruption of a second organised-crime group in Lincolnshire in February 2026 [GOV.UK/MHRA news releases on the Northampton and Lincolnshire operations; Lincolnshire Police, *Significant disruption to OCG producing unlicensed weight loss medication*, February 2026](https://www.lincs.police.uk/news/lincolnshire/news/2026/feb/significant-disruption-to-ocg-producing-unlicensed-weight-loss-medication/).

GOV.UK's consumer-facing GLP-1 guidance (updated 5 February 2026) is blunt about the legal position: "Buying from unregulated sellers exposes people wanting to lose weight to serious health risks – it is also against the law" [GOV.UK, *GLP-1 medicines for weight loss and diabetes: what you need to know*, updated 5 February 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/glp-1-medicines-for-weight-loss-and-diabetes-what-you-need-to-know/glp-1-medicines-for-weight-loss-and-diabetes-what-you-need-to-know). The transplantable lesson from the UK is therefore about **enforcement capacity and resourcing** — a dedicated criminal enforcement unit, asset-denial powers, and standing partnerships with ISPs/platforms to take down listings at scale — not a legal mechanism NZ is missing. Medsafe reports having contacted "22 Kiwi websites selling peptides, and all have now been taken down" since BPC-157/SARMs classification took effect [#713, citing Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026] — a real but two-orders-of-magnitude smaller operation than MHRA's 1,500+ website disruptions, and I found no NZ equivalent of a dedicated criminal enforcement unit or an asset-denial figure for peptide enforcement specifically.

### What this means for NZ's schedule-by-group approach

Medsafe's own June 2025 submission to the MCC frames its strategy explicitly as "group entries," citing precedent uses for vaccines, racetams, SARMs, and GLP-1 agonists (the last recommended for group-entry status at the 73rd MCC meeting, 26 February 2025), and proposing new group entries for thymic peptide hormones, BPC-157 analogues, ACTH analogues, pineal gland peptides, antimicrobial peptides, myostatin modulators and mitochondria-derived peptides [Medsafe, *Classification of Unscheduled Peptides*, June 2025, pp.2, 5-10](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). This is, in substance, closer to Australia's GLP-1 compounding-ban approach (regulate a class at once) than to Australia's or the US's substance-by-substance scheduling model — NZ is already attempting the more efficient design; the documented problem (per the sibling finding on classification status, still in review as of this writing) is that the proposal has stalled in committee since July 2025, not that NZ lacks the right kind of legal instrument. None of the three overseas comparators offers a ready-made fix for a *stalled committee process* — that is a resourcing and political-will problem, not a design problem an overseas model can solve by example.

## What would change this conclusion

- **If Medsafe/the Ministry of Health confirms it has no equivalent to the TGA's compounding-exemption-removal power** under the Medicines Regulations 1984, the "class-wide regulatory ban" lever would need to be struck from the transplantable list entirely (it would require new primary or secondary legislation, not a reachable administrative act) — this is the single biggest open legal question this finding raises and needs either an OIA to Medsafe/Ministry of Health or a lawyer's read of the Medicines Act 1981/Medicines Regulations 1984.
- **If the TGA's amending regulation for the GLP-1 compounding ban turns out to have exceptions or a transition period** I didn't find (I only read the TGA's own press description, not the regulation text, after direct/Wayback fetches of the regulation itself were not attempted), the "no grandfathering" framing above would need softening.
- **If a subsequent PCAC vote in July 2026 recommends against adding BPC-157/TB-500/etc. to the 503A Bulks List**, or FDA declines to act on a favourable recommendation, the "regulatory limbo" framing would firm up into "effectively still banned" — worth a fast follow-up check after 24 July 2026.
- **If NZ has its own dedicated peptide/medicines criminal-enforcement unit or asset-denial mechanism I didn't find** (I searched Medsafe's public releases and the sibling border-enforcement finding [#713] but did not contact Medsafe or NZ Police directly), the UK-enforcement-scale gap claimed above would be overstated.
- I could not verify the TGA's Poisons Standard regulation text or the FDA's live 503A compounding page directly — both fetches were blocked (timeout/403) and I relied on Wayback Machine snapshots taken by `archive-cite.mjs`, which I consider reliable for text that FDA/TGA's own pages independently confirm elsewhere, but this is worth noting for anyone re-verifying.

## Open follow-up questions

- Does the Medicines Act 1981 / Medicines Regulations 1984 give the Minister or Director-General of Health a power analogous to the TGA's regulation-amendment route (stripping a compounding/extemporaneous-dispensing exemption for a whole substance class) that could apply to unscheduled peptides *before* full MCC group-entry classification completes? This is the most consequential open question from this finding and would need a legal/policy read, not further web research.
- Is there an OIA-able record of a formal Medsafe/NZ Police/Customs joint compliance-priority declaration for peptides, comparable to the TGA's June 2026 announcement — and if not, would one require new resourcing or just administrative sign-off?
- What did the PCAC recommend at its 23–24 July 2026 meeting on BPC-157, TB-500, KPV and MOTs-C, and did FDA act on it? (This meeting postdates this research and should be checked directly once minutes are published.)
- Has NZ ever quantified how many of Medsafe's 22 "Kiwi website" takedowns [#713] resulted in any criminal or civil penalty, versus MHRA's UK model where takedowns are paired with a dedicated Criminal Enforcement Unit and asset-denial powers?
