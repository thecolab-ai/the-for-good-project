# NZ aged residential care demand is about 34,500 residents now and Health NZ's model projects 48,100-52,200 residents by 2035/36, with pressure concentrated in older age bands, Canterbury/upper South Island, Auckland, and lower recorded use/access signals for Māori, Pacific, and Asian older people

## Executive answer

- Health NZ's 2024 Health and Independence Report says about 32,000 people lived in aged residential care (ARC) facilities in 2022/23, while Health NZ's public ARC projection workbook gives a 2024/25 average-resident baseline of about 34,452 across all care levels, including about 32,795 residents aged 65+ and about 1,658 residents under 65 [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024 online version](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024-online-version); [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- Health NZ's workbook gives two useful 2035/36 national planning numbers: the dashboard's "5y t + Aligned" series reaches about 48,103 average residents, while the "Base pop + Aligned" series reaches about 52,174; because the workbook does not state which scenario is Health NZ's preferred planning case, this finding treats the public model output as a range rather than a point forecast [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- Under the workbook's "Base pop + Aligned" series, the largest absolute 2035/36 demand centres are Canterbury (about 8,708 average residents), Waitematā (5,939), Auckland (5,603), Waikato (4,507), Southern (3,875), and Counties Manukau (3,520); the fastest-growing districts from 2024/25 to 2035/36 are Nelson Marlborough (+62%), Canterbury (+61%), Waitematā (+56%), West Coast (+53%), Capital and Coast (+52%), and Auckland (+51%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- Demand pressure is age-driven: in the workbook's 2024/25 baseline, about 47% of residents are aged 85+ and about 25% are aged 90+; Stats NZ's 2024-base national projections say the 85+ population was about 95,000 in 2024 and has a 90% probability of reaching 275,000-322,000 by 2051 [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Stats NZ, National population projections 2024(base)-2078](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/).
- Public ethnicity evidence points to lower recorded use/access signals, not simple proportional demand: the workbook baseline is 86.1% "Other", 6.8% Māori, 4.5% Asian, and 2.6% Pacific Peoples, while Sapere reports 2022/23 ARC utilisation rates of 23.8 per 1,000 Māori aged 65+, 16.4 for Pacific, 8.7 for Asian, and 37.6 for NZ European/Other [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Sapere, Review of Aged Care Funding and Services Models, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf). HQSC independently corroborates Māori under-representation and access-barrier mechanisms; the Pacific and Asian national utilisation-rate comparisons are Sapere-only in this finding, so they should be treated as signals for follow-up rather than settled explanations [HQSC, Older Māori and aged residential care in Aotearoa, 2021](https://www.hqsc.govt.nz/assets/Our-work/Improved-service-delivery/Aged-residential-care/Publications-resources/Older_Maori_and_ARC_report_Dec2021_final.pdf); [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).
- No public national ARC-by-deprivation cross-tabulation was found in Health NZ's workbook, Sapere's review, Stats NZ catalogue searching, or the project's NZDep/data.govt.nz skill checks; deprivation should therefore be treated as an evidence gap rather than inferred from district or ethnicity alone [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the workbook extraction and headline current-use figures are strong, but the exact 2035/36 ARC projection and regional percentages are single-model Health NZ workbook outputs, not independently reproduced forecasts. Māori under-representation is better corroborated than the Pacific and Asian national utilisation-rate comparisons, which are treated as Sapere-only signals in this finding.

## Evidence

### Current baseline

Health NZ's Health and Independence Report 2024 says that in 2022/23, around 32,000 people lived in ARC facilities and a further 80,000 people received home and community support, with those aged-care services supporting 2.1% of the population and receiving nearly $2 billion in 2022/23 funding [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024 online version](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024-online-version). Sapere's Health NZ-commissioned 2024 review independently reports around 32,000 older people in 676 ARC facilities, mostly in rest-home care (13,500) or hospital-level care (13,200), with dementia and psychogeriatric units accommodating about 5,500 residents [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

The projection workbook's `baseline_refreshed` sheet gives a more recent 2024/25 model baseline of about 34,452 average residents across all ages and service categories [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). In that workbook baseline, about 32,795 residents are aged 65+ and about 1,658 are under 65 [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

By service category, the workbook's all-age 2024/25 average-resident baseline is Continuing Care 13,985, Rest Home 12,677, Dementia 4,870, Aligned 889, Psychogeriatric 868, MPD 860, and Respite 303 [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). Restricting the same workbook baseline to residents aged 65+ gives Continuing Care 13,841, Rest Home 12,516, Dementia 4,745, Psychogeriatric 815, MPD 334, Respite 290, and Aligned 253 [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

### Projection to 2035/36

The workbook dashboard gives several national projection series, not one single labelled forecast [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). In the dashboard, "Base pop + Aligned" rises from 35,632 average residents in 2024/25 to 52,174 in 2035/36 and 61,645 in 2040/41, while "5y t + Aligned" rises from the same 35,632 in 2024/25 to 48,103 in 2035/36 and 56,804 in 2040/41 [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

The model range matters: quoting only 52,174 treats the population-projection-based series as the answer, while quoting only 48,103 treats the five-year-trend series as the answer [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). This finding uses 48,100-52,200 for 2035/36 because the public workbook does not state which dashboard series Health NZ expects decision-makers to use as the preferred planning scenario [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

Stats NZ supports the demographic direction but does not independently reproduce Health NZ's ARC projection: its 2024-base national projections say the 65+ population was 0.87 million in 2024 and has a 90% probability of reaching 1.44-1.55 million by 2051, while the 85+ population was 95,000 in 2024 and has a 90% probability of reaching 275,000-322,000 by 2051 [Stats NZ, National population projections 2024(base)-2078](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/).

### Where pressure concentrates

Under the workbook's `baseline2` "Base pop + Aligned" series, the four Health NZ regions all grow between 2024/25 and 2035/36, but by different amounts: Te Waipounamu grows from 10,292 to 15,771 average residents (+53%), Northern from 11,146 to 16,851 (+51%), Te Manawa Taki from 7,510 to 10,370 (+38%), and Central from 6,684 to 9,182 (+37%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

At district level under the same workbook series, the largest 2035/36 demand centres are Canterbury (8,708), Waitematā (5,939), Auckland (5,603), Waikato (4,507), Southern (3,875), Counties Manukau (3,520), Capital and Coast (2,897), Bay of Plenty (2,744), and Nelson Marlborough (2,211) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). The fastest percentage growth from 2024/25 to 2035/36 is in Nelson Marlborough (+62%), Canterbury (+61%), Waitematā (+56%), West Coast (+53%), Capital and Coast (+52%), Auckland (+51%), Counties Manukau (+46%), Northland (+46%), Wairarapa (+43%), and Waikato (+42%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

The regional demand finding is about modelled resident demand, not bed supply [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). Sapere separately found substantial current access and supply variation: ARC beds per 1,000 population aged 85+ ranged from 149 in Northland to 272 in Canterbury, and high-priority people admitted after a home-care assessment waited from an average 82 days in MidCentral to 219 days on the West Coast [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

### Age and care level

In the workbook's 2024/25 baseline, residents aged 90+ are the largest age band at about 8,667 average residents (25% of the total), followed by ages 85-89 at 7,694 (22%), ages 80-84 at 6,965 (20%), ages 75-79 at 4,961 (14%), ages 70-74 at 2,855 (8%), ages 65-69 at 1,654 (5%), and under 65 at 1,658 (5%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

Under the workbook's "Base pop + Aligned" series, the fastest-growing age band to 2035/36 is 85-89, rising from 7,989 to 14,242 average residents (+78%), followed by 80-84 (+51%), 90+ (+42%), 75-79 (+31%), 70-74 (+31%), 65-69 (+14%), and under 65 (+1%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). This pattern reinforces that ARC capacity pressure is mainly about the shape of the older-age distribution, especially the 85+ population [Stats NZ, National population projections 2024(base)-2078](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/).

By care level under the same workbook series, Continuing Care rises from 13,985 to 20,831 (+49%), Rest Home from 13,857 to 20,549 (+48%), Dementia from 4,870 to 7,203 (+48%), Psychogeriatric from 868 to 1,234 (+42%), while Aligned and MPD grow much more slowly (+10% and +9%) [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

### Ethnicity and equity dimensions

The workbook's 2024/25 baseline records about 29,676 residents as "Other", 2,337 as Māori, 1,535 as Asian, and 904 as Pacific Peoples, equivalent to 86.1%, 6.8%, 4.5%, and 2.6% of the baseline respectively [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx). Those counts are model/input categories, so they should not be read as a full equity diagnosis by themselves [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).

Sapere provides the strongest national ethnicity utilisation signal found here: using 2022/23 data, it reports ARC utilisation rates per 1,000 ethnic population aged 65+ of 23.8 for Māori, 16.4 for Pacific, 8.7 for Asian, and 37.6 for NZ European/Other [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf). The Pacific and Asian national utilisation-rate comparisons are single-sourced to Sapere in this finding; they are useful signals for planning questions, but not independently corroborated here [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

Sapere also reports a narrower high-acuity access signal: among people first scoring 4 or 5 on the MAPLe priority scale in 2021/22, 25.1% of Māori and 22.6% of Pacific people were admitted to ARC within 12 months, compared with 36.6% of Asian people and 49% of NZ European/Other people [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf). That MAPLe comparison supports concern about lower recorded access for Māori and Pacific people after high-priority assessment, but it is also Sapere-only for the exact percentages and should not be read as proving one cause [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

HQSC's 2021 report corroborates Māori under-representation and gives a longer-run Māori-specific projection: it says Māori were 16% of the total population and 7.6% of the 65+ population, but only 2.98% (1,441) of Māori aged 65+ were residing in ARC, and it projected older Māori in ARC increasing four-fold to 4,986 by 2038 if ageing and access trends continued [HQSC, Older Māori and aged residential care in Aotearoa, 2021](https://www.hqsc.govt.nz/assets/Our-work/Improved-service-delivery/Aged-residential-care/Publications-resources/Older_Maori_and_ARC_report_Dec2021_final.pdf). HQSC identifies structural barriers including the need for culturally safe care, co-designed solutions, preferences for staying at home, and means-testing issues that may be more complex for Māori where collective land ownership and multi-generational homes are involved [HQSC, 2021](https://www.hqsc.govt.nz/assets/Our-work/Improved-service-delivery/Aged-residential-care/Publications-resources/Older_Maori_and_ARC_report_Dec2021_final.pdf).

### Deprivation and supply gap

The public sources checked for this finding do not publish a national ARC resident count by NZDep deprivation decile [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx); [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf). District, ethnicity, and rurality can point to equity questions, but they are not substitutes for a deprivation-linked resident or needs-assessment dataset [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

Sapere estimates that, if new ARC beds continue to be built at historic rates, New Zealand may face a shortage of almost 12,000 ARC beds by 2032 [Sapere, 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf). RNZ reported the same finding from the Sapere review, but because RNZ is reporting that same review, it is corroboration that the report was publicly reported, not an independent second model of the 12,000-bed estimate [RNZ, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/514510/danger-nz-will-be-12-000-residential-care-beds-short-by-2032-report-finds).

## What would change this conclusion

- Health NZ guidance identifying one workbook scenario as the official planning scenario would change the headline from a 48,100-52,200 range to a point estimate [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- Updated Health NZ ARC administrative data for 2025/26 or 2026/27 could move the current baseline, especially because the workbook's 2024/25 figures appear as model baseline/actual columns but the workbook itself does not include a plain-language data dictionary [Health NZ, ARC projection workbook](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/health-pros/topics/aged-residential-care/Aged-care-resident-projection-model.xlsx).
- A public national bed-supply dataset by facility, care level, certification status, and region would allow the demand projection to be turned into a district-level shortage map; Sapere gives strong supply-warning evidence but not a current open bed register for this finding to reconcile [Sapere 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).
- A Health NZ or interRAI cross-tabulation of ARC users and assessed high-need non-users by NZDep2023, ethnicity, age, care level, and district would change the deprivation section from "not publicly verified" to a quantified equity baseline.
- Human review by Māori, Pacific, rural, disability, resident, whānau, provider, and care-worker experts is needed before turning the lower recorded use/access data into a policy conclusion, because lower ARC use can reflect access barriers, preferences for ageing at home, whānau care, cultural safety concerns, or combinations of these [HQSC 2021](https://www.hqsc.govt.nz/assets/Our-work/Improved-service-delivery/Aged-residential-care/Publications-resources/Older_Maori_and_ARC_report_Dec2021_final.pdf); [Sapere 2024](https://srgexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/A-review-of-aged-care-funding-and-service-models-2024.pdf).

What I could not verify:

- A public national ARC-by-NZDep deprivation dataset.
- A current official total bed count by facility, district, and care level that can be directly compared with the workbook's projected resident demand.
- Whether Health NZ treats "Base pop + Aligned" or "5y t + Aligned" as its preferred public planning scenario.
- Whether the workbook's 2024/25 baseline was final actual administrative data or a refreshed model baseline; the workbook labels include "2024 actual" and "2024 actuals", but the public file does not include a full data dictionary.

## Open follow-up questions
