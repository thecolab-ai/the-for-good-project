# China-origin students generate materially more tuition revenue per enrolment than Sri Lanka, Nepal or the Philippines, so replacing China's enrolments 1:1 would not replace its fee revenue 1:1

Part of #438. This finding answers the narrow question left open by [research/findings/other/international-student-china-shock-absorption.md](international-student-china-shock-absorption.md) (#563): using the Ministry of Education's official Export Education Levy (EEL) full-year statistics workbook, what does tuition-fee revenue per international student look like by source country (China, India, Nepal, Sri Lanka, the Philippines, Viet Nam), and is a 1:1 enrolment replacement of China-origin students a 1:1 revenue replacement?

## Executive answer

- **The EEL workbook itself does not publish tuition fee revenue broken out by country of citizenship.** It publishes (a) tuition fee income and levy by *education sector/provider type only* (schools, universities, polytechnics, wānanga, government- and non-government-funded private training establishments — no country split), and (b) *student headcounts* by citizenship and by world region — but never fee/revenue crossed with citizenship. This is a structural gap in the official published data, not a fetch-tooling problem, and it is the reason #563 could not answer this question even with full access to the workbook [Ministry of Education, Export Education Levy: Full-Year Statistics 2025 workbook, obtained via Wayback Machine](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx); [same workbook, 2023 edition](https://web.archive.org/web/20250418221314/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-Full-Year-Statistics-2023.xlsx).
- **Combining the workbook's two published tables (country headcounts by sub-sector, and average tuition fee per EFTS by sub-sector) gives a bounded estimate of implied per-student fee revenue by country, because countries differ sharply in which fee-tier sub-sector they concentrate in.** In 2025, China's government-funded tertiary students were 81% university-enrolled (the highest-fee sub-sector, $35,793/EFTS), giving an implied average of about **$33,600 per student**. India's students were split more evenly across universities, polytechnics and PTEs, giving about **$27,800 per student (-17% vs China)**. Sri Lanka, Nepal and the Philippines had little-to-no published university presence and were concentrated in polytechnics and PTEs (~$23,000-23,400/EFTS), giving implied averages of roughly **$23,200-26,300 (Sri Lanka), $23,200-25,100 (Nepal) and $23,300-24,900 (Philippines) — 22-28% below China**. Viet Nam's tiny published cross-tab (only universities are listed) puts it at **$31,800-35,800, close to or slightly above China**, but with the widest uncertainty band of the six countries [Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2025, "Citizenship" and "Average Tuition Fee" sheets](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx).
- **This directly answers the open question from #563: no, a 1:1 enrolment replacement is not a 1:1 revenue replacement for the markets that currently have the most spare capacity.** Sri Lanka, Nepal and the Philippines — the three fastest-growing alternative markets identified in #563 — sit 22-28% below China on this measure, so absorbing a China enrolment shock through those markets specifically would leave New Zealand with materially less tuition revenue than the lost enrolments implied, even before accounting for lower visa-approval conversion rates (already flagged in #563) [international-student-china-shock-absorption.md](international-student-china-shock-absorption.md).
- **India and Viet Nam are the closer per-student revenue substitutes, not the smaller/faster-growing markets.** India is 17% below China's rate and Viet Nam's estimate range spans from slightly below to slightly above it — but #563 already established that India converts offshore visa applications at a much lower rate (59% vs China's 97% in 2025) and Viet Nam's total volume (842 offshore visa applications in 2025) is too small to matter at China-shock scale on its own, so the two countries that look best on a per-student revenue basis are not the two best positioned on volume or visa conversion [international-student-china-shock-absorption.md](international-student-china-shock-absorption.md).
- **The pattern is stable across two independent annual editions of the same official series.** Repeating the same calculation on the 2023 edition of the workbook (2023 data) shows the same ordering — China ~$31,000, India ~$23,400, Philippines ~$20,400 — with India's gap to China narrowing from about -24% in 2023 to -17% in 2025, consistent with India's mix shifting toward higher-fee sub-sectors over that period [Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2023 edition](https://web.archive.org/web/20250418221314/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-Full-Year-Statistics-2023.xlsx).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The underlying headcount-by-sector-by-country and fee-by-sector figures are High confidence — they come directly from the Ministry of Education's own workbook, not a third-party estimate, corroborated across the 2023 and 2025 editions. The per-country *revenue* figures are a constructed estimate (this finding's own calculation, not a number the Ministry publishes), so confidence is Medium-Low on the precise dollar levels and Medium on the *ordering and rough scale* of the gap between China and the smaller markets, because (a) the workbook applies EFTS-based average fees to headcounts, which is a known approximation, and (b) four of the six target countries have an incomplete sub-sector cross-tab, requiring a bounded range rather than a point estimate.

## Evidence

### The workbook was obtained, but confirms the data simply isn't published by country

Direct `curl`, this repo's `node scripts/fetch.mjs` (which chains a browser-shaped plain HTTP request, a real-Chrome `agent-browser` fetch, and a stealth-Chromium `cloak-fetch` fetch), and the built-in WebFetch tool were all tried first, per the AGENTS.md fetch ladder, and all three returned a 403 (`curl`/plain HTTP) or Incapsula/Cloudflare bot-challenge page for both the live workbook URL and the data.govt.nz CKAN API — the same blocker #563 hit [Education Counts, live workbook URL (blocked)](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/export-education-levy-full-year-statistics.xlsx); [data.govt.nz CKAN API (blocked)](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_show?id=international-students-studying-in-new-zealand). This confirms the block is still live/tooling-side, not a dead link, exactly as #563 classified it.

Escalating to the archive tier of the ladder (`node scripts/archive-cite.mjs`, then the Wayback CDX API directly) found that the *un-suffixed* filename #563 tried (`export-education-levy-full-year-statistics.xlsx`, no year) now permanently 410s even inside the Wayback capture from 17 May 2024 — the Ministry appears to have retired that filename in favour of year-suffixed files. Querying the Wayback CDX API for the `educationcounts.govt.nz` domain filtered on "levy" surfaced two live, successfully-captured (`HTTP 200`, correct `application/vnd.openxmlformats-officedocument.spreadsheetml.sheet` MIME type) year-specific workbook snapshots: `Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx` (captured 5 July 2026, two days before this research) and `Export-Education-Levy-Full-Year-Statistics-2023.xlsx` (captured 18 April 2025) [Wayback Machine capture of the 2025 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx); [Wayback Machine capture of the 2023 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20250418221314/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-Full-Year-Statistics-2023.xlsx). Both files were downloaded (`curl`, HTTP 200, 114,377 bytes and 130,723 bytes respectively) and opened successfully with `openpyxl`, confirming valid, complete Excel workbooks rather than truncated or error-page captures.

Both editions contain the same 11-12 sheets: Levy, Tuition Fee, EFTS, Average Tuition Fee, Provider numbers, Students, Average number of students, Region (NZ region, not source country), Citizenship, World Region, and Field of Study. **None of them cross tuition fee, levy, or average-fee data against country of citizenship.** The "Levy", "Tuition Fee" and "Average Tuition Fee" sheets break every dollar figure down only by education sector and provider type (schools primary/secondary, universities, polytechnics, wānanga, government-funded PTEs, non-government-funded PTEs) for 2003-2025, with no country dimension at all. The "Citizenship" and "World Region" sheets break *student headcounts* down by sector and citizenship/region for 2015-2025, with no dollar figures at all. This is a genuine, confirmed gap in what the Ministry publishes — not a fetch-tooling artefact — and it is the reason #563's attempt to verify source-country tuition-fee-per-enrolment comparisons could not succeed even with full access.

### Country-level student counts by sub-sector, 2025 (the only country-specific breakdown the workbook has)

The "Citizenship" sheet lists, for each education sub-sector, the top handful of source countries (selected and ranked by their 2023 student counts, per the workbook's own footnote) plus an "All international students" total. For the six target countries in the 2025 edition:

| Sub-sector (2025, government-funded tertiary) | China | India | Sri Lanka | Nepal | Philippines | Viet Nam |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Universities | 12,555 | 4,210 | not listed | not listed | not listed | 810 |
| Polytechnics | 1,845 | 4,090 | 1,105 | 1,210 | 1,640 | not listed |
| Government-funded PTEs | 1,160 | 3,630 | 1,210 | 850 | 455 | not listed |
| **Total, all government-funded tertiary** | **15,455** | **11,865** | **3,075** | **2,425** | **2,395** | **1,180** |
| Named sub-sectors sum to % of total | 100.7% | 100.5% | 75.3% | 84.9% | 87.5% | 68.6% |

[Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2025, "Citizenship" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx)

Where "named sub-sectors" don't sum to the published total, the residual is a country's presence in a sub-sector too small to make the workbook's cut-off for that year (the sheet lists roughly the top 5-6 countries per sub-sector). Because Sri Lanka, Nepal and the Philippines never appear in the universities row for 2025 despite having a residual of 300-760 students unaccounted for, that residual most plausibly sits in a mix of universities (fewer than the cut-off, but not necessarily zero) and other small sub-sectors like wānanga; Viet Nam's 370-student residual similarly cannot be confirmed. This residual is the main source of the ranges below.

China's schools-sector count (7,495) and non-government-funded PTE/language-school count (2,025) are also published; none of the other five target countries are large enough to appear in those two sub-sector tables for 2025, so no country comparison is possible there.

|---|---|---|

| Polytechnics (renamed "Te Pūkenga" in the 2023 edition, reflecting the 2020-2023 polytechnic-merger structure) | $23,437 | $19,136 |
| Government-funded PTEs | $22,971 | $21,345 |
| Non-government-funded PTEs / language schools | $9,230 | $7,533 |
| Schools (all) | $17,287 | $16,210 |

[Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2025 and 2023 editions, "Average Tuition Fee" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). Universities charge 55-88% more per EFTS than polytechnics or PTEs in both years, and the gap between the government-funded and non-government-funded PTE tiers is even larger (non-government PTEs charge under half the government-funded PTE rate) — so a country's fee revenue depends heavily on which sub-sector its students are concentrated in, independent of headline enrolment numbers.

### Implied revenue per student by country, 2025 (this finding's calculation, not a Ministry-published figure)

Multiplying each country's published sub-sector headcount by that sub-sector's average fee, summing, and dividing by the country's total (with the unexplained residual bounded between the polytechnic/PTE average and the university rate, since those are the only sub-sectors present in the data for every target country):

| Country | 2025 implied avg fee per student | vs China |
|---|---|---|
| China | $33,600 (point estimate; named sub-sectors cover 100.7% of total) | — |
| Viet Nam | $31,800 - $35,800 | -5.5% to +0.0% |
| India | $27,800 (point estimate; 100.5% coverage) | -17.2% |
| Sri Lanka | $23,200 - $26,300 | -30.9% to -21.7% |
| Nepal | $23,200 - $25,100 | -30.9% to -25.3% |
| Philippines | $23,300 - $24,900 | -30.6% to -25.9% |

Estimated total 2025 government-funded-tertiary tuition revenue implied by these rates: China ~$519m, India ~$330m, Sri Lanka ~$71-81m, Nepal ~$56-61m, Philippines ~$56-60m, Viet Nam ~$38-42m. These are derived figures, not Ministry-published totals, and should be read as "what the published headcount and fee-rate tables imply," not as audited per-country revenue.

Repeating the calculation on the 2023 edition, for the three countries with a near-complete sub-sector cross-tab that year (China, India, Philippines), shows the same ordering: China ~$31,000, India ~$23,400 (-24.3% vs China), Philippines ~$20,400 (from an 86.9%-complete cross-tab). The gap between China and India narrowed from about -24% (2023) to -17% (2025), consistent with #563's finding that India has been a fast-growing "grow" market — the 2025 EEL data suggests that growth has skewed toward higher-fee sub-sectors (India's university headcount grew from 1,870 in 2023 to 4,210 in 2025, more than doubling, while its polytechnic and PTE headcounts grew more slowly) [Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2023 edition, "Citizenship" and "Average Tuition Fee" sheets](https://web.archive.org/web/20250418221314/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-Full-Year-Statistics-2023.xlsx).

### Two important caveats on precision

**Headcount is not the same unit as EFTS.** The average-fee figures above are fee-per-equivalent-full-time-student (EFTS), but this finding applies them to student *headcounts* by country, because the workbook does not publish EFTS by country — only headcounts. Nationally in 2025, EFTS ran well below headcount within every sub-sector (universities: 20,600 EFTS vs 29,295 students, a 0.70 ratio; polytechnics: 6,535 vs 9,570, 0.68; government PTEs: 8,345 vs 12,445, 0.67; non-government PTEs: 5,865 vs 11,995, only 0.49 — reflecting more part-year, part-time or short-course enrolments in that tier) [Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2025, "EFTS" and "Students" sheets](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). If any of the six target countries has a materially different EFTS-to-headcount ratio than its sub-sector's national average — for instance, if Chinese students skew toward longer/full-year university programmes while another country's cohort in the same nominal sub-sector skews toward shorter courses — the true per-student revenue gap could be larger or smaller than estimated here. The workbook provides no way to check this by country.

**The EEL levy-collection population is not identical to ENZ's headline enrolment count cited in #563.** Summing China's three published EEL sub-totals (schools 7,495 + government-funded tertiary 15,455 + non-government-funded PTEs 2,025) gives 24,975 China-citizenship students in the 2025 EEL data, a 30.1% share of the EEL grand total (82,910) — compared with the 34% / ~31,545 figure from Education New Zealand's headline enrolment release that #563 used [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [New Zealand China Council](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/). The EEL workbook's own grand total (82,910 students) is about 10.5% below ENZ's headline total (92,580), consistent across editions — the levy-collection dataset appears to track a slightly narrower or differently-defined population (e.g. it may exclude some exchange/study-abroad or non-levy-liable categories) than ENZ's headline enrolment statistics. This finding's per-country figures should be read as describing the EEL levy-liable population specifically, not as identical in scope to the ENZ enrolment figures used elsewhere in this stream.

Separately, ENZ's Annual Report figure cited in #563 — $1.43b of "education-related travel exports" to China in the year to June 2025 — is a Balance of Payments travel-export concept that includes living costs and other spending by students and their visitors, not just tuition fees, and covers a different population and time period than the EEL levy-year data used here (this workbook's *national* total tuition fee income across all countries in 2025 was $1.32b) [ENZ Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ-Annual-Report-2024-2025.pdf); [Ministry of Education, EEL workbook 2025, "Tuition Fee" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). The two figures are not directly comparable and this finding does not attempt to reconcile them further; readers using #563's $1.43b figure alongside this finding's tuition-only figures should keep that scope difference in mind.

## What would change this conclusion

- A future EEL workbook edition that adds a country × fee cross-tab (unlikely given the Ministry has never published one across the 2013-2025 span checked here) would let this move from an estimate to a directly-published figure.
- An Official Information Act request to the Ministry of Education for country-level tuition fee or EFTS data (the underlying administrative data almost certainly exists at provider level, since GST-excl levy and fee income is already reported by provider) would resolve the residual/coverage gaps for Sri Lanka, Nepal, the Philippines and Viet Nam, and would settle whether headcount and EFTS diverge differently by country.
- Provider-level or subject/programme-level data (not published here) could show that within a sub-sector like "universities," China and India students cluster in different-cost degree programmes (e.g. postgraduate vs undergraduate, STEM vs humanities), which would change the estimate independent of the sub-sector-mix effect measured here.
- If a future edition's country listing includes Sri Lanka, Nepal, the Philippines or Viet Nam in the universities row (they are excluded in 2025 only because they fall outside the workbook's top-N cut-off, not because they are necessarily zero), that would let their residual be resolved into an exact figure rather than a range.

I could not verify: exact per-country EFTS (only headcounts are published by citizenship); the sub-sector location of the 12-31% enrolment residual for Sri Lanka, Nepal, the Philippines and Viet Nam; any programme- or qualification-level fee variation within a sub-sector by country; or whether the EEL levy-liable population's ~10% gap versus ENZ's headline enrolment count is concentrated in particular source countries (which could bias the per-country shares in either direction).

## Open follow-up questions

- Would an OIA request to the Ministry of Education for provider-level, country-disaggregated EFTS and tuition fee data resolve the residual/coverage gaps identified here for Sri Lanka, Nepal, the Philippines and Viet Nam?
- Does the ~10% gap between the EEL levy-collection population and ENZ's headline enrolment total skew toward or away from China specifically, and would that change the 30.1% vs 34% share comparison noted above?
