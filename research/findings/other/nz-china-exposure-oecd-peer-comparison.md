# NZ's China export exposure is high relative to most OECD peers but below Australia and Chile on all standard metrics

## Executive answer

- By **goods-export share**, NZ sent approximately **25% of goods exports** to China in 2024, ranking it well above most OECD economies (Canada ~4%, Netherlands ~3%, Japan ~18%, South Korea ~19%) but below Australia (~33%) and Chile (~36%) — the root claim that NZ is "one of the most China-dependent OECD economies" is partially supported but overstated: NZ is in the upper tier, not at the top. [China Embassy NZ, 2025](https://nz.china-embassy.gov.cn/eng/zxgxs/202506/t20250605_11641456.htm); [World's Top Exports, 2024](https://www.worldstopexports.com/chiles-top-import-partners/); [PIIE, 2024](https://www.piie.com/research/piie-charts/2024/south-koreas-exports-united-states-and-japan-overtook-exports-china-2023); [ABS, 2024-25](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/economy/international-trade/international-trade-supplementary-information-financial-year/latest-release)
- By **exports-to-GDP ratio**, NZ's China-bound goods exports were approximately **4.2% of GDP** in 2024 (goods only) or **~4.9% including services** — below Chile (~11%), South Korea (~7.7%), and Australia (~7%), and broadly comparable to Japan (~3%), placing NZ in a mid-upper tier rather than at the extreme. [NZ China Council, 2025](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/); [World Bank GDP, 2024](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD?locations=NZ)
- NZ's **sector concentration** is distinctively spread across three primary categories all heavily China-bound: logs/wood ~61%, dairy ~31–36%, and meat ~24% — unlike Australia (iron ore >55% of its China exports) or Chile (copper-dominated). This sector diversification does not reduce vulnerability; each product line faces its own Chinese import substitution risk. [China Embassy NZ, 2025](https://nz.china-embassy.gov.cn/eng/zxgxs/202506/t20250605_11641456.htm); [Stats NZ International Trade March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-trade-march-2026-quarter/)
- NZ's **services exposure** (education at 33.5% of international students from China, Chinese tourist spend of NZD 1.4b/yr) adds a dimension largely absent from goods-only comparisons and has no direct peer match in the OECD economies examined here. [NZ China Council, 2025](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/)
- **Historical trend**: NZ's goods-to-China share peaked at approximately **31.6% in 2021**, then fell to ~25% in 2024 — a post-COVID normalisation pattern shared with Australia (peaked 42%, now ~29–33%) and South Korea (peaked ~26%, now ~19%), suggesting NZ's trajectory is not unusual among commodity-exporting peers. [VUW News, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics); [AMP, 2024](https://www.amp.com.au/resources/insights-hub/econosights-australia-and-the-chinese-economy)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — goods-export-share comparisons are High confidence (drawn from official national statistics), but GDP-denominator calculations are derived (not taken from a single unified OECD database using a consistent methodology), and the services comparison is qualitative rather than fully quantified across all peer countries. No single OECD dataset was available that places all countries on identical goods-plus-services, exports-to-GDP measures in the same year.

## Evidence

### 1. The comparative claim: is NZ "one of the most China-dependent OECD economies"?

The framing for stream #438 asserts NZ is "one of the most China-dependent OECD economies." This finding tests that claim using three consistent measures: (a) China's share of total goods exports, (b) China's share of total goods-plus-services exports, and (c) exports to China as a percentage of GDP. A fourth dimension — sector concentration — is examined qualitatively.

**Caveat on Table 3**: these GDP-share figures are derived by combining data from national statistical offices (for exports) with World Bank GDP series (for denominators). They use 2024 data where available but reference periods differ by country. They should be treated as order-of-magnitude comparisons, not precise OECD-comparable measurements. A unified methodology (e.g., OECD Balanced Trade Statistics or IMF DOTS) applied to all countries in a single reference year would give more comparable results; that dataset was not accessible for this finding.

On a GDP-denominator basis, NZ sits fourth in this set — well below Chile, South Korea, and Australia, but above Japan, Netherlands, and Canada.

### 2. What makes NZ's exposure distinctive compared with peers at similar or higher levels

**Sector concentration and substitutability**: Australia's China exposure is dominated by iron ore (more than 55% of its China exports by value, and iron ore "peaked at 10% of GDP in 2020, declining to 8.5% of GDP" [AMP Econosights, 2024](https://www.amp.com.au/resources/insights-hub/econosights-australia-and-the-chinese-economy)). Chile's is dominated by copper. Both are globally traded bulk commodities with established non-China buyer bases. NZ's top China-facing export categories are logs/wood (~61% China-bound), dairy (~31–36%), and meat (~24%). These are processed or agricultural products where China has been actively developing domestic production capacity and sourcing alternatives (New Zealand forestry competes with Scandinavia, Russia, and emerging Southeast Asian suppliers; dairy competes with EU, US, and expanding domestic Chinese dairy) [VUW News, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics).

**Scale of the next-best alternative**: China's food and fibre imports from NZ (NZD 16.8b in year to March 2024) "matched the combined total from New Zealand's next four largest markets — the United States, Australia, the European Union, and Japan" [China Embassy NZ, 2025](https://nz.china-embassy.gov.cn/eng/zxgxs/202506/t20250605_11641456.htm). This concentration in a single buyer is not directly replicated in Australia's goods exports (where Japan, South Korea, India, and the EU together take a large share) or Canada's (where the US dominates overwhelmingly and China is secondary).

**Services exposure**: NZ's education-sector dependency on Chinese enrolments (33.5% of all international students from China in 2024, or approximately 27,980 students [NZ China Council, 2025](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/)) is qualitatively similar to Australia's (Australian education-related travel to China was AU$12.7b in 2024, reflecting a parallel student and tourism dependency [DFAT, 2025](https://www.dfat.gov.au/sites/default/files/chin-cef.pdf)). The NZ China Council's 2025 services report noted NZ services exports to China peaked at NZD 3.37b (YE June 2019) then contracted 68% to NZD 1.1b in YE June 2022 with COVID, and have been recovering. Chinese tourist spend was NZD 1.414b in 2024 at NZD 6,450 per trip, above the all-market average [NZ China Council, 2025](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/).

**Economy size buffer**: Japan and South Korea are both more China-exposed by goods-export share than NZ in absolute dollar terms, but their large domestic economies (GDP of US$4.2 trillion and US$1.7 trillion respectively) provide a significant buffer that NZ's US$260bn GDP does not. A 25% drop in Japan's China exports (~US$31b) represents ~0.7% of Japan's GDP; the same proportional shock on NZ's China exports (~US$2.7b) represents ~1% of NZ's GDP — both small, but NZ's export sector has fewer domestic substitution opportunities.

### 3. Historical trend: has NZ's China exposure grown faster than peers?

NZ merchandise exports to China grew from a negligible share (around NZD 2.5b) in the early 2000s to NZD 20.85b by 2024 — an average annual growth rate of 14.1% over two decades [China Embassy NZ, 2025](https://nz.china-embassy.gov.cn/eng/zxgxs/202506/t20250605_11641456.htm). NZ's China goods-export share peaked at approximately 31.6% in 2021 and has since fallen to ~25% in 2024 [VUW News, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics).

This post-2021 normalisation parallels peer trends: Australia's China goods-export share peaked at 42% in 2021 and is now ~33% [AMP Econosights, 2024](https://www.amp.com.au/resources/insights-hub/econosights-australia-and-the-chinese-economy); South Korea's China total-export share peaked at ~26% and fell to ~19.7% in 2023 — below 20% for the first time in 20 years [PIIE, 2024](https://www.piie.com/research/piie-charts/2024/south-koreas-exports-united-states-and-japan-overtook-exports-china-2023). The shared pattern suggests the peak was driven by the global commodity price boom of 2020–21 and COVID-era demand shifts, not a distinctive NZ-specific acceleration.

The US$11.3b goods exports to China in 2023 (26.85% share, World Bank WITS) compared with a 2022 peak of US$20.1b / NZD equivalent, suggesting a sharper fall in the NZ-China goods relationship than in Australia (where iron ore prices remained robust) [WITS NZ Trade Summary 2023](https://wits.worldbank.org/CountryProfile/en/Country/NZL/Year/LTST/Summarytext).

A Treasury working paper from 2013 (WP 13/15) noted that NZ merchandise exports to China were "3.3% of NZ's nominal GDP in 2012, up from 0.8% in 2000," confirming that the structural shift occurred primarily in the 2000s–2010s and was a China FTA-era phenomenon common to multiple Southern Hemisphere commodity exporters [NZ Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15).

### 4. Peer countries not covered by this finding

Due to limited data availability within scope, this finding does not include full comparable data for Singapore (where China is a major export destination but the trade-hub structure inflates headline figures), Ireland (where Irish exports to China appear modest against a large but pharma-distorted GDP), or other small OECD exporters such as Colombia, Costa Rica, or the Baltic states. A more complete cross-OECD comparison would require access to a unified bilateral trade dataset (OECD Balanced Trade Statistics or IMF DOTS) applied consistently across all members.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A unified OECD or WTO dataset** covering goods-plus-services bilateral trade for all OECD members in a single consistent year (e.g., OECD Balanced Trade Statistics or IMF DOTS) would remove the need to derive and compare figures from national statistical offices with differing methodologies and reference periods — and could raise or lower NZ's relative position.
- **Chile's exact G+S exports to China** (not just goods) could change the GDP-ratio comparison: if Chile's services to China are proportionally smaller than NZ's, the gap narrows; if larger, it widens.
- **Singapore, Ireland, or other small open OECD economies** with high China exposure could displace NZ's position in the ranking if included.
- **Services trade at consistent PPP or current-USD denominators** for education and tourism across all peers would enable a proper total-exposure comparison; no such comparative dataset was found in this search.
- **China's active import-substitution in dairy and forestry** could accelerate the decline in NZ's China share (which has already fallen from 31.6% to 25% since 2021); evidence of that acceleration beyond what is already visible in the 2022–2024 data would change the trajectory analysis.
- **Australia's iron ore price** is the primary driver of Australia's high GDP-ratio score; a sustained iron ore price collapse would reduce Australia's share sharply and narrow the gap with NZ, making NZ look relatively more exposed.
