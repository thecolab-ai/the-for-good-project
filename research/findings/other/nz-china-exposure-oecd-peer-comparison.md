# NZ's China export exposure is high within a selected OECD peer set, but the exact OECD-wide rank is unverified

## Executive answer

- By **goods-export share**, NZ is highly exposed to China within the selected comparator set used here: NZ sent about **25% of goods exports** to China in 2024, above South Korea (~19%), Japan (~18%), Canada (~4%), and the Netherlands (~3%), but below Australia (~33%) and Chile (~36%). This supports a narrower claim that NZ is in the upper tier of the selected peer set; it does **not** prove NZ's rank across all OECD members. [China Embassy NZ, 2025](https://nz.china-embassy.gov.cn/eng/zxgxs/202506/t20250605_11641456.htm); [ABS, 2024-25](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/economy/international-trade/international-trade-supplementary-information-financial-year/latest-release); [World's Top Exports, 2024](https://www.worldstopexports.com/chiles-top-import-partners/); [PIIE, 2024](https://www.piie.com/research/piie-charts/2024/south-koreas-exports-united-states-and-japan-overtook-exports-china-2023); [CBS Netherlands, 2025](https://www.cbs.nl/en-gb/news/2025/08/dutch-exports-to-china-up-again-while-germany-s-have-fallen); [Global Affairs Canada, 2024](https://international.canada.ca/en/global-affairs/corporate/reports/chief-economist/annual/2024)
- A **goods-plus-services ranking** could only be directly supported for NZ and Australia from official current sources: MFAT reports NZ exports to China of **NZ$22.82b**, including NZ$19.77b in goods and NZ$3.05b in services for the year ended September 2024; DFAT reports Australia's goods-and-services exports to China of **A$196b**, or **29%** of Australia's global goods-and-services exports in 2025. I did not obtain a comparable Chile goods-plus-services share, so this finding should not claim NZ is below Chile on that metric. [MFAT, accessed 2026-07-07](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade); [DFAT China country brief, accessed 2026-07-07](https://www.dfat.gov.au/geo/china/china-country-brief)
- By **rough exports-to-GDP estimates**, NZ's China-bound exports are below Chile, South Korea, and Australia within this selected set, and above Japan, Canada, and the Netherlands. These estimates combine bilateral export figures with World Bank current-US-dollar GDP denominators, so they are order-of-magnitude comparisons rather than a unified OECD measurement. [World Bank GDP, 2024](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD?locations=NZ); [World's Top Exports, 2024](https://www.worldstopexports.com/chiles-top-import-partners/); [Trading Economics / UN Comtrade, South Korea exports to China](https://tradingeconomics.com/south-korea/exports/china); [DFAT, 2025](https://www.dfat.gov.au/geo/china/china-country-brief); [CEIC, Japan exports to China](https://www.ceicdata.com/en/indicator/japan/total-exports-to-china); [Global Affairs Canada, 2024](https://international.canada.ca/en/global-affairs/corporate/reports/chief-economist/annual/2024)
- NZ's exposure is distinctive because it combines goods and services dependence. China's share of NZ goods exports is high, China is NZ's largest international-student source economy, and Chinese visitors remain a high-spend tourism market; Australia's education and tourism exposure to China is also substantial, so the services point is **not unique to NZ**, but it means goods-only comparisons understate NZ's relationship with China. [NZ China Council Statistics, 2026](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/); [MFAT, accessed 2026-07-07](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade); [DFAT China factsheet, 2025](https://www.dfat.gov.au/sites/default/files/chin-cef.pdf)
- NZ's goods exports to China peaked at about **NZ$20.1b in 2022**, then fell to NZ$18.4b in 2023 and declined again in 2024; World Bank WITS separately reports 2023 NZ goods exports to China of **US$11.3b**, or **26.85%** of NZ goods exports. The difference is a currency and source-basis difference, not a US$20.1b peak. [VUW News, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics); [WITS NZ Trade Summary 2023](https://wits.worldbank.org/CountryProfile/en/Country/NZL/Year/LTST/Summarytext)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the goods-export-share comparison is relatively well supported for the selected countries, but the GDP-share table is derived from mixed national and international sources, goods-plus-services comparability is limited to NZ and Australia, and this is not a full OECD-wide ranking.

## Evidence

### 1. Scope and comparison method

This finding tests whether NZ is unusually exposed to China among OECD-like peers by comparing NZ with Australia, Chile, South Korea, Japan, Canada, and the Netherlands. Those countries were selected because they include nearby commodity exporters, large Asian exporters, and lower-exposure OECD comparators; the selection is not a complete OECD dataset. A full OECD ranking would require a single bilateral trade dataset applied consistently across all OECD members.

The most defensible metric available within scope is China's share of each country's goods exports. Goods-plus-services exposure is discussed separately because comparable bilateral services data was not obtained for every peer.

### 5. Sector structure and services exposure

NZ's China-facing exposure is concentrated in primary goods and people-movement services. MFAT lists NZ's major goods exports to China as dairy, meat, forestry and wood products, and fruit, while its major services exports to China are tourism, education, and transportation services. [MFAT, accessed 2026-07-07](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade)

China is a particularly important market for some NZ primary sectors: the NZ China Council states that NZ exported almost NZ$20b of goods to China in the year to December 2025, close to double the value sent to either the United States or Australia, and that NZ supplies more than half of the dairy products China imports from the world. [NZ China Council Statistics, 2026](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/) The same source reports that China was NZ's largest international-student source economy in 2025, with 31,545 students, or 34% of total international enrolments, and that China ranked third for international tourists visiting NZ in March 2026 while Chinese visitors had the highest per-day spend among the listed source markets. [NZ China Council Statistics, 2026](https://nzchinacouncil.org.nz/statistics/)

Australia is the closest services-exposure peer in this finding. DFAT reports Australia's 2025 goods-and-services exports to China at A$196b, with education-related travel of A$14.5b listed among the top exports to China, and states that China remains Australia's largest source of overseas students. [DFAT China country brief, 2026](https://www.dfat.gov.au/geo/china/china-country-brief); [DFAT China factsheet, 2025](https://www.dfat.gov.au/sites/default/files/chin-cef.pdf)

The sector-risk conclusion should be kept narrow. For dairy, OECD-FAO projects that expected growth in China's domestic milk production will limit growth in whole milk powder imports, and USDA's 2025 China dairy report says fluid milk imports have fallen as the market adjusts to higher domestic substitution. [OECD-FAO Agricultural Outlook 2025-2034](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/07/oecd-fao-agricultural-outlook-2025-2034_3eb15914/full-report/dairy-and-dairy-products_1dd2e5a6.html); [USDA FAS China Dairy Annual, 2025](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Dairy+and+Products+Annual_Beijing_China+-+People%27s+Republic+of_CH2025-0218) For forestry, USDA's 2025 China softwood market update says China is the world's largest importer, processor, and consumer of wood products, but imported softwood demand has slowed because of weaker real-estate demand and increased domestic timber production, with New Zealand and Russia identified as top suppliers. [USDA FAS China Softwood Market Update, 2025](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Softwood+Market+Update+2025_Shanghai+ATO_China+-+People%27s+Republic+of_CH2025-0227.pdf)

### 6. Historical trend

NZ merchandise exports to China rose sharply over the 2000s and 2010s: Treasury reported that NZ merchandise exports to China were 3.3% of NZ nominal GDP in 2012, up from 0.8% in 2000. [NZ Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15) VUW's Jason Young reported that NZ exports to China peaked at NZ$20.1b in 2022, fell to NZ$18.4b in 2023, and decreased again in 2024; WITS reports NZ goods exports to China of US$11.3b in 2023, or 26.85% of NZ's total goods exports. [VUW News, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics); [WITS NZ Trade Summary 2023](https://wits.worldbank.org/CountryProfile/en/Country/NZL/Year/LTST/Summarytext)

Peer trends show that NZ's recent decline from the China-share peak is not unique. AMP reports that Australia's goods-export share to China peaked at 42% in 2021 and later eased to roughly one-third; PIIE reports that South Korea's China share fell below 20% in 2023 for the first time in 20 years. [AMP Econosights, 2024](https://www.amp.com.au/resources/insights-hub/econosights-australia-and-the-chinese-economy); [PIIE, 2024](https://www.piie.com/research/piie-charts/2024/south-koreas-exports-united-states-and-japan-overtook-exports-china-2023)

The likely explanation is partly global rather than NZ-specific, but this is an inference from broader commodity-market evidence rather than a country-by-country decomposition. World Bank reported that commodity prices recovered in early 2021, lifted by the global economic rebound, and UNCTAD reported a 43% increase in its free-market commodity price index during January-December 2021, attributed to recovery after the pandemic's initial waves. [World Bank Commodity Markets Outlook, April 2021](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2021/04/20/commodity-prices-to-stabilize-after-early-2021); [UNCTAD, 2022](https://indico.un.org/event/1000802/attachments/2184/7232/2211377E.pdf)

### 7. Countries not covered by this finding

This finding does not include full comparable data for Singapore, Ireland, Colombia, Costa Rica, the Baltic states, or all other OECD members. Singapore is especially difficult because re-exports and hub trade can inflate headline bilateral export figures. A complete answer would need a consistent OECD, WTO, IMF DOTS, or UN Comtrade extraction across all OECD members for the same year, preferably separating domestic exports, re-exports, services exports, and value-added exposure.

## What would change this conclusion

- A unified OECD, WTO, IMF DOTS, or UN Comtrade extract covering bilateral goods and services exports to China for all OECD members in the same reference year could materially change NZ's relative rank.
- A verified Chile national-statistics source for bilateral goods-plus-services exports to China could show whether NZ is below Chile on total-export exposure as well as goods-only and rough GDP-share metrics.
- A value-added trade analysis could change the apparent exposure of re-export hubs and manufacturing economies, especially Singapore, the Netherlands, Ireland, South Korea, and Japan.
- Sector-level NZ export data over a full year, rather than selective or quarterly snapshots, would improve confidence about the relative importance of dairy, meat, forestry, and fruit within the China relationship.
- A formal econometric decomposition of the post-2021 decline by price, volume, exchange rate, and destination switching would test the commodity-cycle inference.
