# NZ's 2026 school assessment and reporting system requires the same twice-yearly parent report as before, but replaces National Standards' mandatory named-benchmark and compulsory annual Ministry aggregation with Ministry-encouraged (not regulated) tools, descriptors, and reporting components

**Scope note:** this finding answers the depth-1 question exactly as framed in [issue #639](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/639): what the 2026 system requires (legally and administratively), and how that compares mechanically to National Standards (2010–2017). It does not re-litigate whether either system is evidence-based or whether it risks a ranking/labelling trap — those are separate child questions (#2–#6) already scoped in the stream's [framing analysis](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/pull/638) for #636.

## Executive answer

- **The core parent-reporting *law* has not changed at all, and was not created in 2010 or removed in 2017 either.** Schools must give parents a written, plain-language report at least twice a year on progress and achievement in literacy/maths and across the curriculum. Today that duty sits in **regulation 21 of the Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020**, in force since 1 January 2023 [Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020, reg 21](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2020/193/en/2024-10-01.pdf). What's new for 2026 is *what curriculum content* that report must cover: the revised Years 0–10 English and Mathematics & Statistics learning areas, replacing the 2007 curriculum content for those subjects [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p).
- **Almost everything people associate with "the 2026 assessment reset" — SMART, the five progress descriptors, the progress-over-time graphic, the assessment-results line, the attendance line — is Ministry-***encouraged* **practice, not a regulated requirement.** The Ministry's own guidance is explicit that this is a deliberate design choice: it publishes a "required (i.e. regulated) vs encouraged" table and says schools "are encouraged" to use these "Common Components," while existing report templates can continue if they cover the same ground [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p); [Tāhūrangi requirements page, 2026](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-assessment-requirements-expectations-for-2026).
- **This is the opposite legal shape from National Standards.** National Standards were a *named, regulated benchmark* — National Administration Guideline 2A required schools to report against National Standards specifically and to submit annual whole-cohort data (by level, Māori, Pasifika, gender, year level) to the Secretary for Education [TKI NAGs fact sheet, 2009/2010, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet). The 2026 system has no equivalent named standard and no equivalent compulsory school-by-school annual submission — the national picture instead comes from a sample-based study (Curriculum Insights and Progress Study, Year 8 only) feeding Government Target 7 [Ministry of Education, Improving achievement, 2025](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/improving-achievement); [Beehive, National Standards ended, 2017](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/national-standards-ended).
- **Both systems left the assessment tool itself unmandated**, and both used teacher-judgement-based descriptors rather than a single test score: National Standards used a 4-level Overall Teacher Judgement (Well Below/Below/At/Above) informed by whatever tools a school chose; 2026 uses a 5-level descriptor set (Emerging/Developing/Consolidating/Proficient/Exceeding) informed by SMART, PATs, or e-asTTle plus teacher evidence [TKI NAGs fact sheet, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet); [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p).
- **Māori-medium implementation lags mainstream by about a year in both eras.** "Specified kura board" schools (kura kaupapa Māori, hapū/iwi-based designated-character schools, Ngā Kura ā-Iwi-affiliated schools, hapū/iwi-based state-integrated schools) don't have to move to the new curriculum, SMART, or reporting approach until 1 January 2027 [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p); Māori-medium National Standards (Ngā Whanaketanga Rumaki Māori) similarly rolled out from 2011, a year after the 2010 mainstream start [Education Counts, Ngā Whanaketanga Rumaki Māori readiness workshops report, Aug 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/all-schooling/nga-whanaketanga-rumaki-maori-analysis-of-feedback-from-readiness-workshops).

**Overall confidence:** High on the legal/regulatory mapping and the required-vs-encouraged distinction (multiple current primary sources, including the regulation text itself and two independent Ministry pages that state the same required/encouraged framing). Medium on some second-order details flagged in "What would change this conclusion" below (the literal contents of the Ministry's downloadable required/encouraged PDF table, and the Years 9–10-specific changes), which I could not independently retrieve.

## Evidence

### 1. The regulated core: what schools are legally required to do (both eras)

The only currently-regulated reporting duty is **regulation 21 of the Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020** (SL 2020/193), inserted on 1 January 2023 by the Education (School Boards) Amendment Regulations 2022 (SL 2022/290), made under the Education and Training Act 2020. Verbatim:

> "(1) The board of a school, together with the school's principal and teaching staff, must use good quality assessment or aromatawai information to report to each student at the school and their parents on the student's progress and achievement.
> (2) The reports must — (a) be written in plain language; and (b) include the student's progress and achievement — (i) across any relevant foundation curriculum policy statements and national curriculum statements made under section 90 of the Act; and (ii) in literacy and mathematics or te reo matatini and pāngarau; and (c) be provided at least twice per year.
> (3) In this regulation, good quality... means that the information draws on a range of evidence..."
[Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020, reg 21, reprint as at 1 October 2024](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2020/193/en/2024-10-01.pdf) (also indexed at [legislation.govt.nz](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0193/latest/whole.html)).

This regulation does **not name SMART, PATs, e-asTTle, National Standards, or any progress-descriptor scale** — it only requires *some* good-quality, evidence-based, twice-yearly, plain-language report against the current curriculum statements. It is the direct successor to National Administration Guidelines 1 and 2, which required the same twice-yearly plain-language reporting duty before the National Education Goals/NAGs framework ceased to have effect on 1 January 2023 [see NZCER/Educational Leaders summary confirming the NEG/NAG cessation date, cross-checked against the regulation's own "inserted on 1 January 2023" note](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2020/193/en/2024-10-01.pdf).

What changes for 2026 is not this regulation but *curriculum content*: from 2026, the "national curriculum statements" that regulation 21(2)(b)(i) points to are the **revised Years 0–10 English and Mathematics & Statistics learning areas**, so schools must now report progress against that revised content specifically [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p): "From 2026 all state and state-integrated schools will be reporting against the revised Years 0-10 English, Mathematics & Statistics learning areas. There are new Progress Markers... and new Progress Descriptors..."

### 2. What's Ministry-encouraged, not regulated: descriptors, components, tools

The Ministry's own framing draws this required/encouraged line explicitly. Its 2 February 2026 guidance states: "the Ministry has developed a table showing what is required (i.e. regulated) for Years 0-10 for 2026, and what is encouraged practice" [Tāhūrangi requirements page, 2026](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-assessment-requirements-expectations-for-2026). The parallel reporting-guidance page repeats this: "There are also Common Components that schools are **encouraged** to use... schools are encouraged to report... using these five common components" [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p) (emphasis in source wording, not added).

The five encouraged **Common Components** are:
1. **Progress descriptors** for Reading, Writing and Maths: *Emerging, Developing, Consolidating, Proficient, Exceeding*.
2. A **visual representation** of the student's progress over time (built by the school's student management system provider).
3. A **narrative** explaining why a descriptor was selected and how parents/whānau can support next learning steps.
4. **Assessment results** — which "can include results from e-asTTle, SMART, or PATs," plus Phonics Check | Hihira Weteoro for Years 0–2.
5. An **attendance record**.

[Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p)

The same page is explicit that schools "already have good practices in place... that may be similar to these, but these Common Components will still be encouraged" — i.e. no school is required to adopt this exact report format, only to keep meeting regulation 21. I was not able to independently retrieve the literal contents of the Ministry's downloadable `RequiredEncouraged_20260701.pdf` table referenced on the same page (see limits below); the required/encouraged split above is drawn from the page's own prose description of that table, not the table image itself.

### 3. Assessment tools: SMART, PATs, e-asTTle — a choice, not a mandate

For the 2026 twice-yearly Years 3–8 assessment expectation, schools may use **any one of three tools**: SMART (Ministry, free), PATs (NZCER, not Ministry-funded), or e-asTTle (Ministry, being discontinued) [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p); [Tāhūrangi requirements page, 2026](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-assessment-requirements-expectations-for-2026): "For the 2026 school year, schools can choose from SMART, e-asTTle and PAT for twice-yearly progression monitoring."

- **SMART** ("Student Monitoring, Assessment and Reporting Tool") is a free, bilingual, Ministry digital tool for twice-yearly assessment in Years 3–10, covering reading, writing, maths (English medium) and pānui, tuhituhi, pāngarau (Māori-medium strands), aligned to the refreshed New Zealand Curriculum and Te Marautanga o Aotearoa [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p). The Ministry describes it going live for the "Teaching the Basics Brilliantly" work programme "in Term 1, 2026 for Years 3 to 10" [Ministry of Education, Improving achievement, 2025](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/improving-achievement); the first live assessment window ran Term 2, Weeks 1–5 (20 April – 22 May 2026), with detailed reports released 3 July 2026, and a second window scheduled Term 4, Weeks 1–5 (12 October – 13 November 2026) [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p).
- **PATs** (Progressive Achievement Tests) are NZCER-owned multi-choice standardised assessments for Years 3–10, available pen-and-paper or online, and are explicitly framed by NZCER as "formative, low-stakes assessments" that "sit alongside classroom observations... as part of a broad, balanced approach" — not a single determinative score [NZCER, Progressive Achievement Tests, 2026](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/assessments/pats). NZCER has published an interim mapping of PAT scores onto the same five progress descriptors used for SMART/e-asTTle reporting [NZCER, Progressive Achievement Tests, 2026](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/assessments/pats).
- **e-asTTle** ("Assessment Tools for Teaching and Learning") is the Ministry's pre-existing online literacy/numeracy assessment tool; it remains usable in 2026 but is **not aligned with the refreshed curriculum** and is scheduled for discontinuation at the end of 2026 [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p).

**SMART's reporting data flow** is two-staged: a **Foundation report** with raw-score information is available immediately after marking; after a roughly six-week period (a 2026-only transitional step, described by the Ministry as "similar to what happened when e-asTTle was first introduced"), teachers get three scaled-score reports (**Demonstrated skills and practices**, **Next Learning Focus**, **Gaps**) plus three school-level dashboards broken down by **year level**, **strand/element**, and **demographic** [Tāhūrangi requirements page, 2026](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-assessment-requirements-expectations-for-2026).

### 4. Year levels and scope

- Regulation 21's twice-yearly plain-language reporting duty applies across all year levels (Years 0–13, since it is not itself year-bounded) but the 2026 curriculum-specific reporting requirement is scoped to the **revised Years 0–10 English and Mathematics & Statistics learning areas**; for English Years 11–13 and Maths & Statistics Years 11–13, schools continue reporting against the 2007 curriculum [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p).
- The twice-yearly **standardised-assessment** expectation (SMART/PAT/e-asTTle) for 2026 applies to **Years 3–8** [Tāhūrangi requirements page, 2026](https://tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-assessment-requirements-expectations-for-2026), while the SMART tool itself is built to also serve Years 9–10 [Tāhūrangi SMART guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p). Years 0–2 are covered separately by the Phonics Check | Hihira Weteoro, which can be attached as an assessment result under Common Component 4 [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p).
- The Tāhūrangi reporting page also flags separate material on "What's changing in 2026 for Years 9–10?" as a distinct info sheet, which I could not independently retrieve the content of (see limits) — flagged as an open item rather than asserted.
- By contrast, National Standards applied only to **Years 1–8** in reading, writing and mathematics [TKI NAGs fact sheet, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet), so the 2026 system's reach into Years 9–10 reporting is a genuine scope expansion versus National Standards, independent of the assessment-tool question.

### 5. Māori-medium timing

Schools run by a "specified kura board" — defined as a kura kaupapa Māori, a designated character school with a hapū- or iwi-based character or one affiliating with Ngā Kura ā-Iwi o Aotearoa, or a state-integrated school with a hapū- or iwi-based special character — can keep using the pre-existing (2025) curriculum, assessment, aromatawai and reporting approaches through the end of 2026, and are not required to adopt the new curriculum, SMART, or reporting approach until **1 January 2027** [Tāhūrangi reporting guidance, 2026](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p).

This mirrors the National Standards rollout: mainstream National Standards began in 2010, while the Māori-medium equivalent, **Ngā Whanaketanga Rumaki Māori** (under Te Marautanga o Aotearoa), was implemented from 2011 — a Ministry-commissioned report on that rollout states "From 2011, Māori-medium schools that use Te Marautanga o Aotearoa will implement Ngā Whanaketanga Rumaki Māori, Māori-medium national standards, for students in years 1 to 8" [Education Counts, Ngā Whanaketanga Rumaki Māori: Analysis of Feedback from Readiness Workshops, Aug 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/all-schooling/nga-whanaketanga-rumaki-maori-analysis-of-feedback-from-readiness-workshops). School-level *aggregate* National Standards data reporting to the Secretary for Education for Te Marautanga o Aotearoa schools was pushed out a further year, to the 2012 school year [TKI NAGs fact sheet, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet).

### 6. National Standards' legal basis and mechanics (2010–2017), for comparison

National Standards were established through **National Administration Guideline 2A (NAG 2A)**, a new guideline inserted alongside amendments to NAG 1 and NAG 2, gazetted in October 2009 and distributed to schools via Education Circular 2009/12 on 4 November 2009 [TKI NAGs fact sheet, published 20 Nov 2009, amended 14 May 2010, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet). NAGs were themselves issued by the Minister of Education under the National Education Guidelines power (Education Act 1989, s60A) [New Zealand Gazette, National Administration Guidelines notice, 2009](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2009-go8813).

Under NAG 2A, boards had to:
- **Report to parents** in plain language and in writing, at least twice a year, on students' progress and achievement **specifically in relation to National Standards** (not merely "the curriculum" as today) [TKI NAGs fact sheet, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet). Notably, the NAGs "do not specifically require schools to report to parents whether students are above, at, below, or well below the standards, although they may do so if they wish" — the individual-student-report descriptor was in practice, not black-letter, requirement, similar to the 2026 model.
- **Submit annual school-level aggregate data** to the Secretary for Education, alongside the school's charter (per NAG 7): the numbers and proportions of students at, above, below, or well below the National Standards, broken down by Māori, Pasifika, gender, and year level (subject to privacy limits), from the 2011 school year (first reports due 2012) [TKI NAGs fact sheet, via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20230218194529/https://nzcurriculum.tki.org.nz/Archives/Assessment/National-Standards-archives/Fact-sheets/NAGs-fact-sheet).
- Choose their own assessment tools to inform an **Overall Teacher Judgement (OTJ)** against a **4-level descriptor scale**: Well Below, Below, At, Above.

National Standards were wound back from December 2017: "Starting in 2018, schools will no longer be compelled to report annually on National Standards to the Ministry of Education... Parents will still receive reports at least twice a year on their child's progress and achievement in maths, reading and writing as well as across the curriculum areas. But this reporting will focus on children's progress, rather than measuring them against arbitrary National Standards" [Beehive, National Standards ended, 12 Dec 2017](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/national-standards-ended). This confirms the twice-yearly parent-report duty was **retained**, not abolished — only the compulsory annual whole-cohort submission to the Ministry, and the requirement to report against the named "National Standards" benchmark, were removed. The full National Education Guidelines/NAGs framework (including NAG 2A) persisted for several more years before it, and its predecessor National Education Goals, ceased to have legal effect on 1 January 2023 — the same date regulation 21 was inserted to carry the residual twice-yearly reporting duty forward into regulation [Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020, reg 21 (noting its 1 January 2023 insertion date)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2020/193/en/2024-10-01.pdf).

### 7. Side-by-side comparison

| Element | National Standards (2010–2017) | 2026 system |
|---|---|---|
| Legal instrument | NAG 2A, gazetted Oct 2009, under the National Education Guidelines (Education Act 1989 s60A) | Reg 21, Education (School Boards) Regulations 2020, in force since 1 Jan 2023, under the Education and Training Act 2020 |
| Regulated content | Report **against National Standards by name**, twice yearly, reading/writing/maths, Years 1–8; annual whole-cohort data to the Secretary for Education | Report progress/achievement **against the curriculum** (no named national benchmark), twice yearly, in literacy/maths, plain language, Years 0–10 (2026 curriculum content) |
| Assessment tool | Not mandated — school's choice, feeding an Overall Teacher Judgement | Not mandated — SMART (free), PATs (NZCER, unfunded), or e-asTTle (being phased out) |
| Reporting descriptors | 4 levels (Well Below / Below / At / Above) | 5 levels (Emerging / Developing / Consolidating / Proficient / Exceeding) — Ministry-**encouraged**, not regulated |
| Compulsory national aggregation | Yes — annual school-by-school data to the Secretary for Education, by ethnicity/gender/year level | No — national picture comes from a **sample-based** study (Curriculum Insights and Progress Study, Year 8) feeding Government Target 7, not a compulsory whole-cohort submission |
| Year levels | 1–8 | Reporting: 0–10; standardised twice-yearly assessment window: 3–8 (SMART built for 3–10) |
| Māori-medium lag | From 2011 (mainstream 2010); aggregate reporting from 2012 | From 1 Jan 2027 for "specified kura board" schools (mainstream 2026) |

Sources for this table are the citations given inline in sections 1–6 above.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The literal contents of the Ministry's `RequiredEncouraged_20260701.pdf` table** would either confirm or complicate the required/encouraged split described here. I could not retrieve this PDF directly: the Tāhūrangi pages that link to it render their download links via client-side JavaScript that neither `curl`, the built-in WebFetch tool, real-Chrome `agent-browser`, nor stealth-Chromium `cloak-fetch` exposed as plain `<a href>` PDF URLs I could resolve. My description of "required vs encouraged" rests on the surrounding page *prose*, which states the same distinction independently on two separate Ministry pages — but the table itself could contain edge cases (e.g. a required minimum reporting cadence per component) not visible in the prose.
- **A primary legal citation for the 2017 NAG 2A repeal/amendment itself** (as opposed to the ministerial press release describing its effect) would strengthen section 6. I relied on the Beehive release and the archived NAGs fact sheet rather than the specific 2017/2018 Gazette notice amending NAG 2A; I did not locate and verify that notice directly.
- **Independent (non-Ministry) confirmation of the SMART data-flow description** (Foundation report → six-week scaled reports → dashboards) would raise confidence on that specific mechanic — currently it rests on a single Ministry-authored Tāhūrangi page. Sector commentary from NZEI and PPTA on SMART (already surfaced in the stream's framing analysis, PR #638) is about accuracy/workload concerns, not the mechanics of the reporting pipeline itself, so it doesn't independently corroborate or contradict this description.
- **The Years 9–10-specific reporting changes** referenced on the Tāhūrangi reporting page ("What's changing in 2026 for Years 9-10?") were not independently retrieved — I flagged this as an open item rather than describing its contents, and a future researcher should pull that info sheet directly.
- If a released Cabinet paper or Regulatory Impact Statement for the 2026 reset surfaces (beyond the Treasury OIA release already covered in the stream's framing document), it could clarify whether any part of the "encouraged" layer was originally proposed as regulated and downgraded — which would bear on questions #2 and #4 in the stream, not this one, but would sharpen the legal-history picture here too.

## Open follow-up questions

- What exactly do the Years 9–10-specific reporting changes involve, and how (if at all) do they interact with NCEA literacy/numeracy co-requisite reporting for those year levels?

- Is there a published Cabinet paper, Regulatory Impact Statement, or gazette notice for the 2026 curriculum/reporting change equivalent to the 2009 NAG 2A Gazette notice for National Standards — i.e., what is the precise instrument (beyond Ministry guidance) that made the revised English/Maths learning areas the operative "national curriculum statements" under regulation 21(2)(b)(i)?
