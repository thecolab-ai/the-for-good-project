# NZ publishes almost no completion, placement or retention outcome data for its teacher-supply incentive schemes — one scheme (TeachNZ scholarships) is the exception

Part of #439. This answers the research question: what is publicly known about the uptake, completion, placement and retention outcomes of NZ's teacher-supply incentives — the Teacher Bonding Scheme (TBS), Go Rural, EBITE, TeachNZ scholarships, and the School Onsite Training Programme (SOTP)?

## Executive answer

- **Only one scheme has a genuine public outcomes evaluation: TeachNZ scholarships.** A 2018 Ministry-commissioned NZCER review, analysing 3,531 applicants (2012–2018) and surveying ~2,000 recipients, found a **22.7% default rate** (151 of 665 scholarships) for the 2012 cohort — the only round mature enough to assess — split between defaulting during study (76) and during the post-graduation bond (75); **90% of women and 78% of men completed** their ITE study in that cohort; **16% of respondents who got a job reported "challenges completing their bond"** (mainly a scarcity of jobs matching bonding conditions); and **11% ended up teaching, but not in the role the scholarship was intended for**. A separate, more recent but *unpublished* Ministry-commissioned study (Standard of Proof, c.2022–23) independently arrived at an "estimated 22% default" rate — a rare case of two independent sources converging on the same number, though the second study's underlying report could not be located publicly.
- **For every other scheme, only inputs (dollars, funded places) are published — not completion, placement, or retention.** Teacher Bonding Scheme (TBS, launched October 2025) has no uptake data yet — applications for its first cohort closed 26 November 2025 and no fill rate has been published as of this finding. Go Rural (launched 2026) and the current large-scale SOTP have no completion or placement data at all. EBITE is the partial exception: a September 2024 Ministry paper to the Minister reports its 2020–2024 pilots achieved an **86% completion rate and 100% retention (11 of 11 graduates still teaching in target settings)** — but this is a small, self-selected pilot cohort (all trainees were already community-embedded LAT holders), reported only in an internal Ministry briefing, not an independent evaluation.
- **Where actual uptake/retention numbers for related schemes do exist, they come from Annual Report performance narratives, not scheme-specific evaluations, and are patchy year to year.** The Ministry of Education's 2023 and 2024 Annual Reports disclose, for schemes adjacent to or folded into TBS: 128 BeTTER Jobs Programme placements in 2022/23, and of the 124 who started the 2023 BeTTER cohort, **113 (91%) were still teaching in 2024**; the Voluntary Bonding Scheme (VBS, TBS's direct predecessor) made 297 retention payments in 2023/24 (70 to STM/te reo Māori teachers); SOTP enrolment grew from 92 (2023) to 143 (2024) secondary trainees. These are real, citable figures — but none of them is a scheme evaluation, none covers TBS/Go Rural/EBITE-at-scale/current SOTP directly, and the Ministry does not appear to publish a comparable series in every year (VBS uptake before 2023/24, for example, was not found).
- **No cross-scheme, independent, or Auditor-General evaluation of NZ's teacher-supply incentive toolkit as a portfolio exists.** The closest analogue — a 2021 independent review (commissioned by, and reported to, the Ministry) comparing cost-per-recruit across several overseas-recruitment and study-award schemes, known only via 2022 RNZ reporting — found "most had a lower uptake than expected and budgeted funding had been underspent," but even that review is journalism about an unpublished document, stopped at recruitment/cost-effectiveness, and did not cover TBS, Go Rural, EBITE or SOTP. The Office of the Auditor-General's most recent (2024) education audit does not mention teacher-supply incentives at all. TBS itself replaced VBS and BeTTER "because they're not working" (Minister Stanford, Sept 2025) without a public evaluation being cited to substantiate that claim.
- **Overall confidence: Medium.** The absence-of-evidence claims for TBS, Go Rural, SOTP-at-scale and cross-scheme evaluation are well-supported by an exhaustive multi-angle search (Ministry annual reports, Treasury/Vote Education Estimates, OAG, ERO, Parliament, OIA-release site fyi.org.nz, and general web/news) turning up nothing rather than one missed source. The TeachNZ and EBITE outcome figures are High confidence (primary-source PDFs, directly verified). The residual risk is that a genuine evaluation exists inside the Ministry (an OIA response, an unindexed briefing, or committee testimony) that this search — constrained to public web sources — did not surface.

## Evidence

### TeachNZ scholarships — the one scheme with a real outcomes evaluation, but it is 8 years old

The Ministry of Education contracted the New Zealand Council for Educational Research (NZCER) to review the TeachNZ scholarship programme; the resulting report, *Review of Teach NZ scholarship programme* (Jo MacDonald, Jenny Whatman, Sheridan McKinley, Melanie Berg and Lorraine Spiller, NZCER, 2018), analysed the Ministry's full applicant database (3,531 applicants, 2012–2018) and surveyed roughly 2,000 recipients (2012–2017 cohorts), plus interviews and stakeholder workshops between December 2017 and April 2018. [NZCER, Review of Teach NZ scholarship programme, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/review-of-teach-nz-scholarship-programme) (landing page returns HTTP 403/Cloudflare bot-challenge on direct fetch — BLOCKED, not dead; retrieved via Wayback: [archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20240728103829/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/195433/NZCER-report-Teach-NZ-scholarship-programme.pdf), full text independently extracted and verified for this finding).

The report's Table 14 ("Summary of recipient status, end 2017, by application round") is the clearest bonding-outcome data found anywhere in this research. For the 2012 round — the only round mature enough for the report to draw conclusions from — of 665 scholarships offered: 270 (40.6%) were "Closed—obligations complete," 242 (36.4%) were "Ready to teach or currently teaching" (i.e., still fulfilling the bond), and **151 (22.7%) had defaulted at any point**. The report traces that of the 151 defaults, 76 happened during ITE study and the remaining 75 happened during the post-graduation bond period itself — i.e., roughly half of all defaults occur only after the Ministry has already funded a completed teaching qualification. [NZCER, Review of Teach NZ scholarship programme, 2018, p.27 (Table 14)](https://web.archive.org/web/20240728103829/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/195433/NZCER-report-Teach-NZ-scholarship-programme.pdf) Later rounds (2013: 279 offered, 43 defaulted, 15.4%; 2014: 224 offered, 39 defaulted, 17.4%) show similar default rates but the report cautions these cohorts were still mid-bond in 2017 and the true default rate for them was not yet knowable. [Same source, Table 14]

On study completion, the report finds no meaningful gender difference in the aggregate, but for the mature 2012 round specifically, **90% of women completed their ITE study compared with 78% of men**. [Same source, p.26] On bond compliance in practice, of survey respondents who had completed ITE and got a job, **16% (81 of roughly 500) reported "challenges completing their bond,"** attributed mainly to a scarcity of jobs matching bonding conditions — particularly for Māori-medium/te reo Māori placements in some regions — plus administrative failures (lost paperwork, uncounted relieving hours). Separately, **22% (112 respondents) said the bonding requirement changed a job-application or acceptance decision**, mostly describing pressure to accept the first available job to avoid default; and **11% (50 recipients) were still teaching, but not in a role the scholarship was intended for** (e.g., a Māori-medium scholarship recipient teaching in an English-medium role). [Same source, pp.26, 39–40]

A second, independent-but-unpublished study reached a strikingly similar headline number: Standard of Proof, an NZ research/evaluation consultancy commissioned by Education Workforce (the Ministry unit administering scholarships), used a literature review, recipient interviews and logistic regression on Ministry administrative data to estimate **"an estimated 22% of recipients default on their scholarship, either during their training or during their bonding period."** [Standard of Proof, "Research to inform scholarship bonding practice"](https://www.standardofproof.nz/projects/research-to-inform-scholarship-bonding-practice/) (live URL now 301-redirects to the site homepage — the page has been removed/restructured, not merely blocked, as confirmed by its absence from both current sitemaps; archived via Wayback: [snapshot, 2026-02-01](https://web.archive.org/web/20260201135815/https://www.standardofproof.nz/projects/research-to-inform-scholarship-bonding-practice/), which still shows the "an estimated 22% of recipients default on their scholarship..." text. The underlying report itself could not be located on Education Counts, the Ministry's publication list, or fyi.org.nz — treat the 22% figure as corroborating but not independently checkable against full methodology). The project page's own copyright/asset dates place the work at roughly 2022–2023, materially more recent than the 2018 NZCER review, which strengthens confidence that the ~22% default rate has been reasonably stable over a decade even as named scholarships changed (Te Tipu Whakarito, Te Waka Whakarei and Te Huawhiti all post-date the NZCER review's data window).

Current-era scale, without outcome breakdown: the Ministry of Education Annual Report 2024 states **363 TeachNZ scholarships were awarded in 2023/24**, and the 2023 Annual Report states **185 Te Huawhiti Career-Changer Scholarships were awarded in 2022/23** and **64 Iwi Māori Workforce Support Package scholarships in 2022/23** (28 new "Uri" starters, 36 continuing). [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2024, p.21](https://www.mcguinnessinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/19.-MOE-2.pdf) (mirrored copy of the official report; text independently extracted and verified); [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2023, p.27](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2023-12/annual-report-2023-web.pdf?VersionId=yul0MPNcSVmIhDVfqJXW947BVM2k_bb9) (text independently extracted and verified). None of these more recent Annual Report entries report a completion, bonding-compliance or retention rate for the current cohorts — only award counts, meaning the 2018/2022 default-rate evidence above has not been refreshed publicly for the current, larger, more diversified scholarship suite.

### EBITE — a small pilot with strong reported outcomes, not yet independently verified at scale

A Ministry of Education paper to the Minister of Education (METIS 1332782, dated 4 September 2024, "Improving teacher supply for Kaupapa Māori and Māori medium education – Employment Based Initial Teacher Education") states that EBITE pilot programmes run by Māori providers from 2020 to 2024 **"achieved an 86% completion rate and a 100% teacher retention rate, with all 11 graduates still teaching in these settings,"** with a further 20 trainees expected to graduate that year, bringing the pilot total to 31. [Ministry of Education, Education Report 1332782, 4 September 2024, p.1](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-04/1332782%20-%20Improving%20teacher%20supply%20for%20Kaupapa%20M%C4%81ori_Redacted.pdf) (PDF fetched directly and text independently extracted and verified for this finding). The same paper notes 100% of participants in a recent Ministry survey recommended the programme, and uses this pilot performance to justify scaling to 65 places every two years from 2025, at $2.3 million non-departmental funding for 2024/25. [Same source, p.1]

This is the strongest outcome figure found for any of the five schemes besides TeachNZ — but three caveats limit how far it generalises. First, it is a Ministry-authored internal briefing to its own Minister, not an independent evaluation (unlike the NZCER/Standard of Proof work on TeachNZ). Second, the denominator for the 86% completion figure is not stated explicitly in the extractable text, though it is back-calculable: the same source states 31 graduates resulted in the 86% completion rate, implying roughly 36 starters and roughly 5 non-completers (31/0.86 ≈ 36). Third, and most importantly for the placement/retention conversion story: EBITE's pilot trainees were, by programme design, already Limited Authority to Teach (LAT) holders working in their home kura/community before training began — a highly self-selected, already-embedded group, which plausibly explains the 100% retention figure better than the incentive structure itself would for an "outsider" recruit. [Same source, background paragraphs]

A related, more independent literature review exists but could not be retrieved in this research: Education Counts, "Literature Review: Preliminary insights on Employment-based ITE" ([landing page](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0003/197382/Literature-Review-EBITE-MM-final.pdf) returned HTTP 403 on repeated attempts — BLOCKED, not confirmed dead; a future researcher with browser-escalation tooling should retry via `scripts/fetch.mjs`).

### Teacher Bonding Scheme (TBS) — too new for outcome data; predecessor schemes show real but partial uptake/retention figures

TBS launched 5 October 2025, replacing the Voluntary Bonding Scheme (VBS) and the BeTTER Jobs Programme, offering up to $40,000 over five years to teachers recruited to eligible Priority Staffing Schools, at 185 places/year for the 2025/26 cohort. [Education Workforce, Teacher Bonding Scheme](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/principals-and-schools/initiatives-schools-and-kura/teacher-bonding-scheme); [Beehive.govt.nz, "New Teacher Bonding Scheme supports schools and teachers," 19 September 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-teacher-bonding-scheme-supports-schools-and-teachers) Applications for the first (2025/26) cohort closed 26 November 2025, and **no public source discloses how many of the 185 places were actually filled** as of this finding (July 2026) — this was checked directly against the Education Workforce page, PPTA's coverage, and all major news outlets reporting the scheme, none of which report a fill rate. From the 2026/27 financial year, places rise from 185 to 235/year (+50), alongside a Go Rural expansion of +87 placements/year, funded by $2.35 million/year in "reprioritisations" — announced without any uptake or underspend data cited as justification. [Scoop/Government press release, "Teacher Support Program Expansions," 20 April 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2604/S00101/immediate-fuel-relief-for-school-communities.htm) (quoted directly: "increasing the number of places available in the Teacher Bonding Scheme by 50, from 185 places to 235 per year" and "expanded by a further 87 places per year, from the 2026/27 financial year"); corroborated by [Farmers Weekly, 21 April 2026](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/politics/rural-schools-in-line-for-grants-and-transport-boost-amid-rising-fuel-costs/).

Because TBS itself is too new for outcomes, the most informative evidence is its predecessors' actual (not just funded) uptake and retention, which the Ministry did disclose in Annual Report performance narratives:

- **BeTTER Jobs Programme**: 128 new/returning teachers placed with schools in 2022/23; of the 124 who started the 2023 cohort, **113 (91%) were still teaching in 2024** — a real, one-year retention figure for a genuine predecessor scheme. [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2023, p.27](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2023-12/annual-report-2023-web.pdf?VersionId=yul0MPNcSVmIhDVfqJXW947BVM2k_bb9); [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2024, p.21](https://www.mcguinnessinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/19.-MOE-2.pdf)
- **Voluntary Bonding Scheme**: the Ministry made **297 retention payments to teachers in 2023/24, of which 70 went to STM or te reo Māori teachers**. [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2024, p.21](https://www.mcguinnessinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/19.-MOE-2.pdf) Going further back, a December 2015 NZ Herald report found that **only 620 teaching graduates had completed VBS's 3-year minimum service requirement nationally in the roughly six years since the scheme began in 2009** — a stark gap against the Ministry's own 2009 launch estimate that "close to 1800 teachers may be eligible" in the first year alone. [NZ Herald, "Teachers for hard-to-staff areas still difficult despite scheme"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/teachers-for-hard-to-staff-areas-still-difficult-despite-scheme/IZ3QQMD5AU6U7PMYUPPPR5RRLU/); [Beehive.govt.nz, "New scheme helps schools recruit," 23 February 2009](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-scheme-helps-schools-recruit) (1800 is an eligibility estimate, not a formal uptake target, so this comparison should be read as directional, not a precise miss rate). The same NZ Herald reporting states the Ministry did **not track how many VBS participants failed to meet the three-year requirement**, meaning no default/compliance rate has ever been publicly calculable for VBS. A single secondary source (a union-advocacy site reposting an NZEI claim) puts VBS applications at just 12 nationally in one specific year (2017) against "hundreds" of claimed-eligible teachers — this is Low confidence, single-sourced, and not independently corroborated, but consistent in direction with the other under-uptake evidence. [WATU, "Government not using major tool to attract teachers," 21 August 2017](https://wearetheuniversity.org/archive/2017/08/21/government-not-using-major-tool-to-attract-teachers/)

Minister Erica Stanford's own justification for replacing VBS and BeTTER with TBS was that they were "existing money, things we're closing down because they're not working," citing restrictive eligibility (0.8+ FTTE minimum, graduate-only) rather than a published evaluation with uptake/underspend figures. [NZ Herald, "Government unveils $40k teacher bonding scheme," 11 September 2025](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/government-unveils-40k-teacher-bonding-scheme-for-rural-and-hardtostaff-schools/LYMZACKNVFHGLK6EA46HV6GEHU/); corroborated by [RNZ, "New Teacher Bonding Scheme boosts recruitment for rural schools," 14 September 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/573030/new-teacher-bonding-scheme-boosts-recruitment-for-rural-schools) (rural-schools-association president Andrew King said, in reported speech, that the Voluntary Bonding Scheme and BeTTER Jobs Programme "had not been used to their full potential," available only to new teachers with a minimum 0.8 FTTE).

### Go Rural — launched 2026, genuinely no outcome data exists yet

Go Rural (Isolated Placements Fund) offers a $4,000 grant to student teachers taking 5+ week block placements at one of 454 eligible rural/isolated schools, with up to 123 places funded in its 2026 launch year (total fund $494,000). [Ministry of Education, "Teacher, Demand and Supply 2025 report released"](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/teacher-demand-and-supply-2025-report-released); [Community Scoop/PA, "Getting More Trainee Teachers Into Rural Schools," June 2025](https://m.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2506/S00165/getting-more-trainee-teachers-into-rural-schools.htm); [Education Workforce, "Go Rural (Isolated Placements Fund)"](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/go-rural-isolated-placements-fund) (454 eligible schools and 5+ week minimum placement length, confirmed directly on this page) No source discloses actual applications received or grants paid against the 123-place cap, and — because Go Rural pays out during a training placement rather than a permanent job — there is no possible placement-conversion or retention data yet: no cohort has had time to graduate and take up (or not take up) a permanent rural role. The programme's own rationale is a stated hypothesis, not a measured result: Minister Stanford is quoted that "for most new teachers, school placements play a significant role in deciding where they choose to teach once they qualify" — plausible, but untested by any public data found in this research.

### SOTP (School Onsite Training Programme) — enrolment growth is disclosed; completion, placement and retention are not

SOTP enrolment has been reported in successive Annual Reports: 92 secondary trainees in 2023 (44 studying STM subjects, 5 te reo Māori) rising to 143 in 2024 (54 STM, 8 te reo Māori). [Ministry of Education, Annual Report 2024, p.21](https://www.mcguinnessinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/19.-MOE-2.pdf) Budget 2024 funded 1,200 SOTP places over four years, expanding the programme to include primary and intermediate as well as secondary. [Education Workforce, "Major investment in teacher supply through Budget 24"](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/major-investment-teacher-supply-through-budget-24) Budget 2025 then added "530 places to 1,861 over 4 years" on top of that. [Ministry of Education, "Teacher, Demand and Supply 2025 report released"](https://www.education.govt.nz/news/teacher-demand-and-supply-2025-report-released) These two published figures do not reconcile arithmetically (1,200 + 530 = 1,730, not 1,861); the discrepancy could not be resolved from public sources and is noted here rather than silently corrected. Separately, RNZ reported a further SOTP place figure: "Increasing the number of in-classroom training spots from 242 places in 2025 to 533 places in 2026." [RNZ, "Plan to boost teacher training announced"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/588124/plan-to-boost-teacher-training-announced) This figure is not attributable to the Ministry's Teacher Demand & Supply Planning Projection PDF cited elsewhere in this finding — that document was checked directly and contains no occurrence of "242" or "533" — so it should be treated as RNZ's own reporting rather than a quote from the Projection itself. RNZ's wording does not specify whether the 242/533 figures are secondary-only or include the primary/intermediate places added under Budget 24's SOTP expansion (discussed above); no source found in this research draws that distinction for these two numbers. A 2026 Beehive release notes provider interest for the SOTP round "has exceeded the number of places available" — a demand signal, not an actual-enrolment figure. [Beehive.govt.nz, "Boosting the teacher workforce and investing in success"](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/boosting-teacher-workforce-and-investing-success) No completion rate, ITE-registration rate, placement-into-shortage-role rate, or multi-year retention figure specific to SOTP graduates was found anywhere in Ministry, Education Counts, ERO, or provider (University of Waikato, AUT, ECNZ) sources checked.

### No cross-scheme portfolio evaluation exists

The closest thing to a portfolio-level assessment is a 2021 independent review (reported to the Ministry) — known only through 2022 RNZ reporting, since the underlying document could not be located — comparing cost-per-recruit across overseas finders' fees, overseas relocation grants, TeachNZ study awards, bilingual study awards, refresh courses and Teach First NZ. It found the government had spent roughly $72m recruiting 7,595 teachers/student teachers by mid-2021 against a $142m commitment since December 2017, at costs ranging from under $2,000/recruit (refresh courses) to $174,000/recruit (bilingual study awards), and concluded **"the various schemes for attracting teachers worked, though most had a lower uptake than expected and budgeted funding had been underspent."** [RNZ, "Teacher recruitment: Finders' fees, relocation grants among top incentives, review finds," 11 June 2022](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/468915/teacher-recruitment-finders-fees-relocation-grants-among-top-incentives-review-finds) This review does not cover TBS, Go Rural, EBITE, or the current SOTP, and it stops at recruitment/cost-effectiveness — it does not touch completion or retention.

The Ministry's own supply-planning model treats scheme uptake as a single rounded input assumption rather than a reported outcome: its Teacher Demand & Supply Planning Projection bundles TeachNZ scholarships, three Māori Medium EBITE programmes, Teach First NZ, Co-Design Iwi-Based Scholarships, Teacher Education Refresh, the Overseas Relocation Grant and SOTP into supply-forecast scenarios using "full (100%)," "medium (80%, based on average uptake of initiatives in 2023)" or "low (30%)" uptake assumptions — confirming the Ministry holds an internal aggregate uptake figure, but the public document surfaces it only as a forecasting input, not a scheme-by-scheme accountability report. [Ministry of Education, Teacher Demand & Supply Planning Projection, December](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-04/1319395%20-Teacher%20Demand%20&%20Supply%20Planning%20Projection%20December.pdf)

A 2022 Cabinet-facing paper on overseas recruitment grants shows an evaluation that was planned but appears to have lapsed: **"We had initially signalled that the [Overseas Finders Fee] and [Overseas Relocation Grant] would be temporary while we conducted a wider review of our overseas recruitment approach but have found that the value of these grants is undisputed."** — i.e., continuation was justified by sector feedback rather than the originally-promised review being completed and published. [Ministry of Education, Education Report 1292247, 5 August 2022 (OIA-released March 2024)](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2024-03/ER-1292247-Increasing-teacher-supply-through-overseas-teacher-recruitment_Redacted.pdf) The Office of the Auditor-General's most recent education performance audit, on equitable outcomes (2024), does not mention teacher-supply incentives, bonding, TeachNZ, EBITE, Go Rural, or SOTP anywhere in its full text; no earlier OAG report (2008, 2010, 2012) covers the current incentive suite either. [Office of the Auditor-General, "Ministry of Education: Promoting equitable educational outcomes," B.29[24h], 2024](https://oag.parliament.nz/2024/educational-outcomes/docs/educational-outcomes.pdf) (full text checked directly).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published TBS fill-rate for the 2025/26 cohort** (applications closed 26 November 2025) would be the single most decision-relevant piece of missing evidence, since TBS is the scheme every recent budget announcement is expanding (185→235 places) without any uptake evidence yet available. This should become checkable within the next 6-12 months and is worth a direct OIA request or a repeat search closer to the 2026/27 cohort announcement.
- **Release of the Standard of Proof full report** (commissioned by Education Workforce, only a project-summary page is public) would let the ~22% TeachNZ default figure be checked against its actual methodology, sample and years covered, rather than taken on a marketing-page summary alone.
- **A public denominator for EBITE's 86% completion figure** (how many pilot trainees started, not just how many graduated) would let that statistic be checked rather than taken at face value from a Ministry-authored briefing to its own Minister.
- **Locating the underlying 2021 Ministry review** referenced only via 2022 RNZ reporting (cost-per-recruit across overseas and study-award schemes) would clarify whether it, or a successor, was ever extended to cover TBS/Go Rural/EBITE/SOTP specifically, and whether "lower uptake than expected" was quantified per scheme.
- **An OIA response or Estimates examination disclosing TBS/Go Rural applications-received data** would resolve the biggest current gap directly, since both schemes are too new for the Ministry's own Annual Report cycle to have reported on them yet (the next Annual Report, for FY2025/26, is due around October/November 2026).
