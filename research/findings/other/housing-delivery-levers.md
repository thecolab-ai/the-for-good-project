# NZ housing delivery levers have strongest evidence for direct public procurement and infrastructure unlocks, not for incentive payments alone

## Executive answer

- The strongest NZ evidence that a lever has already produced completed homes is direct public or social-housing procurement: HUD's April 2026 dashboard defines delivered homes as "built and available for tenanting" and reports 211 Budget 2024 social homes, 235 Budget 2025 Auckland social homes, and 97 Maori housing homes delivered from the tracked funding streams [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 dashboard, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026).
- Infrastructure funding has good NZ evidence for enabling and accelerating housing capacity, but weaker public evidence for completed homes: the Infrastructure Acceleration Fund has allocated $908 million and is expected to enable 30,000 to 35,000 dwellings, while the earlier Milldale financing structure says it accelerated about 4,000 houses in Milldale and supported infrastructure for another 5,000 nearby homes [National Infrastructure, IAF, 2026](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/infrastructure-acceleration-fund/); [National Infrastructure, Milldale](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/milldale/).
- Building-consent exemptions and self-certification plausibly reduce time and cost, but NZ evidence is still mostly ex ante: MBIE says the 15 January 2026 granny-flat exemption can save up to $5,650 and 14 weeks for simple standalone dwellings up to 70 square metres, and MBIE says self-certification and inspection targets are intended to free BCA capacity, but neither source yet reports completed-home outcomes [MBIE, granny flats, 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats); [MBIE, self-certification, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/accelerating-building-projects-with-self-certification-and-inspection-targets).
- Fast-track approvals can clearly approve large housing projects, but the current NZ public record is mostly approvals rather than completions: Sunfield received Fast-track approval for about 3,400 residential dwellings and Ashbourne received approval in part for 530 homes and 250 retirement units, but I did not find a public dataset showing how many Fast-track-approved dwellings have been completed [Fast-track, Sunfield, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/sunfield); [Fast-track, Ashbourne, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/ashbourne).
- Modular/offsite construction has NZ project-level evidence of time and cost savings, not strong national evidence of more completed homes: an Auckland case-study paper of 66 projects found prefabrication associated with average time and cost reductions, while BRANZ material still frames offsite construction as a potential productivity response rather than a proven national supply lever [Shahzad et al., 2015](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-5309/5/1/196); [BRANZ, 2025](https://www.branz.co.nz/design-build/articles/can-off-site-construction-deliver-for-aotearoa-new-zealand).
- Council incentive payments have the weakest causal evidence: New Zealand's Incentives for Growth Fund will make first payments on 1 April 2027 based on building consents, and England's New Homes Bonus is a cautionary comparator because the UK National Audit Office found it was too early to see a discernible effect and that the original additional-homes estimate used very limited evidence [HUD, Going for Housing Growth, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [UK NAO, 2013](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/the-new-homes-bonus-2/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high for the existence and design of each lever, medium for ranking them, and low-to-medium for causal claims because NZ public data rarely links a lever to completed homes rather than approvals, enabled capacity, or funded units.

## Evidence

### Evidence standard used

I ranked each lever by the strength of public evidence that it increases completed homes, with completed homes weighted above approvals, consents, enabled capacity, or forecasts because the stream's baseline finding shows that consents are not completions and Stats NZ estimates dwelling stock using lagged, weighted building consents [research finding #486](housing-capacity-consents-completions-gap.md); [Stats NZ, dwelling estimates, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/). I treated NZ official sources as primary evidence and used overseas evidence only where NZ evidence was too thin to test causality [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md).

### Public and social housing procurement is the clearest completed-home lever

HUD's April 2026 delivery dashboard is the most direct NZ evidence I found because it defines "delivered" as homes built and available for tenanting, not merely funded or consented [HUD dashboard, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026). The dashboard reports that Budget 2024 allocated $140 million for 1,500 new social homes over 2025/26 and 2026/27, Budget 2025 funded around 550 to 600 new Auckland social homes to be ready for tenanting by November 2026, and $426 million had been approved since December 2023 for Maori-led delivery of around 1,000 homes [HUD dashboard, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026). The same dashboard reports 211 Budget 2024 homes delivered, 235 Budget 2025 Auckland homes delivered, and 97 Maori housing homes delivered as at April/March 2026, for 543 delivered homes across the tracked streams by my addition [HUD dashboard, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026).

This is strong evidence that procurement can produce completed homes, but weak evidence that procurement alone can close the wider gap because the same tracked streams are small beside national consent volumes and social-housing need, as separately assessed in this stream's public-affordable housing finding [research finding #490](public-affordable-housing-gap.md). Victoria is a useful close comparator on that limit: the Victorian Auditor-General found Homes Victoria had a strong evidence base, but demand still outstripped supply and the gap was likely to continue growing despite the Big Housing Build [Victorian Auditor-General, 2024](https://www.audit.vic.gov.au/report/planning_social_housing?section=).

### Infrastructure tools can unlock many dwellings, but public completion evidence lags

The Infrastructure Acceleration Fund is explicitly designed to fund transport, water, and flood-management infrastructure needed to enable housing development, and the programme page says $908 million has been allocated to critical infrastructure projects across New Zealand [National Infrastructure, IAF, 2026](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/infrastructure-acceleration-fund/). The same source says IAF projects are expected to enable 30,000 to 35,000 dwellings and lists examples such as Hastings, where $18.5 million is expected to enable about 4,000 new homes, Lower Hutt, where $98.9 million is expected to provide capacity for about 3,500 homes, and Lake Hawea, where $24 million for three-waters infrastructure is expected to enable about 880 homes [National Infrastructure, IAF, 2026](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/infrastructure-acceleration-fund/).

Milldale is the stronger NZ case for an infrastructure-finance mechanism changing delivery timing: National Infrastructure says its 2018 financing provided $48.9 million for transport and water infrastructure, used an encumbrance model that preceded the Infrastructure Funding and Financing Act 2020, and allowed earlier delivery without affecting local-authority balance-sheet or debt limits [National Infrastructure, Milldale](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/milldale/). The Milldale page says the transaction accelerated about 4,000 houses in Milldale and supported infrastructure for another 5,000 houses in surrounding areas, while also listing completed wastewater infrastructure stages and a bridge completed in early 2025 [National Infrastructure, Milldale](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/milldale/). I treat this as medium-confidence evidence because the source is the delivery agency and uses "accelerated" and "supported" rather than a counterfactual evaluation of completed dwellings [National Infrastructure, Milldale](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/milldale/).

### Building-consent exemptions are plausible, but NZ outcome evidence is too new

MBIE says the granny-flat exemptions took effect on 15 January 2026 and allow simple standalone dwellings up to 70 square metres to be built without building or resource consents where they meet the Building Code and are carried out by authorised professionals [MBIE, granny flats, 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats). MBIE expects the exemption to save up to $5,650 in direct costs and speed up the process by up to 14 weeks, but that is a forecast process benefit, not a measured completed-home outcome [MBIE, granny flats, 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats). MBIE's self-certification and inspection-target announcement is similar: it says eligible building companies, plumbers, and drainlayers will be able to sign off their own work on simple homes, and that BCAs will need to carry out 80% of inspections within three working days, but it reports expected system-capacity benefits rather than built-home outcomes [MBIE, self-certification, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/accelerating-building-projects-with-self-certification-and-inspection-targets).

The closest strong comparator is California's ADU reform sequence, where state law progressively limited local discretion, capped fees, shortened approval timelines, removed some parking and owner-occupancy restrictions, and strengthened state oversight [Marantz et al., 2023](https://www.furmancenter.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Evaluating_Californias_Accessory_Dwelling_Unit_Reforms_508.pdf). Marantz, Elmendorf, and Kim found that ADUs represented a substantial share of recent permits in their Bay Area and Southern California study areas, including about 13% of Bay Area permits and 19% of Southern California study-area permits in 2018-2021 [Marantz et al., 2023](https://www.furmancenter.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Evaluating_Californias_Accessory_Dwelling_Unit_Reforms_508.pdf). This supports the idea that small-unit liberalisation can add a meaningful side-channel of supply, but it does not prove New Zealand's 2026 exemption will do the same because financing, lot suitability, infrastructure, insurance, and local market conditions differ [Marantz et al., 2023](https://www.furmancenter.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Evaluating_Californias_Accessory_Dwelling_Unit_Reforms_508.pdf); [MBIE, granny flats, 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats).

### Fast-track approvals are approval evidence, not completion evidence

The permanent Fast-track regime is a streamlined process for resource consents and other approvals for infrastructure and development projects with significant regional or national benefits [Fast-track, process overview, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/process/process-overview). It has already approved large residential projects: the Sunfield page says the expert panel granted approvals on 10 March 2026 for a project described as approximately 3,400 residential dwellings, and the Ashbourne page says approvals were granted in part on 21 April 2026 for a project of 530 homes and 250 retirement units [Fast-track, Sunfield, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/sunfield); [Fast-track, Ashbourne, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/ashbourne).

Those approvals matter because they can move projects through a gate that might otherwise delay them, but I did not find a public Fast-track dashboard that tracks approved dwellings through building consents, starts, completions, and occupation [Fast-track, projects, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects). The earlier COVID-19 Recovery Fast-track Consenting Act was also framed as a short-term process to boost employment and economic recovery and was repealed on 8 July 2023, but the public summary I found does not report a completed-homes evaluation [MfE, COVID-19 fast-track, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/acts-and-regulations/acts/covid-19-recovery-act-2020/). I therefore rank fast-track approvals below public procurement and infrastructure funding for completed-home evidence, even though the project pipeline is large [Fast-track, Sunfield, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/sunfield); [Fast-track, Ashbourne, 2026](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/ashbourne).

### Modular and offsite construction have project-productivity evidence, not national supply evidence

The best NZ quantitative evidence I found is project-level rather than system-level: Shahzad, Mbachu, and Domingo analysed 66 Auckland building projects using prefabrication and compared cost, time, and productivity outcomes with traditional building, reporting average reductions in completion times and costs and an average productivity improvement [Shahzad et al., 2015](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-5309/5/1/196). That is useful evidence that offsite methods can deliver faster or cheaper projects under some conditions, but it does not show that New Zealand completed more homes nationally because of modular or offsite adoption [Shahzad et al., 2015](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-5309/5/1/196).

BRANZ's current public discussion also frames offsite construction as promising rather than proven at national delivery scale: it asks whether factory-built homes could help solve New Zealand's construction challenges and discusses offsite construction as a productivity opportunity [BRANZ, 2025](https://www.branz.co.nz/design-build/articles/can-off-site-construction-deliver-for-aotearoa-new-zealand). For this stream, modular/offsite construction should therefore be treated as a delivery-efficiency lever that could amplify procurement or standardised pipelines, not as a standalone lever with strong evidence of increasing completed national housing supply [BRANZ, 2025](https://www.branz.co.nz/design-build/articles/can-off-site-construction-deliver-for-aotearoa-new-zealand); [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report).

### Council incentive payments are largely unproven as a causal delivery lever

New Zealand's Incentives for Growth Fund is not yet evaluable because HUD says payments will be made annually from 1 April 2027 based on building consents approved in the 12 months to 31 January [HUD, Going for Housing Growth, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). HUD says Budget 2026 introduced $400 million over four years in tagged contingency for the fund and that payments will be linked to housing growth to help councils manage the impacts of new development [HUD, Budget 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system). The design rewards consents, not observed completions, so the stream should not count it as completed-home evidence until payment and outcome data exist [HUD, Going for Housing Growth, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Stats NZ, dwelling estimates, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).

England's New Homes Bonus is a cautionary comparator because it had a similar local-incentive logic and weak early evidence. The UK National Audit Office reported in 2013 that the department expected the scheme to deliver about 140,000 additional homes over ten years, but the estimate used very limited evidence and contained an arithmetical error; the NAO also said it was too early for the scheme to have had a discernible influence on the number of new homes [UK NAO, 2013](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/the-new-homes-bonus-2/). The official 2014 evaluation says it assessed whether the New Homes Bonus encouraged local housing growth, but it relied heavily on behavioural and attitudinal evidence rather than a clean causal estimate of additional completed homes [DCLG, New Homes Bonus evaluation, 2014](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/evaluation-of-the-new-homes-bonus). That comparator does not prove New Zealand's fund will fail, but it is enough to keep the NZ lever at low confidence until after 2027 data can be compared with counterfactual council outcomes [HUD, Going for Housing Growth, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [UK NAO, 2013](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/the-new-homes-bonus-2/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public NZ dataset linking each Fast-track project, IAF project, IFF levy project, granny-flat notification, self-certified home, and funded social-housing project to building consent, start, completion, occupation, and net dwelling-stock addition would materially improve the ranking [Fast-track projects](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects); [Stats NZ dwelling estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).
- Post-2027 data showing councils that receive Incentives for Growth Fund payments increase completions faster than comparable councils would raise the council-incentive lever from low confidence [HUD, Going for Housing Growth, 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- Evidence that granny-flat exemptions lead to substantial notified and occupied small dwellings without quality, insurance, or infrastructure failures would raise the exemption lever from plausible to proven in the NZ context [MBIE, granny flats, 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/ready-steady-go-for-granny-flats).
- Evidence that modular/offsite procurement scales through a repeatable pipeline and creates net additional dwellings, rather than merely faster delivery of already-funded dwellings, would raise the offsite-construction lever [BRANZ, 2025](https://www.branz.co.nz/design-build/articles/can-off-site-construction-deliver-for-aotearoa-new-zealand).
- I could not verify a current public NZ completion dataset by delivery lever, a counterfactual evaluation of the Infrastructure Acceleration Fund, or a full completion audit of COVID-19 and permanent Fast-track housing projects; those gaps likely need agency data, OIA requests, or a human housing-delivery specialist.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which public datasets could be joined to create a "project approval to occupied dwelling" tracker for Fast-track, IAF/IFF, public housing, and granny-flat notifications?
- Do granny-flat exemptions produce dwellings in high-need rental markets, or mostly add family/secondary accommodation in already well-housed owner-occupier areas?
- Does the Incentives for Growth Fund change council behaviour after 1 April 2027, or mostly reward places where market demand and infrastructure capacity were already strong?
- Which social-housing procurement models deliver the lowest cost per net new occupied dwelling once demolitions, purchases, leases, and renewals are separated?
