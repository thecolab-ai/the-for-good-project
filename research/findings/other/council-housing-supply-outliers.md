# Queenstown-Lakes, Selwyn, Wellington, Hamilton, and Tauranga are the clearest housing-supply outliers on combined capacity, consent, per-capita, and growth tests

## Executive answer

- **Look hardest at Queenstown-Lakes and Selwyn for "high-output but still capacity-constrained" dynamics.** In Stats NZ's year-ended May 2026 territorial-authority consent data, Queenstown-Lakes ranked 1st on new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents and 4th on raw dwelling consents; Selwyn ranked 2nd per capita and 3rd on raw dwelling consents, but QLDC's 2025 HBA found short-term Whakatipu infrastructure constraints and the Greater Christchurch 2023 HCA found a long-term Selwyn shortfall under its assumptions [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [QLDC HBA 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/media/naadbhlm/qld_2025_hba_final_23sep25.pdf); [Greater Christchurch HCA 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/141191/RCP031-EVIDENCE-SUBMITTER-325-ROISIN-MAGEE-7-AUGUST-135-PM-Greater-Christchurch-Housing-Development-Capacity-Assessment-March-2023-v3.pdf).
- **Look hardest at Wellington City for the opposite pattern: enabled-capacity evidence but very low current consenting.** Wellington City ranked 10th by raw new dwelling consents among territorial authorities, but 56th by consents per 1,000 residents, while the Wellington regional HBA says the wider region requires over 99,000 houses by 2053 and current district plans enable over 206,000 homes [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [WRLC 2023 HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/).
- **Look hardest at Hamilton and Tauranga for "growth pressure but low per-capita consenting" dynamics.** Hamilton was one of Stats NZ's 10 territorial/local-board areas with the highest annual population growth rates in the year ended June 2025, but ranked 31st by year-ended May 2026 new dwelling consents per 1,000 residents; Tauranga ranked 29th per capita and its SmartGrowth HBA material says the western Bay of Plenty subregion has a housing supply shortfall and needs 37,000 to 43,000 homes over 30 years [Stats NZ subnational population release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/); [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [SmartGrowth HBA media release](https://www.smartgrowthbop.org.nz/articles/housing-and-business-capacity-assessment-media-release).
- **Auckland and Christchurch are high-volume, above-national-rate baselines, not the sharpest problem outliers on this screen.** Auckland ranked 1st by raw consents and 8th per capita, Christchurch ranked 2nd by raw consents and 7th per capita, and both have public HBA evidence of aggregate capacity sufficiency with important infrastructure, feasibility, or distribution caveats [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [Auckland Council HBA 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/); [Greater Christchurch HCA 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/141191/RCP031-EVIDENCE-SUBMITTER-325-ROISIN-MAGEE-7-AUGUST-135-PM-Greater-Christchurch-Housing-Development-Capacity-Assessment-March-2023-v3.pdf).
- **Do not treat current HBA sufficiency as final Housing Growth Target compliance.** HUD's current Going for Housing Growth page says new Housing Growth Targets require Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity using high population-growth projections, while older HBAs often use council-specific or medium projection assumptions and pre-date the final compliance machinery [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Auckland Council HBA 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high for the consent rankings and cited HBA statements; medium for the combined outlier classification because public data does not yet provide a single current Housing Growth Target compliance register.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

I interpreted the issue as an outlier screen over the named Tier 1 and Tier 2 urban environments: Auckland, Greater Christchurch, Wellington, Hamilton, Tauranga, and Queenstown-Lakes [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). HUD lists Auckland, Christchurch, Wellington, Tauranga, and Hamilton as Tier 1 urban environments and Queenstown as a Tier 2 urban environment under the Going for Housing Growth material [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).

The consent screen uses Stats NZ's "Building consents issued: May 2026" downloadable CSV, specifically the territorial-authority monthly "Dwelling units / New / Number" series summed from June 2025 through May 2026, and the annual territorial-authority "new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents" series for May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/) (snapshot: [Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260702024016/https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/)); [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Stats NZ's May 2026 release reports the national benchmark as 39,737 new homes consented and 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).

The population-growth signal uses Stats NZ's subnational population estimates release for the year ended June 2025; that release says national population growth slowed from 1.7 percent in the year ended June 2024 to 0.7 percent in the year ended June 2025, and its top-growth territorial/local-board chart includes Hamilton, Selwyn, Queenstown-Lakes, Waimakariri, and Waipa among the 10 areas with the highest annual growth rates [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/) (snapshot: [Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703123520/https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/)). The same Stats NZ release says territorial-authority and Auckland local-board estimates are derived by a cohort-component method from the 2023 Census base and are updated for births, deaths, and net migration [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/).

### What the screen says by test

On raw consent volume, Auckland and Christchurch dominate: Auckland consented 16,862 new dwellings and Christchurch consented 4,524 in the year ended May 2026, ahead of Selwyn at 2,314 and Queenstown-Lakes at 1,909 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). This makes Auckland and Christchurch essential baselines, but raw volume alone mostly reflects their size [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

On per-capita consenting, Queenstown-Lakes and Selwyn are the clear outliers: Queenstown-Lakes recorded 35.5 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents, Selwyn 26.4, Waimakariri 12.1, Christchurch 10.8, Auckland 9.3, Hamilton 4.6, Tauranga 4.7, and Wellington City 2.5 in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Against the national benchmark of 7.4 per 1,000 residents, Hamilton, Tauranga, Western Bay of Plenty, and Wellington City are below the national rate, while Queenstown-Lakes, Selwyn, Waimakariri, Christchurch, Auckland, Waikato District, and Waipa are above it [Stats NZ building-consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

On recent population growth, the sharpest named signals are Hamilton, Selwyn, Queenstown-Lakes, Waimakariri, and Waipa because Stats NZ includes them among the 10 territorial/local-board areas with the highest annual growth rates in the year ended June 2025 [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/). The Stats NZ chart reports population-change components for those five named areas: Hamilton gained about 1,500 people from natural increase, lost about 800 through net internal migration, and gained about 1,900 through net international migration; Selwyn gained about 690, 1,400, and lost about 10 respectively; Queenstown-Lakes gained about 450, 400, and 50 respectively; Waimakariri gained about 170, 800, and lost about 180 respectively; and Waipa gained about 170 and 480, with net international migration shown as zero [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/).

On capacity-target evidence, the key limitation is that public HBAs are not the same as a live HUD compliance register. HUD says the new Housing Growth Targets will require Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity in district plans using high population growth projections, with new tools for central government to monitor and respond to non-compliance [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme) (snapshot: [Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260618100805/https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme)). HUD also says current NPS-UD capacity must be plan-enabled and infrastructure-ready across short, medium, and long-term horizons [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).

### Place-by-place interpretation

**Queenstown-Lakes:** This is the clearest "do not be reassured by high consents alone" case. Queenstown-Lakes led every territorial authority on per-capita consenting and ranked 4th on raw consent volume in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). It also appears in Stats NZ's top-growth territorial/local-board chart for the year ended June 2025 [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/). But QLDC's 2025 HBA found "almost no capacity for further development" within modelled Whakatipu networks in the short term, producing short-term shortfalls across all Whakatipu reporting areas, while medium- and long-term sufficiency is geographically uneven and concentrated toward Frankton/Te Tapuae [QLDC HBA 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/media/naadbhlm/qld_2025_hba_final_23sep25.pdf).

**Selwyn:** Selwyn is a high-output growth outlier with a specific long-term capacity warning. It ranked 3rd nationally on raw consent volume and 2nd on consents per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Stats NZ also included Selwyn in the top-growth territorial/local-board chart for the year ended June 2025 [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/). Greater Christchurch's 2023 HCA found sufficient capacity in Selwyn for 2022-2032, but a long-term Selwyn shortfall of about 3,250 dwellings for 2022-2052 under the assessment assumptions [Greater Christchurch HCA 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/141191/RCP031-EVIDENCE-SUBMITTER-325-ROISIN-MAGEE-7-AUGUST-135-PM-Greater-Christchurch-Housing-Development-Capacity-Assessment-March-2023-v3.pdf).

**Wellington City:** Wellington is the clearest "capacity paper surplus, low consent flow" case. Wellington City ranked 56th among territorial authorities on new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026, despite ranking 10th by raw consent volume [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). The Wellington regional HBA says current district plans enable over 206,000 homes against over 99,000 houses required by 2053, but that regional statement does not explain why Wellington City's current per-capita consenting is so low [WRLC 2023 HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/). This makes Wellington a priority for the stream's next constraint question rather than a conclusion that zoning capacity has solved delivery [WRLC 2023 HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/); [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

**Hamilton:** Hamilton is a growth-pressure and near-term-serviced-land warning. Hamilton ranked 5th by raw consent volume, but only 31st by per-capita consenting, and its 4.6 new dwellings per 1,000 residents was below the 7.4 national rate in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Stats NZ includes Hamilton among the 10 highest-growth territorial/local-board areas for the year ended June 2025, while Hamilton City Council's monitoring page reports only 8.6 years of zoned and serviced housing land against 37,884 dwellings of 30-year demand [Stats NZ subnational population estimates](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/subnational-population-estimates-at-30-june-2025/); [Hamilton City Council HBA monitoring](https://hamilton.govt.nz/your-city/data-and-statistics/understanding-our-economy/housing-and-business-assessment-monitoring).

**Tauranga and Western Bay of Plenty:** The Tauranga urban environment is a shortfall case with weak current per-capita consenting. Tauranga City ranked 7th by raw consent volume but 29th per capita, and Western Bay of Plenty ranked 26th by raw volume and 28th per capita in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). SmartGrowth says the western Bay of Plenty subregion needs 37,000 to 43,000 new homes over 30 years to meet demand and describes a housing supply shortfall [SmartGrowth HBA media release](https://www.smartgrowthbop.org.nz/articles/housing-and-business-capacity-assessment-media-release). The SmartGrowth 2022 HBA says Tauranga City development capacity will be insufficient to meet long-term demand and that Western Bay of Plenty has short-term capacity but medium- and long-term insufficiency when competitiveness margins are included [SmartGrowth HBA 2022](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/639c0b75c31ac6442f8d9994/646d7825b29adbe9a0d965fb_SmartGrowth%20HBA%20Housing%20Capacity%20Assessment%202022%20pdf.pdf).

**Auckland and Christchurch:** Auckland and Christchurch should be retained as comparison cases because they are the two largest consent-volume markets, but this screen does not put them first for lagging supply. Auckland had 16,862 new dwellings consented and 9.3 per 1,000 residents, while Christchurch had 4,524 and 10.8 per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Auckland's 2023 HBA says aggregate reasonably expected-to-be-realised housing capacity is more than sufficient, but it also warns that aggregate sufficiency masks short-term feasibility and infrastructure challenges [Auckland Council HBA 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/). Greater Christchurch's 2023 HCA found Christchurch sufficient in the short-medium and long term under its assumptions [Greater Christchurch HCA 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0017/141191/RCP031-EVIDENCE-SUBMITTER-325-ROISIN-MAGEE-7-AUGUST-135-PM-Greater-Christchurch-Housing-Development-Capacity-Assessment-March-2023-v3.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public central-government compliance register applying the final Housing Growth Target method to every Tier 1 and 2 council, using high population-growth projections and feasible capacity, would change the capacity side of this screen [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- Updated HBAs or Future Development Strategies after the final GfHG rules are implemented could change the status of Auckland, Christchurch, Wellington, Hamilton, Tauranga, Selwyn, Waimakariri, and Queenstown-Lakes, especially where older assessments used medium projections or pre-2026 planning settings [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- Public territorial-authority data on completed dwellings, not just consented dwellings, could change which councils are delivery outliers because consents are an approval-flow measure rather than a completed-homes measure [Stats NZ building-consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).
- A council-level affordability and household-need overlay could move Auckland, Wellington, and Tauranga higher if low-income, renter, or social-housing need remains severe despite aggregate or consent capacity [Auckland Council HBA 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/); [SmartGrowth HBA 2022](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/639c0b75c31ac6442f8d9994/646d7825b29adbe9a0d965fb_SmartGrowth%20HBA%20Housing%20Capacity%20Assessment%202022%20pdf.pdf).
- I could not verify a single public, current, council-by-council Housing Growth Target compliance table, so the capacity status here is based on HUD's rule design plus selected council HBA/FDS evidence rather than a formal compliance finding [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- I did not interview council planners, infrastructure providers, developers, mana whenua, renters, or housing providers; a human review with local planning and lived-experience expertise could change the interpretation of whether a consent outlier is genuinely meeting local need.

## Open follow-up questions

- In Wellington City, what explains the gap between regional enabled capacity and low current per-capita consenting: infrastructure, feasibility, demand, consenting process, plan-change timing, or landowner behaviour?
- In Queenstown-Lakes and Selwyn, do high consent rates translate into completed, occupied homes that match resident household demand, or do visitor accommodation, second homes, affordability, and infrastructure timing weaken the supply effect?
- In Hamilton and Tauranga, what constraint binds first: zoned and serviced land, three-waters/transport infrastructure, development finance, plan-change timing, or construction capacity?
