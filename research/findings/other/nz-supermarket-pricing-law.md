# New Zealand supermarket pricing concerns route mainly through the Fair Trading Act, with grocery-specific monitoring supporting enforcement

## Executive answer

- The practical enforcement line is: ordinary price increases are lawful, but a shelf, checkout, online, "special", "was/now", unit-price, multibuy, or reason-for-increase representation can cross the line if it is false, misleading, likely to mislead, unsupported by evidence, or hides important conditions [Consumer Protection, "Misleading prices or advertising"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising); [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act).
- The core statute is the Fair Trading Act 1986: section 9 covers misleading or deceptive conduct in trade, section 10 covers misleading conduct about goods, section 12A covers unsubstantiated representations, section 13 covers false or misleading representations including price, and section 19 covers bait advertising [Fair Trading Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/121/en/latest/).
- Commerce Commission guidance treats discounts, "usual", "was", "normal", and "everyday" prices as legitimate promotional tools only if the comparison is clear, accurate, unambiguous, and taken from a real usual selling price, not an inflated, stale, rarely charged, or routinely discounted price [Commerce Commission, "Pricing your products or services"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/dealing-with-typical-situations/selling-goods-and-services/pricing-your-products-or-services/).
- Unit pricing is now a specific grocery consumer-information standard: qualifying grocery retailers must display unit prices clearly and legibly, in store, online, and in most advertising, with physical-store compliance from 31 August 2024 and online-store compliance from 31 August 2025 [Commerce Commission, "Information for retailers - unit pricing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/information-for-retailers-unit-pricing/); [Consumer Information Standards (Unit Pricing for Grocery Products) Regulations 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2023/0185/latest/whole.html).
- The Grocery Commissioner and Commerce Commission use the Grocery Industry Competition Act 2023 to monitor competition, publish annual grocery reports, issue disclosure standards, and enforce grocery-specific obligations, but misleading consumer-facing prices and specials remain primarily Fair Trading Act matters [Commerce Commission, "Annual Grocery Report"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/annual-grocery-report/); [Commerce Commission, Consumer Complaints Disclosure Standard decisions and reasons paper, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/consumer-complaints-disclosure-standard/Consumer-Complaints-Disclosure-Standard-Reasons-Paper-29-August-2025.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** High - the main conclusions are supported by current legislation, MBIE Consumer Protection guidance, Commerce Commission pricing and grocery guidance, and current Commission grocery reporting; individual case outcomes still need checking before publication because some proceedings remain live.

## Evidence

### Enforcement-line summary

Businesses in New Zealand are generally free to set and increase prices, but Consumer Protection says the Fair Trading Act still prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct and false representations, and a stated reason for a price increase must be true [Consumer Protection, "Misleading prices or advertising"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising). A transparency artifact should therefore avoid treating a high price or a price rise as unlawful by itself, and should instead record the representation being tested: displayed shelf price versus checkout price, online advertised price versus charged price, "special" versus prior usual price, multibuy versus single-item price, unit price display, or a stated reason for the change [Consumer Protection, "Misleading prices or advertising"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising); [Commerce Commission, "Pricing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/buying-goods-and-services/pricing/).

The main regulator desk for suspected misleading supermarket pricing is the Commerce Commission under the Fair Trading Act, because Consumer Protection says the Commission enforces the Act through compliance advice, warnings, and court action, while the Commission says it has filed or pursued supermarket pricing matters under the Act [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act); [Commerce Commission, "Criminal charges on the way for major supermarkets", 10 December 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/criminal-charges-on-the-way-for-major-supermarkets/). A consumer seeking their own remedy may also need the retailer, a refund policy, the Disputes Tribunal, or a court, because Consumer Protection says the Commission does not act on a consumer's behalf for their specific issue [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act).

Legal review is needed before any public-facing claim that a named current supermarket has breached the law, because the Fair Trading Act test depends on the overall impression and evidence at the time of representation, and the Commerce Commission's own unit-pricing guideline says only courts can decide whether the regulations have been breached [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act); [Commerce Commission, unit pricing guideline, March 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0024/347154/Unit-pricing-regulations-a-guide-for-grocery-retailers-Guideline-March-2024.pdf).

### Fair Trading Act tests relevant to supermarket prices

The Fair Trading Act 1986 is the baseline consumer-pricing law for supermarkets because the Act applies to conduct "in trade" and Consumer Protection says it applies to promotion and sale of goods from advertising and pricing through to sales techniques [Fair Trading Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/121/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act). The legally relevant provisions for supermarket pricing are section 9, which prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct; section 10, which prohibits conduct liable to mislead the public about goods; section 12A, which prohibits unsubstantiated representations; section 13, which prohibits false or misleading representations including price; and section 19, which prohibits bait advertising where advertised goods are not available in reasonable quantities for the advertised or reasonable period [Fair Trading Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/121/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act).

Consumer Protection's current public guidance maps those statutory tests to pricing examples: incorrect or misleading prices, fake discounts, misleading "was/now" promotions, hidden costs, missing important information, and false or unsubstantiated claims are all within Fair Trading Act risk [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act). The same guidance says intent is not required for a misleading-conduct problem, and fine print cannot be used to fix a misleading overall impression or hide important conditions [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act).

### Specials, was/now comparisons, and advertised-versus-charged prices

Commerce Commission business guidance says savings claims often compare a discounted price with a "usual", "was", "normal", or "everyday" price, and may mislead consumers if the business never charged that price, deliberately inflated it, commonly sold the product at many prices, or used a claimed usual price that was out of date or rarely the real selling price [Commerce Commission, "Pricing your products or services"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/dealing-with-typical-situations/selling-goods-and-services/pricing-your-products-or-services/). The same guidance states that where a business routinely sells a product at the promotional price, that promotional price becomes the usual selling price [Commerce Commission, "Pricing your products or services"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/dealing-with-typical-situations/selling-goods-and-services/pricing-your-products-or-services/).

For shelf-versus-checkout mismatches, Consumer Protection's misleading-prices page says a trader does not have to advertise or display prices, but if it does, charging more than the displayed price is likely to be a Fair Trading Act problem; its broader Fair Trading Act page adds a nuance that a genuine pricing mistake may let a trader ask for the correct amount before the sale is completed [Consumer Protection, "Misleading prices or advertising"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising); [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act). The Commerce Commission's 2020 Pak'nSave Mangere case illustrates the enforcement theory for repeated or unresolved mismatches: the store was fined after pleading guilty to false or misleading price representations where promotional prices displayed or advertised did not match till prices [Commerce Commission, "PAK'nSAVE Mangere fined $78,000 for price discrepancies", 28 October 2020](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2020/paknsave-mangere-fined-78000-for-price-discrepancies/).

Current Commission grocery enforcement uses the same line. In December 2024, the Commission announced criminal charges against Woolworths NZ, Pak'nSave Silverdale, and Pak'nSave Mill Street for alleged inaccurate pricing and misleading specials that may have breached the Fair Trading Act [Commerce Commission, "Criminal charges on the way for major supermarkets", 10 December 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/criminal-charges-on-the-way-for-major-supermarkets/). The Commission's case register for Woolworths NZ records alleged breaches of Fair Trading Act section 10 between January 2022 and September 2024, mainly where advertised prices did not match point-of-sale prices or advertised specials did not offer a genuine special [Commerce Commission, Woolworths NZ case register](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/case-register/case-register-entries/woolworths-new-zealand-limited-woolworths-nz/).

### Unit pricing

Unit pricing is governed by the Consumer Information Standards (Unit Pricing for Grocery Products) Regulations 2023, made under the Fair Trading Act consumer-information-standard framework, and the Commerce Commission says it enforces the regulations under the Fair Trading Act [Consumer Information Standards (Unit Pricing for Grocery Products) Regulations 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2023/0185/latest/whole.html); [Commerce Commission, unit pricing guideline, March 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0024/347154/Unit-pricing-regulations-a-guide-for-grocery-retailers-Guideline-March-2024.pdf). The Commission's retailer page says the regulations apply to grocery retailers with internal floor space over 1,000 square metres that sell all ten listed product categories, and to online retailers that sell all those categories [Commerce Commission, "Information for retailers - unit pricing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/information-for-retailers-unit-pricing/).

The required display rule is practical and measurable: the unit price must be clear and legible, must be at least 25% of the size of the marked price, and applies in store, online, and to advertised prices [Commerce Commission, "Information for retailers - unit pricing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/information-for-retailers-unit-pricing/); [Commerce Commission, Consumer Complaints Disclosure Standard decisions and reasons paper, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/consumer-complaints-disclosure-standard/Consumer-Complaints-Disclosure-Standard-Reasons-Paper-29-August-2025.pdf). MBIE's unit-pricing consultation material confirms the policy purpose: consistent unit pricing was designed to help consumers compare grocery prices more easily and to require clear, legible display at no less than 25% of the purchase-price font size [MBIE, draft unit-pricing consultation, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/have-your-say/seeking-feedback-on-an-exposure-draft-of-the-consumer-information-standards-unit-pricing-for-grocery-products-regulations-2023).

### Grocery Commissioner and grocery-regulation role

The Grocery Industry Competition Act 2023 has a competition-monitoring purpose rather than replacing the Fair Trading Act's consumer-pricing prohibitions: the Act's purpose is to promote competition and efficiency in the grocery industry for the long-term benefit of consumers in New Zealand [Grocery Industry Competition Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/0031/latest/LMS817122.html). The Act created the Grocery Commissioner role within the Commerce Commission, and the New Zealand Gazette records Pierre van Heerden's appointment as Grocery Commissioner for a term starting 13 July 2023 and ending 11 July 2028 [New Zealand Gazette, "Appointment of Grocery Commissioner of the Commerce Commission", 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-go3127).

The Commission says its grocery monitoring responsibilities include monitoring competition and efficiency, conducting inquiries, reviews, studies, and international benchmarking, improving transparency by publishing information, and monitoring law and practices relevant to its functions under the Act [Commerce Commission, "Annual Grocery Report"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/annual-grocery-report/). The 2025 Consumer Complaints Disclosure Standard is an example of the grocery-regulation role supporting pricing transparency: the Commission says the standard is intended to help it monitor regulated grocery retailers' price integrity, promotional errors, and unit pricing by requiring complaint and pricing-integrity refund information [Commerce Commission, Consumer Complaints Disclosure Standard decisions and reasons paper, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/consumer-complaints-disclosure-standard/Consumer-Complaints-Disclosure-Standard-Reasons-Paper-29-August-2025.pdf).

The Commission's 2026 Annual Grocery Report expressly links the two regimes: it says misleading representations about goods and services, including price, are unlawful under the Fair Trading Act; businesses need a reasonable basis for promotional claims; and regulated grocery retailers must ensure their price representations are clear, accurate, and unambiguous [Commerce Commission, Annual Grocery Report 2025, published 2 June 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-Grocery-Report-2025-2-June-2026.pdf). The same report states that grocery-sector breaches were an enforcement priority for 2024/25 and would continue to be a priority in 2025/26, including Fair Trading Act charges concerning alleged pricing inaccuracies and misleading specials [Commerce Commission, Annual Grocery Report 2025, published 2 June 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-Grocery-Report-2025-2-June-2026.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A new Fair Trading Act amendment, grocery pricing code, supermarket pricing-accuracy code, or court decision after 2 July 2026 could change the enforcement line, available penalties, or the distinction between ordinary high prices and misleading representations [Fair Trading Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/121/en/latest/); [Commerce Commission, Annual Grocery Report 2025, published 2 June 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-Grocery-Report-2025-2-June-2026.pdf).
- Final judgments or sentencing outcomes in the Pak'nSave Silverdale, Pak'nSave Mill Street, or Woolworths NZ matters could sharpen how New Zealand courts treat "special", shelf-price, and checkout-price evidence in supermarkets [Commerce Commission, "Criminal charges on the way for major supermarkets", 10 December 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/criminal-charges-on-the-way-for-major-supermarkets/); [Commerce Commission, Woolworths NZ case register](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/case-register/case-register-entries/woolworths-new-zealand-limited-woolworths-nz/).
- I could not verify the post-15 June 2026 sentencing status for the two Pak'nSave proceedings from an official Commerce Commission page; any publication that needs current litigation status should check the court record, Commission case register, or a current Commission media release before relying on the Annual Grocery Report's June 2026 wording [Commerce Commission, Annual Grocery Report 2025, published 2 June 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-Grocery-Report-2025-2-June-2026.pdf).
- A human lawyer should review any evidence template, public accusation, or regulator referral protocol that labels a named retailer's conduct as unlawful, because the line between a suspicious pricing pattern and a Fair Trading Act breach depends on representation, timing, consumer impression, evidence held by the trader, and any available defence [Fair Trading Act 1986](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1986/121/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "Fair Trading Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/fair-trading-act).
