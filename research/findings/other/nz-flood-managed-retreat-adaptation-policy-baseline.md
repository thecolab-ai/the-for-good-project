# NZ has no standing national managed-retreat or buyout law — adaptation policy runs on ad hoc, event-triggered, 50/50 central/council deals, with funding for future events explicitly undecided

## Executive answer

- **No general buyout or managed-retreat law exists.** Category 3 buyouts to date are bespoke, event-specific deals, not entitlements under a standing Act. The main post-2023 national programme (the Crown's North Island Weather Events / NIWE response) covers Category 3 voluntary buyouts in **five regions — Auckland, Hawke's Bay, Tairāwhiti, Masterton and Tauranga** — with Crown and councils sharing the buyout cost equally and all sales voluntary; a separate Nelson August 2022 Severe Weather Event (NSWE) package also provides Category 3 voluntary buyouts with Crown support. [National Infrastructure — Cyclone Recovery Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/cyclone-recovery-programme/); [Beehive, 1 Jun 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/govt-support-councils-buyout-and-better-protection-cyclone-and-flood-affected-properties); [Shape Nelson — buy-out consultation](https://shape.nelson.govt.nz/long-term-plan-2024-2034-1/key-issue-buy-out-private-properties-affected-slips); [DPMC proactive release — Nelson severe weather event](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/pr-ewr-23-sub-0076-ncc-address-severe-weather-event-aug22.pdf).
- **The one enacted planning rule is prospective only.** The National Policy Statement for Natural Hazards 2025 (in force 15 Jan 2026) requires councils to *avoid* new development where hazard risk is assessed "very high," but it does not touch existing homes or address managed retreat of already-built communities. [MfE](https://environment.govt.nz/acts-and-regulations/legislation/nps-nh/); [Wynn Williams](https://wynnwilliams.co.nz/insights/environment-planning/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-nps-nh-what-you-need-to-know/); [Southland DC](https://www.southlanddc.govt.nz/news-and-public-notices/news/the-national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-2025-came-into-effect-15-january-2026/).
- **The standalone "Climate Adaptation Act" that would have set a funding/compensation framework has not materialised.** Proposed since 2019/2020, it went through a select-committee inquiry (report Oct 2024) but by 2025 the government folded planned adaptation-planning requirements into an amendment to the existing Climate Change Response Act 2002 instead — which MfE's own page (last updated 22 Apr 2026) says the Government "intends to introduce … in 2026" — rather than passing dedicated retreat/compensation legislation. [MfE — Amending the Climate Change Response Act](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/amending-the-climate-change-response-act/); [Chapman Tripp, 6 Nov 2025](https://chapmantripp.com/trends-insights/significant-further-changes-to-new-zealand-s-climate-policy-architecture/); [MfE, National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/).
- **The government has explicitly deferred "who pays" for future retreat.** The National Adaptation Framework (released Oct 2025) sets four pillars including "cost-sharing pre- and post-event" but contains no funding mechanism or dollar commitment — reporting on its release called this out directly. [Newsroom, 16 Oct 2025](https://newsroom.co.nz/2025/10/16/long-awaited-climate-adaptation-plan-leaves-who-pays-unanswered/); [Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/our-enduring-approach-changing-climate).
- **Where buyouts have happened, the *structure* is consistent but the *offer mechanics differ by region.** Consistent across the NIWE and Nelson examples checked: buyouts are voluntary, council-led, event-specific, and funded on a roughly 50/50 (equal) central/local split of the *net* cost (agreed value less insurance/EQC proceeds), valued at a pre-event date. Category 2 properties (risk manageable at community/property level) get co-funded mitigation, not a buyout offer. But the homeowner-facing formula is **not** uniform — publicly documented offer formulas differ across the regions where councils published detailed policies: Auckland offered 95% of pre-event value for insured / 80–95% uninsured (a 5–20% homeowner haircut); Hawke's Bay offered full market value as at 13 Feb 2023 less insurance proceeds (with an EQC-equivalent land-damage deduction where the council buys uninsured land); Gisborne offered 100% of its assessed valuation but only $1,500 towards legal fees (vs up to $5,000 each for valuation and legal costs in Napier/Hastings); Nelson consulted on principles including market value before August 2022 and options for lower maximum purchase prices or uninsured-property discounts. Masterton and Tauranga are also NIWE buyout regions on the same equal cost-share/voluntary basis, but I did not locate published per-region offer formulas for them (they are much smaller programmes). Do not read any single region's contribution rates as a national rule. [Beehive, 1 Jun 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/govt-support-councils-buyout-and-better-protection-cyclone-and-flood-affected-properties); [Auckland Council](https://www.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/recovery-extreme-weather-disasters/property-categorisation-resolution/Pages/category-3-property-buyout-information-auckland-council-government.aspx); [Napier City Council — Category 3 Buy-Out](https://www.napier.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle/category-3-residential-property-purchase-policy/); [Shape Nelson — buy-out consultation](https://shape.nelson.govt.nz/long-term-plan-2024-2034-1/key-issue-buy-out-private-properties-affected-slips).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the enacted pieces (NPS-NH, Natural Hazards Insurance Act, and event-specific Category 3 buyout deals) are well documented in primary sources. The Low-confidence part is inherent to the *policy itself*, not a research gap: government has openly stated funding for future retreat is undecided, so no source can responsibly claim otherwise.

## Evidence

### 1. What is enacted today

**National Policy Statement for Natural Hazards 2025 (NPS-NH)** — gazetted 18 December 2025, in force 15 January 2026. It requires local authorities to use a standardised risk matrix (likelihood × consequence) for subdivision, use and development decisions, and to *avoid* new development assessed as "very high" risk; lower risk tiers require proportionate mitigation rather than prohibition. It is part of the Government's RMA reform "phase two," intended as interim national direction pending a full replacement system. [MfE](https://environment.govt.nz/acts-and-regulations/legislation/nps-nh/); [NZ Gazette notice](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045); [Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/updated-national-direction-natural-hazards-and-highly-productive-land); [Wynn Williams](https://wynnwilliams.co.nz/insights/environment-planning/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-nps-nh-what-you-need-to-know/). Critically, it is **forward-looking only** — it governs new consents, not what happens to the homes already sitting in mapped flood zones (nationally, ~750,000 people, or 15% of the population, are already exposed to a 1-in-100-year rainfall/river flood event on the first national flood model, rising above 900,000 with 3°C of warming), and it does not mention managed retreat as a tool for existing communities. [Wynn Williams](https://wynnwilliams.co.nz/insights/environment-planning/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards-nps-nh-what-you-need-to-know/); [RNZ, national flood data](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/577238/new-national-flood-data-reveals-the-areas-most-at-risk-now-and-in-future).

**Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023** — in force 1 July 2024, replacing the Earthquake Commission Act 1993 and rebranding EQC as the Natural Hazards Commission Toka Tū Ake. It continues Crown cover for **land** damaged by flood/storm (up to a capped contribution towards clean-up, silt removal, land scour repair) but building damage from flood/storm sits with private insurers from day one, not with the Crown scheme — a structural reason insurance retreat (covered in the sibling finding on insurance, #262) bites harder on flood risk than on earthquake risk. [Natural Hazards Commission](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/news/what-you-need-to-know-about-the-natural-hazards-insurance-act/); [NZ Legislation](https://legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/0001/latest/whole.html); [Bell Gully](https://www.bellgully.com/insights/natural-hazards-insurance-bill-passes-third-reading/) (Confidence: High — corroborated by two independent primary/professional sources).

**Category 3 land categorisation and buyouts** — this is the only precedent I found for actual government-funded managed retreat of existing homes, and it is a case-by-case administrative response, not a legislated entitlement:
- Government announced on 1 June 2023 it would support councils with voluntary buyouts for **Category 3** properties (future severe-weather risk "cannot be sufficiently mitigated" — intolerable risk to life) and co-fund protective works for **Category 2** properties (risk manageable at community/property level), covering roughly 700 Category 3 and up to 10,000 Category 2-area homes across the North Island. The exact cost-share and valuation rules were left to be worked through with councils afterwards. [Beehive, 1 Jun 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/govt-support-councils-buyout-and-better-protection-cyclone-and-flood-affected-properties).
- **The national precedent set is broader than NIWE alone.** The Crown's National Infrastructure Funding and Financing agency describes the North Island Weather Events (NIWE) response programme as providing "Category 3 Voluntary Buyouts in Auckland, Hawke's Bay, Tairāwhiti, Masterton and Tauranga" (purchase of residential properties posing intolerable risk to life), with "the Crown and councils … sharing the cost of these property buyouts equally" and "all property sales … voluntary." The same page separately lists the **Nelson August 2022 Severe Weather Event (NSWE)** package as support to Nelson City Council via Category 3 voluntary buyouts (~$6m) and Category 2 risk-mitigation projects (~$6.3m). So the documented national precedent base is at least six council/region examples: the five NIWE regions plus Nelson. [National Infrastructure — Cyclone Recovery Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/cyclone-recovery-programme/).
- **Auckland**: Category 3 buyout valued at 95% of pre-event (26 Jan 2023) market value for insured properties (net of insurance/EQC payout — a 5% homeowner contribution), 80–95% for uninsured properties, plus a $5,000 grant for professional fees. Funded from a package where central government provided just under $1.1 billion and Auckland Council around $900 million for the wider recovery, with roughly $774 million earmarked for Category 3 purchases. [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/aucklands-2b-storm-recovery-fund-under-pressure-from-more-damaged-homes-qualifying-for-a-buyout/RLBNTWLTD5BM7BD6BEMELLOL5Q/); [Auckland Council](https://www.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/recovery-extreme-weather-disasters/property-categorisation-resolution/Pages/category-3-property-buyout-information-auckland-council-government.aspx).
- **Hawke's Bay**: Hastings and Napier district councils adopted a joint Category 3 Voluntary Buy-out Policy and the accompanying Long Term Plan amendment needed to enable it, with buy-outs funded 50/50 by government and council — roughly $100 million of combined ratepayer/taxpayer money covering purchases, relocation grants and demolition. Its offer formula differs from Auckland's: rather than a fixed 95%/80–95% percentage, an offer is made at market value as at **13 February 2023 less insurance proceeds**; uninsured owners get an offer on "essentially the same basis," and where the council buys land whose damage would ordinarily have been EQC-covered, an EQC-equivalent land-damage deduction is applied. Owners are reimbursed for a registered valuation and legal advice up to $5,000 each. [Hastings DC](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/2873/hawkes-bay-councils-adopt-cyclone-gabrielle-category-3-voluntary-buy-out-policy); [Napier City Council — Category 3 Buy-Out](https://www.napier.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle/category-3-residential-property-purchase-policy/); [NZ Herald](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/hawkes-bay-today/news/cyclone-gabrielle-buyout-details-agreed-for-category-3-homes-in-hawkes-bay/3WV5TVISKRE3VIEPBGNMROM4VM/).
- **Tairāwhiti/Gisborne**: Gisborne District Council reached a cost-sharing agreement with the Crown (announced by then–Finance Minister Grant Robertson) on the same net-cost basis as Hawke's Bay — the government contributes **half the net cost of each buyout** (net = agreed value less insurance proceeds), with its Category 3 contribution expected to be **up to $15 million** within a wider Tairāwhiti package ($125m for roads, $64m for Category 2 flood protection, a $30m zero-interest 10-year Crown-facilitated loan, and a parallel pathway for whenua Māori in Category 3). Council said it offers Category 3 owners **100% of the assessed valuation** but only $1,500 towards legal fees — a thinner support package than Napier/Hastings' up-to-$5,000-each — again showing the offer mechanics are set region by region, not nationally. Council appointed valuers/negotiators and began settling properties from early 2024. [Beehive — Tairāwhiti cost-sharing](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-and-tair%C4%81whiti-agree-cost-sharing-arrangements-cyclone-affected-region); [GDC — Category 3 property land use](https://www.gdc.govt.nz/our-recovery/category-3-property-land-use); [1News, 30 Mar 2024](https://www.1news.co.nz/2024/03/30/gisborne-homeowners-feel-cornered-by-cyclone-buyout-policy/).
- **Masterton and Tauranga**: both are named NIWE Category 3 buyout regions on the same equal Crown/council cost-share, voluntary basis, but they are far smaller programmes than the three above and I did not locate published council policies setting out their per-region homeowner offer formula; they are included here for completeness of the national precedent set rather than as separately-verified mechanics. [National Infrastructure — Cyclone Recovery Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/cyclone-recovery-programme/).
- **Nelson**: the NSWE package is separate from NIWE but follows the same one-off, Crown/council-supported pattern. National Infrastructure says it includes about $6m for Category 3 voluntary buyouts and about $6.3m for Category 2 risk mitigation; DPMC's proactive release records up to $6m from the National Resilience Plan for the buyout of 14 properties as a 50% share, net of insurance and EQC payments; Nelson City Council's consultation described a one-off central-government buy-out support offer to purchase up to 14 slip-affected properties and warned that future events would depend on their own facts and circumstances. [National Infrastructure — Cyclone Recovery Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/cyclone-recovery-programme/); [DPMC proactive release — Nelson severe weather event](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/pr-ewr-23-sub-0076-ncc-address-severe-weather-event-aug22.pdf); [Shape Nelson — buy-out consultation](https://shape.nelson.govt.nz/long-term-plan-2024-2034-1/key-issue-buy-out-private-properties-affected-slips).
- **Category 2 does not get a buyout.** These properties instead face building-consent conditions and may need independent-panel assessment before new consent is granted; a subset originally labelled "2A" was later re-assessed and moved into other categories. [Central Hawke's Bay DC](https://www.chbdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents/building-consents-on-categorised-land/); [Hawke's Bay Regional Council](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1483/land-categorisations-provide-clarity-for-communities-across-hawkes-bay-).
- Category 3 land classifications themselves are being **phased out** once updated flood-hazard modelling is folded into district plans and council hazard portals — i.e., even this ad hoc categorisation tool is a transitional device, not a permanent designation. [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/580182/the-end-of-category-3-cyclone-red-zones-to-be-phased-out).

**Confidence on this section: High** — multiple official council/government sources agree on figures and mechanics.

### 2. What is in development / consulted but not yet law

**National Adaptation Framework** (released October 2025, succeeding/refocusing the 2022 National Adaptation Plan) — four pillars: (1) risk and response information sharing, (2) roles and responsibilities, (3) investment in risk reduction, (4) cost-sharing pre- and post-event; 16 initial actions to be folded into the next National Adaptation Plan update. [MfE](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/); [Beehive](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/our-enduring-approach-changing-climate). Reporting at release was blunt that the framework does **not** answer who pays for adaptation/retreat, with Minister Simon Watts saying it would be "updated as further decisions were taken on funding." [Newsroom, 16 Oct 2025](https://newsroom.co.nz/2025/10/16/long-awaited-climate-adaptation-plan-leaves-who-pays-unanswered/). This is a load-bearing claim (it's the crux of the stream's "who pays" question) and is corroborated by the government's own press release, which describes cost-sharing decisions as still to come rather than settled — two independent sources (Newsroom + Beehive) agree. **Confidence: High** that funding is undecided; the framework's substance beyond the four pillars is otherwise thin (a four-page document per contemporaneous reporting).

**New Zealand Flood Map** — MfE is building a national flood-hazard map consolidating council/regional/national data. Early access to some data is expected by end of 2026, with the first full version expected early 2027; a related NIWA Flood Hazard Viewer (national-scale, lower resolution) was released October 2025 as an interim tool. [MfE](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [RMLA update](https://www.rmla.org.nz/news/new-zealand-flood-map-update); [NIWA](https://niwa.co.nz/hazards/ma-te-haumaru-o-nga-puna-wai-o-rakaihautu-ka-ora-mo-ake-tonu). This matters for the stream because until it exists, there is no single authoritative national picture of who is exposed — regional/council hazard maps and the NIWA viewer are the best current substitutes (Confidence: Medium — timeline is from MfE and RMLA reporting, no legislation locks the date, and NZ infrastructure timelines commonly slip).

**Legislative vehicle for adaptation planning** — rather than a standalone Climate Adaptation Act, the government is implementing initial National Adaptation Framework actions via an amendment to the existing Climate Change Response Act 2002. MfE's official page on the amendment (last updated **22 April 2026**) states the Government "intends to introduce an amendment bill to the Climate Change Response Act in **2026**," and describes its adaptation content as "requiring councils to plan for how to adapt to natural hazards in the highest-priority areas," "helping people know what investment will happen," and "laying out a plan for implementation and covering how different actions will be funded over time," with accompanying regulations to ensure comprehensive options are evaluated and "generally the option most likely to minimise long-term cost is selected." [MfE — Amending the Climate Change Response Act](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/amending-the-climate-change-response-act/). MfE also published a **Policy decisions for a Climate Change Response Amendment Bill** proactive-release page on 4 June 2026; it links a package containing the Cabinet Economic Policy Committee minute, Cabinet minute of decision, the 22 September 2025 Cabinet paper, and appendices, and the amendment page says the full list of changes is in Appendix 1. [MfE — policy decisions Cabinet paper package](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/cabinet-papers-and-regulatory-impact-statements/policy-decisions-for-a-climate-change-response-amendment-bill/). Contemporaneous law-firm commentary reports this vehicle as a **Climate Change Response (Efficiency and Effectiveness) Amendment Bill** that would also let the National Adaptation Plan be amended at any time (subject to ministerial consideration of impacts on the most significant risks and on iwi/Māori). [Chapman Tripp](https://chapmantripp.com/trends-insights/significant-further-changes-to-new-zealand-s-climate-policy-architecture/); [Bell Gully](https://www.bellgully.com/insights/government-announces-changes-to-the-climate-change-response-act-releases-assessment-framework-for-carbon-removals/). Crucially, the official adaptation framing commits only to a *process* — priority-area plans that must set out "how different actions will be funded over time" and a least-long-term-cost decision rule — **not** to a standing who-pays split (Crown/council/homeowner shares) or a dollar commitment for retreat. So the "who pays" gap identified in the Framework persists as an unresolved allocation question, now routed into future priority-area plans rather than settled by a single national formula. **Confidence: Medium-High** on the bill's existence, 2026 timeline and adaptation-planning scope (now confirmed by current primary MfE sources); **Medium** on the precise statutory obligations and any funding clause, because the bill text itself is not yet introduced/published and cannot be read directly.

**Second National Climate Change Risk Assessment (NCCRA2)** — released by the Climate Change Commission on 7 May 2026, covering 37 risks across 7 domains, building on the first assessment (2020). It is explicitly designed to inform the next National Adaptation Plan, due in 2028. [MfE](https://environment.govt.nz/news/second-national-climate-change-risk-assessment-for-new-zealand/); [Climate Change Commission](https://www.climatecommission.govt.nz/reports-and-evidence/publications/2026-national-climate-change-risk-assessment/). This confirms the next major national adaptation policy update (which could include retreat funding decisions) is not due until 2028 — a two-year-plus wait from today (Confidence: High, both official sources agree).

### 3. Consultative input that has *not* been adopted as policy

**Expert Working Group on Managed Retreat** (report published August 2023, commissioned by the previous government) proposed principles for a national retreat-funding system: fairness and "compensatory, restorative and distributive justice," explicitly rejecting "preserving wealth" as an objective; financing options floated included a special levy, a dedicated fund, and/or general tax revenue, with no firm recommendation between them; it suggested local government should not bear retreat costs (while acknowledging this may not be politically feasible), and that support should distinguish owner-occupied homes from baches/rentals. [MfE report](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/report-of-the-expert-working-group-on-managed-retreat-a-proposed-system-for-te-hekenga-rauora/); [Chapman Tripp](https://chapmantripp.com/trends-insights/toward-an-agreed-managed-retreat-strategy/). **None of these recommendations have been legislated or adopted as government policy** — the National Adaptation Framework references the same open questions in 2025 without resolving them, more than two years later. **Confidence: High** that the report exists and says this (primary MfE document); **Low** confidence that any of it will become policy on a predictable timeline, because government has not committed to it.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The introduced Climate Change Response amendment bill** (which MfE's official page, updated 22 Apr 2026, says the Government intends to introduce in 2026) would let us confirm exactly what adaptation-planning/priority-area obligations it creates and whether it fixes any who-pays split. MfE confirms the bill's scope and 2026 timeline, and its 4 June 2026 proactive-release page confirms the underlying policy-decision Cabinet package is now published; but the bill text/explanatory note was not yet introduced/published at the time of this research, so the specific statutory obligations and any funding clause remain to be read against the bill itself. This is the single biggest unresolved thread for the stream. [MfE — Amending the Climate Change Response Act](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/amending-the-climate-change-response-act/); [MfE — policy decisions Cabinet paper package](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/cabinet-papers-and-regulatory-impact-statements/policy-decisions-for-a-climate-change-response-amendment-bill/).
- **Any Cabinet or Budget decision on a standing national cost-sharing formula** for future retreat/buyouts (rather than event-by-event ad hoc deals) would materially change the "no standing policy" conclusion — watch Budget announcements and Treasury's Climate Economic and Fiscal Assessment updates.
- **Confirmation of whether Category 3-style land categorisation will be replaced by an ongoing, NPS-NH-integrated hazard-risk classification** for existing (not just new) properties — current sources say Category 3 labels are being phased out as flood modelling is folded into district plans, but none of my sources confirm what replaces the *buyout trigger* itself once that happens.
- MfE's official "Amending the Climate Change Response Act" page (updated 22 Apr 2026) confirms the 2026 bill timeline and the priority-area adaptation-planning scope, and MfE's 4 June 2026 proactive release confirms the Cabinet paper package is now public. What I still could not read directly is the **introduced bill text**; a reviewer should confirm the precise priority-area obligations once the bill is introduced and check whether any funding-allocation clause goes beyond MfE's process-level "funded over time" language.
- If a future National Adaptation Plan (due 2028) or an earlier Budget sets a levy, insurance-linked fund, or explicit Crown/council/homeowner split for retreat, that would resolve the "who pays" gap this finding documents as currently open.

## Open follow-up questions

- What (if anything) does the Climate Change Response (Efficiency and Effectiveness) Amendment Bill say, once introduced, about funding local adaptation plans in "priority areas" — and does "priority area" designation carry any buyout trigger?
- How does the National Adaptation Framework's "roles and responsibilities" pillar propose to divide responsibility between central government, councils, and property owners for *non-event* (gradual, e.g. sea-level rise) retreat, as distinct from acute-event buyouts like Cyclone Gabrielle's?
