# Energy hardship (could-not-afford-to-keep-accommodation-adequately-warm): who is most affected and what changed between 2019 and 2024

## Executive answer

- MBIE’s 2024 release shows a modest fall in the self-reported warmth-affordability hardship rate from **7.6% (2019)** to **6.7% (2024)**, with an estimate around **132,000 households** in each period after rounding, and the 2024 report notes the headline change is not statistically significant. [MBIE 2024 report](https://web.archive.org/web/20260210064622/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- The highest concentrations remain structural and demographic: not-owned tenure, one-parent households, one-person households, crowded households, Māori and Pacific households. Not-owned households and the cited demographic groups improved in point estimates between 2019 and 2024 but remain materially above national mean. [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- MBIE provides no region-by-region or climate-zone breakdown for this exact indicator in the official release, and no direct disabled-household or older-alone cross-tab is available from the published tables. This creates a data gap for geographically targeted intervention design using this metric alone. [MBIE 2024 report](https://web.archive.org/web/20260210064622/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — source quality is high for MBIE micro-breakdowns and trend statements, but regional/climate concentration for this exact indicator is not released, so geographic priority inference is partially proxy-based.

## Evidence

### National trend and 2019→2024 shift
MBIE’s report and associated data release give the national headline rate for the inability to keep accommodation adequately warm as **7.6% in 2019** and **6.7% in 2024** (with approximately **132,000 households** each period on rounded values), while stating the decline is not statistically significant at headline level. [MBIE 2024 report](https://web.archive.org/web/20260210064622/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

### Where concentration remains highest in MBIE slices
The workbook indicates not-owned households are much more exposed than owners in both years (**2019: 14.6% vs 3.6%**, **2024: 12.2% vs 3.6%**). [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

Household composition remains a clear concentration axis with one-parent households and one-person households above the average in both snapshots (**17.6% → 14.7%**, **11.6% → 9.1%**). [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

Ethnicity and crowding markers remain above-average in the cited workbook rows: Māori (**13.1% → 10.1%**), Pacific (**18.0% → 15.0%**), and crowded households (**16.8% → 15.6%**); households with one-or-more-bedrooms spare are lower but still non-trivial (**5.7% → 5.3%**). [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

### Missing disaggregations requested in the issue
The 2024 MBIE materials do not provide regional or climate-zone breakdowns for this indicator and do not provide direct cross-tab intersections for disabled households or older people living alone. MBIE explicitly cites sampling-error constraints for regional reporting decisions. [MBIE 2024 report](https://web.archive.org/web/20260210064622/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

EHINZ’s 2025 damp and mould report highlights rental housing stress in Tairāwhiti and Northland, which suggests where thermal-housing risk may cluster, but it is a related housing-condition outcome and not equivalent to MBIE’s self-reported warmth affordability indicator. [EHINZ 2025 damp and mould report](https://ehinz.ac.nz/assets/Surveillance-reports/Released_2025/Dampness-and-mould-in-New-Zealand-households-a-tenure-based-analysis.pdf)

## What this means for the shift since 2019 baseline
Compared with 2019, MBIE’s 2024 snapshot suggests modest overall improvement, with persistent excess burden among non-owner tenure households, one-parent households, crowded households, and Māori/Pacific households. Given missing region/climate disaggregation and missing disabled/older-alone intersections, targeted responses from MBIE data alone should therefore prioritise vulnerable household-tenure and demographic pathways rather than named districts. [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260715043945/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 report](https://web.archive.org/web/20260210064622/https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

## What would change this conclusion
- Reliable district-level or climate-zone estimates for the same MBIE hardship question would be needed to convert this into a precise regional priority map.
- Official intersectional cross-tabs for disabled households and older people living alone would alter subgroup concentration conclusions for those groups.
- A matched regional housing-condition proxy tied to household-energy hardship metrics with aligned uncertainty bands would improve targeting, but only as a proxy unless the MBIE indicator itself is released by geography.

## Open follow-up questions
- Can MBIE or Stats NZ release a small-area (confidentiality-safe) version of this self-reported warmth affordability indicator by climate-zone or district for 2024 and 2019?
- Can official microdata access support joint cross-tabs for age alone, disability, tenure and household structure while protecting confidentiality?

## What I could not verify
- Whether regional, tenure- and climate-stratified confidence intervals exist for this exact MBIE indicator through any unpublished microdata access route.
- Whether the apparent 2019→2024 direction is robust after full harmonisation of question wording and sample weights.
- Whether specific intervention targeting by region can be inferred from MBIE microdata with acceptable sampling reliability today.
