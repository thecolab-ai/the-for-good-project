# Energy hardship (could-not-afford-to-keep-accommodation-adequately-warm): who is most affected and what changed between 2019 and 2024

## Executive answer

- MBIE's 2024 release shows a modest fall in the self-reported warmth-affordability hardship rate from **7.6% in 2019** to **6.7% in 2024**, with about **132,000 households** affected in 2024; MBIE says this 2019 to 2024 change is not statistically significant. The same report also says the rise from **5.3% in 2021** to **6.7% in 2024** is statistically significant, so the recent movement is deterioration rather than improvement. This is a single-source-to-MBIE claim: the report and workbook are separate artifacts, but both come from the same MBIE release family and customised Stats NZ HES extract. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- Within MBIE's published 2024 tables for this exact indicator, the largest subgroup rates are for income adequacy and material hardship: households saying income was "not enough" were at **30.9%**, and households in DEP-17 material hardship were at **39.8%**. Tenure, composition, crowding, and ethnicity still show above-average rates, but they are not the highest published MBIE slices for this indicator. This relative-ranking claim is also single-source-to-MBIE because no independent source located for this finding republishes the same 2024 subgroup table. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- MBIE does not publish region-by-region or climate-zone breakdowns for this indicator, and it does not publish direct disabled-household or older-alone cross-tabs in the release. MBIE says it has not analysed regional breakdowns because some measures may have higher sampling errors and less dependable regional results. This is a claim about the MBIE release, not proof that no unpublished or independently modelled estimate exists elsewhere. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE reporting page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/reporting-on-energy-hardship-measures)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - source quality is high for MBIE's national and published subgroup estimates, but several load-bearing claims are single-source-to-MBIE rather than independently corroborated, and the requested regional/climate, disabled-household, and older-alone breakdowns are not published for this exact indicator.

## Evidence

### National trend and 2019 to 2024 shift

MBIE reports that **6.7%**, or **132,000 households**, could not afford to keep their homes adequately warm in the year ended June 2024. MBIE states that this is down **0.9 percentage points** from the year ended June 2019, but that the change is not statistically significant. MBIE also states that the rate increased from **5.3% in 2021** to **6.7% in 2024**, and that this 2021 to 2024 increase is statistically significant. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

The 2024 workbook gives the same national series for this indicator: **7.6% in 2019**, **6.6% in 2020**, **5.3% in 2021**, **5.8% in 2022**, **5.9% in 2023**, and **6.7% in 2024**. The workbook also gives the rounded affected-household count as about **132,000** in both 2019 and 2024, so the rate fell while the count did not materially move after rounding. [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

### Highest published concentration axes: income adequacy and material hardship

For 2024, the workbook shows much higher warmth-affordability hardship among households that reported not having enough income for everyday needs (**30.9%**) than the national average (**6.7%**). MBIE's report separately describes households with not enough income as three to five times more likely than the average household to report energy-hardship measures, and says almost a third of households with not enough income could not afford to keep accommodation adequately warm. The report and workbook corroborate each other internally, but they are not independent sources under the project method because both are MBIE artifacts from the same release family. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

The workbook's DEP-17 material-hardship slice is higher again: households with DEP-17 material hardship were at **39.8%** for this indicator in 2024. MBIE's report says households with a DEP-17 score of 6 or more are four to seven times more likely than the general population to experience energy-hardship measures, and that the 2019 to 2024 decreases for "could not afford to keep accommodation adequately warm" and "put up with feeling cold a lot" among households in material hardship were not statistically significant. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

### Other above-average MBIE slices

Tenure remains important but is not the strongest published concentration axis for this indicator. Not-owned households were at **14.6% in 2019** and **12.2% in 2024**, compared with owned households at **3.6%** in both years. MBIE says renters were three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy-hardship measures in 2024. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

Household composition remains an above-average marker: one-parent households moved from **17.6% in 2019** to **14.7% in 2024**, and one-person households moved from **11.6%** to **9.2%**. MBIE's report says one-parent families show relatively higher rates of energy hardship. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

Ethnicity and crowding also remain above average in the workbook rows for this exact indicator: Maori households moved from **12.3% in 2019** to **10.1% in 2024**, Pacific households moved from **18.0%** to **15.0%**, and crowded households moved from **18.1%** to **15.6%**. MBIE's 2022 report appendix independently identifies the correct 2019 row for "cannot afford to keep accommodation adequately warm" as Maori **12.3%**, Pacific **18.0%**, and crowded households about **18%**, which is why the higher 2019 values for "put up with feeling cold a lot" should not be used for this indicator. [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2022 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31653-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2022)

### Missing disaggregations requested in the issue

MBIE's 2024 report says it has not analysed regional breakdowns because some energy-hardship measures may have higher sampling errors and less dependable regional results. The MBIE reporting page describes the release as national-level and key-demographic-group reporting, and the workbook does not provide region or climate-zone tabs for this indicator. These are all MBIE-release-family artifacts, so this supports a bounded conclusion about what MBIE published rather than an independently corroborated conclusion about all possible regional evidence. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE reporting page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/reporting-on-energy-hardship-measures), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

EHINZ's 2025 damp and mould report supports a related housing-condition proxy rather than the MBIE warmth-affordability indicator itself: it reports that rental households were more than twice as likely as owner-occupied households to report dampness and mould in the 2023 Census, and that Tairawhiti and Northland had high damp/mould rates among rental households. That helps identify places where housing-condition risk may cluster, but it cannot substitute for an MBIE regional breakdown of self-reported ability to afford warmth. [EHINZ 2025 damp and mould report](https://ehinz.ac.nz/assets/Surveillance-reports/Released_2025/Dampness-and-mould-in-New-Zealand-households-a-tenure-based-analysis.pdf)

## What this means for the shift since 2019 baseline

Compared with 2019, MBIE's 2024 snapshot suggests a small, statistically non-significant national improvement for inability to afford adequate warmth. Compared with 2021, however, MBIE reports a statistically significant increase from **5.3%** to **6.7%**, so the recent signal is worsening. The highest published 2024 rates for this exact indicator are among households already reporting income inadequacy or DEP-17 material hardship, while renters, one-parent households, one-person households, crowded households, Maori households, and Pacific households remain above the national mean. Treat these as official MBIE findings rather than independently corroborated subgroup estimates. [MBIE 2024 report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

For targeting, the published MBIE data supports income/material-hardship prioritisation first, with tenure, household composition, crowding, and ethnicity as important secondary design variables. Because that prioritisation rests on one official MBIE release family, it should be treated as a good working hypothesis rather than independently corroborated targeting evidence. The MBIE release does not support naming specific regions or climate zones for this indicator without additional data, though EHINZ's Census-based damp/mould analysis may be useful as a separate housing-quality proxy. [MBIE 2024 workbook](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31652-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [EHINZ 2025 damp and mould report](https://ehinz.ac.nz/assets/Surveillance-reports/Released_2025/Dampness-and-mould-in-New-Zealand-households-a-tenure-based-analysis.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- Reliable district-level or climate-zone estimates for the same MBIE hardship question would be needed to convert this into a precise regional priority map.
- Official intersectional cross-tabs for disabled households and older people living alone would alter subgroup concentration conclusions for those groups.
- A matched regional housing-condition proxy tied to household-energy hardship metrics with aligned uncertainty bands would improve targeting, but only as a proxy unless the MBIE indicator itself is released by geography.
- Direct access to HES microdata, with Stats NZ-approved confidentiality controls and proper confidence intervals, could test whether income/material hardship remains the strongest axis after crossing with tenure, ethnicity, household composition, age, disability, and region.

## Open follow-up questions

- Can MBIE or Stats NZ release a small-area, confidentiality-safe version of this self-reported warmth-affordability indicator by climate zone or district for 2024 and 2019?
- Can official microdata access support joint cross-tabs for age alone, disability, tenure, income adequacy, DEP-17 material hardship, and household structure while protecting confidentiality?
- Where the MBIE indicator cannot be regionally released, which official housing-condition proxies have enough regional precision to guide outreach without overstating energy-hardship prevalence?
