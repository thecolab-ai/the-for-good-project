# NZ small-credit transparency should be framed as a total-cost help front door, not a flat fee comparison

> **Scope note:** this is the stream-framing output for Discover root [#60](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/60) — a Discover-stage framing memo that maps the landscape and fans out the stream's research children, following the same pattern as the merged stream #61 framing. It is not a leaf research finding; the evidence bearing on each fanned-out question lives in the children (#67–#71).

## Executive answer

- The core problem is real enough to research as a stream: small consumer credit in New Zealand spans personal loans, overdrafts, credit cards, BNPL, and high-cost credit, and the law treats those products differently enough that a plain fee table would risk misleading people [Consumer Protection](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [MBIE](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later), [FMA changes to credit laws](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/consumer-credit/changes-to-credit-laws/).
- The right framing is "total cost of credit for realistic scenarios, plus eligibility and risk notes, plus a route to human help", because the CCCFA covers lender disclosure, responsible lending, reasonable fees, and high-cost-credit limits, while MoneyTalks is a public financial-mentoring front door operated by FinCap and funded by MSD through its Building Financial Capability work [Consumer Protection](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [FMA changes to credit laws](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/consumer-credit/changes-to-credit-laws/), [MSD](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html), [FinCap about MoneyTalks](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks/about).
- The regulatory landscape changed on 1 July 2026, the day before this stream was framed: the FMA took over regulatory responsibility for the CCCFA from the Commerce Commission, becoming the single conduct regulator for financial markets including consumer credit [FMA CCCFA](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/legislation/cccfa/), [Commerce Commission transfer notice](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/transfer-of-responsibility-for-regulation-of-consumer-credit/). Any regulator-facing output from this stream now has one primary desk: the FMA.
- Regulator-facing context (not an endorsement of this tool type): MBIE records the Commerce Commission's 2024 personal-banking market study as finding that New Zealand's four largest banks do not face strong competition in personal banking services and as making 14 recommendations to promote competition [MBIE banking study](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/competition-regulation-and-policy/market-studies/competition-study-into-personal-banking-services). That study is about competition between large banks, not about consumer credit comparison tools — it establishes that competition and consumer information are active regulatory concerns, but no regulator has specifically called for the artifact this stream contemplates. CoFI fair conduct programmes are obligations on financial institutions, cited here only as context for how conduct regulation frames consumer treatment [FMA CoFI report](https://www.fma.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Fair-conduct-programme-insights-report.pdf).
- The stream should fan out before ideation: I opened five research children on total-cost scenarios, disclosure comparability, regulatory/advice boundaries, vulnerable-borrower impacts, and data/referral requirements [#67](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/67), [#68](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/68), [#69](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/69), [#70](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/70), [#71](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/71).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the regulatory ownership and public-source landscape are clear from official sources, but this stream-framing memo does not yet calculate product-level total costs, does not validate user needs with borrowers, financial mentors, lenders, or regulators, and rests on an unvalidated demand assumption (see "What would change this conclusion").

## Evidence

### Problem statement and affected users

The discover issue frames the problem as the difficulty of seeing the all-in cost of small borrowing across personal loans, overdrafts, credit cards, BNPL, and high-cost or short-term loans, with particular risk for lower-income households and people under financial pressure [issue #60](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/60). Stats NZ reported that 9.1 percent of people lived in households in material hardship in the year ended June 2025, and its companion child-poverty release for the same period reported 14.3 percent of children living in households in material hardship, which supports prioritising clarity for people with little room to absorb extra borrowing costs [Stats NZ household income](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/), [Stats NZ child poverty](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). Both figures come from the same Stats NZ survey programme, so this is a single-origin official statistic rather than two independently verified numbers; that is flagged in the load-bearing claims table below. MoneyTalks is funded through Building Financial Capability to provide free advice to individuals and whanau who want to improve financial wellbeing, and MSD says trained financial mentors can connect people with local BFC providers [MSD](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html); FinCap's own MoneyTalks page confirms the service is "operated by FinCap and funded by the Ministry of Social Development (MSD)" [FinCap about MoneyTalks](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks/about).

### Product scope

Consumer Protection says CCCFA responsible-lending rules apply to providers of mortgages, loans, agreed overdrafts, BNPL accounts, buying on credit, truck shops, consumer leases, and home buy-backs, and that responsible lending covers advertising, loan agreements, affordability assessments, and limits on interest and fees [Consumer Protection](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). RBNZ publishes lending and monetary statistics that include bank and non-bank sector lending, credit-card balances and average interest rates, credit-card spending, and credit conditions, so aggregate official data exists for some parts of the small-credit landscape but not for every product-level fee or eligibility rule [RBNZ](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/statistics/series/lending-and-monetary).

BNPL needs separate treatment from ordinary interest-bearing loans because MBIE says BNPL lets consumers access goods or services now and pay later in interest-free instalments, and because BNPL providers were not automatically required to comply with CCCFA rules before the 2024 changes when they did not charge interest, credit fees, or take a security interest [MBIE](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later). The FMA's credit-law timeline confirms that BNPL loans were declared consumer credit contracts from 2 September 2024, making BNPL lenders subject to creditor obligations under the CCCFA subject to some exemptions [FMA changes to credit laws](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/consumer-credit/changes-to-credit-laws/).

High-cost credit also needs separate treatment because the CCCFA itself caps the recoverable rate of charge under a high-cost consumer credit contract at 0.8 percent per day (s 45H), limits total recoverable costs of borrowing to the amount of the first advance (s 45E), prohibits compound interest (s 45I), and presumes default fees above the prescribed amount — $30 if no regulations set another figure — to be unreasonable unless the lender proves otherwise (s 45J) [CCCFA s 45H rate cap](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/#LMS352922), [CCCFA s 45E total cost cap](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/#LMS347730), [CCCFA s 45J default fees](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/#LMS347744). The FMA's credit-law timeline summarises the same high-cost rules: a 0.8 percent per day rate cap, a total cost cap of 100 percent of the first advance, a compound-interest ban, and default fees exceeding $30 considered unreasonable unless the lender can prove otherwise [FMA changes to credit laws](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/consumer-credit/changes-to-credit-laws/).

### Current information map

The public information landscape appears to have at least four layers that need separate provenance: regulator and consumer-protection guidance, statutory rules, lender-owned product pages or disclosure documents, and third-party calculators or comparators. Consumer Protection summarises lender duties for borrowers, NZ Legislation publishes the CCCFA text, and RBNZ publishes aggregate credit statistics [Consumer Protection](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [NZ Legislation](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/), [RBNZ](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/statistics/series/lending-and-monetary). Lender-owned examples show that rates, fees, eligibility, and terms can sit in different places: ASB's personal-loan page gives an example total interest cost and says assessment considers regular income, account operation, and credit history, while ANZ's personal-loan rates page states a fixed-rate range, default-rate treatment, and rate-change caveats [ASB](https://www.asb.co.nz/personal-loans), [ANZ](https://www.anz.co.nz/rates-fees-agreements/personal-loans/).

Public education and comparison material already exists: Sorted's personal-loans guide tells users to add up total costs and notes that many personal loans have establishment or documentation fees [Sorted](https://sorted.org.nz/guides/tackling-debt/personal-loans/), and commercial comparison sites (MoneyHub's personal-loans guide is one example, treated here as a lead rather than a formal source because it is an affiliate-revenue commercial site) list rates and fees across lenders. Whether this existing material is *sufficient* for the target cohort is a hypothesis inherited from the parent issue's scoping steer, not something this memo has evidence for: the strongest counter-hypothesis to this stream is that Sorted plus commercial comparators already serve the need, and the gap is distribution or trust rather than a missing artifact. Documenting specifically where (and whether) existing tools fail money-tight borrowers is assigned to [#70](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/70), and demand-side evidence on whether this cohort would use any comparison tool before borrowing is assigned to [#71](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/71) and [#69](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/69).

### Regulatory desks and product guardrails

The primary regulatory desk changed on 1 July 2026. From that date the FMA holds regulatory responsibility for the CCCFA — disclosure, responsible lending, fees, and high-cost-credit rules — becoming the single conduct regulator for financial markets, including consumer credit; the transfer was enacted under the Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Amendment Act 2026, and lender certification has been replaced by market-services licensing under the Financial Markets Conduct Act [FMA CCCFA](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/legislation/cccfa/), [Commerce Commission transfer notice](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/transfer-of-responsibility-for-regulation-of-consumer-credit/). The Commerce Commission's role in consumer credit is now historical: it administered the CCCFA up to 30 June 2026 (including the credit-law changes summarised above) and ran the 2024 personal-banking market study, and its website now directs credit queries to the FMA [Commerce Commission transfer notice](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/transfer-of-responsibility-for-regulation-of-consumer-credit/). FMA was already the conduct desk for CoFI: its fair conduct programme report says CoFI requires financial institutions to be licensed by FMA and to establish, implement, maintain, and comply with an effective fair conduct programme designed to ensure compliance with the fair conduct principle [FMA CoFI report](https://www.fma.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Fair-conduct-programme-insights-report.pdf). MBIE remains the policy desk because it publishes consumer-credit policy material including the BNPL reforms and the personal-banking market-study government response page [MBIE BNPL](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later), [MBIE banking study](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/competition-regulation-and-policy/market-studies/competition-study-into-personal-banking-services). This consolidation materially simplifies the stream's regulator-facing framing — CCCFA, CoFI, and the financial-advice regime now sit with one regulator — and child issue [#68](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/68) must work from the post-transfer allocation.

The proposed AI "reality-check" layer must stay on the information-and-education side until a qualified human confirms the advice boundary. The Financial Markets Conduct Act 2013 provides that a person must not act as a provider of a financial advice service without holding, or being authorised under, a market services licence covering that service (s 388(ba)) [FMC Act s 388](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2013/69/en/latest/#DLM4091564). FMA guidance says the same for its licensing regime: a person providing financial advice to retail clients must hold a FAP licence or be an authorised body under someone else's licence, financial advisers must be registered, and nominated representatives have less discretion and can provide advice only under provider processes and controls [FMA FAP](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/financial-advice-provider/), [FMA adviser](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/financial-adviser/).

## What would change this conclusion

- This framing would weaken if child research finds that product-level public disclosures are too unstable, too licence-restricted, or too non-standardised to support defensible total-cost scenarios.
- This framing would change if the FMA, MBIE, a qualified NZ financial-services lawyer, or a licensed FAP concludes that the intended reality-check layer cannot be safely delivered as general information and education.
- This framing would change if financial mentors or hardship-service providers say a comparison front door would distract from better first-line interventions, or if they do not want referral/warm-handoff integration.
- **Unvalidated demand assumption:** this memo cites no behavioural evidence that money-tight borrowers would consult a transparency tool *before* borrowing rather than after; people in acute hardship often borrow out of necessity, not after comparison-shopping. If [#71](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/71) or NFP co-design under [#69](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/69) finds the cohort would not use a pre-borrowing front door, the framing fails regardless of how good the total-cost data is.
- **Existing-tools counter-hypothesis:** if [#70](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/70) finds that Sorted, commercial comparators (MoneyHub, Canstar, interest.co.nz), or lender disclosure already adequately serve the target cohort, the case for a new artifact weakens to a distribution/trust problem rather than an information-gap problem.
- I could not verify current product-level costs across enough lenders to recommend any named comparison set; that belongs in [#67](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/67) and [#70](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/70).
- I did not contact regulators, lenders, borrowers, MoneyTalks, FinCap, or other NFPs; human validation is required before the stream moves from research into ideation.
