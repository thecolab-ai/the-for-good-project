# Rising NZ business insolvencies are a real, primary-data-backed problem, but the sector, creditor, and support-gap story needs targeted follow-up research

This is the **discover baseline for Stream #257**: it verifies the starting trend, identifies the aggregate data surfaces that can answer the deeper questions, and fans out the work. It deliberately does not name any failed company, director, creditor, or employee.

## Executive answer

- **The trend is real.** Companies Office annual statistics show company liquidations rose from **1,379 in 2021** to **1,823 in 2023**, **2,502 in 2024**, and **2,867 in 2025**, making 2025 the highest calendar-year liquidation count since **2010**; the same official page says the monthly file was last updated **3 July 2026** and covers liquidations, receiverships, and voluntary administrations. [Companies Office annual CSV](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/assets/companies-office/company-statistics/yearly-company-statistics-since-2001.csv); [Companies Office latest company statistics](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/insights-and-articles/latest-company-statistics/)
- **2026 is still elevated, but the month-on-month wording needs care.** The Companies Office monthly CSV shows **1,411 liquidations in January-June 2026**, compared with **1,296** in January-June 2025, **1,131** in January-June 2024, and **837** in January-June 2023; however, monthly liquidations dipped from March 2026 to April 2026 before rising again in May and June, so the safe claim is "year-to-date remains above recent years," not "every month rises." [Companies Office monthly CSV](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/assets/companies-office/company-statistics/monthly-company-statistics-since-2001.csv)
- **The analysis is tractable without publishing personal data.** Companies Office bulk data includes `companies_insolvency.csv` for all companies with an insolvency practitioner appointed, plus joinable public company files for BIC industry classification, core company status, and addresses; the work can aggregate by month, appointment type, industry, and broad region while suppressing individual businesses. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/)
- **The likely concentration story is plausible but not yet primary-verified.** Deloitte's Q1 2026 analysis, drawing on Companies Office data, says Q1 2026 had **709** formal company insolvencies, **94.4%** of them liquidations, with Auckland at **55.7%** of the total and construction the largest distressed sector across Auckland, Canterbury, Wellington, and Waikato; this is useful framing but should be reproduced from official bulk data before being treated as the stream's finding. [Deloitte Q1 2026 insolvency trends](https://www.deloitte.com/content/dam/assets-zone1/nz/en/docs/services/consulting/2026/2026-q1-new-zealand-insolvency-trends.pdf)
- **The support gap is the most important unanswered question.** Existing public help points are real: IRD offers instalment arrangements for tax debt, the Insolvency and Trustee Service explains sole-trader/partnership/company debt routes, Companies Register guidance explains liquidation/receivership/voluntary administration, and MBIE/RBP channels offer business advice; what is not yet evidenced is whether a struggling small operator can find early, independent, affordable restructuring help before liquidation. [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir); [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [Companies Register voluntary administration](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/when-your-company-fails/what-happens-during-voluntary-administration/); [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business); [Regional Business Partner Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the headline trend and data sources are strong and official; the driver, sector/region concentration, creditor-loss, and support-gap claims are plausible but need the child research to reproduce the figures and separate correlation from causation.

## Evidence

### The starting trend verifies against official Companies Office data

The Companies Office publishes monthly, quarterly, and annual company statistics, and its current statistics page says the **monthly data file as at 30 June 2026** provides counts of company incorporations, removals, liquidations, receiverships, and voluntary administrations; the page was last updated **3 July 2026**. [Companies Office latest company statistics](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/insights-and-articles/latest-company-statistics/)

The annual CSV shows liquidations fell to **1,379** in 2021, then rose to **1,550** in 2022, **1,823** in 2023, **2,502** in 2024, and **2,867** in 2025; the same file shows the last higher annual count was **3,024** in 2010. [Companies Office annual CSV](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/assets/companies-office/company-statistics/yearly-company-statistics-since-2001.csv) Receiverships rose from **65** in 2022 to **102** in 2023 and **185** in 2024, then eased to **171** in 2025; voluntary administrations were **31** in 2022, **43** in 2023, **73** in 2024, and **42** in 2025. [Companies Office annual CSV](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/assets/companies-office/company-statistics/yearly-company-statistics-since-2001.csv)

For 2026 year-to-date, the monthly CSV shows January-June liquidations of **1,411**, compared with **1,296** in the same months of 2025, **1,131** in 2024, and **837** in 2023. [Companies Office monthly CSV](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/assets/companies-office/company-statistics/monthly-company-statistics-since-2001.csv) The June 2026 Companies Office commentary reports **259** liquidations, **19** receiverships, and **3** voluntary administrations for that month. [Companies Office latest company statistics](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/insights-and-articles/latest-company-statistics/)

The RNZ story that prompted this stream says insolvency numbers "continue to track up" and reports commentary from economists and Centrix about sector stress and liquidation lag; because RNZ's article is secondary reporting, this framing uses it only as context and uses Companies Office data for the trend. [RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657587/why-business-failures-may-be-a-good-thing)

**Confidence: High** on the official counts; **Medium** on current interpretation because 2026 is incomplete and historical Companies Office statistics are explicitly subject to change when register records change. [Companies Office latest company statistics](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/insights-and-articles/latest-company-statistics/)

### The right primary-data path is aggregate joins, not named cases

The Companies Office bulk data guide says `companies_insolvency.csv` contains all companies that have had an insolvency practitioner appointed for liquidation, receivership, or voluntary administration, with fields including NZBN, entity name, insolvency start date, insolvency type, appointment type, appointee, appointment date, and appointment-vacated date. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/)

The same bulk-data guide lists `companies_business_industry_classification.csv` for BIC industry codes, `companies_core_data.csv` for registration/removal/status data, and public/registered-address files that can support broad regional aggregation. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/) This makes the trend, appointment-type, broad-sector, and broad-region questions researchable from public data, but child work must suppress or drop entity names, appointee names, street addresses, and any small cells that could identify a business. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/)

Stats NZ business demography is the better denominator for "who is exposed": the February 2025 release says New Zealand had **617,330 enterprises**, up **0.5%** from February 2024, and DataInfo+ says business demography covers enterprise/geographic-unit counts by industry, region, business type, births/deaths, survival rates, and employment levels. [Stats NZ business demography 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/new-zealand-business-demography-statistics-at-february-2025/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+ business demography](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/bdb02aa2-866e-418f-83e8-342234867a0f)

Stats NZ also warns that fine-level regional and industry business-demography data needs caution because the Statistical Business Register is not designed for high-quality fine regional/industry estimates and timing lags can affect small and medium-sized enterprises. [Stats NZ DataInfo+ business demography](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/bdb02aa2-866e-418f-83e8-342234867a0f) That warning matters here: a sector/region child should aggregate enough to avoid both data-quality overclaiming and identification risk. [Stats NZ DataInfo+ business demography](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/bdb02aa2-866e-418f-83e8-342234867a0f)

**Confidence: High** that the public data can answer aggregate trend and concentration questions; **Medium** on business-size analysis because the public Companies Office/NZBN data does not obviously expose employee-count size at entity level, so size may need aggregate Stats NZ denominators or a proxy rather than a company-by-company join. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+ business demography](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/bdb02aa2-866e-418f-83e8-342234867a0f)

### Sector and region signals exist, but should be reproduced from primary data

Deloitte's Q1 2026 report says formal insolvency activity remained elevated in Q1 2026 and "may be approaching a cyclical peak"; it reports **709** formal company insolvencies in the quarter, **94.4%** of them liquidations, and says Auckland accounted for **55.7%**, Canterbury **9.4%**, Wellington **8.2%**, and Waikato **7.9%**. [Deloitte Q1 2026 insolvency trends](https://www.deloitte.com/content/dam/assets-zone1/nz/en/docs/services/consulting/2026/2026-q1-new-zealand-insolvency-trends.pdf)

Deloitte also reports construction as the most distressed sector across the four largest regions in its Q1 2026 cut, with construction making up **24.8%** of insolvencies in Auckland, **37.3%** in Canterbury, **37.9%** in Wellington, and **25.0%** in Waikato; it reports accommodation and food services at **12.2%** in Auckland, **9.0%** in Canterbury, **17.2%** in Wellington, and **14.3%** in Waikato. [Deloitte Q1 2026 insolvency trends](https://www.deloitte.com/content/dam/assets-zone1/nz/en/docs/services/consulting/2026/2026-q1-new-zealand-insolvency-trends.pdf)

Those sector/region numbers are useful enough to justify a child research issue, but they are **not** yet this stream's finding because they come from a professional-services report rather than a reproduced aggregate extract. [Deloitte Q1 2026 insolvency trends](https://www.deloitte.com/content/dam/assets-zone1/nz/en/docs/services/consulting/2026/2026-q1-new-zealand-insolvency-trends.pdf) The child should reproduce them from Companies Office/NZBN/BIC/address bulk files and then normalize against Stats NZ enterprise counts where possible. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/); [Stats NZ business demography 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/new-zealand-business-demography-statistics-at-february-2025/)

**Confidence: Medium** — Deloitte is credible and explicitly cites Companies Office data, but the stream should not rely on an unreproduced secondary cut for its core concentration claims.

### The "why now" story is probably a mix of lagged economic stress and renewed tax enforcement

Stats NZ's latest GDP skill output for the March 2026 quarter shows production GDP rose **0.8%** from the previous quarter and **1.5%** from the same quarter a year earlier; that supports a cautious "weak recovery" context, not a claim that macro conditions alone are causing insolvencies. [Stats NZ GDP March 2026 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Gross-domestic-product/Gross-domestic-product-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/gross-domestic-product-march-2026-quarter.csv)

Stats NZ's CPI data shows all-groups CPI was **3.1%** higher annually in the March 2026 quarter and **0.9%** higher than the previous quarter; inflation and interest-rate pressure are plausible background conditions, but this discover pass did not prove a causal link from CPI to liquidation appointments. [Stats NZ CPI March 2026 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Consumers-price-index/Consumers-price-index-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/consumers-price-index-march-2026-quarter-index-numbers.csv)

IRD is a stronger source for one driver: its tax-debt statistics say total tax and entitlement debt increased from **$7.9b in June 2024** to **$9.3b in June 2025**, with GST debt rising from **$2.8b** to **$3.3b**, employer-activities debt rising from **$1.5b** to **$2.0b**, and customers with tax debt rising from **512,000** to **527,000**. [IRD tax and entitlement debt statistics](https://www.ird.govt.nz/about-us/tax-statistics/tax-and-entitlement-debt-statistics)

IRD's 2025 annual-report page says it collected **$4.3b** in overdue tax debt and that more than **129,000** businesses had received Small Business Cashflow Scheme loans totalling **$2.4b**, with nearly **28,000** businesses in default or missing repayments at **30 June 2025**. [IRD annual report 2025: overdue debt collected](https://www.ird.govt.nz/about-us/publications/annual-corporate-reports/annual-report/annual-report-2025/assessment-of-our-operations/4-3-billion-in-overdue-tax-debt-collected)

IRD's April 2026 tax-agent debt webinar says the team handling highest-risk, complex debt was actively managing about **2,000** cases worth more than **$600m**, that around a quarter were in some stage of legal action, and that a trial of increased credit reporting had warned **60** companies, with more than half reported and more than half in liquidation proceedings. [IRD tax-agent debt webinar April 2026 PDF](https://www.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/home/documents/no-number/presentations-and-webinar-pdfs/tax-agent-webinar-on-debt-april-2026.pdf?modified=20260420234851) IRD separately says a business can be credit-reported when unpaid tax is over **$150,000** or has been unpaid for a year and equals at least **30%** of the company's assessable income, after reasonable collection efforts and formal 30-day notification. [IRD unpaid-tax credit reporting update](https://www.ird.govt.nz/updates/news-folder/2025/changes-to-sharing-information-about-unpaid-tax)

**Confidence: Medium** — IRD enforcement and tax debt are clearly relevant and official, but the stream still needs to separate "IRD activity coincides with more liquidations" from "IRD caused the rise" by checking appointment types, petitioning-creditor indicators if available, and practitioner reports.

### The human and creditor impact is real, but aggregate loss data is the hard part

Companies Register guidance says liquidation closes a company down, a liquidator is appointed by court order or creditor resolution at a watershed meeting, and the liquidator takes control of unsecured assets to repay creditors and shareholders. [Companies Register liquidation guide](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/when-your-company-fails/what-happens-during-liquidation/)

The Insolvency and Trustee Service says liquidators must report to creditors on the company's financial position within **25 working days** from liquidation, or within **5 working days** in a voluntary liquidation, and then send six-monthly reports and a final report. [ITS effect of liquidation](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/the-effect-of-liquidation-on-a-company) That means creditor-impact research may need sampled liquidator reports, but any extraction must remain aggregate and avoid publishing business, director, employee, or creditor identities. [ITS effect of liquidation](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/the-effect-of-liquidation-on-a-company)

Employment New Zealand says employees may lose their job if the business stops trading, may transfer to a new employer if the business keeps trading, and must file claims with the liquidator for money owed by a company employer. [Employment NZ liquidation/bankruptcy guide](https://www.employment.govt.nz/fair-work-practices/restructuring-and-workplace-change/when-your-employer-goes-into-liquidation-or-bankruptcy)

New Zealand legislation and official policy material confirm that employee claims are a preferential-creditor issue, but not a guarantee of full payment: MBIE's 2024 proactive-release paper says Schedule 7 of the Companies Act 1993 and section 274 of the Insolvency Act 2006 define distribution priority and include creditors with preference over ordinary unsecured creditors. [MBIE employee preference cap paper](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29638-adjustments-to-the-employee-preference-cap-under-insolvency-law-proactiverelease-pdf) Employment NZ says an employee who is owed money by a liquidated company must file a claim with the liquidator, which indicates the practical pathway but not expected recovery. [Employment NZ liquidation/bankruptcy guide](https://www.employment.govt.nz/fair-work-practices/restructuring-and-workplace-change/when-your-employer-goes-into-liquidation-or-bankruptcy)

**Confidence: High** that liquidation affects jobs and creditors; **Low-to-Medium** on aggregate creditor-loss magnitude because I did not find a ready official dataset of unsecured-creditor shortfalls, employee claims, or recovery rates.

### Existing support is fragmented across tax, insolvency, and business-advice channels

IRD says an instalment arrangement is for tax debt that cannot be paid in full by the due date or is overdue, and that keeping to an agreed repayment plan may stop further collection action. [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir) IRD's broader "I'm struggling to file and pay my tax" page says people can apply for an instalment arrangement and that IRD can work with them on a solution before the situation gets worse. [IRD struggling to file/pay](https://www.ird.govt.nz/situations/unable-to-pay-my-tax-debt)

The Insolvency and Trustee Service separates business debt by structure: a sole trader is personally responsible for all business debts including tax, partners are equally responsible for partnership debts including tax, and a limited-liability company has separate insolvency options. [ITS determining business debt type](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/determining-your-type-of-debt) Its company-insolvency-options page explains receivership, voluntary administration, and liquidation, and says voluntary administration appoints an administrator to review and rearrange a business to avoid liquidation. [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options)

Companies Register guidance says voluntary administration appoints an administrator to review and rearrange the company's business and financial affairs; if the company cannot be saved, the administrator's focus is to get creditors and shareholders a better return than immediate liquidation would have achieved. [Companies Register voluntary administration](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/when-your-company-fails/what-happens-during-voluntary-administration/)

MBIE says it provides advice and support to help New Zealand businesses start, grow, innovate, and raise capability; its 2025 RBP news item says business owners looking to grow, adapt, or get advice are encouraged to connect with their local Regional Business Partner or Business Mentors New Zealand. [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business); [MBIE RBP news, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/record-high-number-of-small-businesses-taking-advantage-of-regional-support-and-advice) The Regional Business Partner Network registration page says businesses can register for free or subsidised access to unbiased business support in their region. [Regional Business Partner Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)

The likely gap is not "no help exists"; it is whether help is early, independent, restructuring-literate, affordable, visible to microbusinesses and sole traders, and connected to IRD/creditor action before a formal liquidation filing. That is a hypothesis for follow-up, not a proven conclusion. [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir); [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [Regional Business Partner Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)

**Confidence: Medium** — support channels are public and current, but evidence about access barriers and timing needs practitioner, SME, and user-centred research.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A reproduced bulk-data extract could change the concentration story.** If Companies Office/BIC/address joins show Deloitte's Q1 sector/region pattern is a temporary or classification-driven artefact, the stream should shift from "construction/hospitality concentration" to whatever the aggregate data actually shows. [Companies Office bulk data guide](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/bulk-data-help-guide/); [Deloitte Q1 2026 insolvency trends](https://www.deloitte.com/content/dam/assets-zone1/nz/en/docs/services/consulting/2026/2026-q1-new-zealand-insolvency-trends.pdf)
- **Better creditor-outcome data could change the social-impact priority.** I could verify employee/creditor process, but not aggregate unsecured-creditor losses, employee-claim recovery, or owner-operator personal consequences; a child finding from liquidator reports, ITS data, or practitioner interviews could move the stream's focus. [ITS effect of liquidation](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/the-effect-of-liquidation-on-a-company); [Employment NZ liquidation/bankruptcy guide](https://www.employment.govt.nz/fair-work-practices/restructuring-and-workplace-change/when-your-employer-goes-into-liquidation-or-bankruptcy)
- **IRD petition/enforcement data would sharpen causation.** I found official evidence of high tax debt, active enforcement, credit reporting, and legal action, but not a public aggregate time series tying IRD actions to court liquidations by month; if that exists, it should anchor the "why now" child. [IRD tax-agent debt webinar April 2026](https://www.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/home/documents/no-number/presentations-and-webinar-pdfs/tax-agent-webinar-on-debt-april-2026.pdf?modified=20260420234851)
- **User evidence could overturn the support-gap hypothesis.** If small operators and advisers report that existing IRD/RBP/ITS channels are easy to find, early enough, affordable, and effective, then the "support gap" may instead be demand shock or solvency reality rather than navigation failure. [IRD struggling to file/pay](https://www.ird.govt.nz/situations/unable-to-pay-my-tax-debt); [Regional Business Partner Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)
- **Could not verify:** exact 2026 sector/region/size concentrations from primary bulk files; creditor shortfall totals; employee counts affected by liquidation; the share of insolvencies initiated by IRD versus other creditors; whether RBP/Business Mentors support handles acute insolvency risk or mostly growth/capability advice.
- **A human is needed before policy direction.** Whether to help more businesses survive, fail faster, restructure earlier, or protect creditors/workers more strongly is a policy and lived-experience judgement; agents can gather evidence but should not make the G1 direction call.
