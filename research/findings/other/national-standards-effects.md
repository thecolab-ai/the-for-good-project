# National Standards produced some usable school data but no clearly attributable achievement lift, and their implementation created reliability, workload, curriculum and labelling harms

## Executive answer

- **National Standards should not be summarised as either "they worked" or "they failed".** The clearest evidence is mixed: schools did use the data to identify need and report to parents, but national and external achievement evidence does not support a confident claim that the policy lifted achievement. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf); [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling)
- **Achievement trends were disappointing but not causal proof.** During and just after the National Standards period, PIRLS 2016 recorded a statistically significant 8-point fall in Year 5 reading from 2011 to 2016, e-asTTle analysis found at least a third of end-of-Year-8 students below expected curriculum levels in writing and mathematics, and later PIRLS 2021 found no significant change from 2016 to 2021; PIRLS itself cautions that trend studies highlight population changes but do not identify causes. [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement); [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling); [Education Counts PIRLS 2021](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-progress-in-international-reading-literacy-study-202021/reading-literacy-at-year-5-new-zealand-participation-in-pirls-2021)
- **The strongest achievement-adjacent benefit was local information use.** In the Ministry-commissioned 2011 school-sample evaluation, just under two-thirds of principals reported using National Standards data to identify students for additional support in reading, writing and mathematics; in the 2016 NZCER survey, 77% of principals reported using the data to identify Māori students' learning needs, 57% for Pasifika students' needs, and 62% for students with additional learning needs. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)
- **The strongest harms were implementation and use harms, not a proven direct achievement fall.** NZCER and RAINS/Thrupp evidence repeatedly identified unreliable comparability, weak moderation, extra workload, curriculum narrowing, student labelling and league-table risks; these are the parts most relevant to any new national assessment-and-reporting system. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380); [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of the implementation findings is well supported across Ministry, NZCER and RAINS/Thrupp sources, but the achievement question cannot be answered causally from trend data or Overall Teacher Judgement aggregates.

## Evidence

### What the policy actually measured

National Standards were not a single national test: teachers made Overall Teacher Judgements in reading, writing and mathematics using multiple sources of evidence, and those judgements were reported on a four-point scale of "well below", "below", "at" and "above". [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380) The Ministry-commissioned school-sample evaluation found that teachers used a range of information sources for OTJs, but it also found large variability in teachers' accuracy when rating individual work or assessment samples against the standards. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011)

That design matters because National Standards data were being asked to do two jobs at once: support local teaching and parent reporting, and produce public achievement information that could be compared across schools and regions. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf) The evidence supports the first use more strongly than the second: local data use appeared common, while between-school reliability remained weak. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

### Achievement outcomes

The policy's own national aggregates did not show a decisive lift: NZCER's 2013 paper reported national at-standard rates of 77% in reading, 74% in mathematics and 70% in writing, with little change between 2011 and 2012, and Thrupp's 2017 retrospective reported that 2016 public achievement information had barely moved from the previous year. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380) The last National-led Government was still targeting Year 8 maths and writing in 2017: its own release said 2016 Year 8 at-or-above rates were 70.7% in maths, 69.3% in writing and 78.2% in reading. [Beehive, 2017](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/plan-raise-achievement-maths-and-writing)

External achievement measures do not rescue a strong positive story. PIRLS 2016 found New Zealand's Year 5 mean reading score remained above the PIRLS centrepoint but fell 8 points from 2011 to 2016, and the Ministry's PIRLS page explicitly says studies like PIRLS cannot identify the cause of a result. [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement) The Ministry's 2018 e-asTTle analysis of 2011-2016 data found at least a third of end-of-Year-8 students scoring below expected curriculum levels in writing and mathematics, and it warned that e-asTTle use was uneven across school types and that the report did not study causality. [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling)

The fairest conclusion is therefore negative but narrow: National Standards did not leave a clear, independently confirmed achievement-improvement signal, but available trend evidence cannot prove that National Standards caused weaker reading or mathematics results. [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement); [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling); [Education Counts PIRLS 2021](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-progress-in-international-reading-literacy-study-202021/reading-literacy-at-year-5-new-zealand-participation-in-pirls-2021)

### Data-use and parent-reporting benefits

National Standards did create or sharpen some local routines for identifying need. In the 2011 school-sample evaluation, around three-quarters of principals had collated school-wide National Standards achievement data, about 85% of teachers reported tracking student progress using OTJs from 2010 to 2011, and just under two-thirds of principals reported using the data to identify students for additional teaching support. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011)

The later NZCER survey suggests that this use persisted. In 2016, 80% of principals agreed that National Standards data were used to focus on students below or well below a standard, and principals reported using the data to identify learning needs for Māori students, Pasifika students and students with additional learning needs. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf) Teachers also reported some professional benefit from moderation: 74% agreed that OTJ moderation with other teachers had given useful insights into practice. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

Parent reporting improved in some respects but remained imperfect. The 2011 school-sample evaluation found nearly 90% of parents received an end-of-year report that directly referred to National Standards, but only 50% of reports that described achievement in relation to National Standards were rated clear for parents, families and whānau. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011) By 2016, NZCER found most parents and whānau reported receiving clear mid-year information on their child's National Standards achievement, while only 51% thought the standards provided a valuable record of their child's learning. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

### Reliability and league tables

Reliability was the policy's most persistent technical weakness. The Ministry-commissioned 2011 evaluation found considerable variability in teachers' accuracy rating individual samples and said large positive shifts for students previously rated "well below" were most likely attributable to teacher inconsistency in making OTJs. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011) NZCER's 2016 survey found only 41% of principals agreed their own school's National Standards data provided a reliable picture of student performance, and only 12% thought local schools' data provided a reliable local picture. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

That weakness made public comparison risky. NZCER's 2013 paper recorded league tables as an early concern and said trustees were sensitive to public comparisons, with 68% thinking league tables unfairly damaged some schools' reputations. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf) Thrupp's 2014 lecture noted that major newspaper groups had not published full school league tables, but also stressed that National Standards data were public on Education Counts and that different assessments made tables unsuitable as a "best-school" guide. [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf)

### Workload, curriculum narrowing and labelling

Workload and curriculum narrowing were not just union slogans; they appear in NZCER survey data and RAINS case-study evidence. NZCER's 2013 paper reported that 70% of principals agreed National Standards created more work for little real gain and 67% agreed they narrowed the school's curriculum, and it also reported that 32% of teachers thought they could not do justice to all students because of workload. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf) In 2016, NZCER reported that 69% of teachers thought National Standards had narrowed the curriculum they taught, up from 50% in 2013, while 83% felt pressure to accelerate students through levels by particular ages or year levels. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

RAINS/Thrupp evidence adds classroom-level detail to those survey patterns. Thrupp's 2014 lecture describes RAINS as a three-year study of six diverse schools using 486 interviews, classroom observation and document analysis, and reports the final RAINS conclusion that favourable impacts were overshadowed by workload intensification, curriculum narrowing, a two-tier curriculum, child labelling and staff tensions. [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf) Thrupp's 2017 retrospective repeats that RAINS conclusion and argues that the policy process gave too little genuine weight to evidence about pros, cons and adverse effects. [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380)

The student-labelling concern is strongly supported, but the exact scale of harm is harder to quantify. NZCER's 2016 survey found concern about students whose performance was repeatedly labelled "below" or "well below", especially where progress was not visible in reporting, and recommended shifting the focus toward individual progress to avoid unhelpful labelling. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf) RAINS provides qualitative evidence of positioning and labelling through grouping, data walls and classroom artefacts, but those data come from six schools rather than a nationally representative sample. [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf)

### Equity effects

National Standards made some equity needs more visible, but the evidence does not show that the policy closed inequities. NZCER's 2016 survey found principals used the data to identify Māori, Pasifika and additional-learning-needs students' learning needs, which is a plausible equity benefit. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf) The same report noted persistent decile-associated differences and said 2015 writing at-or-above rates were almost 61% for Pasifika students compared with 77% for European/Pākehā students. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

The equity risk was also structural: RAINS argued that low-socioeconomic schools were more likely to be pushed toward a "back to basics" or two-tier curriculum while more advantaged schools could retain a broader curriculum. [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf) PIRLS 2021 later found the achievement difference between New Zealand schools serving more affluent and more disadvantaged communities remained high compared with many countries, but that post-dates National Standards and should not be used as causal evidence about the policy. [Education Counts PIRLS 2021](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-progress-in-international-reading-literacy-study-202021/reading-literacy-at-year-5-new-zealand-participation-in-pirls-2021)

### Bottom line for the 2026 assessment reset

The lesson for a new national assessment and reporting system is not "never collect comparable data"; National Standards evidence shows that schools can use shared achievement information for targeting support, moderation and parent communication. [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf) The lesson is that comparable data become harmful when reliability is too weak for the accountability use being made of them, when public reporting invites ranking, when teacher time is pulled into compliance, and when reporting language labels children without showing progress. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380)

## What would change this conclusion

- A credible longitudinal evaluation that linked National Standards exposure or implementation intensity to later achievement while controlling for cohort, school composition, socioeconomic context, attendance and concurrent interventions would change the achievement conclusion. I did not find such a study in the public sources reviewed here. [Education Counts PIRLS 2016](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/2539/pirls-201516/pirls-2016-new-zealands-achievement); [Education Counts e-asTTle analysis, 2018](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/achievement-and-progress-in-mathematics%2C-reading-and-writing-in-primary-schooling)
- A recovered official time series of all National Standards aggregate data from 2011-2016, with documented data-quality caveats, would sharpen the descriptive achievement section. I found official and secondary references to public achievement information, but did not find a current Ministry page preserving the full national time series in a readily citable form. [Beehive, 2017](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/plan-raise-achievement-maths-and-writing); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380)
- A national student/whānau study of labelling effects would sharpen the harm estimate. The public evidence reviewed here combines representative adult survey data and six-school qualitative RAINS evidence, which is enough to treat labelling as a serious risk but not enough to quantify its national effect. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf); [Thrupp, 2014](https://www.canterbury.ac.nz/content/dam/uoc-main-site/documents/pdfs/d-other/2014-Martin-Thrupp-Nuthall.pdf)
- A human with primary-school leadership, Māori-medium, learning-support or whānau experience should review whether this finding underweights practical benefits from clear parent reporting or underweights harm from repeated "below/well below" reporting. The public record is stronger for system and educator perspectives than for lived student/whānau experience. [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)
