# Identity-responsive youth mental-health care is strongly justified, but NZ implementation evidence is strongest for reach and weakest for subgroup outcomes

## Executive answer

- Māori, Pacific, rainbow, disabled, Oranga Tamariki-involved and high-deprivation youth sources point to the same design test: care should be culturally safe, youth-friendly, relational, easy to enter, whānau/family-aware where appropriate, and responsive to intersecting identities rather than designed for an assumed "average" young person [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); [MSD Youth Health and Wellbeing Survey 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/index.html); [Youth19 Oranga Tamariki health report](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Youth19-Rangatahi-Smart-Survey/Youth19_young-people-involved-with-Oranga-Tamaki_mental-physical-health-and-healthcare-access.pdf).
- The implementation match is partial: Access and Choice has dedicated Kaupapa Māori, Pacific and Youth streams and reaches Māori, Pacific and young people at or above their population share, but public reporting does not yet show consistent youth outcome data or reliable subgroup outcome improvement [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice report, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf); [Health NZ response to the Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf).
- Specialist implementation misses the evidence more clearly: the Auditor-General found culturally specific ICAMHS options for Māori and Pacific young people were limited, with only some kaupapa Māori teams and only one Pacific-specific ICAMHS team known to the audit [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).
- For rainbow and disabled youth, current national suicide-prevention policy recognises risk groups and includes Rainbow and disabled people in population-specific actions, but the public implementation record checked here does not show a comparable youth mental-health service stream or outcome reporting for rainbow or disabled young people [Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-09/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029-v2.pdf); [MSD Youth Health and Wellbeing Survey 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/2025/overview-infographic-report-march-2026.pdf).
- The strongest evidence is not a direct proof that culturally tailored services outperform mainstream services on symptoms for every subgroup; Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 review found early intervention can reduce distress and improve wellbeing, but it also says culturally appropriate, co-designed and Indigenous-led approaches are supported mainly for acceptability and engagement, not yet by direct comparative symptom-outcome tests [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the need and implementation gaps are supported by strong NZ public sources, but outcome evidence for identity-specific models remains uneven and often does not separate youth subgroup outcomes.

## Evidence

### What the evidence says care must do

The current national youth survey baseline still shows a large school-age mental-health need: MSD reports that the 2025 Youth Health and Wellbeing Survey reached around 9,400 young people aged 13-19 in school settings, and about 21.2% of those with enough screening answers met high psychological-distress thresholds [MSD YHWS page, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/index.html). The same 2025 survey identifies 23.6% of respondents as Māori, 15.8% as Pacific, 19.9% as disabled using the Washington Group Short Set measure, and an estimated 15.2% as Rainbow, so identity-responsive design is not a niche add-on for youth services [MSD YHWS 2025 infographic, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/2025/overview-infographic-report-march-2026.pdf).

Youth2000's 2024 synthesis says access problems are higher among Māori, Pacific, Asian, Rainbow, disabled young people, low-income communities and small-town youth; it recommends accessible care in schools, communities, online and emergency contexts, and says prevention must include fairer, safer and inclusive communities as well as health services [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf). Youth2000 also says young people in higher-need groups include those in alternative education, NEET, excluded from school, involved with Oranga Tamariki, disabled, Rainbow, Indigenous and minoritised groups, and low-income communities [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf).

For rangatahi Māori, Youth2000's Māori-specific summary says Critical Te Tiriti Analysis found rangatahi Māori-specific policies, Māori leadership and political support aligned with improved outcomes and narrowing inequities in tobacco control, while health-care access remained persistently inequitable [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf). The same synthesis says Te Tiriti, shared governance, by-Māori-for-Māori approaches, culturally safe care, anti-racist services and consumer voice are part of a population youth mental-health framework for Aotearoa [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf).

For Pacific youth and families, Youth2000 reports that Pacific students had high and worsening mental-health need in 2019, including 25.8% with significant depressive symptoms and 11.7% reporting a past-year suicide attempt, and it recommends meeting basic needs and rights, partnering with Pacific young people, families and communities, and investing in research with and for Pacific young people [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf). Te Hiringa Mahara's Pacific wellbeing report says Pacific mental-health improvement requires addressing determinants, resourcing community wrap-around responses, sustainable Pacific provider support, Pacific data improvement, and broader public-service Pacific capability [Te Hiringa Mahara Pacific wellbeing report, 2024](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Pacific-wellbeing/Achieving-Pacific-wellbeing-outcomes-report-May-2024-web.pdf).

For Rainbow and disabled youth, Youth2000's intersectional report summary says Māori Rainbow, Māori disabled, Pacific Rainbow, Pacific disabled and Rainbow disabled young people face more and sometimes higher inequities than young people with only one of those identities; it recommends retaining priority-group focus, increasing attention to intersectional groups, creating opportunities to connect with allies and shared identities, and ensuring families/whānau have resources and supports [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf). What About Me? 2021 independently found mental wellbeing results were significantly worse for disabled and Rainbow young people on all mental-wellbeing questions and worse for most questions for rangatahi Māori, while healthcare access barriers were higher for rangatahi Māori, disabled, Rainbow and female young people [MSD What About Me? overview, 2022](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/the-national-youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-2021-overview-report-september-2022.pdf).

For care-experienced or Oranga Tamariki-involved youth, Youth19's Oranga Tamariki health report is explicit about its scope limit: its questions capture young people who have engaged with Oranga Tamariki/CYFS and do not distinguish care entry, youth-justice custody, or another form of engagement [Youth19 Oranga Tamariki health report](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Youth19-Rangatahi-Smart-Survey/Youth19_young-people-involved-with-Oranga-Tamaki_mental-physical-health-and-healthcare-access.pdf). Within that limit, the report says young people ever involved with Oranga Tamariki were more than twice as likely as never-involved peers to report depressive symptoms and serious thoughts of suicide, more than four times as likely to report a past-year suicide attempt, and more than twice as likely to have been unable to access a health provider when needed [Youth19 Oranga Tamariki health report](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Youth19-Rangatahi-Smart-Survey/Youth19_young-people-involved-with-Oranga-Tamaki_mental-physical-health-and-healthcare-access.pdf). The same report concludes that these youth need comprehensive, mana-enhancing, youth/whānau/community-driven approaches and skilled providers who build strong relationships and address young people's and whānau priorities [Youth19 Oranga Tamariki health report](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Youth19-Rangatahi-Smart-Survey/Youth19_young-people-involved-with-Oranga-Tamaki_mental-physical-health-and-healthcare-access.pdf).

For high-deprivation and disconnected youth, Youth2000 reports widening ethnic and socioeconomic disparities from 2012 to 2019, higher severity clusters over-representing Māori, Pacific, Rainbow and high-deprivation students, and very high mental-health need among Y-NEET and alternative-education samples [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf). The Youth19 Y-NEET report found 84 sampled 16-22-year-old Y-NEET participants in Waikato, Auckland and Northland reported high deprivation and trauma, including 70% with significant depressive symptoms and 29% with a past-year suicide attempt, but this was a convenience sample rather than a national prevalence estimate [Youth19 Y-NEET report, 2022](https://www.youth19.ac.nz/s/YNEET_update_2508.pdf).

### Where implementation matches

Access and Choice partly matches the evidence because it has dedicated GP, Kaupapa Māori, Pacific, Youth and community streams for mild-to-moderate mental-health and addiction support [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice programme page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/). Its five-year report says Youth services operated in all 20 districts by 2023/24, Kaupapa Māori services also operated across all 20 districts, and Pacific services operated in nine targeted districts [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice report, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf).

The reach signal is also directionally positive for priority populations: in 2023/24, Te Hiringa Mahara reported Māori were 26.8% of Access and Choice users, Pacific peoples were 10.9%, and 12-24-year-olds were 20.2%, compared with report-cited population shares of 17.5% Māori, 8.9% Pacific and 16.5% aged 12-24 [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice report, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf). Youth Access and Choice also reached Māori and Pacific young people within the youth stream: the Auditor-General reported rangatahi Māori were 33% and Pacific young people were 11% of Youth Access and Choice clients from 1 November 2022 to 31 October 2023 [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).

Current suicide-prevention implementation also recognises identity-specific need: the 2025-2029 action plan lists Kia Piki te Ora Māori suicide-prevention services, FLO: Pasifika for Life, Māori and Pacific suicide-prevention community funds, Mana Akiaki LifeKeepers for Māori, a Rainbow mental wellbeing and resilience programme, suicide-prevention efforts for children in care and their whānau, and cross-government actions for specific population groups including Pacific peoples, disabled people, women and youth [Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-09/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029-v2.pdf).

### Where implementation misses or is under-proven

The main implementation miss is specialist culturally specific access. The Auditor-General found that only some ICAMHS offered separate kaupapa Māori teams or services, it was aware of only one ICAMHS team catering specifically to Pacific young people, and most Māori and Pacific young people accessed mainstream ICAMHS [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm). That finding sits uneasily beside evidence that Māori and Pacific youth sources call for by-Māori, for-Māori, Pacific-led, family/community-grounded and culturally safe approaches [Youth2000 2024 synthesis](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5bdbb75ccef37259122e59aa/t/67070339e1026c23bbd4fbe1/1728512837095/Youth19%2BMental%2BHealth%2Bsummaries_FINAL_Tuesday%2BOct%2B8.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara Pacific wellbeing report, 2024](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Pacific-wellbeing/Achieving-Pacific-wellbeing-outcomes-report-May-2024-web.pdf).

The second miss is outcomes evidence. Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 early-intervention review found early intervention and secondary-prevention supports reduced mental distress and improved wellbeing/functioning, but it also found many evaluations used non-randomised designs, small samples or limited subgroup analyses, and it explicitly states that none of the evaluations it examined directly tested whether culturally tailored programmes produced stronger symptom improvements [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf). Health NZ's 2026 response to the Auditor-General says Youth Access and Choice providers use outcome tools such as SDQ, Hua Oranga and ORS/SRS/CORS, but use is inconsistent and national-level collection is unreliable [Health NZ response to the Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf).

The third miss is care-system accountability for care-experienced youth. The Auditor-General's Part 4 reports that Oranga Tamariki is required to assess and take reasonable steps to meet health and mental-health needs of children and young people in its care, but it also reports that the Independent Children's Monitor found Oranga Tamariki did not collect enough data to know whether it meets those obligations [Auditor-General, Part 4, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part4.htm). That public-data gap matters because Youth19 shows young people involved with Oranga Tamariki have higher mental-health need and more healthcare access barriers than peers [Youth19 Oranga Tamariki health report](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Youth19-Rangatahi-Smart-Survey/Youth19_young-people-involved-with-Oranga-Tamaki_mental-physical-health-and-healthcare-access.pdf).

The fourth miss is that rainbow and disabled youth are visible in need data and suicide-prevention policy, but less visible in the checked service-implementation evidence. The 2025 YHWS reports substantial Rainbow and disabled respondent groups, What About Me? 2021 reports worse mental-wellbeing and healthcare-access results for Rainbow and disabled youth, and the suicide-prevention action plan identifies disabled and Rainbow young people in disproportionate-risk examples [MSD YHWS 2025 infographic, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/2025/overview-infographic-report-march-2026.pdf); [MSD What About Me? overview, 2022](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/consultations/youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-results/the-national-youth-health-and-wellbeing-survey-2021-overview-report-september-2022.pdf); [Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-09/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029-v2.pdf). However, the public Access and Choice and Auditor-General sources checked here provide clearer implementation detail for Kaupapa Māori, Pacific and Youth streams than for youth-specific Rainbow and disability-responsive mental-health pathways [Te Hiringa Mahara Access and Choice report, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf); [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).

## What would change this conclusion

- Public Health NZ or Te Hiringa Mahara outcome reporting that links Youth Access and Choice, Kaupapa Māori, Pacific, Youth One Stop Shop, school-based, crisis and ICAMHS activity to consistent baseline/follow-up measures by ethnicity, Rainbow identity, disability, deprivation and Oranga Tamariki involvement would change the finding from "reach matched, outcomes under-proven" to a stronger implementation judgement [Health NZ response to the Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf).
- Robust evaluations of Māori-led, Pacific-led, Rainbow-affirming, disability-responsive or care-experienced-youth models with comparison groups, follow-up and subgroup reporting would strengthen or overturn the conclusion that the current evidence is strongest for acceptability/engagement and weaker for direct symptom-outcome superiority [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf).
- A district-by-district public service map showing culturally specific ICAMHS, Youth Access and Choice, integrated youth services, school supports, Rainbow/disability-competent pathways and Pacific services would improve the implementation match assessment [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm).
- I could not verify lived-experience review from Māori, Pacific, Rainbow, disabled or care-experienced youth for this finding; human review by people with those identities and by Te Tiriti/Pacific equity/domain experts could change the framing, priorities or interpretation of what "safe" and "responsive" require [docs/METHOD.md](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/METHOD.md).
- I did not open depth-2 sub-issues because the issue can be answered at synthesis level, but separate future research could go deeper on Rainbow/disability-specific youth pathways and care-experienced youth mental-health accountability [docs/STREAMS.md](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/STREAMS.md).

## Open follow-up questions

- What public or OIA-releasable data can show actual youth outcomes, not only contact counts, for Access and Choice and ICAMHS by Māori, Pacific, Rainbow, disability, deprivation and Oranga Tamariki involvement?
- Which existing Māori-led, Pacific-led, Rainbow-affirming, disability-responsive and care-experienced-youth models in Aotearoa have enough evaluation infrastructure to test outcomes without imposing extractive data collection?
- How should Te Tiriti, Pacific data equity, disability rights and youth consent shape any proposed youth mental-health data linkage or service-performance dashboard?
