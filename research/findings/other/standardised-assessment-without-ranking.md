# Standardised assessment can support equity when it is separated from high-stakes ranking and tied to support

## Executive answer

- International evidence does not support a simple "standardised assessment bad / no assessment good" rule: OECD distinguishes national assessments with no consequence for student progression from examinations with formal progression or certification consequences, and UNESCO frames learning assessment as a way to understand and improve education quality and equity when it uses multiple sources and context data [OECD, 2023](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2023_e13bef63-en/full-report/what-assessments-and-examinations-of-students-are-in-place_79948633.html); [UNESCO, accessed 2026-07-06](https://www.unesco.org/en/learning-assessments).
- The safer comparator menu uses standardised assessment for three bounded purposes: sample-based system monitoring, classroom or school diagnosis, and resource/support targeting; the risky pattern is attaching public ranking, rewards, sanctions, student placement, or teacher/school accountability to the same results [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf); [Levatino et al., 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0091732X241270672).
- Finland is the cleanest low-stakes monitoring comparator: its national learning-outcome evaluations sample about 5-10% of pupils, are designed so results cannot rank schools, and publish system-level evidence for equality, curriculum and teaching improvement [FINEEC, 2025](https://www.karvi.fi/sites/default/files/sites/default/files/documents/Brochure-on-FINEEC-learning-outcomes-evaluations_0.pdf); [Eurydice Finland, 2025](https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/finland/quality-assurance-early-childhood-and-school-education).
- Ceará and Sobral in Brazil show that census assessment can be part of an equity-improvement strategy, but only as one part of a wider system of coaching, targets, grants, and political/administrative follow-through; this is a support-and-governance comparator, not a pure low-stakes comparator [World Bank, 2020](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/444581593599662264/pdf/Getting-Education-Right-State-and-Municipal-Success-in-Reform-for-Universal-Literacy-in-Brazil.pdf); [World Bank, 2023](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/education/when-an-assessment-system-works-to-improve-learning--the-case-of).
- England and Australia illustrate the failure mode: when assessment results feed high-stakes accountability or public reporting, official and peer-reviewed sources report curriculum narrowing, teaching to the test, and wellbeing or inclusion concerns [UK Parliament Education Committee, 2017](https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/203/education-committee/news/102666/highstakes-testing-harming-teaching-and-learning-in-primary-schools/); [NAPLAN Review Interim Report, 2019](https://www.education.vic.gov.au/Documents/about/programs/NAPLANreviewinterimreport_nov2019.pdf); [Au, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0013189x07306523).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of the evidence is strong across OECD, UNESCO, IEA, official country material and peer-reviewed reviews, but transfer to New Zealand depends on 2026 data-governance rules, reporting design, actual classroom use, and whether extra support follows the results.

## Evidence

### The core distinction is purpose and stakes, not whether a test is standardised

OECD defines national or central assessments as standardised achievement tests whose results do not affect student progression or certification, while national or central examinations have formal consequences such as progression, completion or tertiary-entry eligibility [OECD, 2023](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2023_e13bef63-en/full-report/what-assessments-and-examinations-of-students-are-in-place_79948633.html). OECD also reports that national/central assessments commonly serve more than one purpose, including formative feedback and system monitoring [OECD, 2023](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2023_e13bef63-en/full-report/what-assessments-and-examinations-of-students-are-in-place_79948633.html). UNESCO similarly describes system-level learning assessment as evidence for improving quality and equity, while individual formative assessment is teacher-led evidence used while teaching and learning are happening [UNESCO, accessed 2026-07-06](https://www.unesco.org/en/learning-assessments).

The load-bearing warning is that a formally low-stakes test can become high-stakes if accountability incentives are attached to it: OECD's standardised-testing review says attaching accountability measures to low-stakes tests changes how teachers and school leaders perceive them and can lead to teaching to the test, curriculum narrowing, cheating, or exclusion [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf). A 2024 systematic review of 133 empirical studies on performance-based accountability found side effects across accountability designs and notes that these concerns can disproportionately affect disadvantaged students' educational experiences [Levatino et al., 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0091732X241270672).

For New Zealand, the implication is that SMART/PAT/e-asTTle-style data should be governed by use-case: classroom diagnosis can be near the learner, system monitoring can be aggregated or sampled, and public reporting should avoid school or teacher league tables unless the policy is explicitly choosing those stakes [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf); [FINEEC, 2025](https://www.karvi.fi/sites/default/files/sites/default/files/documents/Brochure-on-FINEEC-learning-outcomes-evaluations_0.pdf).

### The harm pattern: public accountability can reshape teaching toward the tested slice

The best-supported harm is curriculum narrowing under high-stakes testing rather than the mere existence of a standardised instrument. Au's qualitative metasynthesis of 49 studies found that the primary effect of high-stakes testing was narrowing content to tested subjects, fragmenting subject knowledge into test-related pieces, and increasing teacher-centred pedagogies, while also noting a minority of cases where test design supported broader or more integrated practice [Au, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0013189x07306523). Black and Wiliam's formative-assessment review points the other way: frequent feedback used to adjust teaching and learning has evidence of substantial learning gains, which implies the design question is whether assessment information is fed back into instruction rather than extracted for ranking [Black & Wiliam, 1998](https://assess.ucr.edu/sites/default/files/2019-02/blackwiliam_1998.pdf).

England is an official-system example of the accountability problem. The UK Parliament Education Committee reported that Key Stage 2 results were used to hold schools and teachers to account, and found that the resulting high-stakes system could narrow curriculum, encourage teaching to the test, and affect teacher and pupil wellbeing [UK Parliament Education Committee, 2017](https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/203/education-committee/news/102666/highstakes-testing-harming-teaching-and-learning-in-primary-schools/).

Australia's NAPLAN is a public-reporting comparator. A 2019 NAPLAN Review interim report for New South Wales, Queensland and Victoria said NAPLAN had potential to narrow curriculum because teachers can focus only on aspects they believe are tested, especially literacy/English and numeracy/mathematics in primary schools [NAPLAN Review Interim Report, 2019](https://www.education.vic.gov.au/Documents/about/programs/NAPLANreviewinterimreport_nov2019.pdf). A peer-reviewed Australian teacher-perception study frames NAPLAN/My School as a local version of the global high-stakes accountability trend, which matters because public school-comparison infrastructure can raise the stakes even when the test is not formally a student-promotion exam [Thompson, 2013](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1017709.pdf).

### Design features for New Zealand to consider

New Zealand's safest path is a deliberately "balanced assessment system": classroom evidence for immediate teaching decisions, moderated common tools for school-level diagnosis, and sampled or aggregated evidence for system monitoring [UNESCO, accessed 2026-07-06](https://www.unesco.org/en/learning-assessments); [OECD, 2023](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2023_e13bef63-en/full-report/what-assessments-and-examinations-of-students-are-in-place_79948633.html). Standardised evidence should trigger additional support, curriculum resources, teacher professional learning, learning-support decisions or resourcing; Ceará and Sobral are useful because their assessment evidence is embedded in action requirements and grants rather than being only a measurement dashboard [World Bank, 2023](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/education/when-an-assessment-system-works-to-improve-learning--the-case-of).

The key guardrails are: do not publish school league tables from learner-level assessment results; do not attach teacher/school sanctions or rewards to one assessment window; report bands/descriptors and progress rather than fine-grained ranks; include context variables for equity interpretation; and keep untested learning areas visible in curriculum monitoring [FINEEC, 2025](https://www.karvi.fi/sites/default/files/sites/default/files/documents/Brochure-on-FINEEC-learning-outcomes-evaluations_0.pdf); [Singapore MOE, accessed 2026-07-06](https://www.moe.gov.sg/psle-fsbb/psle/psle-scoring-system); [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf); [UK Parliament Education Committee, 2017](https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/203/education-committee/news/102666/highstakes-testing-harming-teaching-and-learning-in-primary-schools/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A New Zealand data-use rule that legally prevents school league tables, teacher ranking, sanctions, or fine-grained public comparison from the 2026 assessment results would reduce the risk that standardised assessment becomes high-stakes accountability [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf).
- Evidence that every below-expectation result triggers timely funded support, teacher professional learning, learning-support intervention or culturally grounded aromatawai response would make the Ceará/Sobral support comparator more relevant than the England/Australia narrowing comparator [World Bank, 2023](https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/education/when-an-assessment-system-works-to-improve-learning--the-case-of).
- Independent classroom evidence showing that NZ teachers use SMART/PAT/e-asTTle results mainly for formative feedback and next-step teaching, without reducing time for science, arts, social sciences, physical education, local curriculum or te ao Māori learning, would weaken the curriculum-narrowing concern [Black & Wiliam, 1998](https://assess.ucr.edu/sites/default/files/2019-02/blackwiliam_1998.pdf); [Au, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0013189x07306523).
- I could not verify unpublished 2026 NZ SMART trial data, psychometric reviews, Māori-medium/aromatawai validation evidence, or actual Ministry data-publication rules for learner-, class-, school- and system-level results; these are essential before applying the comparator menu to the specific NZ rollout.
- This finding needs human review from teachers, Māori-medium leaders, disabled learner advocates, parents/whānau and assessment specialists, because desk research cannot settle whether the reporting language is understandable, mana-enhancing, and useful in practice.

## Open follow-up questions

- What public rules govern publication, aggregation, privacy and reuse of NZ's 2026 assessment data?
- What evidence shows whether SMART, PATs or e-asTTle results trigger funded support rather than only measurement and reporting?
- Which curriculum areas, if any, are losing time in schools implementing the 2026 assessment/reporting requirements?
