# NZ plastic packaging recycling trails Australia, the EU average, and high-performing EPR/CRS systems once denominators are kept separate

## Executive answer

- New Zealand should not be ranked using municipal-waste recycling rates for this question: the best current NZ plastic-specific baseline is a 2022 plastic-packaging estimate of 263,231 tonnes placed on market and 45,113 tonnes recycled onshore or exported, giving a 17.1% plastic-packaging recycling rate [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
- On the closest available plastic-packaging comparison, NZ sits below Australia and well below the EU average: APCO/DCCEEW reported Australian plastic-packaging recovery at 20% in 2021-22 and APCO later reported 19% progress in 2022-23, while Eurostat reported the EU recycled 42.1% of generated plastic-packaging waste in 2023 [DCCEEW product stewardship report, 2024](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/publications/national-waste-resource-recovery-reporting/product-stewardship-2024); [APCO National Packaging Targets](https://apco.org.au/national-packaging-targets); [Eurostat plastic packaging waste news, 2025](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-eurostat-news/w/ddn-20251022-1).
- The highest EU plastic-packaging performers are roughly three times NZ's current packaging rate: Eurostat reported Belgium at 59.5%, Latvia at 59.2%, Slovakia at 54.1%, Germany at 52.2%, Czechia at 52.4%, and Slovenia at 51.5% in 2023 [Eurostat packaging waste statistics, 2025](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Packaging_waste_statistics).
- Australia's all-plastics recycling rate is not the same denominator as NZ's packaging figure: DCCEEW reported 3.2 million tonnes of Australian plastics reaching end-of-life in 2023-24, 446 kt recovered, 423 kt recycled, and a 13% recycling rate after excluding energy recovery [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).
- Better-performing systems combine mandatory producer responsibility, public reporting, fee modulation, deposit-return systems for beverage containers, and recycled-content demand; NZ's proposed PPPS would introduce several of these but the 2025 design report says NZ still lacks producer-reported data, detailed material-flow tracking, and some legislative settings for best-practice fees and PCR incentives [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation 2025/40](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/40/oj/eng).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the direction of comparison is well supported, but several rates are not perfectly like-for-like because NZ, Australia, EU packaging statistics and deposit-return systems use different denominators and calculation points.

## Evidence

### Denominators decide the ranking

Eunomia's 2025 report for MfE warns that recycling rates differ by calculation point, including collection, sorted/sold material, or material turned into recovered product; it also warns that rates differ by activity scope, including household, municipal, commercial and industrial, construction and demolition, and placed-on-market approaches [Eunomia for MfE, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/New-Zealand-National-Recycling-Rate-Issues-and-Options-FINAL-30-01-25.pdf). Eurostat's municipal-waste article is therefore useful context but not a plastic-packaging benchmark, because it says municipal waste includes many household-like streams, reports plastic as 7.5% of EU municipal solid waste in 2023, and reports overall municipal recycling rates separately from packaging statistics [Eurostat municipal waste statistics, data extracted Jan 2026](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Municipal_waste_statistics).

For NZ, the PPPS/Valpak estimate is a plastic-packaging placed-on-market denominator: Valpak estimated 263,231 tonnes of plastic packaging placed on the NZ market in 2022, 28,954 tonnes recycled onshore, 16,159 tonnes exported, and 45,113 tonnes recycled onshore or exported in total [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). That produces a 17.1% packaging recycling rate, but it is not an all-plastics recycling rate and it does not include a complete split of landfill, stockpiles, contamination losses, litter, or final overseas treatment [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

For Australia, DCCEEW's plastics flows page uses an end-of-life all-plastics denominator for the headline rate: 4.0 million tonnes of plastic products and packaging were placed on the Australian market in 2023-24, 3.2 million tonnes reached end-of-life, 446 kt were recovered, 423 kt were recycled, and 23 kt went to energy recovery, so the all-plastics recovery rate was 14% and the recycling rate was 13% after excluding energy recovery [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting). DCCEEW also reports plastic recovery by application, including 237 kt of business-to-consumer packaging-related recovery at a 22% recovery rate and 65 kt of business-to-business packaging-related recovery at a 39% recovery rate; those application figures include recovery, not just recycling, and include pre-consumer and post-consumer recovery [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).

For the EU, Eurostat's plastic-packaging rate is closer to the NZ packaging denominator than municipal waste is: in 2023, the EU generated 35.3 kg of plastic packaging waste per person and recycled 14.8 kg per person, giving a 42.1% recycling rate for all generated plastic-packaging waste across households, businesses and government [Eurostat plastic packaging waste news, 2025](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-eurostat-news/w/ddn-20251022-1). Eurostat's packaging article states that packaging recycling targets are calculated by weight as recycled packaging divided by generated packaging, and reports plastic-packaging recycling rates for EU and EFTA countries in 2023 [Eurostat packaging waste statistics, 2025](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Packaging_waste_statistics).

### Policy levers behind better performers

Mandatory EPR is the common packaging-system lever. The EU PPWR says extended producer responsibility schemes were to be established by 31 December 2024 and requires producer or PRO registration, authorisation, whole-territory coverage, and at least annual publication of packaging placed on the market and recovered/recycled material levels [EU PPWR 2025/40](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/40/oj/eng). PPPS's international review found one-PRO models in Ireland, Norway, Italy and the Netherlands, separate household and commercial PROs in Belgium, and multiple competing PROs in Germany [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

Deposit-return systems are high-performing for beverage containers but should be treated as a separate stream. Infinitum reported Norway's 2023 deposit return rate at 92.3% and total collection rate at 96.7%, while Eurostat and the PPWR target the wider EU packaging system separately from single-use beverage bottle/can deposit obligations [Infinitum Annual Report 2023](https://infinitum.no/media/d4dese5n/infinitum_a-rsrapport_2023_en.pdf); [EU PPWR 2025/40](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/40/oj/eng). Germany's official EEA profile identifies a deposit requirement for single-use plastic beverage containers and the Packaging Act's EPR/fee-modulation architecture, but I did not rely on a Germany-wide 98% return-rate claim because the public source I found warning about that claim says no single German agency collects all beverage-container sales and redemption data [EEA Germany waste profile, 2025](https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/topics/in-depth/waste-and-recycling/municipal-and-packaging-waste-management-country-profiles-2025/de-municipal-waste-factsheet.pdf/@@download/file); [Container Recycling Institute Germany profile](https://www.bottlebill.org/index.php/current-and-proposed-laws/worldwide/germany).

Recycled-content demand is increasingly being built into the rules rather than left to voluntary purchasing. The EU Commission says the PPWR will require plastic packaging to be made partly from recycled content with increasing 2030 and 2040 targets, and the legal text says PPWR establishes requirements for recycled content in plastic packaging [European Commission PPWR overview](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/waste-and-recycling/packaging-waste/packaging-packaging-waste-regulation_en); [EU PPWR 2025/40](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/40/oj/eng). Australia has national packaging targets for 50% average recycled content and material-specific targets of 20% plastic packaging, 30% PET, 20% HDPE and 20% PP recycled content, but APCO reported current 2022-23 progress of 44% average recycled content and 19% plastic packaging recycling/composting against the 70% target [DCCEEW packaging covenant](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/packaging/packaging-covenant); [APCO National Packaging Targets](https://apco.org.au/national-packaging-targets).

Public data obligations are a structural difference between NZ and higher-performing EPR systems. PPPS says Valpak found a lack of producer-reported NZ data and recommended enforcing annual reporting by material type, while the EU PPWR requires producer registration and annual reporting and requires PROs to publish packaging placed-on-market and recovered/recycled levels [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [EU PPWR 2025/40](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/40/oj/eng). PPPS also recommends a mandatory digital waste-tracking system and a data warehouse/transaction platform because NZ cannot yet reliably aggregate collection, sorting, export, reprocessing and resin-fate data [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

Disposal restrictions matter, but they do not by themselves prove plastic recycling performance. Germany's EEA profile reports a landfill rate below 1% for municipal waste, waste-stream-specific packaging law, deposit requirements for single-use plastic beverage containers, and a ban on landfilling waste above specified total-organic-carbon thresholds [EEA Germany waste profile, 2025](https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/topics/in-depth/waste-and-recycling/municipal-and-packaging-waste-management-country-profiles-2025/de-municipal-waste-factsheet.pdf/@@download/file). Eurostat's municipal-waste article separately says EU landfill fell from 61% of municipal waste in 1995 to 24% in 2024, partly attributing this to European legislation including packaging and landfill directives, but that municipal landfill trend should not be substituted for plastic-packaging recycling rates [Eurostat municipal waste statistics, data extracted Jan 2026](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Municipal_waste_statistics).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current NZ official dataset that reports all plastic packaging placed on market, collected, sorted, rejected, recycled onshore, exported for recycling, landfilled, stockpiled and littered by resin and application would change both the 17.1% baseline and the confidence rating [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
- A direct release from Australia's packaging regulator after national packaging reforms begin could change the Australia comparator, because current Australian packaging progress relies on APCO/co-regulatory reporting while DCCEEW's strongest 2023-24 dataset is all-plastics end-of-life flows [DCCEEW reforming packaging regulation](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/packaging/reforming-packaging-regulation); [DCCEEW Australian plastics flows and fates, 2023-24](https://www.dcceew.gov.au/environment/protection/waste/plastics-and-packaging/australian-plastic-flows-fates-reporting).
- I could not verify a primary Latvian government or operator source connecting Latvia's new deposit system to the 59.2% national plastic-packaging recycling rate, so Latvia should be treated as a high-performing EU packaging comparator rather than proof that its DRS caused the full national rate [Eurostat plastic packaging waste news, 2025](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-eurostat-news/w/ddn-20251022-1).
- I could not verify a Germany-wide single-use beverage-container return rate from an official German agency, and one public container-deposit reference explicitly warns that no single German agency collects all beverage sales and redemption data; this is why the Germany comparison uses Eurostat/EEA packaging rates and legal instruments rather than a 98% DRS claim [Container Recycling Institute Germany profile](https://www.bottlebill.org/index.php/current-and-proposed-laws/worldwide/germany); [EEA Germany waste profile, 2025](https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/topics/in-depth/waste-and-recycling/municipal-and-packaging-waste-management-country-profiles-2025/de-municipal-waste-factsheet.pdf/@@download/file).
- Human validation from NZ waste operators, councils, reprocessors, packaging producers and regulators would be needed before using this comparison for scheme-cost design, because the public sources do not expose contract-level costs, contamination, bale rejection, stockpiling, end-destination audits or processor yields [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

## Open follow-up questions

- Can NZ publish a recurring plastic-packaging dashboard with the same denominator as Eurostat packaging waste: generated/placed-on-market plastic packaging by resin, recycled at the harmonised calculation point, exported, and disposed?
- Which EPR fee-modulation design would most improve NZ plastic packaging recovery without overstating recyclability or shifting costs in ways the Waste Minimisation Act cannot support?
- If NZ revisits a container return scheme, what beverage-container scope and data obligations would let the CRS rate be reported separately from all plastic-packaging recovery?
