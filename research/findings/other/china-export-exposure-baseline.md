# NZ's China export exposure is concentrated in goods, logs/wood, dairy, China-linked travel, and Auckland-heavy education

## Executive answer

- China took **$19.885b of $82.665b** in annual merchandise exports in the year ended May 2026, or **24.1%** of goods exports; China was nearly twice the next destination, Australia at $10.943b and the United States at $9.734b, calculated from Stats NZ's overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook. This exact current percentage is single-source official Stats NZ arithmetic, while MFAT independently supports the broader point that China is a major, goods-heavy export market at an older annual reference point [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx); [MFAT China trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade).
- The highest current China concentration in the March 2026 quarter was not "all agriculture" but specific products: **logs, wood, and wood articles** at **60.7%** China-bound, **milk powder, butter, and cheese** at **36.3%**, **fruit** at **27.5%**, and **meat and edible offal** at **18.7%**, calculated from Stats NZ's international trade March 2026 quarter workbook [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).
- In services, China took **$1.166b** of exports in the March 2026 quarter, or **10.2%** of all services exports; **$1.034b** of that was travel, equal to **14.7%** of total travel exports that quarter, calculated from Stats NZ's international trade workbook [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).
- Education exposure is clearest by enrolment, not by a public China-specific education-revenue line: ENZ reported **92,580** international student enrolments in 2025, with **China at 34%** and Auckland hosting **around 55%** of international students [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).
- Tourism exposure is lower by visitor count than by spend: MBIE's IVS chart data shows China visitor spend of **$1.463b** out of **$13.735b** total international visitor spend in the year ended March 2026, or **10.7%**, while Stats NZ reported **297,000** China visitor arrivals out of **3.63m** overseas visitor arrivals in the same year, or **8.2%** [MBIE IVS annual summary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualSummary.json); [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - services, student-share, and visitor-spend baselines are High confidence because they come from official workbooks or agency chart data; the exact current goods share is Medium confidence because it is calculated from one official Stats NZ workbook rather than two independent sources; regional goods exposure is Low confidence because I did not find a public current region-by-destination export table in the scoped official sources.

## Evidence

### Scope and calculation method

This finding answers issue #498 as a current baseline for China exposure across merchandise exports, services exports, education, tourism, and the regional exposure that can be verified from public official sources; it does not model substitution, GDP, or employment effects, which are separate stream questions [Issue #498](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/498).

I used Stats NZ's May 2026 overseas merchandise trade workbook for annual goods totals by destination and commodity, Stats NZ's March 2026 international trade workbook for goods-plus-services and destination/category detail, ENZ's 2025 enrolment release for education source-country and regional shares, and MBIE TEIC's March 2026 IVS chart JSON for tourism spend and itinerary regions [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026/); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-trade-march-2026-quarter/); [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [MBIE IVS release page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism-research-and-data/tourism-data-releases/international-visitor-survey-ivs).

### Merchandise exports

Annual merchandise exports were **$82.665b** in the year ended May 2026, and exports to China were **$19.885b**, giving China a **24.1%** share of goods exports; the next largest annual goods destinations were Australia at **$10.943b**, the United States at **$9.734b**, Japan at **$4.061b**, and Korea at **$2.660b** [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx). I did not find a second independent source that reproduces the exact year-ended-May-2026 24.1% share; MFAT reports an older year-ending-September-2024 China export total and product/service composition, and says its trade figures come from the Statistics New Zealand Trade Dashboard, so it should be treated as context rather than an independent verification of this exact percentage [MFAT China trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade).

Stats NZ's annual commodity table shows the largest merchandise export categories across all markets in the year ended May 2026 were **milk powder, butter and cheese** at **$23.954b**, **meat and edible offal** at **$11.377b**, **fruit** at **$6.185b**, and **logs, wood, and wood articles** at **$5.025b** [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx).

Stats NZ's March 2026 international trade workbook gives the current quarter's destination concentration for major export products: China took **$2.366b** of **$6.510b** milk powder, butter, and cheese exports (**36.3%**); **$675m** of **$1.113b** logs, wood, and wood articles exports (**60.7%**); **$614m** of **$3.288b** meat and edible offal exports (**18.7%**); and **$249m** of **$903m** fruit exports (**27.5%**) [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

MPI's latest SOPI page supports the sector framing but not China-specific shares: it says SOPI provides historical and forecast production and trade data, and its December 2025 update forecast food and fibre export revenue of **$62b** in the year to 30 June 2026, with dairy at **$27.4b**, meat and wool at **$13.2b**, forestry at **$6.3b**, and horticulture at **$9.2b** [MPI SOPI page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/resources-and-forms/economic-intelligence/situation-and-outlook-for-primary-industries); [MPI SOPI data page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/science/open-data-and-forecasting/situation-and-outlook-for-primary-industries-data).

### Goods-plus-services and service categories

In the March 2026 quarter, total exports of goods and services were **$31.363b**, with **$19.874b** goods and **$11.489b** services; China took **$6.381b** total exports, made up of **$5.215b** goods and **$1.166b** services [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

That makes China **20.3%** of total goods-and-services exports in the March 2026 quarter, **26.2%** of goods exports, and **10.2%** of services exports, calculated from the same Stats NZ workbook [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

Travel was the largest export category in the March 2026 quarter at **$7.035b**, and education-related travel was **$1.288b**; China-specific travel exports were **$1.034b**, or **14.7%** of all travel exports, but the workbook's top destination-by-category table does not publish a China-specific education-related travel line [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

MFAT's China trade facts page independently confirms the same composition at a slightly older annual reference point: it reports New Zealand exports to China of **$22.82b** for the year ending September 2024, made up of **$19.77b** goods and **$3.05b** services, and lists dairy, meat, forestry/wood, fruit, tourism, education, and transportation as major China export categories [MFAT China trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-new-zealand-china-trade).

### Education exposure

ENZ reported **92,580** international students enrolled with New Zealand providers in 2025, an **11%** increase from 2024 and **80%** of the pre-COVID peak [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

China and India were the top two source countries in ENZ's 2025 enrolment release, at **34%** and **14%** respectively, followed by Japan at **9%** and several smaller markets at **3-4%** each [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

The same ENZ release says international students study across all New Zealand regions but Auckland remains the largest centre, hosting **around 55%** of international students, followed by Canterbury and Waikato [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

The most defensible current education exposure baseline is therefore **34% of enrolments from China**, approximately **31,500 enrolments** if the 34% share is applied to ENZ's 92,580 total, while the public sources checked here do not provide a current China-specific dollar value for education-related travel exports [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

### Tourism exposure and visitor regions

MBIE's IVS annual summary chart JSON reports **$13.735b** of total international visitor spend for the year ended March 2026, including **$4.238b** from Australia, **$1.969b** from the United States, and **$1.463b** from China [MBIE IVS annual summary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualSummary.json).

China's IVS spend share was therefore **10.7%** of international visitor spend in the year ended March 2026, and MBIE's data also reports a China mean spend per visitor of **$5,543**, above the all-country mean of **$4,386** [MBIE IVS annual summary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualSummary.json).

Stats NZ's international travel release gives a visitor-arrival cross-check: in the year ended March 2026, New Zealand had **3.63m** overseas visitor arrivals, including **1.56m** from Australia and **297,000** from China, so China was about **8.2%** of arrivals and Australia was about **43.0%** [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/).

The tourism regional pattern for China is concentrated in a few itinerary places: MBIE's IVS itinerary JSON shows the highest China visitor itinerary-night region percentages in the year ended March 2026 were **Queenstown-Lakes District** at **74.0%**, **Auckland** at **56.3%**, **Christchurch City** at **44.8%**, and **Mackenzie District** at **34.9%** [MBIE IVS itinerary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualItinerary.json).

MBIE warns that the March 2026 IVS release corrected an error where China had been incorrectly grouped with Macau and Hong Kong; MBIE says the correction had no material impact on overall visitor spend or most market breakdowns, but the only notable changes were within the China and Rest of Asia categories [MBIE IVS release page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism-research-and-data/tourism-data-releases/international-visitor-survey-ivs).

### Regional exposure

Regional exposure is measurable for education and tourism from the checked sources, but not for merchandise exports by China destination: ENZ gives Auckland's share of international students, and MBIE IVS gives China visitor itinerary regions, while the Stats NZ and MPI workbooks checked here give export destination, product, and sector but not a current region-by-destination goods table [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [MBIE IVS itinerary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualItinerary.json); [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx); [MPI SOPI data page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/science/open-data-and-forecasting/situation-and-outlook-for-primary-industries-data).

The supported regional baseline is therefore: education exposure is Auckland-heavy because Auckland hosts around **55%** of international students, and tourism exposure is concentrated for China visitors in Queenstown-Lakes, Auckland, Christchurch, and Mackenzie itinerary nights; merchandise regional exposure should be treated as unverified until a region-by-product-by-destination dataset is obtained [ENZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [MBIE IVS itinerary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualItinerary.json).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Stats NZ, MPI, NZTE, port, or customs dataset giving current **region x product x destination** export values would materially improve or change the regional merchandise conclusion.
- An independently maintained source reproducing the current **year-ended-May-2026 China share of total goods exports** would raise confidence in the exact 24.1% goods baseline.
- A public China-specific split of **education-related travel exports** would change the education baseline from enrolment-share exposure to revenue-share exposure.
- Annual product-by-destination data could change the product ranking if the March 2026 quarter is seasonally atypical, especially for fruit.
- Revised MBIE IVS China estimates could change the tourism spend share because MBIE specifically notes a corrected China/Macau/Hong Kong grouping issue in the March 2026 release.
- A sector substitution study showing that China-bound logs, dairy, fruit, or travel demand can be redirected quickly at comparable prices would reduce the risk implied by these concentration shares.

I could not verify a current official regional merchandise-by-China table, a current China-specific education export revenue value, or product-level China concentration on a full-year annual basis rather than the March 2026 quarter.
