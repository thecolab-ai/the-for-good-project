# Post-Gabrielle recategorisation shows a recovery precedent for crediting mitigation, but not yet a business-as-usual designation system for existing homes

## Executive answer

- **Best answer:** yes, post-Gabrielle FOSAL created an institutional precedent for "responsive designation": properties can have a flood-risk category lowered or removed once mitigation is sufficiently real, evidenced and funded. I found five completed Hawke's Bay Category 2C-after-mitigation cases: 45 Waiohiki properties moved to Category 1 in April 2025; 42 Havelock North properties moved to Category 1 in July 2025; 38 Whirinaki homes removed from FOSAL in early 2026; 11 Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu homes removed from FOSAL in March 2026; and 75 northern Pōrangahau homes removed from FOSAL in July 2026. [HBRC, Waiohiki, 2025](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1829/post-cyclone-land-categorisation-changed-as-community-level-flood-mitigation-picks-up-speed-); [Hastings District Council, Havelock North, 2025](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/home/article/3519/havelock-north-flood-hit-homes-now-category-1?s=1&t=featured); [HBRC, Whirinaki, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1953/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-whirinaki-homes-as-work-ramps-up); [HBRC, Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1973/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-hiti-road-homes-as-project-nears-completion); [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured).
- **The administrative test was not "works fully finished".** The most explicit public threshold is that Category 2C properties can be released once there is a sound concept, consenting pathway, secured land access and confirmed funding; Whirinaki describes the same practical test as a viable mitigation option, land access, consents and funding; Waiohiki was changed when stopbank work commenced; Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu was changed as the project neared completion. [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured); [HBRC, Whirinaki, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1953/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-whirinaki-homes-as-work-ramps-up); [HBRC, Waiohiki, 2025](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1829/post-cyclone-land-categorisation-changed-as-community-level-flood-mitigation-picks-up-speed-); [HBRC, Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1973/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-hiti-road-homes-as-project-nears-completion).
- **Who decides is context-specific.** The original FOSAL information pack put categorisation decisions with territorial authorities and flood-protection delivery with regional councils, but Hawke's Bay's public guidance says HBRC gathers technical data, assesses future severe-weather risk and makes land-categorisation decisions, while city and district councils handle local engagement; Cabinet minutes also show initial categorisations were to be jointly finalised by Ministers and local government after council officer assessments and necessary assurances. [Civil Defence / Cyclone Recovery Unit, 2023](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/emergency-uploads/Cyclone-Gabrielle/Cyclone-Recovery-Unit/Future-of-Severely-Affected-Land-FOSAL-Information-Pack.pdf); [Hastings District Council, land categorisation information](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/information/); [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf).
- **Auckland Category 2P is the closest property-level analogue, but it is a funded mitigation pathway rather than a public completed-recategorisation register.** Auckland requires mitigations to be technically possible, cost no more than 25% of capital value, and be deliverable within two years; normal building/resource consent still applies; and completed works are noted on the LIM while event-history information remains on the property file. [OurAuckland, 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/11/grants-confirmed-to-support-2p-property-mitigations/); [OurAuckland, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/); [OurAuckland, 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/12/category-2p-getting-on-with-your-work/).
- **Could this become business-as-usual outside recovery frameworks? Partly, but not by FOSAL itself.** The National Policy Statement for Natural Hazards 2025 already requires decision-makers to consider existing and proposed mitigation, residual risk, cost-effectiveness and best available information when assessing natural-hazard risk for subdivision, use and development; the New Zealand Flood Map programme also says future generations will incorporate more property-specific and building data, including floor heights and steps taken to reduce risk. But neither source creates a standing process to remove an existing-home label, fund mitigation, issue reassessment letters, or trigger buyouts outside recovery programmes. [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/); [NZ Gazette, NPS-NH, 2025](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045); [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the completed Hawke's Bay cases and the public administrative thresholds are well supported by council and government sources, but I did not find a single official national register of every recategorisation, and the exact technical sign-off papers for each community were not public in the sources I could access.

## Evidence

### What FOSAL was designed to do

The Cyclone Recovery Unit's July 2023 FOSAL information pack defined Category 1 as repair-to-previous-state being sufficient, Category 2P as needing property-level interventions such as drainage or house raising, Category 2C as needing community-level interventions such as repaired or enhanced flood-protection schemes, Category 2A as requiring further assessment, and Category 3 as future severe-weather risk that cannot be sufficiently mitigated for residential use. [Civil Defence / Cyclone Recovery Unit, 2023](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/emergency-uploads/Cyclone-Gabrielle/Cyclone-Recovery-Unit/Future-of-Severely-Affected-Land-FOSAL-Information-Pack.pdf). The same pack described a process of gathering data from insurance, flood plans, geotechnical studies and historical information, notifying initial categorisations, refining them through owner engagement, using individual property assessments where needed, and making Category 3 buyout decisions after final risk assessments and cost-share negotiations. [Civil Defence / Cyclone Recovery Unit, 2023](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/emergency-uploads/Cyclone-Gabrielle/Cyclone-Recovery-Unit/Future-of-Severely-Affected-Land-FOSAL-Information-Pack.pdf).

Cabinet papers released by DPMC show the framework was deliberately provisional and assurance-heavy: officials noted that councils needed detailed information, internal assurance, and a common central/local understanding of risk thresholds before initial assessments could be confirmed. [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf). The same release records Cabinet agreement that council officer assessments and "necessary assurances" would precede minister/local-government finalisation, that the Taskforce would validate categorisations with the insurance sector, and that RiskScape modelling with GNS and NIWA was being explored for further detailed risk assessment. [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf).

### Administrative mechanism extracted

**Trigger:** The completed Hawke's Bay cases all started from Category 2C: an intolerable or unacceptable future flood risk to life that could be reduced by a feasible community-level intervention. [Hastings District Council, next steps](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/next-steps/); [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured).

**Evidence threshold:** The clearest public release threshold is a bundle rather than a single engineering number: sound concept, consenting pathway, secured land access and confirmed funding. [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured). Whirinaki confirms the same kind of threshold with slightly different words: viable mitigation option, secured land access, granted consents and confirmed funding. [HBRC, Whirinaki, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1953/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-whirinaki-homes-as-work-ramps-up). Waiohiki adds a level-of-service example: the planned stopbank was described as 1-in-100-year protection at post-Cyclone Gabrielle levels. [HBRC, Waiohiki, 2025](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1829/post-cyclone-land-categorisation-changed-as-community-level-flood-mitigation-picks-up-speed-). Havelock North shows a stormwater variant of the threshold: HBRC approval of Hastings District Council's flood-mitigation plans followed land access, project design, access works, channel widening, and maintenance planning. [Hastings District Council, Havelock North, 2025](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/home/article/3519/havelock-north-flood-hit-homes-now-category-1?s=1&t=featured).

**Quality assurance:** Public sources do not expose a single QA checklist, but they show three layers: central/local threshold assurance before initial categorisations, insurance-sector validation, and local technical review of flood-protection options and consents. [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf); [Hastings District Council, land categorisation information](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/information/). Hastings District Council's older guidance also says Category 2C* areas could change to Category 1 after quality assurance of existing stopbank rebuilds, and that many such areas had already been recategorised. [Hastings District Council, land categorisation information](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/information/).

**Decision-maker:** For the national FOSAL model, territorial authorities were described as making affected-property and land decisions while regional councils planned and implemented flood-protection measures. [Civil Defence / Cyclone Recovery Unit, 2023](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/emergency-uploads/Cyclone-Gabrielle/Cyclone-Recovery-Unit/Future-of-Severely-Affected-Land-FOSAL-Information-Pack.pdf). In Hawke's Bay, public guidance assigns HBRC the role of gathering technical data, assessing future severe-weather risk and making land-categorisation decisions, with district/city councils delivering local engagement. [Hastings District Council, land categorisation information](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/information/). Cabinet minutes sit above both: proposed categorisations were to be shared with ministers and jointly finalised by ministers and local-government governing bodies after council officer assessment and assurances. [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf).

**Record change:** Before December 2025, Waiohiki could move to Category 1; after Category 1 was retired in Hawke's Bay, Whirinaki, Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu and Pōrangahau were removed from the FOSAL framework and no longer carried a post-Gabrielle risk category. [HBRC, Waiohiki, 2025](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1829/post-cyclone-land-categorisation-changed-as-community-level-flood-mitigation-picks-up-speed-); [Hastings District Council, next steps](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/next-steps/); [HBRC, Whirinaki, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1953/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-whirinaki-homes-as-work-ramps-up); [HBRC, Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1973/flood-risk-category-lifted-for-hiti-road-homes-as-project-nears-completion); [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured).

### Auckland 2P as the property-level analogue

Auckland's Category 2P pathway tests the same idea at property scale: a home is 2P where changes such as raising the house, drainage or retaining works can reduce intolerable risk, and the council created design/consent and construction grants for that work. [OurAuckland, 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/11/grants-confirmed-to-support-2p-property-mitigations/). Auckland's feasibility rule is more explicit than Hawke's Bay's public 2C release rule: mitigation must be effective, cost no more than 25% of capital value, and be reasonably deliverable within two years. [OurAuckland, 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/11/grants-confirmed-to-support-2p-property-mitigations/); [OurAuckland, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/).

The Auckland scheme also shows the limits of "responsive designation" once the work is done: the council says the LIM will be updated to note completed risk-mitigation works, but the underlying event-history information remains on the property file. [OurAuckland, 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/12/category-2p-getting-on-with-your-work/). If a proposed 2P mitigation is declined resource or building consent, Auckland says the property changes to Category 3 and there are no extra grants for rescoping, which makes consentability an actual gate rather than an administrative formality. [OurAuckland, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/).

### Can this become business-as-usual outside FOSAL?

The strongest route to business-as-usual is not the recovery category labels themselves, because Hawke's Bay is retiring Category 1 and will progressively retire Category 3 as updated flood-hazard modelling is folded into the HBRC hazard portal. [Hastings District Council, next steps](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/next-steps/). Instead, the route is the ordinary planning and hazard-information system: the NPS-NH 2025 requires decision-makers to consider existing and proposed mitigation measures, residual risk beyond design thresholds, mitigation cost-effectiveness, and best available information when assessing natural-hazard risk for subdivision, use and development. [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/); [NZ Gazette, NPS-NH, 2025](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2025-sl7045).

The New Zealand Flood Map programme points in the same direction but remains informational. MfE says the map will bring council flood information and national modelling together, support planning and adaptation decisions, provide homeowners/buyers/sellers with flood-risk information, and in future incorporate more property-specific building data such as floor heights and steps taken to reduce risk. [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/). That supports a future data architecture for responsive risk information, but it does not itself create a statutory reassessment application, mandatory council decision timeframe, QA standard, funding source, appeal right, LIM wording rule, or insurance obligation. [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/).

My conclusion is therefore narrow: **responsive designation can become business-as-usual as a risk-assessment principle for new consents and public flood data; it has not yet become business-as-usual as a funded, rights-bearing recategorisation pathway for existing homes.** [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/); [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [Hastings District Council, next steps](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/next-steps/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A national or HBRC register of all FOSAL category changes, with dates and reasons, would change the "every case found" section if it showed additional completed recategorisation-after-mitigation cases or corrected the property counts. I found public notices and project documents, not a comprehensive official register. [HBRC, Waiohiki, 2025](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/news/article/1829/post-cyclone-land-categorisation-changed-as-community-level-flood-mitigation-picks-up-speed-); [Hastings District Council, Havelock North, 2025](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/home/article/3519/havelock-north-flood-hit-homes-now-category-1?s=1&t=featured); [Hastings District Council, next steps](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/next-steps/).
- Technical sign-off papers for Waiohiki, Whirinaki, Ōhiti Road/Ōmāhu and Pōrangahau would raise confidence in the precise QA mechanism: the public record gives thresholds such as concept, consent pathway, land access and funding, but not the full modelling review, peer review, governance paper or legal basis for each release. [HBRC, Pōrangahau, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/home/article/2013/flood-risk-category-removed-for-prangahau-homes-as-resilience-project-advances?s=1&t=featured); [DPMC OIA release, 2024](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-09/dpmc-roia-oia-2023-24-0985.pdf).
- A statutory or council policy creating an ordinary reassessment right for existing homes after mitigation, including application requirements, independent QA, decision-maker, appeal/review rights, LIM wording and insurer notification, would change the answer from "precedent only" to "business-as-usual mechanism." The NPS-NH and New Zealand Flood Map currently support better risk assessment and data but do not supply that pathway. [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/); [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map, 2026](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/).
- Evidence that completed Auckland 2P works routinely lead to a category being removed, rather than a LIM note recording mitigation while retaining event history, would strengthen the property-level precedent. The Auckland source I found says completed works are noted on the LIM and underlying event information remains on the property file. [OurAuckland, 2025](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/12/category-2p-getting-on-with-your-work/).
- This finding needs review by a planner, resource-management lawyer or council practitioner before being used as a design brief for a new mechanism, because the operational legal basis for removing FOSAL labels and the interaction with LIMs, district plans, existing-use rights and insurance practice was only partly visible in public sources. [Hastings District Council, land categorisation information](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/land-categorisation-hb/information/); [MfE, NPS-NH text, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/publications/national-policy-statement-for-natural-hazards/).

## Open follow-up questions

- What exact QA pack did HBRC require before removing Category 2C from each completed community: hydraulic model version, design standard, peer review, consent status, land access, funding agreement and residual-risk communication?
- Should the New Zealand Flood Map include a formal "mitigation evidence" field, such as verified floor height, stopbank level of service or completed property works, and who should certify it?
- How should LIMs distinguish between historical event exposure, current modelled risk and verified mitigation, so buyers see both residual risk and risk reduction?
- Would insurers recognise a council-certified mitigation or FOSAL-removal decision in underwriting, excesses or premiums, and what evidence would they require?
