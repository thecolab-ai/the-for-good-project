# NZ's teacher shortage looks different depending which of five measures you read, and the gap is concentrated in secondary STEM, te reo Māori and a handful of regions

## Executive answer

- **Reconciled picture, medium confidence:** New Zealand does not have one teacher shortage — it has at least five different measures that each capture a different failure mode, and they currently point in different directions. **Primary** is projected to have a small national *surplus* in 2026-2028 under the Ministry's central scenario. **Secondary** is projected to have a persistent national *shortfall* (-710 teachers in 2026, -510 in 2027, -190 in 2028 under the medium scenario), concentrated in STEM subjects and te reo Māori, and unevenly spread across regions and hard-to-staff schools. [MoE, 2025 Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, published Feb 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc) (archived: [Wayback, 2026-07-05](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705095323/https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc)); independently reported by [RNZ, 27 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/588154/education-ministry-figures-reveal-teacher-shortage-worse-than-previous-forecast).
- **The five measures move independently, and each tells you something different:**
  1. **Live/advertised vacancies** (Education Gazette): a raw, constantly-changing count of open ads across *all* education roles (early learning to tertiary, including non-teaching roles like teacher aides and principals) — 568 listed when checked on 2026-07-05, up from 445 in a mid-January 2026 news snapshot. It is the noisiest and least specific measure. [Education Gazette vacancies](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/vacancies/) (live count, 2026-07-05, no stable archive possible — see limitations); [NZ Herald, 19 Jan 2026 snapshot](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/schools-start-year-hundreds-of-teachers-short-as-vacancies-climb-12/QBPJ55TWCNE7TPWNVSO4MMNJWM/).
  2. **Unadvertised vacancies**: 24% of secondary schools had a vacancy not yet advertised as of 9 March 2026 (0.41 positions/school, ≈209 positions nationally) — down sharply from 34%/0.69 per school in 2025 but still above the pre-COVID 2019 baseline (14%/0.19), with the positions-per-school rate a little over twice as high. [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655) (archived: [Wayback, 2026-07-05](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705095435/https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)).
  3. **Unfilled appointments**: among advertised secondary positions with sufficient outcome information, 20% could not be filled by a registered teacher at all as of the survey date — down from the 2023-2025 peak period and essentially level with 2019 (19%), but still well above 2013 (8%). [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); corroborated by [RNZ, 3 July 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/657354/one-in-five-secondary-teacher-vacancies-unable-to-be-filled-survey).
  4. **LAT-filled roles** (Limited Authority to Teach, i.e. untrained/unqualified people teaching): 8% of advertised positions with known outcomes were filled by LAT holders — the highest LAT-fill rate since 2003 — and separately, 47% of principals had to employ at least one untrained/unqualified teacher this year (down from a 56-57% peak in 2024-2025, but about 2.6-2.8x the 2013/2019 baseline of 17-18%). [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
  5. **Non-specialist ("out-of-field") teaching**: 47% of principals had teachers covering subjects outside their specialism because no specialist could be found, and 24% had to cancel or transfer a class (e.g. to Te Kura/correspondence) for the same reason. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
- **Subject pattern:** shortages concentrate in STEM (mathematics, physics, science, digital/hard materials technology) and te reo Māori across both the Ministry's demand-supply model and the Gazette/PPTA measures — these are flagged as "hard to staff" independent of region. [MoE, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); [NZ Herald, 19 Jan 2026](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/schools-start-year-hundreds-of-teachers-short-as-vacancies-climb-12/QBPJ55TWCNE7TPWNVSO4MMNJWM/) (maths/physics ~13% of secondary Gazette listings in its Jan snapshot).
- **Regional pattern:** for secondary, the largest 2026 shortfalls by headcount are Auckland North and West (-140), Auckland South and Southwest (-140) and Otago (-90); by percentage of regional demand, Auckland South and Southwest (-6.3%), Otago (-6.2%) and Auckland North and West (-4.2%) are worst-affected. For primary — where the *national* picture is a small surplus — Waikato (-140), Bay of Plenty (-100) and Northland (-70) still show shortfalls by headcount, and Taranaki (-5.0%), Northland (-4.5%) and Bay of Plenty (-3.7%) by percentage. [MoE, 2026, pp.6, 16-18](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The Ministry projection and PPTA survey are both official/established, current (published Feb and June 2026), methodologically transparent, and independently corroborated by RNZ news reporting of the same releases. Confidence is capped at Medium rather than High because (a) the live-vacancy measure could not be broken down by subject/region with the tooling available in this research pass (see limitations), (b) the PPTA survey has a 30% response rate from principals — a real but incomplete sample — and (c) the two official sources use different units (headcount projections vs. per-principal-reported percentages), so "reconciling" them is more a matter of triangulation than a single combined number.

## Evidence

### Measure 1 — Live/advertised vacancies (Education Gazette)

The Education Gazette is the single national job board for state and state-integrated education vacancies, covering early learning through tertiary. Fetched directly on 2026-07-05, its vacancies page showed **568 total listed vacancies** across all sectors and role types (teacher, teacher aide, principal, etc.), with filters for region, sector, learning area, employment type and tenure. [Education Gazette vacancies](https://gazette.education.govt.nz/vacancies/), accessed 2026-07-05.

This is a genuinely different measure from the other four: it is a live, unmanaged count of *currently open advertisements*, not a survey of principals' actual staffing state, and it is not restricted to classroom teachers. A dated news snapshot from 19 January 2026 gives a directly comparable but smaller total — 445 vacancies, up 12% year-on-year, with 164 early-childhood, 107 primary and 132 secondary listings, about 75% in the North Island and 125 in Auckland alone, and maths/physics the hardest-to-fill secondary subjects at 18 vacancies (~13% of secondary listings). That article also records Education Minister Erica Stanford disputing the headline Gazette number's meaning, noting many listed vacancies are not full-time roles — a useful caution against reading the raw Gazette count as a teacher-FTE shortfall. [NZ Herald, 19 Jan 2026](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/schools-start-year-hundreds-of-teachers-short-as-vacancies-climb-12/QBPJ55TWCNE7TPWNVSO4MMNJWM/).

The rise from 445 (19 Jan) to 568 (5 Jul, this research pass) is consistent with normal within-year hiring-cycle variation (schools advertise more heavily around term starts) and should not be read as a 28% worsening of the underlying shortage — it is two different points in the advertising cycle, not a trend line. Comparable dated Gazette snapshots at the same point in successive years would be needed to say anything about the trend from this measure alone.

### Measure 2 — Unadvertised vacancies (PPTA staffing survey)

The PPTA has run an annual Term 1 survey of secondary/composite school principals since 1996; the 2026 edition (its 30th) drew responses from 155 principals (30% of those surveyed), baselined to 9 March 2026. [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026, p.2](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

Twenty-four percent of responding schools had at least one vacancy that had **not yet been advertised** at the survey date, equivalent to 0.41 positions per school, which the report scales to roughly 209 unadvertised positions nationally. This is down substantially from 2025 (34% of schools, 0.69 per school) but remains far above the pre-shortage baseline: 14% of schools/0.19 per school in 2019, and 9%/0.15 in 2013. [PPTA, 2026, p.5](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655). This measure captures shortage that is invisible to both the Gazette count and to any "advertised positions" tally — schools that know they will be short-staffed but have not yet gone to market.

### Measure 3 — Unfilled appointments

The survey recorded 249 advertised actual vacancy positions, but only **101 positions** (87 permanent, 14 fixed term) had sufficient information to identify how they had been filled; outcome information was missing for 43% of advertised positions and a further 16% had not yet closed. Among the known-outcome positions, **20% could not be filled by a registered teacher at all**, and a further 8% were filled by holders of a Limited Authority to Teach (LAT) rather than a registered/qualified teacher. [PPTA, 2026, pp.5, 11](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655). The report's own time series (back to 2003) shows the combined share of positions not filled by a registered teacher remains the highest outside the acute 2023-2025 period; the "could not be filled" rate alone improved year-on-year and was essentially level with 2019 (19%), but still well above 2013 (8%). [PPTA, 2026, p.11, Figure 5](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

RNZ's coverage of the same report, published 3 July 2026, independently confirms these figures — "one in five" vacancies unfilled, 23% with no suitable applicant, 28% with only one, and notes the average number of NZ-trained applicants per vacancy (2.8-2.9) is among the lowest on record. [RNZ, 3 July 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/657354/one-in-five-secondary-teacher-vacancies-unable-to-be-filled-survey). The PPTA president's framing — the shortage has "moved from an availability crisis to a suitability crisis" — is that raw applicant numbers have partly recovered (13.3 applicants per position on average in 2026 vs 11.4 in 2025) but the *suitable* applicant share has kept falling, especially for New Zealand-trained applicants (49% suitable in 2026, down from 61-66% in 2020-2022 and a near-historic low). [PPTA, 2026, pp.3, 9-10](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

### Measure 4 — LAT-filled roles and untrained/unqualified appointments

Two related but distinct statistics: (a) 8% of known-outcome advertised positions were filled by LAT holders (measure 3's flip side), and (b) at the whole-school level, **47% of principals reported employing at least one untrained or unqualified teacher** in the first term of 2026 because they could not find a trained, qualified candidate — an average of 1.02 such appointments per school (2.15 among schools that made at least one). [PPTA, 2026, pp.11-12](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655). This is down from a 56-57% peak in 2024-2025 but about 2.6-2.8x the 2013/2019 baseline of 17-18%; the report's full 2002-2026 time series (Figure 6) shows this measure was flat around 15-28% for two decades before jumping to 36-57% from 2021 onward. [PPTA, 2026, p.12](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

The Ministry's own TDS report corroborates this from the supply side: it notes reliance on untrained LAT teachers is "higher than at any time during the recent crisis and at levels not seen since 2003," flags this as a particular concern given major concurrent curriculum/assessment changes, and separately notes that Waikato and Manawatū-Whanganui in particular are "increasingly relying on untrained teachers with Limited Authority to Teach," with Kaupapa Māori and Māori-medium kura facing persistent shortages of qualified kaiako because language and cultural expertise narrows the pool further. [MoE, 2026, p.16](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf); [PPTA, 2026, p.3](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

### Measure 5 — Non-specialist ("out-of-field") teaching and course cancellation

Forty-seven percent of principals had teachers working outside their specialist subject area this year because no specialist could be found (down from a high of 66% in 2025, above the 2019 rate of 39%, and more than triple 2013's 15%). Twenty-four percent of principals had to cancel a class or transfer it to distance provision (e.g. Te Kura, a polytechnic, or electronic delivery) for the same reason — an average of 0.52 classes per school — again down from 2024/2025 peaks (29-30%) but above 2019 (18%) and 2013 (15%). Affected subjects span the sciences, technology, commerce, languages, mathematics, and the arts. [PPTA, 2026, pp.16-17](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).

This measure is arguably the closest proxy for the "signal that actually reaches students" — a school can technically report zero unfilled vacancies while still running large numbers of classes with non-specialist or LAT staff, or cutting the class outright. It is not visible at all in the Gazette live-vacancy count.

### Reconciling the five measures against the Ministry's demand-supply projection

The Ministry of Education's Teacher Demand and Supply (TDS) Planning Projection, now in its eighth annual edition, is a distinct sixth data point: rather than surveying what happened, it forecasts headcount demand (driven by school rolls, staffing ratios, and policy-driven entitlements like release time) against three supply scenarios (low/medium/high), based on retention rates, new overseas teachers, returning teachers, and training pipeline throughput. [MoE, 2026, pp.7, 19-21](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). Its scope explicitly includes "qualified teachers and untrained teachers on a Limited Authority to Teach (LAT) employed on a permanent or fixed-term basis," and explicitly *excludes* day-relief/reliever teachers — so it is measuring a different population than the Gazette (which includes non-teaching roles) and closer to, but still distinct from, the PPTA survey's per-school reporting. [MoE, 2026, p.7](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf).

Under the medium (most-likely) supply scenario:

| Sector | 2026 gap | 2027 gap | 2028 gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary | +530 | +830 | +1,350 |
| Secondary | -710 | -510 | -190 |

[MoE, 2026, pp.10, 12, Tables 1-2](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). RNZ's independent report on the same release confirms these exact figures and notes the 2026 secondary shortfall is worse than the previous year's forecast (revised up from an earlier -550 estimate for 2026). [RNZ, 27 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/588154/education-ministry-figures-reveal-teacher-shortage-worse-than-previous-forecast).

The Ministry itself frames the 2026 primary surplus as "very small — less than 1% of the workforce (around 0.25 of a teacher per school)" and explicitly warns it "will not be evenly distributed, with some regions and individual schools continuing to experience recruitment challenges" — i.e. even the "surplus" sector shows all five of the above shortage symptoms in specific regions and hard-to-staff schools. [MoE, 2026, p.4](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). For secondary, the Ministry states plainly: "secondary teachers are not easily interchangeable across subjects, and shortages in areas such as sciences, technology, mathematics and te reo Māori cannot be offset by surplus in other subjects" — the core reason a national headcount gap of "only" 710 still produces the acute subject-level and school-level pain visible in the PPTA and Gazette measures. [MoE, 2026, p.13](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf).

### Regional detail

The Ministry's 2026 TDS is only the second edition to publish sub-national projections, now covering 15 regional councils plus a three-way split of Auckland (Central and East / North and West / South and Southwest). [MoE, 2026, p.16](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). For 2026:

- **Primary shortfalls (headcount):** Waikato (-140), Bay of Plenty (-100), Northland (-70). **By percentage of regional demand:** Taranaki (-5.0%), Northland (-4.5%), Bay of Plenty (-3.7%).
- **Secondary shortfalls (headcount):** Auckland North and West (-140), Auckland South and Southwest (-140), Otago (-90). **By percentage:** Auckland South and Southwest (-6.3%), Otago (-6.2%), Auckland North and West (-4.2%).

[MoE, 2026, p.6](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). Secondary shortages are described as widespread — gaps projected in 13 of the 17 regions for 2026 — while primary shortages concentrate in smaller/more rural regions with more Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium kura (Northland, Waikato, Taranaki, Bay of Plenty). [MoE, 2026, pp.16-18](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf). The NZ Herald's January 2026 Gazette snapshot independently corroborates the North Island/Auckland concentration from the live-vacancy side (75% of listings in the North Island, 125 of 445 in Auckland alone). [NZ Herald, 19 Jan 2026](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/schools-start-year-hundreds-of-teachers-short-as-vacancies-climb-12/QBPJ55TWCNE7TPWNVSO4MMNJWM/).

This finding does not attempt the deeper equity-index/decile analysis of *which schools* within a region are hardest-hit — that is the explicit scope of a sibling issue in this stream (see [#506](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/506)) and is deliberately left there.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A subject/region breakdown of the live Gazette count** would strengthen or overturn the "Gazette is too noisy to use for reconciliation" conclusion. The Gazette site supports region/sector/learning-area filters in its UI and via URL parameters (e.g. `?LearningAreasStrand=mathematics`, confirmed to exist because it appears in Google's indexed URLs and was described correctly by a news article), but this research's automated fetch tooling (WebFetch) consistently returned the unfiltered total (568) regardless of query parameters supplied — the filters appear to be applied client-side by JavaScript rather than reflected in server-rendered output reachable by simple fetch. A follow-up with a real headless-browser session (e.g. the repo's `agent-browser`/Chrome DevTools MCP path) could very likely extract a live subject-by-region breakdown directly, which would substantially sharpen measure 1. **This is flagged as a genuine capability gap, not a dead end** — see Open follow-up questions.
- **A newer PPTA survey or Ministry TDS release** would supersede these numbers; both are annual and the next editions (2027) will show whether the 2026 "easing" (falling unfilled/non-specialist rates alongside a still-worsening suitability rate) is a real trend or a one-year blip — the PPTA report itself flags the suitability decline as possibly "a one-off... or...the start of a worrying new trend." [PPTA, 2026, p.3](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655).
- **Independent verification of the NZ Herald's "1,550 primary / 1,035 secondary teachers needed over three years"** figure, which surfaced in the same article as the January Gazette snapshot, was not attempted here — it appears to use a different framing (gross recruitment need, not the net demand-supply gap reported in the TDS tables above) and citing it without confirming its derivation risked conflating two different metrics. It is mentioned here only as a flag for a future researcher, not as a verified claim of this finding.
- **What could not be verified:** the exact current (2026-07-05) subject/region composition of live Gazette vacancies; whether the Gazette's "568" includes duplicate cross-postings or expired-but-not-removed listings; and whether the PPTA's 30% principal response rate is representative (the report does not test for non-response bias).

## Open follow-up questions

- Could a headless-browser fetch (this repo already has `scripts/fetch.mjs` and Chrome DevTools MCP access) extract a live, filterable subject-by-region Gazette vacancy count, to make measure 1 genuinely comparable to measures 2-5? This looks buildable as a small script and would be a strong candidate for a `.skills` request (`nz-education-vacancies` or similar) so future researchers don't re-solve the JS-filter problem — flagged for a future contributor, not opened as an issue here per this task's scope.
- How does the Ministry's TDS regional clustering (Tables 3-4 in the source PDF) map onto the Equity Index / priority-staffing-school analysis being done in [#506](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/506)? The two should be read together.
- Does the rise in Gazette listings from 445 (19 Jan) to 568 (5 Jul) reflect a real mid-year increase in advertised need, normal seasonal cycling, or accumulation of stale listings? A same-month year-over-year comparison (e.g. July 2025 vs July 2026) would settle this and was outside this pass's scope.
