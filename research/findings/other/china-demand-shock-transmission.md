# A 25% or 50% China-facing demand shock would be material, but GDP and jobs depend more on substitution than on gross receipts

## Executive answer

- I answer the issue as a transparent stress test, not a forecast: starting from Stats NZ's March 2026 quarter China exports of NZ$6.381b, annualising that quarter gives a NZ$25.526b China-facing goods-and-services receipt base, while the Stats NZ March 2026 GDP CSV gives nominal annual GDP of NZ$450.959b, so the annualised China-facing receipt base is about 5.7% of nominal GDP before any input-output, substitution, price, or exchange-rate adjustment. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx); [Stats NZ GDP March 2026 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Gross-domestic-product/Gross-domestic-product-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/gross-domestic-product-march-2026-quarter.csv)
- I run a sensitivity with three first-year recapture assumptions (15%, 30%, 50%), where “recaptured” means the share of gross lost China-facing receipts offset by alternative buyers, lower prices that preserve volume, inventories, exchange-rate movement, or delayed contracts. A 25% shock implies net receipts at risk between NZ$5.42b and NZ$3.19b and domestic GDP at risk between NZ$4.51b and NZ$2.65b; a 50% shock implies NZ$10.85b to NZ$6.38b and NZ$9.02b to NZ$5.30b respectively. The 30% row is a convenience midpoint for communication, not a calibrated estimate. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx); [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15)
- I do not claim the 15/30/50% split is a sourced forecast: it is a stress-test scaffold to show how much the output moves with recapture. No official source among those I checked provides short-run sector-level recapture rates for a China-specific shock; this is the primary uncertainty in the result. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx); [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15)
- Employment should be stated as wage-income and full-year employee-count equivalents, not as predicted layoffs: the same input-output coefficients put compensation of employees at 0.364 per dollar of export final demand, so the mid-recapture (30%) 25% shock exposes about NZ$1.63b of wage income, or about 20,000 all-industry employee-count equivalents at the Stats NZ AES all-industry wage-per-employee-count proxy; the mid-recapture (30%) 50% shock exposes about NZ$3.25b of wage income, or about 40,000 employee-count equivalents. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv)
- Public revenue is exposed mainly through weaker wages, profits, GST bases, and nominal GDP, not through export receipts directly: applying Treasury BEFU 2026 fiscal sensitivities suggests roughly NZ$0.9b to NZ$1.0b less core Crown tax revenue under a mid-recapture (30%) 25% shock, and roughly NZ$1.9b to NZ$2.1b under a mid-recapture (30%) 50% shock, before any discretionary policy response. [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Low - the trade, GDP, input-output, AES, and Treasury fiscal baselines are official and high-confidence, while substitution and lag timing are explicit scenario assumptions with no short-run calibrated benchmark point in the public data used here.

## Evidence

### Scope and baseline

The stress-test baseline is all China-facing exports of goods and services in the March 2026 quarter because Stats NZ's international trade workbook publishes China as a destination for both goods and services in the same table; it records NZ$5.215b of goods exports, NZ$1.166b of services exports, and NZ$6.381b of total exports to China in that quarter. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx) Annualising one quarter is a modelling assumption rather than a seasonally adjusted annual forecast; the goods-only cross-check is Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade, which records NZ$19.885b of annual merchandise exports to China in the year ended May 2026. [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx)

The direct China-exposure mix is concentrated enough that sector coefficients matter: in the March 2026 quarter, the six China-destination rows in Stats NZ's top-export table were milk powder, butter, and cheese at NZ$2.366b; travel at NZ$1.034b; logs, wood, and wood articles at NZ$0.675b; meat and edible offal at NZ$0.614b; preparations of milk, cereals, flour, and starch at NZ$0.354b; and fruit at NZ$0.249b, together NZ$5.293b or about 83% of China-facing total exports in that workbook. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx)

Nominal GDP is the right denominator for nominal receipt shocks because Stats NZ's GDP CSV gives nominal expenditure GDP of NZ$450.959b for the year ended March 2026, and RBNZ's GDP series notes that expenditure-based GDP is available in current prices while production and expenditure GDP are also published as chain-volume measures. [Stats NZ GDP March 2026 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Gross-domestic-product/Gross-domestic-product-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/gross-domestic-product-march-2026-quarter.csv); [RBNZ GDP series](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/statistics/series/economic-indicators/gross-domestic-product)

### Receipts are not GDP

Stats NZ says input-output tables describe relationships between industries, products, and users, and its March 2020 release breaks the economy into 109 industries and 197 product groups; Stats NZ also warns that the March 2020 table is the latest available balanced IO table at the time of this finding, with the March 2024 table scheduled for 16 July 2026. [Stats NZ IO release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020/) I therefore use the 2020 IO table for structural coefficients, not as a current receipt baseline; that is appropriate because the release says IO tables' chief purpose is to show structural relationships over an entire year, while the latest current-dollar trade and GDP baselines come from 2026 releases. [Stats NZ IO release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020/); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx)

For all exports as a final-demand category, Stats NZ's IO table gives cumulative primary-input coefficients of 0.169 for imports, 0.364 for compensation of employees, 0.284 for operating surplus, 0.138 for consumption of fixed capital, 0.024 for other taxes on production, 0.035 for taxes on products, and -0.015 for subsidies, so the implied domestic GDP coefficient is 0.831 and the net tax/subsidy component inside GDP is 0.044 per dollar of export final demand. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx) RBNZ's GDP notes make the same accounting point in plain terms: production GDP is value added after deducting goods and services used in production, while expenditure GDP adds exports because they are produced in New Zealand and subtracts imports because they are produced elsewhere. [RBNZ GDP series](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/statistics/series/economic-indicators/gross-domestic-product)

Sector coefficients vary around the export average: the IO domestic GDP coefficient is 0.847 for dairy product manufacturing, 0.844 for meat and meat product manufacturing, 0.889 for forestry and logging, 0.845 for wood product manufacturing, 0.860 for horticulture and fruit growing, 0.869 for accommodation, 0.865 for food and beverage services, 0.720 for air and space transport, and 0.929 for tertiary education. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx) The practical implication is that an education-heavy or accommodation-heavy services shock carries more domestic value added per dollar than an air-transport-heavy shock, while dairy, meat, horticulture, and forestry goods are all close to the aggregate export coefficient. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx)

### Scenario mechanics

This finding uses three first-year substitution assumptions: 15% recaptured, 30% recaptured, and 50% recaptured, where "recaptured" means the share of gross lost China receipts avoided through alternative buyers, lower prices that preserve volume, inventories, exchange-rate movement, or delayed contracts rather than disappearing from New Zealand final demand. This is an assumption, not an observed statistic, because public Stats NZ trade tables identify historical destination totals but do not publish a short-run sector-by-sector redirection schedule for a China shock. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx)

Treasury and RBNZ both describe broad China channels (including volumes, prices, services, imports, global commodity prices, and terms of trade), which is why this table should be read as a first-round IO stress test, not a full macro forecast. In this context the recapture and timing knobs are assumptions, and their width is shown explicitly. [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15); [Treasury WP 13/16](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/outlook-chinas-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-exports-wp-13-16); [RBNZ Analytical Note 2014/06](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2014/an2014-06.pdf); [RBNZ Analytical Note 2026/03](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2026/tariff-ripples-modelling-the-effects-of-us-trade-policy-on-the-new-zealand-economy.pdf)

### Employment transmission

Stats NZ's AES 2025 provisional CSV records all-industry total income of NZ$976.077b, salaries and wages paid of NZ$160.833b, and total income per employee count of NZ$488,300; dividing income by income per employee count gives about 1.999m employee-count equivalents, and dividing salaries and wages by that employee count gives about NZ$80,460 of salaries and wages per employee-count equivalent. [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/); [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv)

Using the all-industry AES wage proxy, the compensation-at-risk column in the scenario table corresponds to about 14,000 to 25,000 full-year employee-count equivalents for a 25% shock and about 29,000 to 49,000 for a 50% shock, with the mid recapture (30%) case around 20,000 and 40,000. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv) These are not layoff forecasts because firms can absorb demand shocks through hours, margins, inventories, prices, debt, owners' income, and delayed investment before reducing headcount; they are a comparable measure of full-year wage income exposed under the IO coefficients. [Stats NZ IO release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020/); [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/)

The sector wage proxy also changes the job-equivalent interpretation: AES 2025 implies about NZ$85,000 salaries and wages per employee-count equivalent in food product manufacturing, about NZ$97,000 in forestry and logging, about NZ$45,000 in horticulture and fruit growing, about NZ$43,000 in accommodation, and about NZ$39,000 in food and beverage services. [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv) A travel/hospitality-heavy shock therefore looks larger in employee-count equivalents per dollar of wage income than a forestry or food-processing shock, while an export-processing-heavy shock can show more impact in operating surplus, farm/processor cashflow, and regional suppliers. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv)

### Regional transmission

Stats NZ regional GDP gives current regional totals for the year ended March 2025, but its public CSV has 2025 rows for total regional GDP and 2024 rows for industry-by-region GDP, so the most detailed public regional-industry proxy I used is the year ended March 2024 industry split. [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025/); [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv) This is an exposure proxy, not a China-specific regional allocation, because the public table does not say which region produced China-bound exports or hosted China-origin visitors and students. [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv)

Using the 2024 industry-by-region GDP proxy, agriculture exposure is concentrated in Canterbury at 19.6% of national agriculture GDP, Waikato at 16.7%, Southland at 9.9%, Bay of Plenty at 9.6%, and Manawatu-Whanganui at 7.6%; manufacturing exposure is concentrated in Auckland at 34.8%, Canterbury at 14.6%, Waikato at 11.7%, Bay of Plenty at 5.6%, and Southland at 4.9%. [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv) Travel-facing exposure is more Auckland/Otago/Canterbury weighted: accommodation GDP is led by Auckland at 24.2%, Otago at 17.2%, and Canterbury at 16.6%, while food and beverage services GDP is led by Auckland at 40.2%, Canterbury at 12.1%, and Wellington at 11.0%. [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv)

The regional conclusion is therefore qualitative but important: a dairy/meat/fruit-heavy China shock transmits first through Waikato, Canterbury, Southland, Bay of Plenty, Hawke's Bay, Manawatu-Whanganui, processors, farms, ports, and logistics, while a travel/education-heavy China shock transmits first through Auckland, Otago, Canterbury, Wellington, education providers, accommodation, food service, and transport. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx); [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv)

### Public revenue transmission

Treasury's BEFU 2026 says nominal GDP matters for the fiscal position because tax is paid on nominal income, and it forecasts core Crown tax revenue at 27.6% of GDP in 2025/26 and around 28.0% to 28.8% over the forecast years shown in Table 1.1 and the fiscal outlook. [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf) Treasury's BEFU 2026 downside scenario also gives an empirical fiscal sensitivity: a cumulative NZ$17b reduction in nominal GDP over the forecast period is associated with aggregate tax receipts around NZ$4.2b lower over the forecast period, or about 25% of the nominal GDP shortfall. [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf)

Applying a 25% to 28% core Crown tax sensitivity to the domestic GDP-at-risk column gives about NZ$0.7b to NZ$1.3b less tax revenue for the modelled 25% China shock range and about NZ$1.3b to NZ$2.5b for the modelled 50% China shock range, with the mid recapture (30%) cases around NZ$0.9b to NZ$1.0b and NZ$1.9b to NZ$2.1b. [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf); [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx) That is a broad fiscal exposure, not a direct export-tax calculation, because the main tax bases are wages, profits, GST-able domestic spending, and nominal incomes rather than exports themselves. [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf)

### Prices, exchange rates, and lag times

The shock could be smaller in real GDP than in nominal receipts if exporters redirect product at lower prices, if the New Zealand dollar depreciates, or if inventories smooth the hit, but it could be larger in income and public revenue if China weakness lowers global commodity prices and the terms of trade. [RBNZ Analytical Note 2014/06](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2014/an2014-06.pdf); [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15) This matters because RBNZ argues New Zealand's most significant China linkage may be through terms of trade and global prices rather than only the direct trade channel, while Treasury's China paper says China's expansion affected New Zealand through merchandise exports, services exports, imports, and other channels. [RBNZ Analytical Note 2014/06](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2014/an2014-06.pdf); [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15)

The lag profile below is an explicit planning assumption, not an official forecast. Public sources support that transmission occurs through receipts, then income/output, then slower real-side adjustment, but they do not provide sector-by-sector quarter-by-quarter timing for a China-specific shock. For planning use (low-confidence), I treat these as indicative windows:
receipts/cashflow: early adjustment around 0–2 quarters;  
wage, hours, and supplier adjustment: around 1–4 quarters;  
fiscal effects: the affected tax year and one cycle afterwards;  
capital, land-use, course-enrolment, route-capacity, and migration: 1–3 years. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Treasury BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/befu26.pdf); [RBNZ Analytical Note 2026/03](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2026/tariff-ripples-modelling-the-effects-of-us-trade-policy-on-the-new-zealand-economy.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A current computable general equilibrium or macro-econometric scenario for a China-specific New Zealand export, education, and tourism shock would supersede this first-round IO model, because it would endogenise substitution, exchange rates, prices, monetary policy, and second-round demand effects. [RBNZ Analytical Note 2026/03](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2026/tariff-ripples-modelling-the-effects-of-us-trade-policy-on-the-new-zealand-economy.pdf)
- Stats NZ's scheduled March 2024 input-output table, due 16 July 2026 according to the March 2020 IO release page, could change the domestic value-added, compensation, import, and tax coefficients used here. [Stats NZ IO release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020/)
- A product-by-destination dataset with firm-level or region-level redirection outcomes after a real China demand fall would tighten the substitution assumption; I could not verify that from public aggregate Stats NZ trade tables. [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx)
- China-origin visitor-spend by region, China-origin student fee/living-cost split by provider and region, and China-bound commodity exports by production region would change the regional ranking; I found regional GDP by industry, but not a public China-specific regional allocation. [Stats NZ regional GDP 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regional-gross-domestic-product/Regional-gross-domestic-product-Year-ended-March-2025/Download-data/regional-gross-domestic-product-year-ended-march-2025.csv)
- Publicly available sector-specific export-margin data by destination would improve the operating-surplus and public-revenue estimates; I used Stats NZ IO operating-surplus coefficients and AES industry surplus/wage ratios because I could not verify public China-specific export margins for the exposed sectors. [Stats NZ IO workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/National-accounts-input-output-tables/National-accounts-input-output-tables-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/national-accounts-input-output-tables-year-ended-march-2020-revised-22-december-2021.xlsx); [Stats NZ AES 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-enterprise-survey/Annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional/Download-data/annual-enterprise-survey-2025-financial-year-provisional.csv)
- A human economist or sector expert should review the substitution bands before this is used for policy advice, because the difference between 15%, 30%, and 50% first-year recapture is larger than most accounting refinements in this finding. [Treasury WP 13/15](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/chinas-recent-growth-and-its-impact-new-zealand-economy-wp-13-15); [RBNZ Analytical Note 2014/06](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/-/media/project/sites/rbnz/files/publications/analytical-notes/2014/an2014-06.pdf)

## Open follow-up questions

- What product-market evidence supports a 15%, 30%, or 50% first-year recapture rate for dairy products, logs, meat, fruit, international education, and visitor spending separately?
- Can MBIE, Stats NZ, ENZ, or tourism data produce a China-origin regional exposure table that separates Auckland education exposure from Otago/Canterbury visitor exposure and provincial commodity exposure?
- How would the result change under the Stats NZ March 2024 input-output table after it is released, especially for travel, education, air transport, dairy processing, forestry, and food manufacturing?
- What would a full Treasury/RBNZ-style macro scenario show once exchange-rate depreciation, monetary-policy response, global commodity prices, and household confidence are endogenised?
