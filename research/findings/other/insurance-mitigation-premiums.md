# NZ home insurers publish risk-based hazard pricing, but not a verified property-mitigation premium credit pathway

## Executive answer

- **Best answer:** NZ home insurers increasingly price natural-hazard risk at address or property level, but I found no published NZ equivalent of a FORTIFIED-style certificate that automatically or predictably converts verified property works into a premium discount. RBNZ says dwelling premiums increasingly reflect detailed property flood and earthquake risk, while the insurer pages surveyed show risk-based pricing by location/address, natural-hazard model, building characteristics, or underwriting review rather than a public mitigation-credit schedule. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/news-and-events/news/2024/04/dwelling-insurance-pricing-becoming-more-risk-based); [Tower, 2025](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/); [AMI, risk-based pricing](https://www.ami.co.nz/hub/insurance/risk-based-pricing); [AA Insurance, risk-based pricing](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/help/article/360020785511-What-is-risk-based-pricing); [Vero, Quarterly Market Outlook May 2024](https://www.vero.co.nz/content/dam/suncorp/insurance/vero-nz/images/over-and-above/insights-content/vero-quarterly-market-outlook-may2024.pdf); [MAS, house insurance policy details](https://www.mas.co.nz/insurance/house-insurance/house-insurance-policy-details/).
- **Tower is the closest documented re-rating pathway.** Tower lets anyone see a property's natural-hazard ratings through a house or landlord quote, says its ratings are property-specific, and tells customers to contact it if ratings seem wrong or if they have made alterations to manage risks; however, Tower does not publish a rule that a specified mitigation, certificate, floor height, drainage work, or council note will lower a premium. [Tower, property risk profile](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/). Tower also says its flood model includes publicly available flood defences and mitigation efforts, which supports model-level recognition of some mitigations but not a customer right to re-rate after private works. [Tower, 2025 news release](https://www.tower.co.nz/news/kiwi-in-dark-about-risks-facing-homes/).
- **Other major published positions are weaker for mitigation rewards.** Consumer NZ reports Suncorp saying home adaptations are "not currently reflected in our standard pricing," while IAG said it considers a wide range of data and customers could discuss mitigation; AMI/IAG, AA/Suncorp, Vero/Suncorp, MAS and FMG publish hazard-risk or flood-risk pricing material, but I did not find a public premium-credit formula for property-level flood adaptation on those sites. [Consumer NZ, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/building-and-renovation/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding); [AMI, risk-based pricing](https://www.ami.co.nz/hub/insurance/risk-based-pricing); [AA Insurance, premiums](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/premiums); [Vero, Quarterly Market Outlook May 2024](https://www.vero.co.nz/content/dam/suncorp/insurance/vero-nz/images/over-and-above/insights-content/vero-quarterly-market-outlook-may2024.pdf); [MAS, policy changes](https://www.mas.co.nz/about-mas/news/policy-changes/); [FMG, climate-related disclosures 2025](https://www.fmg.co.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0014/163121/Climate-related-disclosures-2025.pdf).
- **The public NHC levy does not create a mitigation reward either.** NHCover is accessed through a qualifying private fire policy, covers storm or flood damage to residential land only, and the most common NHI levy is calculated from the building cover cap, with a maximum currently $480 plus GST per year; that levy structure does not contain a property-mitigation pricing variable in the public guide. [Natural Hazards Commission, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [NHCover Insurers' Guide, 2024](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/assets/Publications-Resources/Insurance-related/NHCover-Insurers-Guide-July-2024-v2.pdf).
- **An NZ analogue of FORTIFIED or Flood Performance Certificates would need more than better modelling.** It would need a recognised technical standard, independent assessors, a certificate with insurer-readable data, maintenance/expiry rules, privacy-safe data sharing, regulator or industry acceptance, and published rating or underwriting consequences. FORTIFIED works because certified designations are tied to insurer discounts in some US states; Flood Re's FPC roadmap proposes standardised property flood-resilience assessment so insurers can measure risk reduction and potentially reflect it in pricing. [FORTIFIED incentives](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives/); [Louisiana Department of Insurance, 2026 report](https://ldi.la.gov/docs/default-source/documents/propertycasualty/act-533-fortified-discount-report-2025.pdf); [Flood Re FPC roadmap, Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260610083610/https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/21594-Flood-Performance-Certificates-Roadmap-16pp_AW1_Digital_PD-1.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the published insurer/NHC/IFSO positions are clear, but the absence of a mitigation-credit pathway is partly an inference from public material; insurers may have unpublished manual underwriting practices or broker-only processes.

## Evidence

### Scope and survey method

I treated a published "risk-based pricing" page as evidence that an insurer recognises hazard risk in price, but not as evidence that it rewards property-level mitigation unless the page also described a customer action, evidence pathway, certificate, rating change, or discount consequence. [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md); [Consumer NZ, flood protection guide](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/building-and-renovation/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding).

The insurer/public-body sources checked were Tower, AMI/IAG, AA Insurance/Suncorp, Vero/Suncorp, MAS, FMG, ICNZ, NHC, RBNZ and IFSO. [Tower, property risk profile](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/); [AMI, risk-based pricing](https://www.ami.co.nz/hub/insurance/risk-based-pricing); [AA Insurance, premiums](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/premiums); [Vero, Quarterly Market Outlook May 2024](https://www.vero.co.nz/content/dam/suncorp/insurance/vero-nz/images/over-and-above/insights-content/vero-quarterly-market-outlook-may2024.pdf); [MAS, policy changes](https://www.mas.co.nz/about-mas/news/policy-changes/); [FMG, climate-related disclosures 2025](https://www.fmg.co.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0014/163121/Climate-related-disclosures-2025.pdf); [ICNZ, disaster recovery](https://www.icnz.org.nz/industry/disaster-recovery/); [NHC, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/news-and-events/news/2024/04/dwelling-insurance-pricing-becoming-more-risk-based); [IFSO, premiums and no cover](https://www.ifso.nz/news/insurers-not-providing-cover-premium-increases).

### NZ insurers publish risk-based pricing, not a mitigation-credit schedule

RBNZ's public summary of its May 2024 work says insurance premiums are increasingly reflecting detailed assessments of a property's flood and earthquake risk, which supports the narrow claim that NZ premiums are becoming more property-specific. [RBNZ, 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/news-and-events/news/2024/04/dwelling-insurance-pricing-becoming-more-risk-based).

Tower gives the most granular public explanation found: it says it assesses earthquake, flood, landslide and sea-surge risk at the property, calculates risks for the main building, may use community-level data where no address-specific data exists, and allows a customer to contact Tower if the risk ratings seem wrong or if the customer has made alterations to manage some risks. [Tower, property risk profile](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/). Tower's 2025 release also says it introduced inland-flood risk-based pricing in 2021, that about 90% of home customers received a reduction in the flood portion of their premium at an average of about $25 per property, and that its Moody's flood model includes publicly available flood defence and mitigation efforts. [Tower, 2025 news release](https://www.tower.co.nz/news/kiwi-in-dark-about-risks-facing-homes/). That is a functioning address-risk pricing system, but I did not find a published Tower rule saying what private evidence changes a rating, who validates alterations, how long a re-rating takes, or how a rating change maps to premium.

AMI, a business division of IAG New Zealand, says risk-based pricing means environmental risks across the country are reflected in insurance price, and that premiums can change depending on whether a home is in a place more prone to earthquakes, flooding or storms. [AMI, risk-based pricing](https://www.ami.co.nz/hub/insurance/risk-based-pricing). AMI's common-questions page says premiums take into account individual factors including location, age of home, claims, sum insured, higher-risk flood-prone locations and claims of people considered similar risk. [AMI, common questions](https://www.ami.co.nz/hub/insurance/common-questions-answered). Consumer NZ reports IAG saying it considers a wide range of data and that customers could discuss mitigations with it, but that statement is not a published re-rating process or discount schedule. [Consumer NZ, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/building-and-renovation/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding).

Suncorp-linked brands also publish risk-based pricing positions without a visible mitigation-credit schedule in the sources found. AA Insurance says it uses third-party data to estimate the probability and impact of natural-hazard events, meaning higher-risk areas could pay higher premiums and lower-risk areas vice versa. [AA Insurance, premiums](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/premiums). AA Insurance's 2025 climate statement says it invested in third-party models and address-level data, introduced flood risk-based pricing in FY24, and paused new home cover where exposure in a higher-risk area reached a threshold. [AA Insurance climate statement 2025](https://www.aainsurance.co.nz/documents/AAInsurance_2025_Climate_Statement.pdf). Vero's May 2024 market outlook says it uses data sources and modelling to identify properties with greater flood risk and will price and underwrite higher-risk home and contents policies accordingly, potentially including higher premium and additional flood excess. [Vero, Quarterly Market Outlook May 2024](https://www.vero.co.nz/content/dam/suncorp/insurance/vero-nz/images/over-and-above/insights-content/vero-quarterly-market-outlook-may2024.pdf). Consumer NZ reports Suncorp saying home adaptations are not currently reflected in standard pricing, which directly supports the "unrewarded in standard pricing" limb for that part of the market. [Consumer NZ, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/building-and-renovation/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding).

MAS publishes regional risk-based pricing and flood-prone premium pressure rather than property-mitigation credits: its house-insurance page says it takes greater account of particular risks faced by houses in different regions, and its policy-changes page says flood-prone areas' premiums will increase over time as those risks become better understood. [MAS, house insurance policy details](https://www.mas.co.nz/insurance/house-insurance/house-insurance-policy-details/); [MAS, policy changes](https://www.mas.co.nz/about-mas/news/policy-changes/). FMG's 2025 climate disclosure says underwriting processes can canvas information from clients about actions taken or planned to reduce sea-level-rise flood exposure, and that individual reviews provide an opportunity to consider whether cover fairly reflects identified risks. [FMG, climate-related disclosures 2025](https://www.fmg.co.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0014/163121/Climate-related-disclosures-2025.pdf). That is evidence of possible manual underwriting recognition, but not evidence of a standard customer premium-credit pathway.

ICNZ's recovery guidance supports insurability after successful mitigation but still frames the decision as ordinary underwriting rather than a discount entitlement: if Category 2P property-level interventions reduce risk to an acceptable level, ICNZ says the home will be insurable like any other home and insurers will look at flood models, previous claims and mitigations; for ongoing insurance, it says insurers will look at property-level information, models, previous claims and mitigations and apply terms if needed. [ICNZ, disaster recovery](https://www.icnz.org.nz/industry/disaster-recovery/).

### The NHC levy is not a mitigation incentive

Natural Hazards Commission says homeowners have NHCover if they have a home insurance policy that includes fire insurance, and that the home-insurance premium automatically includes the NHI levy. [NHC, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/). The same page says NHCover covers storm or flood for land cover only, not as direct private-building flood cover. [NHC, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/). The NHCover Insurers' Guide says the levy is 16 cents for every $100 of the building cover cap for a one-year policy, gives the formula "NHI Levy = building cover cap x 0.0016", and says the most common levy is $480 for a residential building with a $300,000 building cover cap. [NHCover Insurers' Guide, 2024](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/assets/Publications-Resources/Insurance-related/NHCover-Insurers-Guide-July-2024-v2.pdf).

That public levy formula has no hazard-risk or mitigation variable, so a raised floor, flood barrier or drainage work would not itself reduce the NHI levy unless it changed the insured building cover cap. [NHCover Insurers' Guide, 2024](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/assets/Publications-Resources/Insurance-related/NHCover-Insurers-Guide-July-2024-v2.pdf). This matters for incentives because the levy is a visible part of the home premium but not a pathway for rewarding household mitigation. [NHC, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [ICNZ pricing guide, 2024](https://www.icnz.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/ICNZ-Consumer-Guide-2024-%E2%80%93-Insurance-Pricing.pdf).

### IFSO rulings show limits, not a premium reward

IFSO says insurers are under no obligation to provide cover, insurance is a risk-based product, and IFSO has no power to make an insurer offer cover. [IFSO, premiums and no cover](https://www.ifso.nz/news/insurers-not-providing-cover-premium-increases). The same IFSO page says insurers may decline or exclude cover where a property is too risky, and that concentration-risk decisions may change as risks, reinsurance or risk-reduction/climate-adaptation measures such as flood protection change. [IFSO, premiums and no cover](https://www.ifso.nz/news/insurers-not-providing-cover-premium-increases). It also says IFSO does not have jurisdiction over what insurers charge, because premiums are set by insurers based on risk and cost factors. [IFSO, premiums and no cover](https://www.ifso.nz/news/insurers-not-providing-cover-premium-increases).

In the IFSO "Rebuilding a flood damaged home" case study, the insured family wanted a rebuilt flood-damaged home raised above the minimum level to reduce future flood risk and avoid possible financial effects on insurance or mortgage access. [IFSO, rebuilding a flood damaged home](https://www.ifso.nz/case-studies/rebuilding-a-flood-damaged-home). IFSO found the insurer only had to pay costs reasonably required to rebuild and meet legal or council requirements, and that a higher floor level was recommended for resilience but not clearly required for building consent at that stage. [IFSO, rebuilding a flood damaged home](https://www.ifso.nz/case-studies/rebuilding-a-flood-damaged-home). This case is about claim scope, not premium re-rating, but it is relevant because it shows an insurer was not required to fund a resilience upgrade merely because it might reduce future flood risk. [IFSO, rebuilding a flood damaged home](https://www.ifso.nz/case-studies/rebuilding-a-flood-damaged-home).

### What an NZ FORTIFIED or FPC analogue would require

The US FORTIFIED programme is useful because the insurance consequence is visible: IBHS says insurers in some areas offer discounts for homes with a FORTIFIED designation, and its incentives page says some discounts can be as high as 55% in some states. [FORTIFIED homepage](https://fortifiedhome.org/); [FORTIFIED incentives](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives/). North Carolina's Department of Insurance says mitigation credits may be available for qualifying homeowners policies in beach and coastal territories, with different credit levels depending on mitigation qualifications. [North Carolina Department of Insurance](https://www.ncdoi.gov/consumers/homeowners-insurance/fortified-homes-mitigation-credits). Louisiana's Department of Insurance publishes insurer-by-insurer IBHS FORTIFIED Roof discounts and says consumers can ask insurers for a premium calculation worksheet or similar document showing how the discount is applied. [Louisiana Department of Insurance, 2026 report](https://ldi.la.gov/docs/default-source/documents/propertycasualty/act-533-fortified-discount-report-2025.pdf).

Flood Re's Flood Performance Certificate roadmap is useful because it describes the data architecture NZ currently lacks: an FPC would set out a property's flood-resilience level, show what flood depth causes what damage, and let a householder explain protection levels to buyers, insurers and mortgage lenders. [Flood Re FPC roadmap, Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260610083610/https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/21594-Flood-Performance-Certificates-Roadmap-16pp_AW1_Digital_PD-1.pdf). The roadmap says property flood resilience can alter both event risk and restoration cost, and that an FPC gives insurers the ability to measure the corresponding fall in risk; it proposes a model for household-level damage reduction, a standardised method for assessing homes, testing across locations and scenarios, and a certification system deciding what information is included and how it is presented. [Flood Re FPC roadmap, Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260610083610/https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/21594-Flood-Performance-Certificates-Roadmap-16pp_AW1_Digital_PD-1.pdf).

For NZ, the minimum viable analogue would need six components: a technical standard for flood, landslide, sea-surge and/or earthquake mitigation; accredited assessors or engineers; a certificate field that distinguishes permanent features from deployable measures; a way to verify completion, maintenance and expiry; insurer-readable data sharing that protects privacy; and an agreed rating/underwriting consequence such as lower excess, eligibility, endorsement or premium factor. [Flood Re FPC roadmap, Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260610083610/https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/21594-Flood-Performance-Certificates-Roadmap-16pp_AW1_Digital_PD-1.pdf); [FORTIFIED incentives](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives/); [Louisiana Department of Insurance, 2026 report](https://ldi.la.gov/docs/default-source/documents/propertycasualty/act-533-fortified-discount-report-2025.pdf). NZ has partial building blocks, including the New Zealand Flood Map's future property-specific data and IAG's note that Standards New Zealand is developing flood-hazard modelling and natural-hazard risk-analysis standards, but those are not yet a household mitigation certificate or insurer discount mechanism. [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [IAG, risk reduction report 2026](https://www.iag.co.nz/content/dam/corporate-iag/iag-nz/nz/en/documents/corporate/iag-risk-reduction-report-2026.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A published insurer process saying "submit these documents after mitigation, we will re-run these rating fields, and these premium/excess/eligibility consequences follow" would change the answer for that insurer. Tower is the most likely place to find such evidence because it already publishes property risk ratings and invites customers to contact it about alterations, but the public pages I found stop short of a verified premium-credit rule. [Tower, property risk profile](https://www.tower.co.nz/manage/your-property-risk-profile/).
- A set of real, non-identifying before/after quotes or renewal notices showing a premium, excess or eligibility improvement after a specified mitigation and no other material policy changes would overturn the "unverified" part of the conclusion for that pathway. This would need to be aggregated or anonymised because individual addresses and insurance records are personal/identifying information. [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).
- A regulator, ICNZ, NHC, council or Standards New Zealand framework that certifies property-level flood/landslide/sea-surge mitigation and is accepted by multiple insurers would change the conclusion from "manual or opaque" to "standardised market mechanism." Current public sources show pieces of this system, not a full mechanism. [MfE, New Zealand Flood Map](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/new-zealand-flood-map/); [IAG, risk reduction report 2026](https://www.iag.co.nz/content/dam/corporate-iag/iag-nz/nz/en/documents/corporate/iag-risk-reduction-report-2026.pdf); [Flood Re FPC roadmap, Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260610083610/https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/21594-Flood-Performance-Certificates-Roadmap-16pp_AW1_Digital_PD-1.pdf).
- I could not verify internal underwriting manuals, broker-only guidance, individual policy endorsements, or post-mitigation re-rating outcomes from private insurer systems. A broker, actuary, insurer underwriter, FMA/Commerce Commission reviewer, or IFSO practitioner would be needed to test how often "tell your insurer about mitigation" actually leads to cheaper or more available cover. [Consumer NZ, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/building-and-renovation/how-to-protect-your-home-from-flooding); [IFSO, premiums and no cover](https://www.ifso.nz/news/insurers-not-providing-cover-premium-increases).

## Open follow-up questions

- Can Tower publish a mitigation re-rating checklist for raised floor levels, flood barriers, drainage works, retaining works and council-certified Category 2P completion?
- Would ICNZ members accept a council/NZ Flood Map mitigation certificate as rating data, or would each insurer require its own model and evidence rules?
- Should the FMA/Commerce Commission insurance-affordability work test whether risk-based pricing rewards risk reduction as well as penalising high exposure?
- Should NZ's developing flood-hazard and natural-hazard risk-analysis standards include a household certification annex usable by insurers, lenders, councils and conveyancers?
