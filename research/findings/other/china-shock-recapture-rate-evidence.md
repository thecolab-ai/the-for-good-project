# Real-world China trade shocks show recapture is product-specific, not a single number — Australia's 2020-23 restrictions and NZ's 2021 log slump imply #500's 15/30/50% bands should be applied per-sector, not to the whole China-facing basket

Part of #438. Stream: #438. This finding answers the question raised in #724: what do documented outcomes from Australia's 2020-23 China trade restrictions and NZ's own 2021 log-demand slump imply for the 15%/30%/50% first-year recapture bands used in [china-demand-shock-transmission.md (#500)](china-demand-shock-transmission.md)?

## Executive answer

- **There is no single "first-year recapture rate" in the real-world evidence — it splits sharply by how substitutable the product is.** Australia's Treasury found that within about 12 months of China's May 2020 barley tariff, Australian barley export *volumes* were up 225.1% year-on-year (favourable harvest plus redirection), with 26.5% of May 2021 volumes going to Saudi Arabia alone, versus zero a year earlier — near-complete volume substitution for a fungible bulk commodity, though sold "without the price premium historically paid by Chinese buyers" [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Over the same window, wine told a different story: Australian wine exports to China were down 95.9% through the year to May 2021 while *total* wine export volumes were down only 8.2%, but the wine industry's losses then *grew* over the following two years: Wine Australia's own export data shows total wine export value fell 19% year-on-year to A$2.08 billion in the year to June 2022, a decline the report attributes largely to the continued loss of the China market (which had taken 39% of total wine export value in Treasury's 2019 baseline) — bottled/branded wine cannot be redirected the way an undifferentiated grain can. That said, the same Wine Australia report shows this was not near-total non-recapture: excluding China, wine export value to the rest of the world actually *grew* 5% year-on-year to A$2.06 billion (+A$105 million, the highest since 2009-10) on a 9% rise in average price per litre, driven by premium-segment growth in Singapore, the US, Malaysia, Thailand, India and NZ — real, first-year, price-and-new-market recapture, just not enough in dollar terms to offset the China-specific loss [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf); [Wine Australia, "Export Report June 2022"](https://www.wineaustralia.com/news/media-releases/export-report-june-2022); [Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion) (Lowy's own text on wine is qualitative only — "more significantly affected as it is more difficult to divert this product to alternative markets" — cited here for that point, not for a figure). **Confidence: Medium** — the Treasury percentages are a primary official source, but they are a single-month snapshot (May 2021), not a calibrated annual recapture rate.
- **The one peer-reviewed, whole-basket quantification available (Wickes 2024) is explicit that it is not a first-year number and is only "very approximately" precise.** Across nine sanctioned commodities (coal, copper, beef, wine, cotton, barley, rough wood, rock lobster, hay), gross export losses to the China market rose from A$3 billion in 2020 to A$31 billion in 2022 at current prices; net losses — after diverting sanctioned trade to third-country markets — are estimated at "very approximately" A$11 billion in 2022 alone and A$20 billion cumulatively over 2020-22, at 2019 prices [Ron Wickes, "Chinese trade sanctions against Australia: Quantifying the impact," *Australian Economic Papers* 63(2), 2024, abstract](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/ausecp/v63y2024i2p220-236.html). Comparing the 2022-only gross and net figures (A$31b vs. A$11b) implies roughly two-thirds of that year's gross loss was recaptured (1 − 11/31 ≈ 64.5%) — but the abstract gives no cumulative gross figure for 2020-22 to pair against the A$20b cumulative net figure, so no genuine cumulative 2020-22 recapture ratio can actually be computed from it, and even this single-year comparison mixes price bases (A$31b is current prices, A$11b is 2019 prices), a mismatch the abstract does not itself adjust for. This is at best a rough, single-year (2022), whole-basket average across products with very different substitutability, not a clean "first-year" figure comparable to #500's bands. **Confidence: Medium** — the abstract's own numbers are precise, but the author repeatedly flags the estimate itself as approximate, and I could not access the full text (paywalled) to check the year-by-year, commodity-by-commodity breakdown behind the headline.
- **At the whole-of-economy level the shock was swamped by an unrelated commodity boom, which is a confound, not evidence of "recapture."** Australia's Productivity Commission found China's trade actions reduced the total value of Australian exports to the world by only about 0.2%, and Australia's real national income by less than one-tenth of one percentage point, because a small number of sanctioned sectors (~1% of GDP to begin with) were dwarfed by booming iron ore and LNG exports to China itself over the same period [Productivity Commission, cited in Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion). This is the wrong lens for calibrating #500, which already isolates the *China-facing* receipt base rather than measuring against total GDP — I flag it here only to warn against citing "trade war cost Australia almost nothing" as if it were a recapture rate; it mixes an unrelated boom into the denominator. **Confidence: Medium** — sourced to a Lowy Institute citation of the Productivity Commission rather than the primary Productivity Commission report itself, which I could not locate in a form I could directly verify (see Sources).
- **NZ's own 2021 log-demand slump is the most directly analogous domestic case, and it shows a third, distinct mechanism: near-zero buyer-diversification but high price-driven revenue recapture in the short run, which softened over the following two years before partially rebounding.** China took about 85% of NZ log export value in the year to June 2021, and log export revenue was *up* 34% that year on booming Chinese demand [MPI, Situation and Outlook for Primary Industries, December 2021, p.44-45](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). Wharf-gate A-grade export prices then fell roughly 29% within about two months in Q3-Q4 2021 as Evergrande-linked contagion hit Chinese construction demand — from about NZ$170/JAS to sub-NZ$120/JAS [NZ Herald/Whanganui Chronicle, "Comment: Evergrande fallout stymies log export recovery"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/whanganui-chronicle/news/comment-evergrande-fallout-stymies-log-export-recovery/B4BX7S75BLTFUM6E7A7O7VNBWQ/), corroborated by MPI's own quarterly indicative price series showing unpruned A-grade export prices falling from a NZ$172-196/m³ range in the June quarter to NZ$112-135/m³ by the December quarter [MPI, Historic indicative New Zealand radiata pine log prices](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/forestry/forest-industry-and-workforce/forestry-wood-processing-data/historic-indicative-new-zealand-radiata-pine-log-prices). Yet MPI's own full-year forecast (issued December 2021, after the crash had already hit) put log export revenue down only 1% for the year to June 2022 — MPI's own stated reason is simply that softening Chinese demand (construction-sector slowdown, Evergrande-linked contagion) was only beginning to weigh on the forecast, not a harvest-volume cushion: the 37.6 million m³/+18% harvest figure the same document cites is the *prior* year's (year to June 2021) statistic, and MPI explicitly forecasts harvest volumes to *fall slightly* in the year it was forecasting [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). Critically, this was *not* buyer diversification: MPI's own data shows South Korea's share of NZ log export volumes fell from 58% in 2005 to just 8% in the year to June 2021, and India's share fell from 12% in 2011 to 2% over the same window — the alternative-market channel has been *shrinking*, not growing, for 15 years [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021), consistent with the sibling finding that logs are NZ's single hardest China exposure to substitute on both the buy and sell side [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md). The cushion also thinned over the next two years: China's own property downturn cut its total log imports and NZ's China-bound log export value fell 24% from the 2021 peak to NZ$3.6 billion by 2023 — though it then partially recovered, with NZ's soft-log export value to China up 5% year-on-year in the year to date through June 2024 on continued price competitiveness against European/US substitutes, so the multi-year path was a decline followed by a partial rebound, not a monotonic slide [interest.co.nz, citing MFAT briefing, 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). **Confidence: Medium-High** for the price and revenue figures (two independent primary/near-primary sources agree), **Low** for how generalisable the "price absorbs the shock" mechanism is beyond logs.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the qualitative pattern (recapture is product-specific, and ranges from near-total for fungible bulk commodities to near-zero for differentiated or structurally concentrated ones) is well supported by multiple independent primary and near-primary sources across both case studies. No source in either country gives a clean, calibrated, first-year, sector-by-sector recapture percentage that could simply replace #500's 15/30/50% illustrative bands; the honest contribution here is to show which end of that range applies to which kind of NZ China exposure, and to flag that a single uniform recapture assumption across the whole China-facing basket is less defensible than the evidence for treating recapture as sector-specific.

## Evidence

### Australia 2020-23: recapture split sharply between fungible and differentiated products

China imposed a sequence of restrictions on Australian exports from May to November 2020: an 80.5% combined anti-dumping/countervailing tariff on barley (May 2020), suspensions of several beef export abattoirs, informal restrictions on coal, cotton, timber and lobster, and tariffs of up to 218% on wine (announced November 2020, confirmed March 2021) [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Treasury's own internal briefing (released under FOI, dated across mid-to-late 2021) put the 2019 baseline value of exports covered by these actions at roughly A$23.0 billion across nine commodity lines — coal (A$13.7b to China of A$63.9b to the world), beef (A$2.7b), copper ore (A$2.3b), wine (A$1.1b), cotton (A$1.1b), crustaceans (A$0.84b), barley (A$0.59b) and rough wood (A$0.60b) — giving a sense of scale before any redirection [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001, Table 2](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf).

The same document records within-year outcomes as of May 2021 (roughly 12 months after the barley tariff and 6 months after the wine tariff): barley export volumes were up 225.1% year-on-year (aided by a strong 2020 harvest as well as redirection), with 26.5% of May 2021 barley export volumes going to Saudi Arabia versus none a year earlier, though "for some exports this has occurred without the price premium historically paid by Chinese buyers" [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Wine told the opposite story in the same document: total wine export volumes were down only 8.2% year-on-year in May 2021 even though exports to China specifically were down 95.9% — a small headline effect in that snapshot, but one that worsened rather than resolved over the following year: Wine Australia's own Export Report puts total Australian wine export value down 19% year-on-year to A$2.08 billion in the year to June 2022, a decline it attributes largely to the continued impact of China's tariffs, on top of China previously taking 39% of total wine export value per Treasury's 2019 baseline (A$1,137m of A$2,953m) — much harder to redirect than a bulk grain because wine is bottled, branded, and relationship-dependent. But the same report also shows this was not zero-recapture for the rest of the market: with mainland China excluded, wine export value to all other destinations rose 5% year-on-year to A$2.06 billion (+A$105 million, the highest since 2009-10), on a 9% increase in average value per litre to A$3.32 FOB, with Wine Australia's Manager of Market Insights attributing the growth to premium-segment gains in Singapore, the US, Malaysia, Thailand, India and NZ (Southeast Asia alone up 51% to A$314 million) — a real first-year price-and-new-market recapture channel operating alongside the still-negative China-driven aggregate [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001, Table 2](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf); [Wine Australia, "Export Report June 2022"](https://www.wineaustralia.com/news/media-releases/export-report-june-2022); [Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion) (Lowy is cited here only for its qualitative "more difficult to divert" point — it gives no percentage or dollar figure for wine).

The most rigorous published attempt to quantify the whole nine-commodity basket is Ron Wickes' 2024 peer-reviewed paper in *Australian Economic Papers*. Working from declines in Australia's share of the Chinese import market, it finds gross export losses in the China market rose from A$3 billion in 2020 to A$31 billion in 2022 (current prices); net losses, after accounting for diversion of the sanctioned trade to third-country markets, are estimated — the author's own words — "very approximately" at A$11 billion in 2022 alone and A$20 billion cumulatively over 2020-22, at 2019 prices [Ron Wickes, "Chinese trade sanctions against Australia: Quantifying the impact," *Australian Economic Papers* 63(2):220-236, 2024, abstract via RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/ausecp/v63y2024i2p220-236.html). I was blocked from the paywalled full text (Wiley returned HTTP 403 on direct fetch) and could not verify the commodity-by-commodity or year-by-year breakdown behind these totals, only the headline abstract figures, which I fetched and read directly rather than relying on a secondary summary.

At the whole-of-economy level, Australia's Productivity Commission is reported to have found the trade actions reduced total Australian export value by only about 0.2%, and real national income by under one-tenth of a percentage point, because the ~1%-of-GDP set of sanctioned sectors was dwarfed by a boom in unrelated exports (iron ore, LNG) to China over the same period [Productivity Commission finding, cited in Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion). I was not able to locate and directly verify the primary Productivity Commission report containing this exact 0.2%/national-income figure (the Trade and Assistance Review editions I could access — 2020-21 and an attempted fetch of 2021-22 — did not contain a matching passage; see "What would change this conclusion" below), so this point rests on a single secondary citation. The Productivity Commission's own public writing on the episode is consistent with this being a small effect in aggregate, though it does not restate the 0.2% figure itself: in its own article on the trade bans, the Commission estimates that "less than 1-in-100 Australian exports is vulnerable to concentrated sources of global demand," identifying 35 such vulnerable products (including bauxite, rock lobster, and certain timber products) as the actual base at risk, which is consistent with a small whole-of-economy effect even though it is not the same figure as the 0.2%/national-income numbers reported by Lowy [Productivity Commission (Australia), "Our trade fortunes rely less on China than you think"](https://www.pc.gov.au/media-speeches/articles/our-trade-fortunes).

### NZ 2021 log-demand slump: a different shock, a different mechanism

NZ's 2021 log episode differs from the Australian cases in kind, not just degree: it was a demand-side price shock transmitted through China's own property-sector slowdown (compounded by Evergrande-linked financial contagion from around September 2021), not a deliberate government trade restriction aimed at New Zealand. This matters for how directly it can calibrate a stress test framed around a "China-facing demand shock" (#500's framing) — the mechanism of transmission (a genuine buyer-side demand fall, not a policy-imposed market closure) is arguably closer to what #500 is actually stress-testing than Australia's coercion cases are.

China took about 85% of NZ log export value in the year to June 2021, a year in which log export revenue rose 34% on the back of very strong Chinese demand [MPI, Situation and Outlook for Primary Industries, December 2021, p.44-45](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). From around September-October 2021, wharf-gate export prices for A-grade logs fell from about NZ$170/JAS to sub-NZ$120/JAS — roughly 29% — as the Evergrande crisis exposed weakening Chinese property construction demand [NZ Herald/Whanganui Chronicle, "Comment: Evergrande fallout stymies log export recovery"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/whanganui-chronicle/news/comment-evergrande-fallout-stymies-log-export-recovery/B4BX7S75BLTFUM6E7A7O7VNBWQ/). MPI's own quarterly indicative price data corroborates the scale and timing: unpruned A-grade export prices ranged NZ$172-196/m³ in the June 2021 quarter, falling to NZ$134-180/m³ in the September quarter and NZ$112-135/m³ by the December quarter [MPI, Historic indicative New Zealand radiata pine log prices](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/forestry/forest-industry-and-workforce/forestry-wood-processing-data/historic-indicative-new-zealand-radiata-pine-log-prices).

Despite this, MPI's own December 2021 forecast — issued after the price crash was already visible in the data — put full-year log export revenue for the year to June 2022 down just 1% to NZ$3.8 billion. MPI's own stated reason is softening Chinese demand (construction-sector slowdown and Evergrande-linked contagion) only beginning to weigh on the forecast — not a harvest-volume cushion: the 37.6 million m³/+18% year-on-year harvest figure the same document cites is for the *prior* year (to 30 June 2021), and MPI explicitly forecasts harvest volumes to *fall slightly* in the year being forecast (to 30 June 2022) [MPI SOPI December 2021, Table 5 and surrounding text](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). The quarterly price series above also shows the crash landing in the June, September and December 2021 quarters — the first half of the July 2021-June 2022 year being forecast, not the second half — so there is no "elevated first-half, collapsed second-half" pattern within that year to invoke; MPI's forecast simply reflects that, writing partway through the year, it judged the softening not yet severe enough to erase the prior year's revenue gains. In other words, in revenue terms the shock looked far smaller over a full year than the ~29% in-quarter price crash suggested — but not because NZ found new buyers, and not because of a harvest-volume offset. MPI's own report is explicit that South Korea's share of NZ log export volumes fell from 58% in 2005 to 8% in the year to June 2021, and India's share fell from 12% in 2011 to 2% over the same period — both alternative markets have structurally shrunk relative to China, not grown, over the 15 years leading into this shock [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). This matches the sibling research finding on substitutability, which independently concludes logs are NZ's single hardest China exposure to substitute on both the buy and sell side, because the next-largest markets (South Korea, Japan) are an order of magnitude smaller than China's offtake and India's market collapsed after a 2022 biosecurity rule change broke compatibility with its fumigation requirements [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md).

The mechanism, then, was price absorption on continued sales to the same buyer, not buyer diversification — and #500's own recapture definition ("the share of gross lost China-facing receipts offset by alternative buyers, lower prices that preserve volume, inventories, exchange-rate movement, or delayed contracts") explicitly includes "lower prices that preserve volume" as one recapture channel [china-demand-shock-transmission.md (#500)](china-demand-shock-transmission.md). Read this way, the 2021 log episode is a real example of that specific channel operating at a high rate in its first year — but it was not free, and it did not hold at that level: China's own property downturn continued to depress log demand over the following two years, and NZ's log exports to China fell 24% from the 2021 peak to NZ$3.6 billion by 2023, back to 2018 levels [interest.co.nz, citing an MFAT briefing, 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). A separate industry data point corroborates continued softness two years on: an ANZ-cited commodity price index showed NZ log prices down 21% over the 12 months to mid-2023, with an ANZ agricultural economist quoted as saying "demand from China for log[s] [is] weak, there are few alternatives for raw logs" (sic — the source's own text reads "log weak," singular, likely a typo in the original) [Wood Central, "ANZ: NZ Log Prices Drop 21% in 12 Months Due to China Slowdown"](https://woodcentral.com.au/anz-nz-log-prices-drop-21-in-12-months-due-to-china-slowdown/). But this decline was not monotonic through to the article's own December 2024 publication date: the same interest.co.nz piece — whose own headline is "Why pine log exports to China have a future" — reports that NZ's soft-log export value to China actually *grew* 5% year-on-year in the year to date through June 2024, against the continuing downward trend in China's total log imports from all suppliers, attributing this to NZ radiata pine's price competitiveness against European and US substitutes (NZ$198/m³ in 2023 versus NZ$326/m³ for German spruce and NZ$243/m³ for US hardwoods) and its flexibility for lower-value end-uses like packaging [interest.co.nz, "Why pine log exports to China have a future," 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). So the "recapture" in year one understated the multi-year exposure through the 2023 trough, but by mid-2024 the same price-absorption mechanism that cushioned 2021 appears to have started recapturing share again, rather than eroding without limit.

### What this implies for #500's 15%/30%/50% bands

Putting the two case studies together against #500's stress-test table:

1. **For NZ's most fungible, bulk, commodity-graded exposure** (comparable to Australian barley or cotton — arguably NZ's bulk dairy powder and standard-grade meat cuts, though these carry their own constraints not tested here; see [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md) on dairy's world-price mechanics), the Australian evidence supports year-one *volume* recapture at or above #500's 50% high-recapture band, but typically at a price discount, meaning *revenue* recapture is usually lower than volume recapture — #500's use of a single recaptured-receipts percentage (rather than separate volume/price recapture terms) likely overstates how much of that headline percentage would show up in export dollars for a bulk-commodity shock.
2. **For NZ's most differentiated, branded, or relationship-dependent exposure** (the closest domestic analogues being high-value branded food/beverage exports, international education, and tourism — none of which are bulk-substitutable the way grain is), the Australian wine case suggests #500's 15% low-recapture band may still be optimistic if the shock persists beyond the first year: losses compounded over 2-3 years rather than resolving. But it also shows this recapture is not zero even for a differentiated product — Wine Australia's own data records real, if partial, first-year recapture through premium-segment growth in new markets (rest-of-world value +5%/+A$105m in the same year China-bound value collapsed), so #500's bands should reflect "meaningfully less than 50%, but not near-zero" for this category, not an assumption of total non-recapture.
3. **For NZ's single largest concentrated exposure, logs**, the evidence shows two things #500 does not currently distinguish: buyer-diversification recapture close to 0% (below even the 15% low band), but potentially high price-driven revenue recapture in a good year — a mechanism unlikely to survive a shock that turns out to be prolonged rather than short, as China's own multi-year property downturn has since shown.

None of this evidence lets #500's Low confidence be upgraded to Medium or High for a single recapture number, because no source — Australian or NZ — offers a calibrated, first-year, whole-basket recapture percentage; the strongest quantification available (Wickes 2024) explicitly hedges its own multi-year estimate as "very approximate." What the evidence does support is narrowing the *use* of #500's bands: applying the 50% high-recapture band uniformly across NZ's whole China-facing basket is not well supported by real-world precedent for the differentiated and services-heavy parts of that basket (education, tourism, branded food), while it is a defensible-to-conservative assumption for the most fungible bulk-commodity parts.

## What would change this conclusion

- Direct access to the full text of Wickes (2024) — currently paywalled — would let this finding replace the whole-basket, multi-year approximation with the paper's actual year-by-year and commodity-by-commodity breakdown, which may contain an explicit first-year figure I could not see from the abstract alone.
- Direct access to the specific Productivity Commission report containing the "0.2% of exports / <0.1pp of national income" finding (I could not locate a matching passage in the Trade and Assistance Review editions I fetched) would let me either confirm this figure to a primary source or drop it.
- A published NZ Treasury, RBNZ, or MPI study modelling the 2021 log slump specifically as a China-demand-shock case study (rather than the routine market commentary I relied on here) would let this finding move from "consistent, independently-sourced facts assembled into a narrative" to "an official NZ analysis of recapture."
- Firm-level or shipment-level data on whether any *specific* NZ log volumes were actually re-routed to non-China buyers during the September-December 2021 window (as opposed to simply discounted to the same China buyers) would confirm or overturn the "near-zero buyer diversification" reading; I inferred this from aggregate multi-year market-share trends and an economist's public comment, not from within-shock shipment data.
- If a future, deeper, or longer China-demand shock affecting NZ (e.g. the 2022-2024 property downturn, which cut NZ's China-bound log export value 24% from the 2021 peak to NZ$3.6 billion by 2023) is formally studied, it would show more precisely whether the "price absorbs the shock" mechanism that worked for one year in 2021 holds up over a multi-year shock. The evidence available here is mixed, not one-directional: NZ's own 24% decline to 2023 sits inside a broader, steeper collapse in China's total log imports from all suppliers worldwide (down 36% by value, 2021-2023, from NZ$16.4b to NZ$10.3b — a different, whole-of-market figure, not NZ-specific), which argues against extrapolating the 2021 "only down 1%" result as a stable steady-state — but the same source also reports NZ's soft-log export value to China grew 5% year-on-year in the year to date through June 2024, driven by continued price competitiveness, meaning the erosion had already partially reversed by the time of the most recent data cited here rather than continuing indefinitely [interest.co.nz, citing MFAT briefing, 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength).
- A human trade economist or forestry-sector analyst should review whether "lower prices that preserve volume" should really be scored as full recapture in #500's framework, given that it represents a real income loss to NZ producers (lower margins, farmgate/stumpage returns) even when export volumes and headline revenue are largely preserved — this finding treats it as a distinct, imperfect substitute for buyer diversification, but #500's stress test does not currently separate the two mechanisms, and a sector economist may judge that conflating them understates producer-level (as opposed to macro/GDP-level) harm.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would the Wickes (2024) full-text commodity-by-commodity table show for a true first-year (not multi-year cumulative) recapture rate per Australian commodity, and could that be mapped onto NZ's equivalent export categories (dairy, meat, logs, education, tourism)?
- Is there a comparable documented case of a genuine NZ-specific (not China-side) demand or policy shock in another sector — e.g. a past NZ dairy or meat market-access disruption — that would provide a second domestic data point beyond logs?
- Given that #500's recapture definition bundles "alternative buyers" and "lower prices that preserve volume" into one percentage, would the stress test be more useful reported as two separate parameters (a volume-recapture rate and a price-discount rate) rather than one blended number, given how differently they behaved in the log case versus the Australian barley/cotton cases?
