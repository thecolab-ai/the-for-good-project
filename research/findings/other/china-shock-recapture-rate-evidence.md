# Real-world China trade shocks show recapture is product-specific, not a single number — Australia's 2020-23 restrictions and NZ's 2021 log slump imply #500's 15/30/50% bands should be applied per-sector, not to the whole China-facing basket

Part of #438. Stream: #438. This finding answers the question raised in #724: what do documented outcomes from Australia's 2020-23 China trade restrictions and NZ's own 2021 log-demand slump imply for the 15%/30%/50% first-year recapture bands used in [china-demand-shock-transmission.md (#500)](china-demand-shock-transmission.md)?

## Executive answer

- **There is no single "first-year recapture rate" in the real-world evidence — it splits sharply by how substitutable the product is.** Australia's Treasury found that within about 12 months of China's May 2020 barley tariff, Australian barley export *volumes* were up 225.1% year-on-year (favourable harvest plus redirection), with 26.5% of May 2021 volumes going to Saudi Arabia alone, versus zero a year earlier — near-complete volume substitution for a fungible bulk commodity, though sold "without the price premium historically paid by Chinese buyers" [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Over the same window, wine told a different story: Australian wine exports to China were down 95.9% through the year to May 2021 while *total* wine export volumes were down only 8.2%, but independent reporting shows the wine industry's losses then *grew* over the following two years to about 30% of total export value by 2022, because bottled/branded wine cannot be redirected the way an undifferentiated grain can [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf); [Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion). **Confidence: Medium** — the Treasury percentages are a primary official source, but they are a single-month snapshot (May 2021), not a calibrated annual recapture rate.
- **The one peer-reviewed, whole-basket quantification available (Wickes 2024) is explicit that it is not a first-year number and is only "very approximately" precise.** Across nine sanctioned commodities (coal, copper, beef, wine, cotton, barley, rough wood, rock lobster, hay), gross export losses to the China market rose from A$3 billion in 2020 to A$31 billion in 2022 at current prices; net losses — after diverting sanctioned trade to third-country markets — are estimated at "very approximately" A$11 billion in 2022 alone and A$20 billion cumulatively over 2020-22, at 2019 prices [Ron Wickes, "Chinese trade sanctions against Australia: Quantifying the impact," *Australian Economic Papers* 63(2), 2024, abstract](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/ausecp/v63y2024i2p220-236.html). Read literally, that implies roughly two-thirds of the *cumulative 2020-22* value was recaptured by year three — but this is a multi-year, whole-basket average across products with very different substitutability, not a clean "first-year" figure comparable to #500's bands. **Confidence: Medium** — the abstract's own numbers are precise, but the author repeatedly flags the estimate itself as approximate, and I could not access the full text (paywalled) to check the year-by-year, commodity-by-commodity breakdown behind the headline.
- **At the whole-of-economy level the shock was swamped by an unrelated commodity boom, which is a confound, not evidence of "recapture."** Australia's Productivity Commission found China's trade actions reduced the total value of Australian exports to the world by only about 0.2%, and Australia's real national income by less than one-tenth of one percentage point, because a small number of sanctioned sectors (~1% of GDP to begin with) were dwarfed by booming iron ore and LNG exports to China itself over the same period [Productivity Commission, cited in Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion). This is the wrong lens for calibrating #500, which already isolates the *China-facing* receipt base rather than measuring against total GDP — I flag it here only to warn against citing "trade war cost Australia almost nothing" as if it were a recapture rate; it mixes an unrelated boom into the denominator. **Confidence: Medium** — sourced to a Lowy Institute citation of the Productivity Commission rather than the primary Productivity Commission report itself, which I could not locate in a form I could directly verify (see Sources).
- **NZ's own 2021 log-demand slump is the most directly analogous domestic case, and it shows a third, distinct mechanism: near-zero buyer-diversification but high price-driven revenue recapture in the short run, which then eroded over the following two years.** China took about 85% of NZ log export value in the year to June 2021, and log export revenue was *up* 34% that year on booming Chinese demand [MPI, Situation and Outlook for Primary Industries, December 2021, p.44-45](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). Wharf-gate A-grade export prices then fell roughly 29% within about two months in Q3-Q4 2021 as Evergrande-linked contagion hit Chinese construction demand — from about NZ$170/JAS to sub-NZ$120/JAS [NZ Herald/Whanganui Chronicle, "Comment: Evergrande fallout stymies log export recovery"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/whanganui-chronicle/news/comment-evergrande-fallout-stymies-log-export-recovery/B4BX7S75BLTFUM6E7A7O7VNBWQ/), corroborated by MPI's own quarterly indicative price series showing unpruned A-grade export prices falling from a NZ$172-196/m³ range in the June quarter to NZ$112-135/m³ by the December quarter [MPI, Historic indicative New Zealand radiata pine log prices](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/forestry/forest-industry-and-workforce/forestry-wood-processing-data/historic-indicative-new-zealand-radiata-pine-log-prices). Yet MPI's own full-year forecast (issued December 2021, after the crash had already hit) put log export revenue down only 1% for the year to June 2022, because H1 prices and a record 37.6 million m³ harvest (+18% y/y) cushioned the H2 price collapse [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). Critically, this was *not* buyer diversification: MPI's own data shows South Korea's share of NZ log export volumes fell from 58% in 2005 to just 8% in the year to June 2021, and India's share fell from 12% in 2011 to 2% over the same window — the alternative-market channel has been *shrinking*, not growing, for 15 years [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021), consistent with the sibling finding that logs are NZ's single hardest China exposure to substitute on both the buy and sell side [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md). The cushion was also short-lived: China's own property downturn cut its total log imports and NZ's China-bound log export value fell 24% from the 2021 peak to NZ$3.6 billion by 2023 [interest.co.nz, citing MFAT briefing, 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). **Confidence: Medium-High** for the price and revenue figures (two independent primary/near-primary sources agree), **Low** for how generalisable the "price absorbs the shock" mechanism is beyond logs.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the qualitative pattern (recapture is product-specific, and ranges from near-total for fungible bulk commodities to near-zero for differentiated or structurally concentrated ones) is well supported by multiple independent primary and near-primary sources across both case studies. No source in either country gives a clean, calibrated, first-year, sector-by-sector recapture percentage that could simply replace #500's 15/30/50% illustrative bands; the honest contribution here is to show which end of that range applies to which kind of NZ China exposure, and to flag that a single uniform recapture assumption across the whole China-facing basket is less defensible than the evidence for treating recapture as sector-specific.

## Evidence

### Australia 2020-23: recapture split sharply between fungible and differentiated products

China imposed a sequence of restrictions on Australian exports from May to November 2020: an 80.5% combined anti-dumping/countervailing tariff on barley (May 2020), suspensions of several beef export abattoirs, informal restrictions on coal, cotton, timber and lobster, and tariffs of up to 218% on wine (announced November 2020, confirmed March 2021) [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Treasury's own internal briefing (released under FOI, dated across mid-to-late 2021) put the 2019 baseline value of exports covered by these actions at roughly A$23.0 billion across nine commodity lines — coal (A$13.7b to China of A$63.9b to the world), beef (A$2.7b), copper ore (A$2.3b), wine (A$1.1b), cotton (A$1.1b), crustaceans (A$0.84b), barley (A$0.59b) and rough wood (A$0.60b) — giving a sense of scale before any redirection [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001, Table 2](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf).

The same document records within-year outcomes as of May 2021 (roughly 12 months after the barley tariff and 6 months after the wine tariff): barley export volumes were up 225.1% year-on-year (aided by a strong 2020 harvest as well as redirection), with 26.5% of May 2021 barley export volumes going to Saudi Arabia versus none a year earlier, though "for some exports this has occurred without the price premium historically paid by Chinese buyers" [Australian Treasury, FOI 3001](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/foi-3001.pdf). Wine told the opposite story in the same document: total wine export volumes were down only 8.2% year-on-year in May 2021 even though exports to China specifically were down 95.9% — a small headline effect in that snapshot, but one that independent later reporting shows worsened rather than resolved: total Australian wine exports fell around 30% in value between 2020 and 2022 as the loss of the China channel (previously about a third of the industry's export value) proved much harder to redirect than a bulk grain, because much of it is bottled, branded, and relationship-dependent [Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion).

The most rigorous published attempt to quantify the whole nine-commodity basket is Ron Wickes' 2024 peer-reviewed paper in *Australian Economic Papers*. Working from declines in Australia's share of the Chinese import market, it finds gross export losses in the China market rose from A$3 billion in 2020 to A$31 billion in 2022 (current prices); net losses, after accounting for diversion of the sanctioned trade to third-country markets, are estimated — the author's own words — "very approximately" at A$11 billion in 2022 alone and A$20 billion cumulatively over 2020-22, at 2019 prices [Ron Wickes, "Chinese trade sanctions against Australia: Quantifying the impact," *Australian Economic Papers* 63(2):220-236, 2024, abstract via RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/ausecp/v63y2024i2p220-236.html). I was blocked from the paywalled full text (Wiley returned HTTP 403 on direct fetch) and could not verify the commodity-by-commodity or year-by-year breakdown behind these totals, only the headline abstract figures, which I fetched and read directly rather than relying on a secondary summary.

At the whole-of-economy level, Australia's Productivity Commission is reported to have found the trade actions reduced total Australian export value by only about 0.2%, and real national income by under one-tenth of a percentage point, because the ~1%-of-GDP set of sanctioned sectors was dwarfed by a boom in unrelated exports (iron ore, LNG) to China over the same period [Productivity Commission finding, cited in Lowy Institute, "It's no surprise Australia shrugged off China's campaign of trade coercion"](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/it-s-no-surprise-australia-shrugged-china-s-campaign-trade-coercion). I was not able to locate and directly verify the primary Productivity Commission report containing this exact figure (the Trade and Assistance Review editions I could access — 2020-21 and an attempted fetch of 2021-22 — did not contain a matching passage; see "What I could not verify" below), so this point rests on a single secondary citation.

### NZ 2021 log-demand slump: a different shock, a different mechanism

NZ's 2021 log episode differs from the Australian cases in kind, not just degree: it was a demand-side price shock transmitted through China's own property-sector slowdown (compounded by Evergrande-linked financial contagion from around September 2021), not a deliberate government trade restriction aimed at New Zealand. This matters for how directly it can calibrate a stress test framed around a "China-facing demand shock" (#500's framing) — the mechanism of transmission (a genuine buyer-side demand fall, not a policy-imposed market closure) is arguably closer to what #500 is actually stress-testing than Australia's coercion cases are.

China took about 85% of NZ log export value in the year to June 2021, a year in which log export revenue rose 34% on the back of very strong Chinese demand [MPI, Situation and Outlook for Primary Industries, December 2021, p.44-45](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). From around September-October 2021, wharf-gate export prices for A-grade logs fell from about NZ$170/JAS to sub-NZ$120/JAS — roughly 29% — as the Evergrande crisis exposed weakening Chinese property construction demand [NZ Herald/Whanganui Chronicle, "Comment: Evergrande fallout stymies log export recovery"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/whanganui-chronicle/news/comment-evergrande-fallout-stymies-log-export-recovery/B4BX7S75BLTFUM6E7A7O7VNBWQ/). MPI's own quarterly indicative price data corroborates the scale and timing: unpruned A-grade export prices ranged NZ$172-196/m³ in the June 2021 quarter, falling to NZ$134-180/m³ in the September quarter and NZ$112-135/m³ by the December quarter [MPI, Historic indicative New Zealand radiata pine log prices](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/forestry/forest-industry-and-workforce/forestry-wood-processing-data/historic-indicative-new-zealand-radiata-pine-log-prices).

Despite this, MPI's own December 2021 forecast — issued after the price crash was already visible in the data — put full-year log export revenue for the year to June 2022 down just 1% to NZ$3.8 billion, because record harvest volumes (37.6 million m³, up 18% year-on-year) and elevated first-half prices largely offset the second-half price collapse [MPI SOPI December 2021, Table 5](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). In other words, in revenue terms the shock looked far smaller over a full year than the ~29% in-quarter price crash suggested — but not because NZ found new buyers. MPI's own report is explicit that South Korea's share of NZ log export volumes fell from 58% in 2005 to 8% in the year to June 2021, and India's share fell from 12% in 2011 to 2% over the same period — both alternative markets have structurally shrunk relative to China, not grown, over the 15 years leading into this shock [MPI SOPI December 2021](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/dmsdocument/49066-Situation-and-Outlook-for-Primary-Industries-SOPI-December-2021). This matches the sibling research finding on substitutability, which independently concludes logs are NZ's single hardest China exposure to substitute on both the buy and sell side, because the next-largest markets (South Korea, Japan) are an order of magnitude smaller than China's offtake and India's market collapsed after a 2022 biosecurity rule change broke compatibility with its fumigation requirements [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md).

The mechanism, then, was price absorption on continued sales to the same buyer, not buyer diversification — and #500's own recapture definition ("the share of gross lost China-facing receipts offset by alternative buyers, lower prices that preserve volume, inventories, exchange-rate movement, or delayed contracts") explicitly includes "lower prices that preserve volume" as one recapture channel [china-demand-shock-transmission.md (#500)](china-demand-shock-transmission.md). Read this way, the 2021 log episode is a real example of that specific channel operating at a high rate in its first year — but it was not free, and it did not last: China's own property downturn continued to depress log demand over the following two years, and NZ's log exports to China fell 24% from the 2021 peak to NZ$3.6 billion by 2023, back to 2018 levels [interest.co.nz, citing an MFAT briefing, 14 Dec 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). A separate industry data point corroborates continued softness two years on: an ANZ-cited commodity price index showed NZ log prices down 21% over the 12 months to mid-2023, with an ANZ agricultural economist quoted as saying "demand from China for logs [is] weak, there are few alternatives for raw logs" [Wood Central, "ANZ: NZ Log Prices Drop 21% in 12 Months Due to China Slowdown"](https://woodcentral.com.au/anz-nz-log-prices-drop-21-in-12-months-due-to-china-slowdown/). So the "recapture" in year one understates the true multi-year exposure once the shock turned out to be a sustained structural downturn (China's property sector) rather than a one-off spike.

### What this implies for #500's 15%/30%/50% bands

Putting the two case studies together against #500's stress-test table:

1. **For NZ's most fungible, bulk, commodity-graded exposure** (comparable to Australian barley or cotton — arguably NZ's bulk dairy powder and standard-grade meat cuts, though these carry their own constraints not tested here; see [china-export-substitutability-goods.md (#502)](china-export-substitutability-goods.md) on dairy's world-price mechanics), the Australian evidence supports year-one *volume* recapture at or above #500's 50% high-recapture band, but typically at a price discount, meaning *revenue* recapture is usually lower than volume recapture — #500's use of a single recaptured-receipts percentage (rather than separate volume/price recapture terms) likely overstates how much of that headline percentage would show up in export dollars for a bulk-commodity shock.
2. **For NZ's most differentiated, branded, or relationship-dependent exposure** (the closest domestic analogues being high-value branded food/beverage exports, international education, and tourism — none of which are bulk-substitutable the way grain is), the Australian wine case suggests #500's 15% low-recapture band may still be optimistic if the shock persists beyond the first year: losses compounded over 2-3 years rather than resolving.
3. **For NZ's single largest concentrated exposure, logs**, the evidence shows two things #500 does not currently distinguish: buyer-diversification recapture close to 0% (below even the 15% low band), but potentially high price-driven revenue recapture in a good year — a mechanism unlikely to survive a shock that turns out to be prolonged rather than short, as China's own multi-year property downturn has since shown.

None of this evidence lets #500's Low confidence be upgraded to Medium or High for a single recapture number, because no source — Australian or NZ — offers a calibrated, first-year, whole-basket recapture percentage; the strongest quantification available (Wickes 2024) explicitly hedges its own multi-year estimate as "very approximate." What the evidence does support is narrowing the *use* of #500's bands: applying the 50% high-recapture band uniformly across NZ's whole China-facing basket is not well supported by real-world precedent for the differentiated and services-heavy parts of that basket (education, tourism, branded food), while it is a defensible-to-conservative assumption for the most fungible bulk-commodity parts.

## What would change this conclusion

- Direct access to the full text of Wickes (2024) — currently paywalled — would let this finding replace the whole-basket, multi-year approximation with the paper's actual year-by-year and commodity-by-commodity breakdown, which may contain an explicit first-year figure I could not see from the abstract alone.
- Direct access to the specific Productivity Commission report containing the "0.2% of exports / <0.1pp of national income" finding (I could not locate a matching passage in the Trade and Assistance Review editions I fetched) would let me either confirm this figure to a primary source or drop it.
- A published NZ Treasury, RBNZ, or MPI study modelling the 2021 log slump specifically as a China-demand-shock case study (rather than the routine market commentary I relied on here) would let this finding move from "consistent, independently-sourced facts assembled into a narrative" to "an official NZ analysis of recapture."
- Firm-level or shipment-level data on whether any *specific* NZ log volumes were actually re-routed to non-China buyers during the September-December 2021 window (as opposed to simply discounted to the same China buyers) would confirm or overturn the "near-zero buyer diversification" reading; I inferred this from aggregate multi-year market-share trends and an economist's public comment, not from within-shock shipment data.
- If a future, deeper, or longer China-demand shock affecting NZ (e.g. the ongoing 2022-2025 property downturn, which cut NZ's China-bound log export value 24% from the 2021 peak) is formally studied, it would show whether the "price absorbs the shock" mechanism that worked for one year in 2021 continues to hold over a multi-year shock, or whether revenue losses eventually show up in full once inventories, contracts, and price flexibility are exhausted — the available evidence already suggests the latter (a sustained ~20-36% value decline by 2023), which argues against extrapolating the 2021 "only down 1%" result as if it were the steady-state recapture rate.
- A human trade economist or forestry-sector analyst should review whether "lower prices that preserve volume" should really be scored as full recapture in #500's framework, given that it represents a real income loss to NZ producers (lower margins, farmgate/stumpage returns) even when export volumes and headline revenue are largely preserved — this finding treats it as a distinct, imperfect substitute for buyer diversification, but #500's stress test does not currently separate the two mechanisms, and a sector economist may judge that conflating them understates producer-level (as opposed to macro/GDP-level) harm.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would the Wickes (2024) full-text commodity-by-commodity table show for a true first-year (not multi-year cumulative) recapture rate per Australian commodity, and could that be mapped onto NZ's equivalent export categories (dairy, meat, logs, education, tourism)?
- Is there a comparable documented case of a genuine NZ-specific (not China-side) demand or policy shock in another sector — e.g. a past NZ dairy or meat market-access disruption — that would provide a second domestic data point beyond logs?
- Given that #500's recapture definition bundles "alternative buyers" and "lower prices that preserve volume" into one percentage, would the stress test be more useful reported as two separate parameters (a volume-recapture rate and a price-discount rate) rather than one blended number, given how differently they behaved in the log case versus the Australian barley/cotton cases?
