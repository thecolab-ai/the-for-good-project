# NZ is a clear high-income outlier for adolescent and under-30 suicide, but not every youth mental-health indicator supports a single "worst" ranking

## Executive answer

- **The "NZ is among the worst" claim is strongest for suicide mortality, especially adolescents and under-30s.** UNICEF Report Card 19 ranks New Zealand 36th of 36 countries with sufficient mental-health data and says the latest comparable 15-19 suicide rate was 17.1 per 100,000, the highest in its wealthy-country comparison; OECD says under-30 suicide rates are highest in Korea, New Zealand, Japan and Estonia, at 17+ per 100,000 youth [UNICEF Aotearoa, 2025](https://www.unicef.org.nz/stories/report-card-19-what-it-means-for-aotearoa); [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19, 2025](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf); [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html).
- **When the same WHO GHO 2021 estimate is used for 15-19-year-olds, NZ still looks high, but not literally worst among close comparators.** WHO GHO reports 15-19 crude suicide rates of 9.7 for New Zealand, 10.9 for Australia, 5.6 for the UK, 13.0 for the US, 11.8 for Japan and 11.3 for Korea; this differs from UNICEF because UNICEF used WHO Mortality Database three-year averages and additional New Zealand suicide-web-tool data, not the single-year GHO estimate [WHO GHO indicator metadata, accessed 5 July 2026](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age); [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19 appendix, 2025](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf).
- **For 20-somethings, NZ is again high, but the cleanest international public age band I could verify is 20-29 or 15-29, not 20-24.** WHO GHO 2021 has NZ at 17.7 per 100,000 for ages 20-29 and 15.2 for ages 15-29, above Australia and the UK in both bands but below Korea, Japan and the US in this small comparator extraction; OECD independently places New Zealand among the highest under-30 countries [WHO GHO OData API, accessed 5 July 2026](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SDGSUICIDE); [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html).
- **Distress, self-harm and service-access indicators do not support a simple international rank.** New Zealand's 2024/25 survey found 22.9% of 15-24-year-olds had high or very high K10 psychological distress, Australia's 2020-2022 survey found 38.8-39% of 16-24-year-olds had a 12-month mental disorder, and NHS England's 2023 survey found 23.3% of 17-19-year-olds and 21.7% of 20-25-year-olds had a probable mental disorder; these are different constructs, instruments and age bands [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/wellbeing/nz-health-survey-mental-health-and-substance-use-data/); [Ministry of Health NZ, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey); [AIHW, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/children-youth/health-of-young-people); [NHS England, 2023](https://www.england.nhs.uk/2023/11/one-in-five-children-and-young-people-had-a-probable-mental-disorder-in-2023/).
- **Bottom line:** use "NZ is among the worst high-income countries for adolescent/under-30 suicide" rather than "NZ has the worst youth mental health overall." The latter overstates the comparable evidence because mortality, distress, self-harm hospitalisation, self-reported self-harm and access indicators are measured differently across countries [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html); [WHO GHO indicator metadata](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - strong for the suicide-mortality conclusion, medium for distress/self-harm/service-access comparison because public sources use non-harmonised measures and age bands.

## Evidence

### Harmonisation choices

I treated five indicators separately: adolescent suicide, 20-something/under-30 suicide, distress or disorder prevalence, self-harm, and service access, because source agencies define and age-band these measures differently [WHO GHO indicator metadata](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age); [ONS suicides dataset page, 2025](https://www.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/birthsdeathsandmarriages/deaths/datasets/suicidesintheunitedkingdomreferencetables/current); [AIHW young people suicide/self-harm page, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/suicide-self-harm-monitoring/population-groups/young-people/suicide-self-harm-young-people).

For suicide deaths, WHO defines the crude suicide rate as suicide deaths in a year divided by population and multiplied by 100,000, and says GHO estimates derive from Global Health Estimates using WHO Mortality Database vital-registration input for countries with high-quality cause-of-death systems, adjusted where necessary [WHO GHO indicator metadata](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age). OECD cautions that international suicide comparisons are affected by reporting criteria, ascertainment of intent, death certification, forensic investigation and confidentiality rules, so variation across countries needs care even where the data are official [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html).

### Adolescent suicide: NZ is an outlier, but "worst" depends on dataset and period

UNICEF Aotearoa's summary of Innocenti Report Card 19 says New Zealand ranked lowest, 36th of 36 countries with available mental-wellbeing data, and that New Zealand's 15-19 adolescent suicide rate rose from 14.9 per 100,000 before COVID to 17.1 per 100,000 using available confirmed 2018-2020 data, making it the highest in that comparison and nearly three times the high-income-country average of about 6.5 [UNICEF Aotearoa, 2025](https://www.unicef.org.nz/stories/report-card-19-what-it-means-for-aotearoa). The UNICEF Innocenti report itself says the league table uses six indicators, including adolescent suicide and life satisfaction, and its appendix says the adolescent-suicide figure is a 15-19 rate per 100,000 using three-year averages from the WHO Mortality Database with additional New Zealand data from Te Whatu Ora's suicide web tool [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19, pp. 4, 15, 68](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf).

WHO GHO's 2021 single-year harmonised 15-19 estimate gives a different comparator picture: New Zealand is high at 9.7 per 100,000, above the UK at 5.6 and below Australia at 10.9 in the extracted country set; the US, Japan and Korea are also higher than New Zealand on that same GHO 2021 15-19 measure [WHO GHO OData API extraction, accessed 5 July 2026](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SDGSUICIDE). This does not refute UNICEF's finding, because the UNICEF and WHO GHO numbers use different source series and periods, but it does mean a current finding should avoid saying New Zealand is unconditionally worst on every adolescent-suicide dataset [WHO GHO indicator metadata](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age); [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19 appendix, 2025](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf).

### 20-somethings and under-30 suicide: NZ is high versus Australia and the UK, but not uniquely highest

OECD's Society at a Glance 2024 says the average OECD suicide rate for ages 15-29 is about 10 per 100,000, and that Korea, New Zealand, Japan and Estonia have the highest under-30 rates, at 17 or more per 100,000 youth [OECD Society at a Glance 2024](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/society-at-a-glance-2024_918d8db3-en/full-report/suicides_357d50c7.html). WHO GHO's 2021 OData extraction gives New Zealand 15.2 per 100,000 for ages 15-29 and 17.7 for ages 20-29; Australia is slightly lower at 14.7 and 16.3 respectively, while the UK is much lower at 10.3 and 12.4 respectively [WHO GHO OData API extraction, accessed 5 July 2026](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SDGSUICIDE).

New Zealand domestic suspected-suicide material also supports the 20-24 concern: the stream framing cites Ministry Cabinet material saying 20-24-year-olds had the highest suspected suicide rate among five-year age groups in 2023/24, at 19.9 per 100,000 [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf). I did not find a clean, public, same-method Australia/UK/NZ 20-24 table; ONS publishes 20-24 at 10.2 per 100,000 for England and Wales 2024 registrations, while AIHW publishes Australian suicide counts as 15-17 and 18-24 on its young-people page rather than a directly comparable 20-24 rate in the page text [ONS 2024 dataset](https://www.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/birthsdeathsandmarriages/deaths/datasets/suicidesintheunitedkingdomreferencetables/current); [AIHW young people suicide/self-harm page, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/suicide-self-harm-monitoring/population-groups/young-people/suicide-self-harm-young-people).

### Distress and disorder: NZ is high, but survey constructs are not harmonised

New Zealand's latest NZ Health Survey summary says 22.9% of young adults aged 15-24 experienced high or very high psychological distress in the four weeks before the 2024/25 survey, the highest percentage of any age group [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/wellbeing/nz-health-survey-mental-health-and-substance-use-data/). The Ministry of Health confirms the same 22.9% figure and notes that K10 psychological distress is a screening tool rather than a measure of the prevalence of mental-health conditions [Ministry of Health NZ, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey).

Australia's public youth comparator is more severe-looking but not the same measure: AIHW says nearly two in five young people aged 16-24, 39%, had experienced a mental disorder in the previous 12 months in the 2020-2022 National Study of Mental Health and Wellbeing, with female prevalence 46% and male prevalence 32% [AIHW health of young people, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/children-youth/health-of-young-people). ABS's national study page says the all-age 16-85 12-month mental-disorder prevalence was 21.5% in 2020-2022 and gives the survey's disorder categories, but the youth 16-24 figure is most directly stated in AIHW's youth page [ABS National Study of Mental Health and Wellbeing 2020-2022](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/health/mental-health/national-study-mental-health-and-wellbeing/latest-release); [AIHW health of young people, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/children-youth/health-of-young-people).

England's child/youth survey is also not a direct K10 comparison: NHS England says 20.3% of 8-16-year-olds, 23.3% of 17-19-year-olds and 21.7% of 20-25-year-olds had a probable mental disorder in 2023 [NHS England, 2023](https://www.england.nhs.uk/2023/11/one-in-five-children-and-young-people-had-a-probable-mental-disorder-in-2023/). NHS Digital's 2023/24 Adult Psychiatric Morbidity Survey separately says common mental-health-condition prevalence among 16-24-year-olds rose from 17.5% in 2007 to 25.8% in 2023/24, but that measure covers England adults aged 16+ and is not the same as the child/youth probable-disorder series [NHS Digital APMS 2023/24](https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/adult-psychiatric-morbidity-survey/survey-of-mental-health-and-wellbeing-england-2023-24).

### Self-harm: Australia has clear youth hospitalisation rates; NZ and England comparators use different surfaces

AIHW reports that in 2023/24 Australian intentional self-harm hospitalisation rates were highest for ages 15-19 at 256 per 100,000, lower for ages 20-24 at 182 per 100,000, and much higher for females aged 15-19 at 405 per 100,000 than for males of the same age at 114 per 100,000 [AIHW young people suicide/self-harm page, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/suicide-self-harm-monitoring/population-groups/young-people/suicide-self-harm-young-people).

For New Zealand, public Figure.NZ tables sourced from Te Whatu Ora show 2023 publicly funded hospital discharges for intentional self-harm of 2,339 in ages 15-19 and 1,577 in ages 20-24, with female counts much higher than male counts in both age bands; this is a count table, not a harmonised international rate [Figure.NZ / Te Whatu Ora intentional self-harm hospitalisations, 2023](https://figure.nz/chart/N1rdnqrvR5jM4vKw-ozh5iZfCzJUw2Tsm). The official Ministry youth self-harm hospitalisation page exists and says reports are now published biannually from 2024/25, but the page text available through the fetch did not expose a same-method rate table for Australia/UK comparison [Ministry of Health youth self-harm hospitalisations page](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/planning-and-performance-data/youth-self-harm-hospitalisations).

For England, NHS Digital's 2023/24 APMS reports that lifetime non-suicidal self-harm among 16-74-year-olds rose to 10.3%, and says younger adults were more likely to report lifetime non-suicidal self-harm than older age groups [NHS Digital APMS 2023/24](https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/adult-psychiatric-morbidity-survey/survey-of-mental-health-and-wellbeing-england-2023-24). The APMS suicidal-thoughts/self-harm chapter says 10.8% of adults reported ever self-harming without suicidal intent, around 5 million adults in England, but the web text I accessed did not expose a full age-specific table suitable for a 15-24 NZ/Australia/UK comparison [NHS Digital APMS self-harm chapter, 2023/24](https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/adult-psychiatric-morbidity-survey/survey-of-mental-health-and-wellbeing-england-2023-24/suicidal-thoughts-suicide-attempts-and-self-harm).

### Service access: the access problem is real in all three countries, but public metrics are not rankable

New Zealand has specific evidence of youth access pressure: Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 service-access summary says 19-24-year-olds had 4,919 fewer people using specialist services over five years, 0-18-year-olds had the lowest proportion seen within three weeks at 69.9% in 2024/25, and 0-18-year-olds had the highest declined-referral rate at 14.9% [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf). The Auditor-General's 2024 youth mental-health audit found the available system fragmented and said not all young people have timely and barrier-free access to appropriate mental-health care [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health).

Australia has public evidence of high need and service use, but the headline measures differ: AIHW's youth mental-health page says 16-24-year-olds had the highest 12-month disorder prevalence, females aged 16-24 accessed Medicare-subsidised mental-health services at nearly twice the rate of males of the same age, and young people face barriers including cost, long waits and limited service access [AIHW young people's mental health, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/mental-health/topic-areas/populations/young-people-s-mental-health). England's NHS source says Mental Health Support Teams covered 35% of pupils and learners in further education in 2023, which is a coverage metric rather than a wait-time or unmet-need metric [NHS England, 2023](https://www.england.nhs.uk/2023/11/one-in-five-children-and-young-people-had-a-probable-mental-disorder-in-2023/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A full extraction from WHO Mortality Database, not only GHO estimates, reproducing UNICEF's three-year moving averages for all Report Card countries would either strengthen or soften the exact adolescent-suicide ranking; I could verify UNICEF's NZ figure and method but did not reproduce the whole 36-country table from raw WHO Mortality Database files [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19 appendix, 2025](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf); [WHO GHO indicator metadata](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/suicide-rates--crude--%28per-100-000-population%29-%28sdg-3.4.2%29--15-19-years-of-age).
- A same-year, same-ICD, same-registration/occurrence-basis 20-24 suicide table for NZ, Australia and the UK would improve the 20-24 comparison; I found solid NZ domestic and ONS England-Wales figures, but not a clean public Australia/NZ/UK 20-24 table in the time available [Ministry of Health Cabinet material, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf); [ONS 2024 dataset](https://www.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/birthsdeathsandmarriages/deaths/datasets/suicidesintheunitedkingdomreferencetables/current).
- A harmonised cross-national youth mental-health survey using the same instrument, age bands and fieldwork period would change the distress/disorder conclusion; current NZ K10 distress, Australian diagnostic-disorder, and England probable-disorder measures are related but not interchangeable [Ministry of Health NZ, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey); [AIHW health of young people, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/children-youth/health-of-young-people); [NHS England, 2023](https://www.england.nhs.uk/2023/11/one-in-five-children-and-young-people-had-a-probable-mental-disorder-in-2023/).
- A public, audited youth service-access dataset covering wait times, declined referrals, unmet need and effective treatment for NZ, Australia and England would allow a service-access ranking; current public evidence supports access pressure but not a defensible cross-country rank [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf); [AIHW young people's mental health, 2026](https://www.aihw.gov.au/mental-health/topic-areas/populations/young-people-s-mental-health).
- Human clinical, youth, Māori, Pacific and lived-experience review is still needed before this finding is used to choose interventions, because comparative mortality rankings do not identify which supports are culturally safe, trusted or effective for young people in Aotearoa [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health); [UNICEF Innocenti Report Card 19, 2025](https://unicef-nz.cdn.prismic.io/unicef-nz/aCO_OCdWJ-7kSCq__UNICEF-Innocenti-Report-Card-19-Child-Wellbeing-Unpredictable-World-2025.pdf).
