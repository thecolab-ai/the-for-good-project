# What harm evidence exists for unregulated peptides, and how large is the NZ-specific data gap?

## Executive answer

- **International peer-reviewed evidence is strong and consistent:** online-purchased semaglutide products showed endotoxin in every sample tested and 7.7–14.37% actual purity against a 99% label claim [Ashraf et al., *J Med Internet Res* 2024;26:e65440](https://www.jmir.org/2024/1/e65440); products marketed as SARMs contained a labelled SARM in only 52% of cases with 59% mismatched dosing across two independent studies on two continents [Van Wagoner et al., *JAMA* 2017;318(20):2004-2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29183075/); [Leaney et al., *Drug Testing and Analysis* 2021;13:122-127](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/dta.2908).
- **Australia's TGA has documented real, serious harm from unapproved peptides**, including systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) requiring hospital management and severe allergic reactions requiring adrenaline and hospitalisation, and made peptides a formal compliance priority in June 2026 after a joint operation seized peptide/PIED products with an estimated street value over AU$2 million [TGA, safety alert, 13 April 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/safety/safety-monitoring-and-information/safety-alerts/understanding-your-responsibilities-when-importing-compounding-and-supplying-unapproved-peptide-products); [TGA, media release, 10 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/tga-strengthens-compliance-focus-unapproved-peptide-products-part-evolving-risk-response); [TGA/CMO joint statement, 19 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/concerns-regarding-public-health-risks-associated-unapproved-peptide-products).
- **New Zealand has no equivalent published dataset for these specific substances.** We directly queried Medsafe's own public adverse-reaction search tool (SMARS) for the 11 unscheduled/newly-scheduled peptides and SARMs named in this issue and by the TGA — BPC-157, GHK-Cu, TB-500, kisspeptin, melanotan, CJC-1295, ostarine, ligandrol, andarine, retatrutide, and thymosin — and every one returned **zero ingredient-level matches**, while the two ARTG/Medsafe-approved GLP-1 medicines in the same broader drug class (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are populated with real report histories. This shows the CARM reporting and search apparatus works and is queryable, but nobody has ever lodged (or the system has never indexed) a report against any of these named grey-market substances.
- **The National Poisons Centre and NZ's licensed drug-checking services confirm the same gap independently, in their own words.** The NPC publishes no substance-level statistics and only supplies custom data on request, for a fee, with no public precedent we could find for peptides/SARMs specifically [National Poisons Centre, Statistics](https://poisons.co.nz/poisons-centre-info/statistics/). The NZ Drug Foundation's own drug-checking service states plainly that it currently **cannot test peptides at all** ("Not at the moment... regardless of dilution... Our further testing lab also cannot detect these substances") and can only sometimes test SARMs, and that it cannot quantify the "surge" in inquiries it has seen because its service is anonymous by design [NZ Drug Foundation/The Level, Emily Hughes, 19 February 2026](https://thelevel.org.nz/news-and-stories/drug-checking-for-steroids-peptides); [NZ Herald/Newstalk ZB, 19 April 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/steroids-peptides-and-the-growth-of-an-unregulated-grey-market-of-drugs-in-nz/).
- **The size of the gap cannot be expressed as a number, because none of NZ's three plausible harm-surveillance channels (poison centre calls, CARM/Medsafe adverse reactions, licensed drug checking) currently measures these substances at all.** That absence — not a small or partial dataset — is the finding.

**Overall confidence:** High — the international harm evidence is High confidence (peer-reviewed, partly replicated across independent studies, plus primary regulator statements). The claim that NZ holds no data on these specific substances is also High confidence, because it is corroborated independently across three different NZ systems and directly verified against a live government database rather than inferred from silence. This is graded slightly cautiously within "High" because one load-bearing international claim (semaglutide purity/endotoxin) rests on a single study, and because a "zero" result from a live database query, however clean, is not the same as a written confirmation from the agency that runs it — both flagged explicitly below rather than smoothed over.

## Evidence

### International peer-reviewed contamination and mislabelling evidence

**Semaglutide/GLP-1 products bought online without a prescription.** A 2024 market-surveillance study made test purchases from six illegal online pharmacies; of the products actually delivered, laboratory analysis found **endotoxin in every sample**, at levels between 2.1645 and 8.9511 EU/mg, and **measured semaglutide purity of only 7.7–14.37%**, against a 99% purity claim on the label. The authors classified the products as "probable substandard and falsified" [Ashraf, Mackey, Vida, Kulcsár, Schmidt, Balázs, Domián, Li, Csákó & Fittler, "Multifactor Quality and Safety Analysis of Semaglutide Products Sold by Online Sellers Without a Prescription," *J Med Internet Res* 2024 Nov 7;26:e65440, doi:10.2196/65440](https://www.jmir.org/2024/1/e65440) (also at [PMC11582493](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11582493/)). This is the source of the "endotoxin in every sample" and "8–14% purity against a 99% claim" figures cited in the framing issue. We could not find a second, independent laboratory study replicating these exact figures for semaglutide specifically — see limits, below. A separate, non-laboratory US data point corroborates that the harm signal is real at scale: the FDA had received **392 adverse event reports for compounded semaglutide and 215 for compounded tirzepatide** as of 30 November 2024 [Sood & Garg, "Global Rise of Compounded Weight-Loss Medicines: A Worrisome Trend," *J Endocr Soc* 2025 Jun 7;9(8):bvaf084](https://academic.oup.com/jes/article/9/8/bvaf084/8158112).

**SARMs (selective androgen receptor modulators) sold online.** The most-cited study bought 44 products marketed as SARMs over the internet and found that **only 23 (52%) contained one or more actual SARMs** (ostarine, LGD-4033, or andarine); no active compound was detected in 4 products (9%); 11 products (25%) contained an unlisted substance; and in only 18 of 44 products (41%) did the labelled amount match what was actually present [Van Wagoner, Eichner, Bhasin, Deuster & Eichner, "Chemical Composition and Labeling of Substances Marketed as Selective Androgen Receptor Modulators and Sold via the Internet," *JAMA* 2017 Nov 28;318(20):2004-2010, doi:10.1001/jama.2017.17069, PMID 29183075](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29183075/). This is the source of the "~52% purity" figure in the framing issue. A second, independent study on a different continent found a similar pattern of unreliability with different exact numbers: of 20 UK dietary supplements purporting to contain SARMs, only 6 (30%) were accurately labelled, with the rest showing no active ingredient, undeclared prohibited substances, or mismatched quantities [Leaney, Beck et al., "Analysis of Supplements Available to UK Consumers Purporting to Contain Selective Androgen Receptor Modulators," *Drug Testing and Analysis* 2021;13:122-127, doi:10.1002/dta.2908](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/dta.2908). The two studies disagree on the exact proportion accurately labelled (41–52% vs 30%) because they sampled different products in different markets at different times, but they agree on the underlying conclusion: **most peptide/SARM products bought online are unreliable, and a meaningful share contain nothing of the labelled substance at all.**

### TGA-documented harm and regulatory response (Australia)

Medsafe's own June 2025 submission to the Medicines Classification Committee, already cited in the sibling finding on border enforcement, traces the market to a 2013 Australian Crime Commission investigation into peptide use in professional sport, after which peptide use broadened into general wellness, anti-ageing and cosmetic use [Australian Crime Commission, *Organised Crime and Drugs in Sport*, February 2013, mirrored at medsafe.govt.nz](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consultations/acc-pieds.pdf). Since then, the TGA has documented and escalated a formal harm and compliance response:

- The TGA's safety alert states plainly that "adverse events and other concerns received by the TGA for these unapproved products include: severe allergic reactions, requiring doses of adrenaline and hospitalisation; systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) (severe inflammation in the body), requiring hospital management; generalised hypersensitivity symptoms such as full‑body intense itching, palpitations, pain and sweating; wide ranging body side effects, including insomnia, blurred vision, extreme tiredness, flu‑like illness, joint pain and skin sensitivity; musculoskeletal injuries, including plantar fasciitis and Achilles tendinitis, and poor product quality, including unsealed packaging from an online supplier" [TGA, "Understanding your responsibilities when importing, compounding and supplying unapproved peptide products," published 13 April 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/safety/safety-monitoring-and-information/safety-alerts/understanding-your-responsibilities-when-importing-compounding-and-supplying-unapproved-peptide-products) (fetched via plain HTTP, accessed 2026-07-07).
- A joint statement from the TGA and Australia's Chief Medical Officer, Professor Michael Kidd AO, states that "reports received by the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA), along with hospitalisation data from states and territories, have identified serious adverse effects associated with the use of unapproved peptides," including liver damage, severe allergic reactions requiring hospitalisation, and inflammation and other complications requiring medical attention, and names BPC‑157, GHK‑Cu, TB‑500, retatrutide and CJC‑1295 as examples [TGA/CMO joint media release, 19 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/concerns-regarding-public-health-risks-associated-unapproved-peptide-products) (accessed 2026-07-07).
- On 10 June 2026 the TGA formally added unapproved peptide products to its compliance priority list, citing "increased detection of unlawful importation, supply and advertising." The same release discloses that a joint operation with the Australian Border Force and Victoria Police had just seized peptide, PIED and steroid products "with an estimated street value in excess of $2 million" [TGA, "TGA strengthens compliance focus on unapproved peptide products as part of evolving risk response," 10 June 2026](https://www.tga.gov.au/news/media-releases/tga-strengthens-compliance-focus-unapproved-peptide-products-part-evolving-risk-response) (accessed 2026-07-07).

None of these TGA documents publishes an exact count of adverse event reports or hospitalisations; they describe reaction *categories* and cite hospitalisation data "from states and territories" without a published figure. We searched specifically for a numeric TGA count and could not find one published anywhere — this is a real limit even on the Australian side of the evidence base, not just the NZ side (see limits, below).

### The NZ National Poisons Centre: no published statistics, no free public data

The National Poisons Centre (NPC), based at the University of Otago, does not publish substance-level call statistics. Its own statistics page states only that statistics can be requested by emailing poisons@otago.ac.nz with the subject line "Statistics Request," and warns that "charges for supplying statistics may apply" [National Poisons Centre, *Statistics*](https://poisons.co.nz/poisons-centre-info/statistics/) (accessed 2026-07-07). We searched the abstracts of the 2025 Toxicology and Poisons Network Australasia (TAPNA) scientific assembly, held in Wellington and featuring National Poisons Centre-linked case reports, for any peptide, SARM, BPC-157, GLP-1/semaglutide, steroid or "performance and image enhancing drug" abstract; the only related presentation was an unrelated case of copper poisoning from self-injected veterinary calcium copper EDTA reported by the NZNPC, and a case series on dinitrophenol (DNP) overdose in a bodybuilder — neither is peptide or SARM data [Abstracts of the TAPNA 2025 scientific assembly, Wellington, *Clinical Toxicology* / Taylor & Francis, 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/24734306.2025.2561304), fetched via a stealth-browser rung after a plain-HTTP 403 (accessed 2026-07-07). We did not submit a paid statistics request to the NPC in this session — see follow-up, below.

### CARM/Medsafe: we directly queried the public adverse-reaction database

Medsafe's Centre for Adverse Reactions Monitoring (CARM) data is not simply undocumented online — it is exposed through a genuinely public, government-run search tool, the Suspected Medicine Adverse Reaction Search (SMARS), released "in keeping with the purpose of the Official Information Act 1982" [SMARS Disclaimer](https://azapp-medsafe-smars.azurewebsites.net/SMARS/Disclaimer) (accessed 2026-07-07). We used this tool directly (accepting its terms, then submitting ingredient searches through its "Find Ingredient" function, database current to 30 April 2026 as of the 7 July 2026 update) to search for every unscheduled or newly-scheduled peptide and SARM named in this issue and in the TGA's own list of examples: **BPC-157 (both "BPC157" and "BPC-157"), GHK-Cu, TB-500, kisspeptin, melanotan, CJC-1295, ostarine, ligandrol, andarine, retatrutide, and thymosin.** Every single one returned the tool's own message: **"No ingredient was found containing the characters entered."** By contrast, searching "semaglutide" and "tirzepatide" — the two GLP-1 peptide medicines in the same broader drug class that *are* approved, ARTG/Medsafe-listed medicines in New Zealand — immediately returned a matching ingredient with an active, populated report history.

This is a stronger and more current result than an absence of press coverage: it is a direct, reproducible negative result against Medsafe's own live database, current to within ten weeks of this research. It shows two things at once: (1) the CARM/SMARS reporting and search machinery is real, functioning, and does capture substantial data when reports exist, and (2) as of 30 April 2026, **not one adverse reaction to any of these eleven named unscheduled/grey-market substances has ever been lodged with, or is indexed by, CARM under a recognisable ingredient name.**

Per SMARS's own published reuse terms, "prior to any publication of data you must contact Medsafe (medsafeadrquery@health.govt.nz)" and any publication must state the source, the limitations, and that the information does not represent the opinion of CARM or Medsafe [SMARS Disclaimer, "Use of SMARS data"](https://azapp-medsafe-smars.azurewebsites.net/SMARS/Disclaimer). We have not contacted Medsafe to clear a citation, so **we deliberately do not reproduce the semaglutide/tirzepatide report counts, reaction breakdowns, or any other case-level content returned by our searches** in this document — only the qualitative fact that a match exists for those two ingredients and none exists for the eleven named grey-market substances. A human steward wanting the exact figures, or wanting to cite them, should complete that contact step first (see follow-up, below). Separately, Medsafe's formal channel for requesting detailed CARM data (rather than querying the public search tool) is by email to carmreport@health.govt.nz, specifying either a date range or up to five specific report references, with a stated two-week response aim [Medsafe, "Requesting adverse reaction information – Guidance for industry"](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/regulatory/requesting-adverse-reaction-information.asp) (accessed 2026-07-07). We did not submit a formal request through that channel in this session.

### NZ's licensed drug-checking services: peptides cannot currently be tested at all

New Zealand has three licensed drug-checking providers: KnowYourStuffNZ, the NZ Drug Foundation, and the NZ Needle Exchange Programme [PHF Science, *Drug checking services*](https://www.phfscience.nz/expertise/reducing-drug-harm/drug-checking-services/). The NZ Drug Foundation's own consumer-facing guidance is direct and primary-sourced:

> "Can drug checking test peptides (such as GLP-1 inhibitors like Ozempic)? **Not at the moment.** GLP-1 inhibitors are becoming more popular on the grey market, but unfortunately, we don't have these in our libraries, so we cannot detect them, regardless of their dilution. Our further testing lab also cannot detect these substances."
> — Emily Hughes, Principal Science Advisor, NZ Drug Foundation, ["Here's how to get your PIEDs (steroids, peptides) tested," The Level, 19 February 2026](https://thelevel.org.nz/news-and-stories/drug-checking-for-steroids-peptides)

The same source states SARMs testing is only "sometimes" possible — the service's Fourier-transform infrared spectrometer library includes ostarine, andarine, ligandrol and testolone but "can't detect some of the newer or less common SARMs" — and that even where a substance is detectable, drug checking "can't provide info about the 'strength' of the PIED" or identify bacterial contamination or heavy metals. KnowYourStuffNZ's own public drug-info page on steroids, updated 29 May 2025, covers anabolic steroids and post-cycle therapy in detail but does not yet contain a SARMs-specific section at all, despite the page's title ("Steroids and SARMS") — itself a small, direct indicator that the service's public-facing content on SARMs has not caught up to its title [KnowYourStuffNZ, "Steroids and SARMS," updated 29 May 2025](https://knowyourstuff.nz/drug-info/steroids-and-sarms/) (accessed 2026-07-07). KnowYourStuffNZ's public "Results & Reports" page contains no mention of steroids, SARMs, or peptides at all [KnowYourStuffNZ, *Results & Reports*](https://knowyourstuff.nz/results-reports/) (accessed 2026-07-07).

On the inquiry-volume side, NZ Drug Foundation principal science adviser Emily Hughes is quoted independently in two separate outlets making the same point: "over the past 18 months, they had seen a surge in inquiries" about peptides and SARMs, but "because people accessed their service anonymously it was impossible to give exact numbers" [NZ Herald/Newstalk ZB (Eva de Jong), "Steroids, peptides and the growth of an unregulated 'grey market' of drugs in NZ," 19 April 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/steroids-peptides-and-the-growth-of-an-unregulated-grey-market-of-drugs-in-nz/) (NZ Herald original at [nzherald.co.nz](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/steroids-peptides-and-the-growth-of-an-unregulated-grey-market-of-drugs-in-nz/ROGBILXHYRATHJVO2ZB2PSHXRY/), paywalled; the Newstalk ZB republication carries the same byline, date and quotes and is freely accessible). A separate, independent RNZ article a few weeks earlier quotes a different named NZ expert, Dr David Gerrard (Emeritus Professor in Sports Medicine, University of Otago), warning that synthetic peptides are dangerous and carry risks "without medical supervision," and quotes KnowYourStuffNZ's Casey Spearin confirming that buyers "couldn't be sure of what's actually in them" because the clinics cannot test these substances [RNZ, "Far too many risks come with synthetic peptide use, expert says," 5 February 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/586075/far-too-many-risks-come-with-synthetic-peptide-use-expert-says). Two independent NZ experts and two independent outlets, roughly six weeks apart, describing the same underlying gap in the same terms, is a reasonably strong corroboration that the gap is real and not one journalist's framing.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A written statistics request to the National Poisons Centre** (poisons@otago.ac.nz, subject "Statistics Request") asking specifically for call volumes tagged to peptides, SARMs, or the named substances above, for the period since 2022. We did not send this request in this session — sending an email on a named organisation's behalf from a personal account is a step we judged should be confirmed with a human first, not taken autonomously mid-research. A ready-to-send draft: *"Requesting a breakdown of NPC exposure calls relating to peptides (e.g. BPC-157, TB-500, melanotan, thymosin) and SARMs (e.g. ostarine, ligandrol, andarine) for 2022–2026, for public-interest research into the NZ-specific data gap on these substances."*
- **A formal CARM data request** to carmreport@health.govt.nz (per Medsafe's published guidance) specifying the same substance list and a 2022–2026 date range would give a citable, written confirmation of the "zero reports" result we obtained by directly querying SMARS ourselves — turning a reproducible negative search result into an authoritative agency statement. We did not send this request in this session.
- **Contacting Medsafe at medsafeadrquery@health.govt.nz before any publication of the semaglutide/tirzepatide SMARS figures**, per SMARS's own reuse terms, would let a future version of this document responsibly report the exact report counts and reaction breakdowns we saw but chose not to reproduce here.
- **A published TGA count of adverse event reports or hospitalisations** (rather than category lists) would let us grade the Australian harm evidence as more than "documented but unquantified." We searched specifically for this and could not find one.
- **Any future National Poisons Centre or CARM publication naming these substances** — for example, if the December 2025 scheduling of peptides as prescription medicines leads Medsafe or CARM to start tracking and reporting cases distinctly — would directly update the "how large is the gap" answer, since scheduling itself does not automatically generate case data; it only makes future cases easier to see coming.
- **What we could not verify:** whether the National Poisons Centre holds unpublished internal data on these substances that would answer this question directly (we did not pay for or request a custom statistics pull); whether any hospital in NZ codes peptide- or SARM-related presentations distinctly enough in the National Minimum Dataset to be queried (we did not attempt this — it would likely require an OIA to the Ministry of Health and is a plausible follow-up); and whether CARM has received reports naming these substances under an alternate or trade name our search terms did not match (we tried both hyphenated and unhyphenated BPC-157 forms but did not exhaustively test every possible product/trade name).

## Open follow-up questions

- What does a formal OIA-style request to the National Poisons Centre and CARM actually return for these substances, once someone with authority to send it on the project's behalf follows through on the draft requests above? This is the single most valuable next step and does not require a new research issue — it is a data-request task, not a research task.
- Now that peptides are scheduled prescription medicines (per the sibling finding on border enforcement, PR #719), will Medsafe or CARM begin publishing substance-specific safety signals for them, the way it does for approved GLP-1 medicines? Worth revisiting in 12 months once there has been time for cases to accumulate and be reported.
- Could NZ hospital admission data (National Minimum Dataset, Ministry of Health) be queried for peptide- or SARM-related presentations using procedure/diagnosis codes, independent of whether CARM has a matching adverse-reaction report? This would need its own OIA and is a plausible chunky follow-up question if a human steward wants it opened as a separate research issue.

We did not open a fan-out sub-issue for this question. The scope — international harm evidence plus a direct check of the three plausible NZ data sources — was answerable to a full standard within this single document once we found that Medsafe's CARM data is itself a live, publicly queryable tool rather than something we could only describe as "absent" secondhand.
