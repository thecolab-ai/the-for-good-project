# Sector reactions to NZ's 2026 assessment rollout echo early National Standards concerns on workload and trust, but add new AI-marking and data-sovereignty issues the 2009-2012 debate never had

## Executive answer

- **The 2026 rollout has drawn sector concern on five fronts: readiness/timeline, workload, AI-marking accuracy, data sovereignty, and timetable fit.** These come from unions (NZEI, PPTA), the principals' federation (NZPF), a principals' collective, a free-market think tank (NZ Initiative), and researchers — but only the union, NZPF, and collective statements are criticisms; the NZ Initiative is broadly supportive, and official sources frame the tool as "low-stakes, light-touch." NZPF's president has directly criticised SMART's rollout for lacking "adequate consultation and engagement" and linked it to "high-stakes ERO reviews" and "the looming threat of league tables." [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [NZPF Moot 2026 keynote](https://www.nzpf.ac.nz/moot-2026-keynote-speech-from-nzpf-president/); [Principals Today, 2026](https://principalstoday.co.nz/concerns-raised-over-low-uptake-of-new-assessment-tool/); [NZ Initiative, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/opinion/smart-educational-assessment/); [Beehive, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202053921/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-on-learning)

- **The pattern of concern overlaps with early National Standards reactions (2009-2012) on rushed timeline, no/insufficient trial, workload, and league-table/labelling risk — but the 2026 debate is distinct in two ways.** It centres on a specific digital tool (SMART) rather than a standards framework, and it introduces AI marking and offshore data storage as new concerns that did not exist in 2009-2012. [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380); [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback)

- **None of the 2026 sector claims surveyed here is representative outcome evidence.** They are position statements, a small deputy-principal survey, a collective's uptake claim, and opinion pieces. The issue brief explicitly cautions against treating union or media claims as representative, and that caution holds: there is no 2026 equivalent yet of the NZCER national survey or the Ministry-commissioned school-sample evaluation that gave the National Standards debate its quantitative backbone. [NZCER, 2010 survey](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/nzcer-2010-primary-intermediate-schools-national-survey); [Education Counts school-sample evaluation, 2011](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling/national-standards-school-sample-monitoring-and-evaluation-project-2011)

- **A key difference in the political economy of the two debates: in 2009 the principals' federation (NZPF) and the primary teachers' union (NZEI) actively boycotted the National Standards launch and co-hosted an opposition forum; in 2026 NZPF has not issued a comparable public boycott call over SMART specifically, but the federation is on the record criticising the rollout's process and accountability risk, not staying silent.** NZPF President Jason Miles told the federation's Annual President's Moot (13 March 2026) that "tools like the SMART assessment are prescribed without adequate consultation and engagement," linking it to "high-stakes ERO reviews" and "the looming threat of league tables." NZPF also co-organised a joint statement with NZEI (a hui on 24 March 2026, republished nationally in April 2026 and endorsed by a range of principals' associations and subject bodies) opposing the pace and consultation quality of the Government's broader 2026 curriculum reforms — evidence of unified principal/teacher-union opposition, though that statement addresses the curriculum overhaul generally rather than SMART specifically. So the strongest SMART-specific public criticism still comes from NZEI's Principals' Council and PPTA's deputy-principal membership, but it is not accurate to say NZPF has been silent or uninvolved: it has criticised SMART's consultation process directly and joined NZEI in a wider, unified sector statement — short of a formal boycott call, but a materially more active federation position than "no comparable" criticism implies. [NZPF/National Standards page (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20230610111613/http://www.nzpf.ac.nz/national-standards.html); [NZPF Moot 2026 keynote](https://www.nzpf.ac.nz/moot-2026-keynote-speech-from-nzpf-president/); [NZEI joint statement release, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/education-sector-backlash-against-governments-curriculum-reforms-intensifies); [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback)

- **Parents and whānau have no published 2026 reaction survey.** Official parent-facing material (Ministry guides) is descriptive and supportive; the only parent-voice in the public record is filtered through union and media framing. A lived-experience gap exists and should be flagged for a human with whānau or principal authority. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress); [Ministry SMART guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-smart-new-assessment-tool-your-child)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the 2026 sector statements are well-sourced as statements (what each actor said), and the early National Standards comparison rests on peer-reviewed and NZCER evidence; the limitation is that 2026 reactions are early (first assessment window closed 22 May 2026, detailed reports released 3 July 2026) and no representative 2026 sector survey exists yet.

## Evidence

### What the 2026 system requires (context for the reactions)

From 2026, state and state-integrated schools must carry out twice-yearly assessment of Year 3-8 students in reading, writing and maths using specified tools; schools can choose between SMART, PATs, or e-asTTle in 2026, with e-asTTle discontinued at the end of 2026. For kura kaupapa Māori, Ngā Kura ā-Iwi, and specified hapū/iwi-based special-character schools, the requirement does not apply until 1 January 2027. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) SMART is a bilingual digital tool for twice-yearly assessment and aromatawai in Years 3-10, aligned with the refreshed NZ Curriculum and Te Marautanga o Aotearoa, and is free for schools and kura. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

The Government says the new approach was developed following consultation with principals' associations and teachers, and trialling in 85 schools involving around 12,000 student assessment engagements, with positive feedback from participating schools. [Beehive, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202053921/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) Treasury's released advice, however, recorded significant reservations: it recommended against early pre-commitment, said key building blocks were unlikely to be in place, and warned the Ministry did not have enough time, stakeholder engagement or item-development capacity for the proposed schedule. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### 2026 sector reactions — by actor

**NZEI Te Riu Roa (primary teachers' union) — AI accuracy and data sovereignty.** NZEI's Principals' Council chair, Stephanie Madden, called the introduction "rushed" and urged parents to check whether their child's school uses SMART, citing "serious concerns over data protection and the accuracy of the AI-generated results." NZEI reported teachers saying the AI "failed to evaluate the quality of writing, instead giving identical marks to completely different student scripts based merely on word count." On data sovereignty, NZEI flagged contradictory Ministry messaging: a school-leader information sheet states "all SMART data will be stored securely in Aotearoa, with backups for disaster resilience and recovery stored in Australia," while a separate parent sheet claims the information is "stored in New Zealand" and the teacher sheet omits data storage entirely. NZEI also sought "water-tight guarantees regarding data privacy to prevent the creation of school league tables." [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [1News, 2026](https://www.1news.co.nz/2026/02/02/clarity-standardised-school-reports-on-students-learning/)

The data-storage contradiction NZEI identified is verifiable against the official source: the Tāhūrangi SMART overview confirms "All data is stored securely in Aotearoa New Zealand... Backups for disaster resilience and recovery are stored in Australia." [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) The Ministry frames this as compliant with NZ government data standards; NZEI frames the difference between the parent and leader sheets as misleading. Both are characterisations of the same documented fact.

**PPTA Te Wehengarua (secondary/post-primary teachers' union) — readiness, workload, timetable fit, AI fairness.** PPTA president Chris Abercrombie said deputy principals of Year 7-10 students, surveyed and interviewed after the first testing period, reported teachers "pretty underwhelmed" and that the tool "raises serious concerns about the quality of assessment." PPTA's specific concerns: the process demands "substantial additional resourcing, including extra teaching sessions, administrative time, professional learning, and staff backfilling"; the tool was "designed for 90-minute assessments whereas most schools in Aotearoa worked to timetables of 60-minute classes" (a "logistical challenge"); the choice of Dickens and Shakespeare texts was questioned for relevance to "junior students in 21st century Aotearoa"; the AI writing marker "seemed to mark students higher based on how much they wrote, as opposed to the quality of the writing"; several students received identical marks despite "significant differences" in script quality and structure; and it is a "one size fits all system that does not cater for students with learning needs." PPTA called for "an independent review into the purchase and rollout of this tool." [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback)

PPTA's methodology was a survey and interviews of deputy and assistant principals "around the motu" — not a nationally representative sample of teachers, and PPTA does not publish the n. It is sector feedback, not a survey-weighted finding.

**New Zealand Principals' Federation (NZPF) — consultation and accountability-risk critique.** In his keynote to NZPF's Annual President's Moot on 13 March 2026, National President Jason Miles criticised the Government's approach across several reforms using a food-service metaphor: "the owner is mandating menu changes, dictating cooking methods, and imposing new rules without asking the people serving plates what they actually need. Tools like the SMART assessment are prescribed without adequate consultation and engagement." He went on to link the reforms directly to accountability risk: with "high-stakes ERO reviews and the looming threat of league tables, we're being forced to serve fast food when our communities deserve and need a rich, varied, nourishing educational experience." [NZPF Moot 2026 keynote](https://www.nzpf.ac.nz/moot-2026-keynote-speech-from-nzpf-president/) This is the clearest evidence that NZPF itself — not just NZEI's Principals' Council — has criticised SMART specifically, on both consultation-process and accountability-risk grounds, even though it stops short of a boycott call. Separately, NZPF co-organised a joint statement with NZEI following a hui on 24 March 2026, republished in major newspapers in April 2026 and endorsed by "a breadth of educators, peak bodies, and subject associations," opposing the "frantic pace, flawed direction, and lack of genuine consultation" in the Government's wider 2026 curriculum reforms. That statement is about the curriculum overhaul broadly (of which SMART's aligned curriculum is one part), not a SMART-specific complaint, but it shows NZPF and NZEI acting in a unified front rather than NZPF sitting out the reaction. [NZEI joint statement release, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/education-sector-backlash-against-governments-curriculum-reforms-intensifies)

**Aotearoa Educators Collective (via Principals Today) — uptake.** An educators' collective reported that "only around 60 percent of schools have signed up so far," and that "some principals have indicated they have registered for the tool but may not fully intend to use it in practice." The same article notes the Ministry's position that schools can choose between SMART, e-asTTle, and PATs. [Principals Today, 2026](https://principalstoday.co.nz/concerns-raised-over-low-uptake-of-new-assessment-tool/) The ~60% uptake figure is attributed to the collective but not independently verified here; it should be treated as a lead, not a measured national statistic. The Ministry does not publish a live school-uptake percentage or dashboard in the sources reviewed, but it does publish two more-current official figures that speak to active use, not just sign-up. Its 23 June 2026 School Leaders Bulletin (issue 215) told schools "we've had strong engagement with the first SMART assessment window, with over 303,000 students registered." [Ministry of Education School Leaders Bulletin, 23 June 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/23-06-26) And a Beehive release six days earlier, on 17 June 2026, gave a more specific figure directly on active use: Education Minister Erica Stanford said "more than 1300 schools and 220,000 students have used" SMART "since it was introduced in February," and that "student completion rates of more than 70 percent across participating schools reflects that schools and kura are actively integrating SMART into classroom practice." [Beehive, 17 June 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260623214527/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/220000-students-take-smart-test) Neither figure settles the collective's ~60%-of-schools claim, because the denominators and dates differ (1300+ schools is not stated as a percentage of the roughly 2,500 state and state-integrated Year 3-8 schools the mandate covers, and both official figures predate the collective's report), but the 70%-completion figure directly answers the "signed up but not intending to use" concern for participating schools: it is evidence of active classroom use, not mere registration, even though it does not show what share of all eligible schools that 1300+ represents.

**New Zealand Initiative (free-market think tank) — supportive.** The NZ Initiative's Dr Michael Johnston argued parents "have a right to know how their children are faring at school" and that lack of national consistency "hamper[s] the clarity of school reports," calling SMART's progress-over-time emphasis "never before" required. He argued SMART will have formative uses (identifying students needing attention or extension), systems-level uses (allocating the Budget 2025 teacher-aide and learning-support increase), and accountability uses — proposing that "continued underperformance after the provision of support and time to improve should trigger a compulsory change in school leadership." [NZ Initiative, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/opinion/smart-educational-assessment/) This is an opinion piece, not research evidence, but it is the clearest public articulation of the pro-accountability case.

**Education researchers — curriculum-timeline concerns (adjacent).** Researchers David Pomeroy (University of Canterbury) and Lisa Darragh, writing in October 2025 about the new maths curriculum (which SMART aligns to), called the timeline "unrealistic, some say a 'nightmare'," noting teachers had "about eight school weeks to plan for the changes, alongside teaching, planning, marking, reporting, pastoral support and extracurricular activities." They contrasted this with England's 2013 National Curriculum, where "teachers had it 12 months ahead of implementation." [1News/Pomeroy & Darragh, 2025](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/10/29/a-rushed-new-maths-curriculum-doesnt-add-up-experts-call-for-more-time/) This is a researcher reaction to the curriculum timeline, not to SMART specifically, but it directly informs the readiness framing that unions apply to SMART.

**Official parent-facing material — descriptive, supportive, and silent on AI.** The Ministry's parent guide describes the five progress descriptors (Emerging, Developing, Consolidating, Proficient, Exceeding), progress-over-time visuals, and says descriptor selection should draw on "multiple sources" and teacher judgement. [Ministry parent guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-your-childs-reporting-and-progress) The Ministry's SMART guide for parents describes assessments as short (around 30-45 minutes) and answered by "selecting an option, dragging and dropping, or typing a short response," but does not mention AI at all. [Ministry SMART guide, 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/parent-portal/guide-smart-new-assessment-tool-your-child) The Ministry's separate, more technical Tāhūrangi overview is the source that confirms SMART "uses AI" for marking open-ended questions and states SMART "is not intended to replace teachers' professional judgement" — this document, not the parent guide, is the official confirmation of AI use. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) No parent reaction survey is published in the sources reviewed.

### Early National Standards reactions (2009-2012) — the comparison baseline

**Sector opposition was organised, public, and included a boycott.** The NZ Principals' Federation (NZPF) "joined in the Forum hosted by the New Zealand Educational Institute in November 2009," where the forum "believes that national standards represent considerable risk to student learning." Regional principals' associations (Hawke's Bay, Ōtaki, Tai Tokerau, Auckland, South Canterbury) submitted formal responses, and a "Letter to PM March 2010 calling for trial and opposing league tables" was organised. [NZPF National Standards page (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20230610111613/http://www.nzpf.ac.nz/national-standards.html) Thrupp's retrospective records that NZEI and NZPF "announced that they would boycott the official launch on October 23, 2009." [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380); [ResearchGate/Thrupp, 2007 onward](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/43647181_National_Standards_for_New_Zealand's_Primary_and_Intermediate_School_Pupils)

**The 2009 public consultation showed parental diffidence, not support.** NZCER analysed almost 5,000 responses to the 2009 consultation and found "38% of parents making negative comments about the National Standards system and only 14% making positive comments" (Wylie, Hodgen & Darr, 2009). [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380) This figure is Thrupp's secondary report of the underlying NZCER report to the Ministry (Wylie, Hodgen & Darr, 2009), which we could not independently locate online — the Ministry consultation-summary page Thrupp cites is no longer live and has no Wayback snapshot, so the percentages rest on Thrupp's single account of it. The then-Minister Anne Tolley's press release nonetheless claimed "consultation feedback shows strong support from parents for National Standards" — a claim independently verifiable against the primary source itself: the Beehive release states "Consultation feedback shows strong support from parents for National Standards, Education Minister Anne Tolley has announced," dated 20 September 2009. [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380); [Beehive/Tolley, 2009 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20221124091524/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/parents-support-national-standards)

**The "no trial" criticism was central from the start.** NZCER's 2013 conference paper records that "the haste with which the standards were developed and the unwillingness of the government to trial the standards added to this distrust," and that "the timeframe for implementation was unrealistic. Schools were asked to report against the standards in mid-year reports to their parents in 2010." [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf) Thrupp notes that because National Standards "had never been trialled, the first few years needed to be treated as a trial." [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380)

**Workload, curriculum narrowing and labelling were early, evidenced concerns.** The 2010-2012 school-sample evaluation (Ward & Thomas) found "high levels of concern among principals... about narrowing of the curriculum, league tables, demotivation of students who were consistently below the standards, and national testing being introduced; levels of concern that stayed high or grew even more between 2010 to 2012." [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf) The 2010 NZCER national survey (350 schools, conducted July 2010) showed "low levels of confidence among teachers and principals about their OTJs and the consistency across schools." [NZCER, 2010 survey](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/research/publications/nzcer-2010-primary-intermediate-schools-national-survey); [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf)

**By 2011, roughly a third of schools had signalled dislike.** NZ Herald reported in 2011 that National Standards had "generated much debate since it was introduced last year, with nearly a third of schools signalling their dislike of it," and that "Labour's stance has brought praise from the educational sector union NZEI and the Principals' Federation - both of which say the system is unwanted and unnecessary." [NZ Herald, 2011](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/national-standards-tops-hot-topics/FD52DZZ537IFNJHQIUCAFLURAA/)

**The seven-year NZCER survey (2016) showed support never recovered.** Only 16% of teachers believed National Standards had a positive impact; teacher support was 35%; 48% thought results provided a reliable picture (down to 23% of principals who thought it provided a "valuable picture" by 2016); 70% believed it had narrowed the curriculum (up from 50% in 2013). [NZ Herald, 2016](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/national-standards-no-positive-impact-on-achievement-say-teachers/PJVHTVPTCYRHOKSHL7JRBC7PDY/); [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

### What is different and what repeats

**What repeats.** The "rushed/no trial" frame is the single strongest continuity. In 2009-2010 it was "the unwillingness of the government to trial the standards" and "the timeframe for implementation was unrealistic" [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); in 2026 it is NZEI's "rushed," PPTA's "not ready," and Treasury's own warning that the Ministry lacked time and engagement capacity. [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [Treasury OIA, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) Workload, labelling, and league-table/data-misuse concerns also repeat, with the same actors (NZEI) raising them. The consultation-legitimacy dispute repeats in form: a government citing positive trial/consultation feedback [Beehive, 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202053921/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) versus sector actors and Treasury saying engagement was insufficient.

**What is new.** AI-marking accuracy and data sovereignty are genuinely novel concerns with no 2009-2012 analogue, because National Standards used teacher judgements on paper/local evidence, not a centrally-marked digital tool. The timetable-fit issue (90-minute assessments vs 60-minute NZ periods) is also new and specific to a digital timed tool. The shape of opposition has shifted rather than disappeared: in 2009 the principals' federation co-led a formal boycott and opposition forum; in 2026 there is no equivalent SMART-specific boycott call, but NZPF's president has directly criticised SMART's consultation process and linked it to "high-stakes ERO reviews" and "the looming threat of league tables," and NZPF co-organised a joint statement with NZEI against the pace and consultation quality of the wider 2026 curriculum reforms. SMART-specific criticism is still led by NZEI's Principals' Council and PPTA, with a principals' collective (Aotearoa Educators Collective) reporting uptake resistance, but NZPF is an active critic of the reform programme SMART sits within, not a silent or absent one. [NZPF (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20230610111613/http://www.nzpf.ac.nz/national-standards.html); [NZPF Moot 2026 keynote](https://www.nzpf.ac.nz/moot-2026-keynote-speech-from-nzpf-president/); [NZEI joint statement release, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/education-sector-backlash-against-governments-curriculum-reforms-intensifies); [Principals Today, 2026](https://principalstoday.co.nz/concerns-raised-over-low-uptake-of-new-assessment-tool/)

**Why the difference matters.** The 2009-2012 opposition was organised against a standards-and-OTJ framework that teachers could (and did) moderate locally; the 2026 concerns are increasingly about a centralised technical artifact (an AI-marked, Australian-sourced, centrally-stored tool) where the locus of trust shifts from teacher judgement to a vendor and an algorithm. [NZEI, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool); [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) That is a structural change in what the sector is being asked to trust, not just a repeat of the same politics.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A representative 2026 sector survey** (an NZCER national survey equivalent, or a Ministry-commissioned school-sample evaluation) would replace the position statements surveyed here with measured attitudes. None exists yet in the public sources reviewed; the first SMART detailed reports were released 3 July 2026 and the 2026 NZCER national survey (if run) would not be published until later in the year. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)
- **An independent technical audit of SMART's AI marking** (inter-rater reliability, bias by script length, accessibility) would either confirm or refute the NZEI/PPTA accuracy claims. PPTA has called for one; none is published in the sources reviewed. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback)
- **A published Ministry uptake figure expressed as a percentage of the full eligible school cohort** would settle the ~60% uptake claim. The Ministry's 23 June 2026 School Leaders Bulletin gives a national student-registration count (over 303,000), and a 17 June 2026 Beehive release gives a schools-and-students-used count (1300+ schools, 220,000 students) with a completion rate above 70% — the latter is a genuine active-use figure, not just sign-up — but neither expresses uptake as a share of the roughly 2,500 state and state-integrated Year 3-8 schools the mandate covers, so neither resolves the collective's ~60%-of-schools claim either way. [Ministry of Education School Leaders Bulletin, 23 June 2026](https://www.education.govt.nz/bulletins/school-leaders/23-06-26); [Beehive, 17 June 2026 (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260623214527/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/220000-students-take-smart-test)
- **A clear, single Ministry data-storage statement** (one message to parents, teachers, and leaders) would dissolve the NZEI "contradictory messaging" charge even if the underlying NZ-plus-Australia-backup arrangement remains.
- **Māori-medium sector voice** is thin in the public sources reviewed. The 2027 requirement for kura kaupapa Māori, Ngā Kura ā-Iwi, and hapū/iwi-based schools is documented [Tāhūrangi (Wayback)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p), but Māori-medium leader reactions to aromatawai implementation are not captured here. This needs a human with Māori-medium leadership or aromatawai authority.
