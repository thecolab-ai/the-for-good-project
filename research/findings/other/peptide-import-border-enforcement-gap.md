# How do peptides enter New Zealand, and how big is the border-enforcement gap for unscheduled peptides?

## Executive answer

- **Entry channel:** peptides overwhelmingly enter NZ as **personally-imported parcels** bought from overseas websites — not through licensed pharmacy supply chains. Sellers routinely label products **"for research purposes only"** to dodge advertising/sale restrictions, while marketing directly to consumers for therapeutic and performance/image-enhancing use. Medsafe's own submission to the Medicines Classification Committee (MCC) says this explicitly: "There are many websites overseas selling these products 'for research purposes only'. However, these products are being purchased by individuals who are importing with the intention to administer them for a therapeutic purpose" [Medsafe, *Classification of Unscheduled Peptides*, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf).
- **The enforcement gap, mechanically:** until a peptide is scheduled as a prescription medicine, Medsafe has **no legal grounds under the Medicines Act 1981** to seize it at the border — the parcel is released to the importer with a "high-risk medicine letter" instead. This is stated as the operative rule in Medsafe's June 2025 submission, not just reported by media: "When substances that are not scheduled as a prescription medicine are intercepted at the border, the product is released to the individual with a high-risk medicine letter (Appendix A) as Medsafe does not have the grounds, pursuant to the Medicines Act 1981, to seize them" [Medsafe, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf).
- **Nobody measures the denominator.** Customs/Medsafe track *interceptions*, not the *total inflow*. RNZ reported plainly: "Customs said it couldn't say how many got through without interception" [RNZ, 27 July 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/568172/medsafe-considers-crackdown-on-import-of-unregulated-peptide-medications). That sentence is the honest answer to "how big is the gap": the numerator (parcels caught) is published; the denominator (parcels sent, or in circulation) is not measured by any agency we could locate.
- **Interception counts, reconciled:** 15 parcels (2022) → **56 parcels in the ~7 weeks from 1 April to 19 May 2025** (Medsafe's own count, in its June 2025 MCC submission) → **370 parcels for all of 2025** → **>200 parcels in the first four months of 2026**. These are not four independent, conflicting series — see the reconciliation below — and the trend across all of them is a steep, apparently still-accelerating increase.
- **What changes once a peptide is scheduled:** from that point it becomes a prescription medicine, so importing, possessing or procuring it without authorisation is unlawful — Customs/Medsafe can lawfully **seize** rather than merely warn-and-release, and Medsafe gains a domestic enforcement lever it did not have before (it reports having "contacted 22 Kiwi websites selling peptides, and all have now been taken down" since the December 2025 classification) [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the mechanism (why the gap exists, and what changes on scheduling) is High confidence, drawn directly from Medsafe's own MCC submission and a Medsafe-sourced May/July 2026 statement. The interception *numbers* are Medium confidence: they come from Medsafe's internal recording as relayed through media, not from a published, citable NZ Customs statistical table, and the exact accounting period behind each headline figure required reconciling across three separate articles (see below).

## Evidence

### Who actually enters the country, and how

Peptides identified by Medsafe as being imported include thymic peptide hormones (e.g. thymosin alpha-1, thymosin beta-4/TB-500), BPC-157 and analogues, adrenocorticotropic hormone analogues, pineal gland peptides (e.g. epithalon), anti-microbial peptides, myostatin modulators, mitochondria-derived peptides, erythropoietin analogues, tuftsin, kisspeptins, and named individual peptides such as larazotide, PTD-DBM, AICAR, B7-33, PNC-27 and SS-31 [Medsafe, June 2025, pp.4–11](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). Medsafe's May 2026 consumer advisory adds BPC‑157, CJC‑1295, GHRP‑6, Ipamorelin, Kisspeptin, KLOW, melanotan II, retatrutide, "sealank", "seamax", TB‑500 and thymosin to the list of products "commonly advertise[d]" by online sellers [Medsafe, *Consumer advisory: Unapproved peptide products health warning*, 21 May 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp).

Medsafe traces the market back to a 2013 Australian Crime Commission investigation into peptide use in professional sport, after which "a variety of peptides have been developed for a range of therapeutic purposes, including... cognitive enhancement, hair growth, libido enhancers, sexual dysfunction, immune enhancers, and cancer treatment" — i.e. the market has broadened well past sports doping into general wellness/anti-ageing use [Medsafe, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). RNZ's July 2025 reporting on the same MCC submission is consistent: peptides are bought from overseas websites making claims about "cognitive enhancement, hair growth, libido enhancers, sexual dysfunction, immune enhancers and cancer treatment", often with "little, if any, evidence to back up the claims" [RNZ, 27 July 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/568172/medsafe-considers-crackdown-on-import-of-unregulated-peptide-medications).

Consistent with a personal-importation model rather than commercial/bulk smuggling, Medsafe frames the problem as "individuals... importing with the intention to administer them for a therapeutic purpose" [Medsafe, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf), and its 2026 advisory targets consumer behaviour ("Do not use them"; dispose via pharmacy/needle exchange) rather than describing organised trafficking [Medsafe, 21 May 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp). We found no evidence in official sources of large-scale commercial smuggling networks for peptides specifically (as distinct from the individual-parcel pattern) — this is a gap, not a finding that it doesn't happen (see "What would change this conclusion").

### The "research chemical" / "not for human consumption" labelling loophole

Medsafe treats the "for research purposes only" disclaimer as having no legal effect once it is clear the buyer intends human use. Its June 2025 MCC submission calls this out directly: "There are many websites overseas selling these products 'for research purposes only'. However, these products are being purchased by individuals who are importing with the intention to administer them for a therapeutic purpose" [Medsafe, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf).

Medsafe has since made the same point about social-media sellers using disclaimers to skirt promotion rules: "Medsafe said using disclaimers still did not make any sales lawful" [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides). Its 21 May 2026 consumer advisory states this as settled regulatory position, post-classification: "Many sellers label these products as 'for research purposes only'. This wording has no legal effect and does not permit sale, supply, or use in people. All clinical trials of 'new medicines' in New Zealand require approval from Medsafe and an ethics committee. Medsafe has not approved any clinical trials in New Zealand for these products. If a product is being offered for personal use, injection, or consumption, it is being supplied unlawfully" [Medsafe, 21 May 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp).

**Important nuance the issue's framing over-simplifies:** the labelling loophole is not a loophole in the sense of being *legally effective* — Medsafe's position (both before and after classification) is that the disclaimer never worked as a legal shield. The real "loophole" is narrower and more mechanical: **before a peptide is scheduled, the Medicines Act simply gives Medsafe no seizure power over it regardless of labelling** — the label is a marketing fig-leaf for the seller and a "not for human consumption" cover story if questioned, but the actual gap is the absence of scheduling, not the label's legal force.

### Personal importation rules — and why they don't reach unscheduled peptides

NZ Customs' and Medsafe's published personal-importation rules apply only to substances already scheduled as **prescription medicines**:

- Travellers carrying prescription medicines (other than controlled drugs) must declare them, carry a doctor's prescription or letter, keep them in original containers, and not exceed **three months' supply** (six months for oral contraceptives) [Customs, *Medicines*, updated 9 July 2025](https://www.customs.govt.nz/travel-to-and-from-new-zealand/medicines); [Medsafe, *Importing Medicines*](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consumers/miet/importmedicines.asp).
- Controlled drugs carried by travellers are capped at **one month's supply**, with mandatory declaration [Customs, *Medicines*](https://www.customs.govt.nz/travel-to-and-from-new-zealand/medicines).
- Prescription medicines **sent** from overseas (not carried by a traveller) require the recipient to show "a reasonable excuse" and, per Medsafe, an original letter/prescription from a NZ-authorised prescriber matching the product's name, strength, quantity and form [Medsafe, *Importing Medicines*](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consumers/miet/importmedicines.asp).

None of this machinery mentions unscheduled substances, because none of it applies to them — an unscheduled peptide is not legally a "medicine" for Medicines Act purposes, so there is no quantity limit, no prescription requirement, and no declaration obligation specific to it. This is a structural gap, not an oversight: the personal-importation regime is entirely downstream of scheduling. Confirmed independently by Medsafe's and Customs' own guidance pages, both of which describe the same prescription-medicine-only regime with no separate provision for unscheduled "research chemical" imports [Medsafe, *Importing Medicines*](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consumers/miet/importmedicines.asp); [Customs, *Medicines*](https://www.customs.govt.nz/travel-to-and-from-new-zealand/medicines).

### The "high-risk medicine letter" release practice

This is the border-enforcement mechanism at the centre of the gap, and it is documented in Medsafe's own words, not just paraphrased by media:

> "The Investigation & Enforcement Team at Medsafe have been recording data on a wide variety of unscheduled peptides that have been intercepted at the border... Whilst SARMs are classified as prescription medicines (as a group entry) in New Zealand, many peptides are not scheduled... When substances that are not scheduled as a prescription medicine are intercepted at the border, the product is released to the individual with a high-risk medicine letter (Appendix A) as Medsafe does not have the grounds, pursuant to the Medicines Act 1981, to seize them. This is posing a risk to health, as the product's quality, efficacy and interactions with other medicines is unknown."
> — [Medsafe, *Classification of Unscheduled Peptides*, submission to the MCC, June 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf)

RNZ's contemporaneous reporting on the same submission corroborates this independently: "Medsafe could not seize peptides at the border, if they were not classed as prescription medicine. Instead, it released the products to the purchasers with a 'high-risk medicine letter'" [RNZ, 27 July 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/568172/medsafe-considers-crackdown-on-import-of-unregulated-peptide-medications). We could not retrieve the text of Appendix A itself (the letter template) — the PDF's Appendix A/B pages (13–14) are image scans with no extractable text layer, and we did not attempt OCR — so we cannot describe exactly what the letter tells recipients beyond Medsafe's own gloss above. **What Appendix A actually says to the person receiving their parcel is unverified and worth a specific follow-up.**

### Reconciling the interception numbers: 56 vs 370, and the "15 → 370 → 200" series

The framing issue (#709/#711) flagged a "56-vs-370" reconciliation problem. Having read the primary document, this is not a real conflict — it's two different accounting windows that the secondary reporting blurred together:

| Figure | Period | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 15 parcels | 2022 (full year) | [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides); [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/) |
| 56 parcels | **1 April 2025 to 19 May 2025** (49 days) | [Medsafe, June 2025 MCC submission, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf) — the primary document's own words |
| 370 parcels | 2025 (full calendar year) | [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides); [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/) |
| >200 parcels | First four months of 2026 | [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/) (RNZ's 25 May 2026 piece independently says "200" "so far this year") |

Annualising the 49-day figure (56 parcels ÷ 49 days × 365 days ≈ 417 parcels/year) lands close to the reported 370-for-2025 total — well within the range you'd expect given the 56-day window sits inside 2025 and the true rate could vary across the year. **These are consistent, not contradictory.** The 417 estimate being somewhat above 370 could reflect normal within-year variation, a slightly front-loaded April–May surge (plausible, since interest in peptides is reportedly driven by social-media/influencer cycles), or just small-number noise over a 49-day sample.

**One discrepancy worth flagging explicitly:** RNZ's own 27 July 2025 article describing the *same* Medsafe submission paraphrases the period as "the year to May" — i.e. implying a full 12-month window — rather than the 49-day window (1 April–19 May 2025) the primary PDF actually states. This looks like a media simplification/error rather than a second dataset: the primary source is unambiguous ("From 1 April 2025 to 19 May 2025, 56 parcels have been intercepted"), so we treat the PDF's wording as authoritative and RNZ's "year to May" phrasing as an inaccurate gloss on the same underlying figure, not a competing count.

**Where these numbers actually come from matters.** NZ Customs' own public statistics page for drug interceptions does not break out peptides or SARMs as a category at all — its published categories are built around the Misuse of Drugs Act 1975 (controlled drugs) and prescription-medicine declaration failures, with no reference to peptides, SARMs, or "unscheduled" substances in the page's own description of what it tracks [Customs, *Drug interceptions*](https://customs.govt.nz/about-us/statistics/drug-interceptions). Newstalk ZB's July 2026 reporting attributes the 15/370/200+ figures to "Medsafe" directly ("Medsafe intercepted more than 370 parcels..."), not to a published Customs dataset [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/), consistent with the June 2025 submission's statement that it is Medsafe's own Investigation & Enforcement Team doing the recording, working from parcels Customs refers to it. In short: **the headline figures are Medsafe's internal count of border referrals, not a Customs statistical release** — there is no publicly downloadable time series we could locate, only the four snapshot numbers reported piecemeal across three news articles and one MCC submission over roughly a year. A direct OIA to Medsafe (and/or Customs, to confirm the referral counts on its side) requesting a monthly interception series since 2022 would resolve this properly; we did not file one in this session (see limits, below).

### What changes operationally once a peptide is scheduled

The 74th MCC meeting (23 July 2025) considered Medsafe's proposal to add ten group entries and six named peptides as prescription medicines, but **deferred** the decision "pending additional information" about possible legitimate non-medical uses (e.g. cosmetics industry) [Medsafe, *Minutes of the 74th meeting of the Medicines Classification Committee*, 23 July 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm). The minutes record a Secretariat's Note that Medsafe subsequently consulted the cosmetics industry, received no objections, and "therefore recommends the peptides outlined in this proposal are classified as prescription medicines" [same source]. The 75th MCC meeting (19 November 2025) agenda shows the formal objection period for the 74th meeting's recommendations closed 13 November 2025, with no new peptide-specific item on that agenda — consistent with the classification proceeding administratively after that objection window rather than at a further full committee vote [Medsafe, *Agenda for the 75th MCC Meeting*, published 30 October 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen75/agen75.htm).

We could not locate the actual December 2025 Gazette notice on Medsafe's own "Recent NZ Gazette Notices" page — that page's most recent listed entries are from August 2025 and it is marked "Revised: 4 September 2025", i.e. it appears not to have been updated to include the December action [Medsafe, *Recent NZ Gazette Notices Relating to Classification*](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp). That the classification did take effect in December 2025 is corroborated by two independent, dated media reports rather than the primary Gazette text itself: RNZ (25 May 2026) states "In December, Medsafe classified some peptides as prescription medicines" [RNZ, 25 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/596283/fears-for-safety-as-influencers-push-synthetic-peptides), and Newstalk ZB (2 July 2026) states "Since December last year, many peptides have been classified as prescription medicines, making it illegal to import, possess or procure them without authorisation" [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/). This is also directly confirmed, in Medsafe's own voice, by its 21 May 2026 consumer advisory: "Under the Medicines Act 1981, many peptide products and SARMs are prescription medicines. It is unlawful to import, supply, possess or procure prescription medicines unless this is authorised" [Medsafe, 21 May 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp) — so while we lack the primary Gazette instrument, we have a primary regulator statement plus two independent, dated secondary sources agreeing on both the fact and the month.

The operational effect of scheduling, per these sources:

1. **Seizure becomes lawful.** Newstalk ZB reports Medsafe's own framing: "the change has made it easier to stop illegal imports at the border" [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/). Medsafe's May 2026 advisory says it will "continue to seize these products" [Medsafe, 21 May 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/safety/Alerts/Consumer-advisory-Unapproved-peptide-products-health-warning.asp) — a shift in verb from "release... with a high-risk medicine letter" (pre-classification) to "seize" (post-classification).
2. **Domestic supply-side enforcement is unlocked.** Medsafe reports it has "contacted 22 Kiwi websites selling peptides, and all have now been taken down" since the classification [Newstalk ZB, 2 July 2026](https://www.newstalkzb.co.nz/news/national/unapproved-peptides-seized-at-border-as-medsafe-crackdown-intensifies/) — a lever against NZ-based sellers that a purely border-focused "release with a letter" regime did not give it. (Single-sourced: we found this only in the one Newstalk ZB article; treat the "22 websites, all taken down" figure as Medium/Low confidence pending a second source.)
3. **Legitimate users are not cut off.** The 74th MCC minutes record that the committee "noted that, should these peptides be classified, if there is legitimate use for any of these products patients can continue to access them, provided they obtain a prescription from a health professional" [Medsafe, 74th MCC Minutes, 23 July 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm) — classification restricts unauthorised personal importation, not therapeutic access via a prescriber.
4. **New/derivative substances still slip the net.** Even for BPC-157 specifically — one of the longer-scheduled peptides — Medsafe's June 2025 submission notes "derivatives of BPC-157 have been intercepted [since 2023], including NL-BPC-157 Hexadecapeptide", which "are not currently captured by the BPC-157 classification" [Medsafe, June 2025, p.6](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). This is exactly why Medsafe pursued *group* entries rather than only naming individual substances — but it also shows scheduling doesn't close the gap permanently; new analogues can still emerge unscheduled, restarting the same "release, not seize" problem at the border for that specific analogue until it too is captured (either individually or by falling within a group entry's definition).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A month-by-month interception series direct from Customs and/or Medsafe** (via an OIA request to both agencies, since the referral relationship means each may hold a slightly different count) would let us build the clean, continuous time series the framing issue asked for, rather than reconciling four snapshot numbers scattered across a 2025 MCC submission and three news articles. We did not file this OIA in this session — this is the single most valuable next step and a strong candidate for a follow-up research issue if a human steward wants to open one.
- **The actual text of the "high-risk medicine letter" (Appendix A of the June 2025 submission)** would let us describe precisely what an importer is told when their unscheduled parcel is released — we could not extract it (image-only PDF pages) and did not attempt OCR.
- **Confirmation of the December 2025 Gazette notice itself** (rather than inferring it from two dated news reports plus a primary regulator advisory that assumes it happened) would firm up the "High" confidence claim above to something closer to definitive. Medsafe's own gazette-notice tracking page appears stale (last revised September 2025) and does not yet list it.
- **Independent corroboration of the "22 websites taken down" claim** and of "Customs said it couldn't say how many got through" — both currently rest on a single outlet's reporting.
- **Evidence on whether commercial/bulk importation (as opposed to individual parcels) is a meaningful channel.** Every source we found frames this as a personal-importation phenomenon (one buyer, one parcel, "for their own use"); we found no data on whether any NZ-based reselling or bulk-import operation exists. Its absence from official sources could mean it's genuinely rare, or that it isn't being looked for — we can't distinguish those from what's public.
