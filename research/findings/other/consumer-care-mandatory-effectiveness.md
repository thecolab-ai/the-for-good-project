# NZ's mandatory Consumer Care Obligations raised the floor for postpay customers but leave prepay self-disconnection — the largest source of disconnection events — structurally outside their core protections

## Executive answer

- **The rule is now mandatory, monitored, and enforceable — a real change from the voluntary regime.** Since 1 April 2025 the Consumer Care Obligations sit in the Electricity Industry Participation Code (Part 11A), retailers must file an annual, CEO-certified compliance report, and the Authority can escalate to the Rulings Panel for a pecuniary penalty of up to $2 million [Electricity Authority, 2025 Consumer Care Obligations Annual Compliance Report, 9 Feb 2026](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf); [Scoop / EA press release, 10 Feb 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU2602/S00107/room-for-improvement-in-power-companies-compliance-with-consumer-care-rules.htm).
- **First-year compliance is broadly high among the big players but far from universal, and it is self-assessed, not audited.** Of 51 retailers who reported, only 18 (35%) self-assessed full compliance; the four large gentailers (78% of the market) were largely compliant, but Mercury alone logged 22 non-compliances, and the single most common high-priority failure was around *sharing information about medically dependent consumers* (MDCs) [EA compliance report, 2026](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).
- **The protection that matters most — never disconnecting a medically dependent consumer — was still breached.** The report records **two instances where retailers disconnected medically dependent consumers**, both self-reported before the deadline, with "enforcement action … undertaken" (no penalty outcome disclosed) [EA compliance report, 2026](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).
- **The biggest gap is structural, not a compliance failure: prepay self-disconnection largely sits outside the disconnection-for-non-payment rules.** Prepay supply simply stops when credit runs out, which the Authority itself says "do[es] not necessarily indicate a gap in customer care." So while postpay disconnections averaged ~810/month (Jan–Oct 2025), prepay auto-disconnections averaged ~27,000/month affecting ~10,000 households (~35% of prepay customers) — and an MDC on prepay is *not* protected from losing supply the way an MDC on postpay is [EA, "Strengthening visibility of disconnections", 15 Dec 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/general-news/strengthening-visibility-of-disconnections-in-the-retail-electricity-market/); [EA, "If you rely on electricity for medical reasons"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-you-rely-on-electricity-for-medical-reasons/); [Consumer NZ, 24 Jan 2024](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-disconnection-an-unregulated-threat-for-households).
- **Net answer to the question:** "Mandatory" has demonstrably improved the *floor* and *visibility* for postpay customers, and gives the regulator teeth it previously lacked. But on the evidence available, the Obligations do **not** yet protect the most exposed households — low-income prepay users and MDCs on prepay — because the rules regulate the prepay *experience* (warnings, monitoring, fast reconnection) without prohibiting self-disconnection or extending the MDC no-disconnect protection to prepay. Advocates (FinCap, Consumer NZ, the Everyone Connected campaign) argue this is the central remaining defect.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the compliance and disconnection *data* are High (drawn from the Authority's own primary documents), but any judgement about whether the rules *work in outcome terms* is limited by a partial first year, self-assessed reporting, and the absence of independent outcome evaluation.

## Evidence

### What became mandatory, and the 2023 baseline it replaced

The Consumer Care Guidelines were voluntary until 1 April 2025, when they became the **Consumer Care Obligations** under Part 11A and Schedule 11A.1 of the Electricity Industry Participation Code 2010; two key protections (no disconnection where a medically dependent consumer may live, and a requirement that fees be reasonable) took effect earlier, on 1 January 2025 [EA compliance report, 2026, §2.2](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf); [Consumer NZ, "Consumer Care Obligations: what you need to know"](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-authority-s-consumer-care-obligations-what-you-need-to-know).

The move to mandatory answered a specific failure. A 2023 Electricity Authority compliance review of the voluntary guidelines found only **18 of 38 retailers (47%) were fully complying**, and that roughly **a quarter of households with a medically dependent consumer were not fully protected** from disconnection; the Consumer Advocacy Council's then-chair described a "level of contempt for the guidelines that exists in the industry" [RNZ, 15 May 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/489951/fewer-than-half-of-electricity-retailers-complying-with-consumer-care-guidelines-report). (This baseline is single-sourced here to RNZ, which reports the Authority's own 2023 review — see load-bearing-claims table.)

### Post-mandate compliance: broadly high for large retailers, uneven overall, self-assessed

The first annual compliance report covers a **partial year** (clauses 37 and 68 from 1 January, all remaining clauses from 1 April, to 30 June 2025), so it is an early read, not a full-year verdict [EA compliance report, 2026, §2.5](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf). Key figures:

- **51 retailers (incl. brands) submitted; 18 (35%) self-assessed full compliance**, the rest "mostly compliant" — only a slight rise on the 48 alignment statements filed under the voluntary guidelines, suggesting most in-scope retailers were already engaged [EA compliance report, 2026, §5.1, Summary of findings](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).
- **Large gentailers (78% of residential ICPs) were largely compliant**: only Contact self-assessed as fully compliant on all clauses; **Mercury identified 22 non-compliances — the most of any retailer** — attributing them to an inefficient initial approach needing system rework, and noting it does not disconnect for non-payment except in clear fraud cases [EA compliance report, 2026, §5.16–5.18](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).
- The single **most common non-compliance across all retailers was Part 8 clause 51 — the obligation to share information about medically dependent consumers (17 non-compliances)** — followed by clauses on declining contracts, requesting contact information, and other MDC-information duties [EA compliance report, 2026, Table 1](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).
- **30 in-scope-looking participants did not submit**, but they represent just 95 residential ICPs (~0.005% of the national total); the smallest non-submitters cited lack of awareness [EA compliance report, 2026, §5.5–5.7](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).

**Two structural caveats the Authority states itself.** First, the results are **self-assessed** — "self-reporting is subjective … self-reporting biases may be present," and "there may be areas of non-compliance present which a retailer has not identified" (the Authority does its own checks against submitted evidence, but this is not an independent audit) [EA compliance report, 2026, §4.1–4.3, 4.9](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf). Second, a common reason given for non-compliance was that processes "were still preparing for implementation," so the Authority expects (but has not yet demonstrated) a reduction next year [EA compliance report, 2026, Common reasons for non-compliance](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf).

### Enforcement: real teeth, but the outcomes are not yet visible

The Authority operates a "Voluntary, Assisted, Directed, Enforced" (VADE) model and has designated Parts 7–9 (disconnection, MDCs, fees/bonds) as **high-priority** for enforcement because of the harm potential [EA compliance report, 2026, §3.1–3.3](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf). Its top sanction is a referral to the independent **Rulings Panel, which can impose a pecuniary penalty of up to $2 million** [Scoop / EA press release, 10 Feb 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU2602/S00107/room-for-improvement-in-power-companies-compliance-with-consumer-care-rules.htm).

Concretely, the report states **enforcement action has been undertaken** in relation to the **two reported disconnections of medically dependent consumers**, and GM Legal, Monitoring and Compliance Airihi Mahuika said "protecting medically dependent consumers is non-negotiable … where harm is possible, we will act" [EA compliance report, 2026, §5.31](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf); [Scoop / EA press release, 10 Feb 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/BU2602/S00107/room-for-improvement-in-power-companies-compliance-with-consumer-care-rules.htm). **What "enforcement action" produced (a warning, a Rulings Panel referral, a penalty) is not disclosed** — I found no published Rulings Panel decision on these cases, so the *effectiveness* of enforcement, as opposed to its existence, is unverified (see "what would change this conclusion").

### The core gap: prepay self-disconnection sits largely outside the disconnection protections

The Obligations *do* impose prepay-specific duties — retailers must warn a prepay customer when their balance falls below roughly two days' usage, **monitor the frequency and duration of self-disconnections** and proactively offer help to customers who frequently run out, and reconnect within 30 minutes of a top-up (Part 7 clause 45) [Consumer NZ, "Consumer Care Obligations: what you need to know"](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-authority-s-consumer-care-obligations-what-you-need-to-know); [EA compliance report, 2026, Appendix D (Glo-Bug, cl 45)](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf). But they **do not prohibit self-disconnection when credit runs out, and do not extend the MDC no-disconnect protection to prepay**. The Authority's own consumer guidance is explicit: postpay MDCs "cannot be disconnected for non-payment," but "on prepay plans, electricity supply will stop if/when the account runs out of credit," and it advises that "it's safer for households with medically dependent consumers to be on postpay" [EA, "If you rely on electricity for medical reasons"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-you-rely-on-electricity-for-medical-reasons/).

This distinction is not incidental — it is the reason disconnection events are concentrated where the rules are weakest (the sources measure disconnection exposure, not downstream harm to households, which remains unquantified — see confidence note and "what would change this conclusion"). The Authority's December 2025 disconnections dashboard (its first, covering Jan–Oct 2025) shows:

| Measure | Postpay (contract) | Prepay |
|---|---|---|
| Avg disconnections/month | ~810 | ~27,000 |
| Households affected/month | <0.1% of postpay base | ~10,000 (~35% of prepay customers) |
| Repeated disconnections | — | 58% disconnected more than once |
| % lasting <1 day | 44% | 94% |

[EA, "Strengthening visibility of disconnections", 15 Dec 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/general-news/strengthening-visibility-of-disconnections-in-the-retail-electricity-market/); [RNZ, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/582029/1000-customers-each-weekday-have-power-disconnected).

Crucially, the Authority frames prepay disconnections as automatic-on-credit-depletion events that "do not necessarily indicate a gap in customer care," whereas postpay disconnections only occur after the mandatory contact/support steps [EA, 15 Dec 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/general-news/strengthening-visibility-of-disconnections-in-the-retail-electricity-market/). Consumer NZ made the same structural point a year earlier: the guidelines treat postpay disconnection as a "last-resort measure," but prepay auto-disconnection is "framed as a choice" not to top up, so it falls outside those protections — and prepay disconnections were **excluded from the Authority's official disconnection data collection from 2013** until the new dashboard, keeping them statistically invisible [Consumer NZ, 24 Jan 2024](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-disconnection-an-unregulated-threat-for-households).

### Who this hits, and what advocates say remains unfixed

Prepay is disproportionately a low-income, poor-credit, last-resort product, and it is more expensive: prepay customers are charged on average about **14% more than the cheapest available plan**, and roughly 6% of households end up on prepay because of payment difficulty [Consumer NZ, 24 Jan 2024](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-disconnection-an-unregulated-threat-for-households). Payment pressure is unevenly distributed — around 26% of Pasifika and 15% of Māori households report it, versus ~10% overall [Consumer NZ, 24 Jan 2024](https://www.consumer.org.nz/articles/electricity-disconnection-an-unregulated-threat-for-households).

FinCap's Jake Lilley offered a pointed international comparison: **Victoria, Australia — which prohibits prepay automatic disconnection — recently averaged just over a thousand disconnections a *month* despite a larger population**, against New Zealand's ~27,000 prepay disconnections a month [RNZ, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/582029/1000-customers-each-weekday-have-power-disconnected). Consumer NZ's Jessica Walker noted "many households do not actively choose pre-pay; they end up on it as a last resort" [RNZ, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/582029/1000-customers-each-weekday-have-power-disconnected). The **Everyone Connected** campaign's asks are: ban disconnection/reconnection fees; require that companies "never charge their prepay customers higher prices than those paying by other means"; publish prepay disconnection data; and make the (then-voluntary) guidelines mandatory and properly enforced [Everyone Connected](https://www.everyoneconnectednz.com/winter-2025). The mandate ask has been met and the data-publication ask is partly met (the new dashboard); the **prepay pricing and self-disconnection asks remain open**.

### The Authority itself has flagged policy/Code gaps

The report identifies **three clauses where compliance was "not possible in limited circumstances," which may be a Code issue**, and says it will consider Code changes — for example, "type 3" retailers on islanded/customer networks with no ICPs in the registry cannot share MDC information via the standard EIEP4A file process (Part 8 clause 51) [EA compliance report, 2026, §5.33 and Table 5](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9266/2025_Consumer_Care_Obligations_Annual_Compliance_Report.pdf). So the regulator is on record that the current drafting leaves some vulnerable-consumer information-sharing structurally impossible for certain retailers — an acknowledged gap, distinct from the prepay one.

## What would change this conclusion

- **An independent audit** (rather than self-assessment) that surfaced materially more non-compliance than retailers reported would weaken the "broadly high compliance" reading — the Authority itself flags self-reporting bias.
- **The 2026 full-year compliance report** is the real test: if non-compliance does not fall as the Authority predicts, "mandatory" will look weaker than it does after one partial year.
- **Disclosed enforcement outcomes.** "Enforcement action undertaken" against the two MDC disconnections is not the same as a penalty. A published Rulings Panel decision (or its absence) would show whether the $2m sanction is a live deterrent or a theoretical one.
- **Whether the Authority extends protections to prepay.** If a Code change prohibited prepay self-disconnection (or extended the MDC no-disconnect rule to prepay, à la Victoria), the central gap identified here would close; if it declines to, the gap is a deliberate policy choice, not an oversight.
- **Outcome data, not process data.** The strongest refutation or confirmation would be evidence on whether households actually stayed connected, self-rationed less, or fell into less arrears after April 2025 — none of the sources here measure that.

**What I could not verify:**
- The specific enforcement sanction (if any) for the two MDC disconnections — no Rulings Panel decision found.
- The primary 2023 EA compliance-review document (relied on RNZ's reporting of it).
- The exact Victorian disconnection figure (advocate-stated).
- Whether the prepay monitoring clauses (warnings, frequency tracking) are measurably reducing prepay self-disconnection — the Jan–Oct 2025 data was roughly stable with a slight decline, but the series is too short and the mandate too new to attribute causation.
- **Lived experience.** Whether these rules feel protective to a prepay household with a medically dependent member is a question that needs people with that experience, not documents — flagged for a human.

## Open follow-up questions

- Does an outcome evaluation (arrears, self-rationing, reconnection times, health harm) show the Obligations improving household outcomes, beyond process compliance? (Would need MBIE/EA/health data not surfaced here.)
- Should NZ follow Victoria and prohibit prepay automatic disconnection or extend the MDC no-disconnect rule to prepay — what would that cost retailers and save households? (An ideate/build question — gated; not opened here.)
