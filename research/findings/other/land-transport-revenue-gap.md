# NZ land transport revenue has a funding-gap problem before it has a petrol-tax-collapse problem

## Executive answer

- **Petrol excise duty has not collapsed.** Net NLTF fuel excise duty was $1.926b in 2024/25, close to $1.933b in 2023/24, while net road user charges rose from $1.802b to $1.998b and became the largest net land-transport revenue line in 2024/25 [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25, pp. 222-223](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf) ([archived copy used for extraction](https://web.archive.org/web/20260616071144/https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf)).
- **The observed revenue shock since 2015 was policy-driven, not EV-driven.** The 2021/22 and 2022/23 accounts include temporary Crown revenue replacing reduced fuel excise duty and RUC, and by 2022/23 that Crown replacement was $697m for fuel excise duty and $713m for RUC [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2022/23, pp. 270-271](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-nzta/2022-23/nltf-annual-report-2022-23.pdf).
- **The bigger current gap is between the investment programme and user-charge revenue.** GPS 2024 says NLTF revenue comprises user charges including FED, RUC, vehicle registration, tolls and state-highway property income, supplemented by Crown grants and loans [Ministry of Transport, GPS 2024, p. 11](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Government-Policy-Statement-on-land-transport-2024-FINAL.pdf); Te Waihanga says the 2024-27 NLTP has $12.8b of Crown grants and loans, nearly 40% of planned expenditure [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan online, 2026](https://tewaihanga.govt.nz/national-infrastructure-plan-online/3-planning-what-we-can-afford/33-fixing-land-transport-funding-and-investment).
- **EV uptake is not automatically a revenue hole if EVs pay current light RUC.** At the post-temporary ordinary petrol excise rate of 70.024 cents/litre and a 9.2 L/100km real-world light-petrol benchmark, an average petrol vehicle contributes about $64/1,000km in FED; a light EV pays $76/1,000km in RUC, and a plug-in hybrid paying $38/1,000km plus half the benchmark petrol use contributes about $70/1,000km [Ministry of Transport, FED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges); [NZTA, RUC for electric vehicles](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/ruc-for-electric-vehicles); [Zhu et al., ATRF 2015, p. 11](https://australasiantransportresearchforum.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/ATRF2015_Resubmission_9.pdf).
- **Efficient petrol and non-plug-in hybrid vehicles create the clearer revenue-proxy problem.** A petrol vehicle using 5.0 L/100km pays about $35/1,000km in FED at the same post-temporary ordinary rate, roughly $29/1,000km below the 9.2 L/100km benchmark, unless or until petrol vehicles move to RUC [Ministry of Transport, FED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges); [Zhu et al., ATRF 2015, p. 11](https://australasiantransportresearchforum.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/ATRF2015_Resubmission_9.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high for the historical NLTF accounts and current rates; medium for the scenario model because it is deliberately simple and uses a literature fuel-consumption benchmark rather than a full Ministry fleet micro-simulation.

### Crown and local-government contributions are now central, not peripheral

GPS 2024 says the NLTF is funded by user charges such as FED, RUC, vehicle registration, tolls and state-highway property income, supplemented by substantial Crown grants and loans [Ministry of Transport, GPS 2024, p. 11](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Government-Policy-Statement-on-land-transport-2024-FINAL.pdf). Te Waihanga's 2026 National Infrastructure Plan says 2024-27 Crown grants and loans total $12.8b, nearly 40% of planned expenditure, and frames the issue as planned land-transport spending exceeding user revenue [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan online, 2026](https://tewaihanga.govt.nz/national-infrastructure-plan-online/3-planning-what-we-can-afford/33-fixing-land-transport-funding-and-investment).

Local government matters because co-funded local roads and public transport use rates, targeted rates, borrowing, development contributions and fare revenue alongside NLTF funding; Auckland Transport's RLTP funding page describes Auckland transport activities as funded from both the NLTF and council sources, and says Crown funding is also often used to supplement the NLTF [Auckland Transport, RLTP funding](https://haveyoursay.at.govt.nz/rltp-funding). The best national local-share baseline found here is programme-level rather than annual audited actuals: 2015-18 implies about $1.1b/year, 2018-21 implies about $1.3b/year, and GPS 2021/GPS 2024 sources point to about $1.5b/year from local government [NZTA 2015-18 NLTP release](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/media-releases/139-billion-partnership-to-strengthen-transport-links-across-new-zealand/); [Auckland Transport/Waka Kotahi Pukekohe DBC, p. 13](https://nzta.govt.nz/assets/projects/te-tupu-ngatahi-supporting-growth/south-auckland/resources/pukekohe-detailed-business-case.pdf); [Ministry of Transport, Draft GPS 2021 Q&A, p. 1](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Paper/Draft-GPS-Questions-and-Answers-2021.pdf); [Ministry of Transport, GPS 2024, p. 7](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Government-Policy-Statement-on-land-transport-2024-FINAL.pdf). LGNZ's GPS 2024 submission independently described the GPS as requiring around $1.5b per year from local government, which supports the order of magnitude but is not a substitute for audited annual actuals [LGNZ submission on GPS 2024, p. 4](https://d1pepq1a2249p5.cloudfront.net/media/documents/LGNZ_Submission_GPS_Land_Transport_2024_final.pdf).

### Official projections: the Ministry expects transition pressure, not a near-term collapse

The Ministry of Transport's 2025 RUC retail-market RIS says light RUC revenue is projected to grow consistently, excise revenue is expected to peak around 2035 and decline gradually towards 2054, and total transport revenue has been increasing because the fleet is growing, diesel vehicles have increased and the light RUC rate is slightly higher than average FED per kilometre [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail-market RIS, 2025, p. 4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). The same RIS says EV uptake is expected to be the primary future driver of declining excise revenue and could add 0.7m to 1.1m additional EVs by 2035 [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail-market RIS, 2025, p. 4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

The Government's current policy direction is to transition the petrol fleet to RUC rather than leave EVs outside road funding: the Ministry says RUC is paid by diesel, heavy and electric vehicles as a prepaid per-kilometre charge while FED applies to petrol, and that the government is working towards transitioning the petrol fleet to RUC [Ministry of Transport, Road User Charges system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system). NZTA similarly says the Government has announced next steps for replacing petrol tax with electronic road user charges [NZTA, replacing fuel tax with RUC](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/replacing-fuel-tax-with-road-user-charges).

### Transparent scenario model

The model below is not a Ministry forecast; it is a transparent sensitivity test for the revenue mechanism. It uses 2024/25 net FED of $1.926b as the starting pool, the post-temporary ordinary petrol excise rate of 70.024 cents/litre, current light EV RUC of $76/1,000km, current plug-in hybrid RUC of $38/1,000km, a 9.2 L/100km real-world light-petrol benchmark, and an illustrative one-quarter shift of current petrol kilometres to show scale rather than forecast uptake [NZTA, NLTF annual report 2024/25, pp. 222-223](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf) ([archive](https://web.archive.org/web/20260616071144/https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/annual-report-national-land-transport-fund-annual-report-202425/annual-report-2025-nltf.pdf)); [Ministry of Transport, FED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges); [NZTA, RUC for electric vehicles](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/ruc-for-electric-vehicles); [Zhu et al., ATRF 2015, p. 11](https://australasiantransportresearchforum.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/ATRF2015_Resubmission_9.pdf).

Formula: benchmark petrol FED per 1,000km = `0.70024 * litres_per_100km * 10`; scenario revenue change = `2024/25 FED base * affected_petrol_km_share * ((scenario $/1,000km - benchmark $/1,000km) / benchmark $/1,000km)`, where `affected_petrol_km_share` is set to 25% only as a round-number sensitivity.

| Scenario | Revenue per 1,000km | Change versus 9.2 L/100km petrol benchmark | Illustrative annual impact if 25% of current petrol-km shifted |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Benchmark petrol at 9.2 L/100km | $64.42 | $0 | $0 |
| BEV paying current light EV RUC | $76.00 | +$11.58 | +$87m |
| PHEV paying $38 RUC and using petrol for half benchmark energy | $70.21 | +$5.79 | +$43m |
| Petrol fleet becomes 20% more fuel efficient but stays on FED | $51.54 | -$12.88 | -$96m |
| Efficient petrol/hybrid at 5.0 L/100km and no RUC | $35.01 | -$29.41 | -$220m |

This simple model says an EV transition **with current light EV RUC applied** is not the main revenue-volume threat, because the light EV rate is above the estimated average petrol-FED equivalent [NZTA, RUC for electric vehicles](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/ruc-for-electric-vehicles); [Ministry of Transport, RUC retail-market RIS, 2025, p. 4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). The more immediate revenue proxy problem is that efficient petrol and non-plug-in hybrid vehicles can pay materially less per kilometre while still using the road, which is why a broader petrol-to-RUC transition is fiscally more important than EV-only RUC [Ministry of Transport, Road User Charges system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system); [NZTA, replacing fuel tax with RUC](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/replacing-fuel-tax-with-road-user-charges).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Ministry or NZTA fleet-revenue model showing that EVs pay materially lower effective RUC per kilometre than petrol vehicles pay in FED, after exemptions, avoidance, odometer lag, administration costs and real VKT are included, would weaken the conclusion that EVs under current RUC are not the central revenue hole.
- A current official real-world New Zealand light-petrol fuel-consumption series materially below 9.2 L/100km would make the EV-RUC comparison stronger; a series materially above 10.9 L/100km would make current light EV RUC lower than average petrol-FED equivalent.
- I could not verify a single machine-readable official year-by-year NLTF revenue dataset covering all lines from 2014/15 to 2024/25; the baseline is transcribed from annual-report PDFs, with several live NZTA PDFs bot-blocked in this environment and therefore fetched through Wayback snapshots under the repo's ADR-0006 fetch ladder.
- I did not model behavioural response, compliance/avoidance, odometer-tampering risk, RUC administration cost, distribution by household income, rurality, vehicle weight, congestion externalities, or local-government rating constraints; those need a fuller Ministry-style model and human policy judgement.
- The local-government contribution series is a year-by-year programme baseline built from official NLTP/GPS local-share signals, not audited annual actuals. A consolidated audited national local-share actual dataset would improve this materially, and would be the first thing to use if a later agent or human can obtain it.

- How large are RUC compliance, enforcement and administration costs likely to become under universal light-vehicle RUC?
