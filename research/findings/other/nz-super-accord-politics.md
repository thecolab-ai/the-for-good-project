# NZ Super reform is technically feasible but durable change needs cross-party and public trust

## Executive answer

- New Zealand has not resisted NZ Super reform because reform is technically impossible: governments introduced a surcharge in 1985, raised the age from 60 to 65 over 1992-2001, legislated the 1993 Accord framework, abolished the surcharge in 1998, restored the wage floor in 2000, and created the NZ Super Fund in 2001 [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf); [Treasury/MSD framework paper, 2007](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf).
- The hard line is political durability: the 1985-1998 surcharge reduced payments to higher-income superannuitants but became "highly unpopular", New Zealand First campaigned against it, and it was abolished under the 1996 National-New Zealand First coalition agreement [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf); [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf).
- The age rise is the clearest technically successful reform: Treasury's 2005 working paper says the predominant reason for the 1991-2001 rise in older people's labour-force participation was the phased rise in NZ Super eligibility from 60 to 65 [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09). That does not make a new age rise automatically durable, because current party and advocacy positions remain split on age 65 versus 67 and on any means test [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697); [National Party commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [ACT, 2026](https://www.act.org.nz/news/hipkins-buries-his-head-in-the); [NZ First seniors policy](https://www.nzfirst.nz/improving-the-lives-of-our-seniors); [Grey Power](https://www.greypower.co.nz/maintaining-nz-super-benefits).
- Universality is politically sticky because it is simple, predictable, and trusted: the 2025 Retirement Commission review describes NZ Super as simple and universal, cites survey evidence that universality without means-testing was the highest-ranked feature, and records strong submitter support for maintaining NZ Super as a universal non-means-tested entitlement [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- A durable accord would likely need all of these conditions: cross-party machinery before detailed cuts, public consultation in parallel, long lead times, explicit transition protection for near-retirees and disadvantaged groups, and system-wide treatment of KiwiSaver, NZ Super, related transfers, work, housing, health, and savings incentives [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf); [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the historical chronology and current stated positions are well supported, but the judgement about what would be politically durable is inferential and would need polling, party-negotiation evidence, and older people's lived-experience input.

## Evidence

### Technically feasible reform has already happened

NZ Super settings have changed repeatedly through ordinary politics and legislation: a National Superannuitant surcharge was introduced in 1985, the qualifying age was lifted from 60 toward 65 from 1991/1992, a multi-party accord was signed and formalised in 1993, the surcharge was abolished in 1998, the 65% wage floor was restored in 2000, and the New Zealand Superannuation Act 2001 established the NZ Super Fund [Treasury/MSD framework paper, 2007](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf); [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf). Current NZ Super remains a residence-based payment from age 65 that is not income- or asset-tested, while the residence requirement is already phasing from 10 to 20 years by cohort for people turning 65 from 1 July 2024 through 1 July 2042 [Work and Income](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/index.html); [New Zealand Government](https://www.govt.nz/browse/tax-benefits-and-finance/new-zealand-superannuation-and-the-veterans-pension/applying-for-nz-superannuation/).

The 1992-2001 age rise shows technical and behavioural effect, not just statutory possibility: Treasury's 2005 paper says New Zealand experienced a sharp rise in older people's labour-force participation over 1991-2001 and attributes the predominant reason to raising the eligibility age for the universal public pension from 60 to 65 over nine years [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09). Treasury's same paper estimated that if the eligibility age had already been 65 in 1991, labour-force participation rates for 60-64-year-old men and women would each have been about 21 percentage points higher, but it also warned that the strength and speed of New Zealand's response might not be repeatable in other settings [Treasury WP 05/09](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/wp/effect-new-zealand-superannuation-eligibility-age-labour-force-participation-older-people-wp-05-09).

### Means testing failed politically under the surcharge

The surcharge was a targeted clawback rather than a formal income test, but the Retirement Commission's 2008 history paper says it had a similar effect: in its first year, about 10% of superannuitants paid the equivalent of their full superannuation back through the surcharge and another 13% repaid a partial amount [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf). The same history records that the surcharge was highly unpopular, was tightened in the early 1990s instead of an initial income-test proposal, and was abolished from 1 April 1998 under the National-New Zealand First coalition agreement [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf).

The RPRC/Jeff Todd account reaches the same political lesson from a participant perspective: the 1993 Accord retained an income-reduction mechanism through the surcharge, New Zealand First stayed outside the Accord, Labour and New Zealand First later committed to remove the surcharge, and the report states that "for all of its strengths, it could not stand the political heat" [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf). This is the main caution for a new means test: a measure can be fiscally rational and administratively designable, but still unstable if it lacks broad community support and is easy to campaign against as a broken universal promise [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf); [KASPANZ, 2025](https://kaspanz.com/means-testing-discredited-policy-be-wary-of-those-proposing-such-legislation/).

### The 1993 Accord bought stability but did not bind later coalition politics

The 1993 Accord was signed by Alliance, Labour and National, accepted the main elements of the then reforms, introduced a Transitional Retirement Benefit for people most affected by the age rise, established the Retirement Commission, and required periodic retirement-income reports [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf); [Treasury/MSD framework paper, 2007](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf). The Accord provided several years of stability, but differences had emerged by 1996 over longer-term direction and the surcharge, New Zealand First was not a party to the Accord, and the 1996 coalition agreement triggered a 1997 compulsory retirement savings referendum and the surcharge's abolition [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf); [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf).

The 1997 compulsory scheme result shows that public legitimacy can overpower elite design: the Retirement Commission's history paper records that the compulsory retirement savings scheme was rejected by 91.8% of voters, while the RPRC/Jeff Todd paper reports the final referendum result as about 92% opposed and treats it as evidence that reforms without broad acceptance do not last under New Zealand's political system [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf); [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf).

### Current party positions are not aligned enough for a durable reform package

The current Government's Speech from the Throne says the NZ Super entitlement age will remain 65 and that the universal Winter Energy Payment will be maintained [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697). National's party page says it would keep the age at 65 until 2044 and then gradually lift it to 67, with no effect on anyone born before 1979, while ACT says it would gradually raise the age to 67 over eight years and allow compassionate early access at 65 for people with at least 20 years of physical work [National Party commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [ACT, 2026](https://www.act.org.nz/news/hipkins-buries-his-head-in-the). NZ First's seniors page says the retirement age will remain 65 with "No ifs, buts, or maybes", and says NZ First got rid of the surtax and supports maintaining the 66% net-average-wage link [NZ First seniors policy](https://www.nzfirst.nz/improving-the-lives-of-our-seniors).

Labour's 2026 position is less settled in the public sources found: an April 2026 Newstalk ZB/Apple Podcasts summary says Labour was calling for the Super age to remain 65, Hipkins did not back full means-testing, and he said any conversation would need to be constructive and bipartisan [Newstalk ZB via Apple Podcasts, 2026](https://podcasts.apple.com/nz/podcast/chris-hipkins-labour-leader-discusses-election-policy/id1768672662?i=1000764995450). The Green Party's current seniors policy page emphasises dignity, independence, ageing services, and that every senior should have enough money for daily living, health, and wellbeing needs, but I did not find a current Green Party page in this search that states a specific NZ Super age-rise or means-testing position [Green Party seniors policy](https://www.greens.org.nz/seniors_policy).

### Older people's organisations reinforce the trust argument

Grey Power states support for universal NZ Super from the age of entitlement, age 65 eligibility, maintaining annual adjustment settings, and resisting any attempt to make NZ Super means-tested [Grey Power](https://www.greypower.co.nz/maintaining-nz-super-benefits). KASPANZ argues that means testing is difficult to target, carries administrative costs, creates a "them and us" mentality, and weakens community trust and confidence [KASPANZ, 2025](https://kaspanz.com/means-testing-discredited-policy-be-wary-of-those-proposing-such-legislation/). Age Concern's public entitlements page is not a reform manifesto, but it directs older people to government support and benefits rather than arguing for an age rise or means test [Age Concern](https://ageconcern.org.nz/information-advice/entitlements-and-subsidies/).

The Retirement Commission's 2025 review records the same public-trust theme in more formal terms: it says NZ Super is a universal public pension available to eligible residents aged 65 and over, is not means-tested, and provides a universal safety net, and it reports that universality without means-testing was the most highly ranked feature in cited 2014 and 2022 survey work [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). The review also says submissions from Basic Income New Zealand, Grey Power, KASPANZ, and John Trezise opposed raising the age of eligibility or introducing means testing, arguing that such changes would undermine simplicity and effectiveness [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

### What would make an accord politically durable

The strongest evidence points to process conditions more than to one predetermined parameter change: the Retirement Commission's 2025 review recommends a new cross-party retirement-income accord, a cross-party Parliamentary working group for a 10-year roadmap, a pan-sector group led by the Commission, and a roadmap that covers KiwiSaver, NZ Super, and retirement-planning innovation [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). The same review says major decisions such as changing NZ Super's eligibility age or making KiwiSaver compulsory should not be made until the roadmap is completed and publicly consulted [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

The 1990s record suggests four practical durability tests for any future accord: include the parties that can veto or campaign hardest against the package, run public consultation alongside political talks, protect cohorts close to retirement with long lead times or transitional payments, and avoid presenting a technically coherent package as settled before public legitimacy is built [RPRC/Jeff Todd accord paper, 2008](https://cdn.auckland.ac.nz/assets/business/about/our-research/research-institutes-and-centres/RPRC/OtherPapers/Superannuation-task-forces-in-the-1990s-and-the-political-accord.pdf); [Retirement Commission history paper, 2008](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/Background-papers/History-and-trends/27b4c9b6d8/RI-Review-BP-Retirement-Income-History-2008.pdf). The distributional lens is not optional: the 2025 review warns that changing the age before improving support for older workers or addressing health inequities could harm people unable to work longer because of caregiving responsibilities or chronic illness [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current, representative 2026 poll or deliberative survey showing majority support for a specific means test or age path, especially among older voters and near-retirees, would weaken the conclusion that universality remains the dominant political constraint [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- A signed cross-party agreement among National, Labour, ACT, NZ First, the Greens, and other likely parliamentary parties on a specific age, means-test, or savings package would change the current-position section, which now shows visible divergence rather than accord [NZ Gazette, Speech from the Throne, 2023](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2023-vr5697); [National Party commitments to seniors](https://www.national.org.nz/policies/nationals-commitments-to-seniors); [ACT, 2026](https://www.act.org.nz/news/hipkins-buries-his-head-in-the); [NZ First seniors policy](https://www.nzfirst.nz/improving-the-lives-of-our-seniors).
- A distributional model showing that a particular reform package protects manual workers, carers, disabled people, renters, Maori, Pacific peoples, and near-retirees better than the status quo would strengthen the case that a reform is politically durable as well as technically feasible [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- I could not verify a current official Labour policy page for the 2026 election or a current Green Party NZ Super-specific age/means-testing policy page; the Labour entry relies on a public media/podcast summary, and the Green entry is limited to the party's seniors policy page [Newstalk ZB via Apple Podcasts, 2026](https://podcasts.apple.com/nz/podcast/chris-hipkins-labour-leader-discusses-election-policy/id1768672662?i=1000764995450); [Green Party seniors policy](https://www.greens.org.nz/seniors_policy).
- This finding did not include interviews with older people, Maori and Pacific organisations, disability advocates, carers, retirement-income officials, or party negotiators; those perspectives are needed before translating the process conditions into a concrete accord design [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## Open follow-up questions

- What would a concrete cross-party accord text look like if it preserved universality but altered tax treatment, abatement through the tax system, residence rules, indexation, or age over very long lead times?
- Which groups would be most exposed under each feasible reform option: manual workers, carers, renters, disabled people, Maori, Pacific peoples, migrants with shorter residence histories, and people with interrupted KiwiSaver histories?
- Would a citizens' assembly or deliberative polling process shift public preferences on age rises or targeting after participants see distributional and fiscal trade-offs?
