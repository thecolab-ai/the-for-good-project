# NZ residential flood cover is retreating through price and reduced availability, not yet through mass withdrawal — and Natural Hazards Cover does not backstop building flood damage

## Executive answer

- **The dominant, confirmed change is risk-based pricing, not withdrawal.** NZ insurers historically took a "partially community-based" approach to residential flood risk; they are now pricing flood at the individual-property level as modelling improves. By late 2023 Treasury's monitoring found "three out of seven" monitored insurers were "pricing to flood at an address level," and there is "no standard among them." [RBNZ FSR, May 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2); [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf)
- **How the retreat shows up today: higher premiums and thinner availability, concentrated in high-risk areas.** Home insurance premiums have grown at three times general CPI since 2011, with a ~40% rise in the two years to early 2026; online premiums rose ~24% in the ~18 months to April 2024. About 6% of properties are assessed at high flood risk; of the online quotes for high-risk homes, ~25% carried an additional flood premium of at least $250/yr (most under $1,000, but "up to $4,500" in the worst cases). [Cabinet paper ECO-26-SUB-0003, Feb 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf); [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf); [RNZ, 12 Jun 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/522988/treasury-data-shows-insurers-honing-in-on-flood-risk-properties)
- **Confirmed *full* withdrawal is still uncommon — but the system can flip fast.** RBNZ says full retreat is "uncommon so far," yet complete withdrawal "could occur relatively quickly given that contracts are typically annual." Availability is measurably reducing where high flood risk *and* high earthquake risk overlap; in a few Christchurch suburbs, no high-risk property in Treasury's sample could get more than one online quote. So today's picture is pricing pressure + patchy availability, not mass exclusion. [RBNZ FSR, May 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2); [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf)
- **Natural Hazards Cover (NHC) is not the flood backstop people assume.** For **storm or flood, NHC provides land cover only** — building flood damage is entirely private-insurer exposure. Worse, NHC only exists *if* you hold a qualifying private fire/home policy, so if a private insurer withdraws, the household loses NHC too (for the perils NHC does cover). NHC therefore cannot catch a household when private flood cover retreats. [Natural Hazards Commission — About NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/1/en/latest/)
- **The public policy response I could verify is an affordability review, not a named flood-premium backstop.** The insurance industry (ICNZ) opposes a UK-style "Flood Re" subsidised scheme, calling it the wrong signal, and the Government has *paused* a proposed NHC levy increase (16c→24c per $100 of building cover) while it runs a 2026 review of insurance affordability. I did not find an official source in this research that rules out a future subsidy or public backstop. [interest.co.nz, 24 Oct 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/insurance/130393/icnz-opposes-uk-style-flood-reinsurance-scheme-nz-saying-it-could-send-wrong); [Cabinet paper ECO-26-SUB-0003, Feb 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the pricing, availability, premium-inflation, and NHC-structure claims are backed by official RBNZ / Treasury / Natural Hazards Commission sources; the weaker spots are insurer-specific *excess* and *exclusion* changes (thin public data) and the timing of future full withdrawal (a projection, and the leading NZ retreat model is coastal-specific).

## Evidence

### 1. Risk-based flood pricing is confirmed and spreading (this is the main mechanism)

RBNZ's May 2024 Financial Stability Report special topic states insurers historically "have taken a partially community-based approach to flood risks for most residential properties," and are moving to risk-based pricing as flood modelling improves; it notes higher premiums for high-risk properties and competitive pressure on other insurers to follow so they don't inherit a disproportionate share of high-risk homes. [RBNZ FSR, May 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2) (fetched via r.jina.ai text proxy; the rbnz.govt.nz page returns a 403 bot-challenge to plain fetchers — tooling, not a dead link)

Treasury's monitoring (actuarial firm Finity, contracted from 2023 to track flood risk) found insurers diverge sharply in method: "Insurers differ in how they assess flood risk, with no standard among them. All insurers use a variety of sources and models to determine flood risk and pricing." Its November 2023 report found "three out of seven insurance companies included in the monitoring appeared to be pricing to flood at an address level." [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf); [RNZ, 12 Jun 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/522988/treasury-data-shows-insurers-honing-in-on-flood-risk-properties)

Individual insurers confirm the direction. Tower prices at property level — "Premiums are calculated based on the flood, earthquake, sea surge, and landslide risk Tower sees at that particular property, not someone else's" (CEO Paul Johnston) — and from 1 July 2026 its risk assessments are surfaced to home-buyers inside Westpac's banking app at the point of quote. [Insurance Business NZ, 2026](https://www.insurancebusinessmag.com/nz/news/property/westpac-nz-embeds-natural-hazard-risk-in-insurance-quotes-581079.aspx)

### 2. The observable effect: premium increases concentrated on risk

The Cabinet paper (Feb 2026) states: "Home insurance premiums have grown at three times the rate of general CPI since 2011, with a 40% rise in the last two years. Premiums have grown even faster for some people in high risk areas." It attributes part of this explicitly to pricing behaviour: moves "to greater risk-based-pricing for some risks (such as flooding) have also contributed to increased premiums for those subject to those risks." [Cabinet paper ECO-26-SUB-0003, Feb 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf)

Treasury's online-quote survey (3,510 profiles, seven insurer websites) found premiums up ~24% since October 2022, and quantified the flood loading: ~6% of properties are high flood risk, and of high-risk homes quoted, "around 25%… had flood risk premiums costing at least an extra $250 per year." The maximum additional flood premium observed was "up to $4,500," though "the vast majority of elevated flood premiums were less than $1,000." [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf); [RNZ, 12 Jun 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/522988/treasury-data-shows-insurers-honing-in-on-flood-risk-properties)

This repricing follows real losses that reset reinsurance and pricing: the 2023 Auckland Anniversary floods drove ~$1,978m of insured loss and Cyclone Gabrielle ~$1,835m — the two costliest weather events in ICNZ's catastrophe record. [ICNZ — Cost of natural disasters](https://www.icnz.org.nz/industry/cost-of-natural-disasters/) (figures fetched via r.jina.ai proxy; icnz.org.nz returns 403 to plain fetchers)

### 3. Availability is reducing at the margin — but not mass withdrawal

Both regulators describe *reducing* availability rather than collapse. Treasury: "availability is starting to reduce in areas that have both a high seismic and high flood risk." The Cabinet paper echoes it: "Insurance remains largely available, but access is becoming more difficult in areas facing both high earthquake and flood risk." [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf); [Cabinet paper ECO-26-SUB-0003, Feb 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf)

The availability picture is nuanced: for high-flood-risk properties, "92 percent could also get online quotes from at least two underwriters" (April 2024) — but in specific Christchurch suburbs (e.g. Avondale, Woolston) "no high-risk properties in the dataset were able to get more than one online quote." Treasury also cautions that a *declined online quote* does not mean insurance is unavailable — "insurance companies may still be willing to do a more tailored quote over the phone." So online availability understates true availability, and the binding constraint in the hardest areas is often the compounding of earthquake risk on top of flood, not flood alone. [Treasury Insurance Update, Oct 2024](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-10/t2023-2206-insurance-update.pdf); [RNZ, 12 Jun 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/522988/treasury-data-shows-insurers-honing-in-on-flood-risk-properties)

### 4. Confirmed *full* withdrawal vs. the forward risk (the distinction the issue asks for)

RBNZ is explicit that wholesale retreat has not yet happened but could arrive suddenly: "Full insurance retreat is uncommon so far, even for properties exposed to high seismic and flood risks," but "it could occur relatively quickly given that contracts are typically annual." Because a home policy renews yearly, an insurer can decline to renew a whole risk class within one cycle — there is no multi-year lock-in protecting the household. [RBNZ FSR, May 2024](https://www.rbnz.govt.nz/hub/publications/financial-stability-report/2024/may-2024/fsr-may-24-special-topic-2)

The most-cited NZ modelling of *future* withdrawal is Belinda Storey / Climate Sigma's Deep South Challenge "insurance retreat" work — but it is **coastal-inundation specific** (driven by sea-level rise), not pluvial/fluvial rainfall flooding, and it is a projection, so it should not be read as current-market fact. It finds insurers begin partial retreat around a 2% annual-exceedance-probability (AEP) flood frequency and that cover becomes "completely unavailable" by ~5% AEP, and that homes currently at 1% AEP coastal flooding "are likely to experience insurance retreat over the next 15 years"; in Wellington just ~10cm of sea-level rise raises flood probability roughly fivefold. [Deep South Challenge — Insurance retreat research](https://deepsouthchallenge.co.nz/insurance-retreat-and-climate-change-much-anticipated-research-released/); [Storey et al., *Climatic Change*, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10584-024-03699-1)

**Bottom line on the distinction:** *risk-based pricing* and *reducing availability* are confirmed and measurable now; *excess/exclusion* changes are under-documented publicly (see limits); *full withdrawal* of flood cover for whole areas is not yet widespread and is a forward risk, sharpest for coastal-inundation homes.

### 5. What Natural Hazards Cover does — and does not — do when private flood cover retreats

NHC is the public first layer, but for flood it is structurally thin and *contingent on private cover*:

- **Flood/storm building damage is not covered by NHC.** NHCover for buildings applies to "earthquake, landslide…, volcanic activity, hydrothermal activity, tsunami, storm or flood (land cover only)." The Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023 provides *residential land* cover for storm and flood, not direct residential-building flood damage — that sits with the private insurer. [Natural Hazards Commission — About NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [Natural Hazards Insurance Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/1/en/latest/)
- **NHC exists only if you hold a private fire/home policy:** "You have natural hazards cover if you have a home insurance policy that includes fire insurance." So if the private insurer withdraws, the household loses NHC access as well — NHC cannot function as a fallback when private cover retreats. [Natural Hazards Commission — About NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)
- **Even the land cover is capped and limited:** building cover (for the perils NHC does cover) is "generally $300,000 plus GST," with anything above provided privately; land cover is limited to land under the home, within 8m of it, and up to 60m of accessway, with sub-caps (e.g. $50,000+GST for all retaining walls, $25,000+GST for bridges/culverts). [Natural Hazards Commission — About NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)

So the public safety net for a flooded *home* is essentially the private insurer; NHC covers the land around it (partially) and only while a private policy is in force.

### 6. Policy response is a review, not a backstop

I found no official source in this research that says a flood-premium subsidy, Flood Re-style pool, or other public backstop is currently being built or ruled out. The evidence supports a narrower conclusion: ICNZ publicly opposes a UK-style Flood Re subsidised reinsurance pool, saying subsidy schemes "will send the wrong signals and encourage people to live in high-risk areas" (24 Oct 2024), having earlier called it a "grossly disproportionate response." [interest.co.nz, 24 Oct 2024](https://www.interest.co.nz/insurance/130393/icnz-opposes-uk-style-flood-reinsurance-scheme-nz-saying-it-could-send-wrong)

On the public side, Treasury recommended raising the NHC levy from 16c to 24c per $100 of building cover (lifting the maximum annual levy per dwelling from $554 to $828 incl. GST), because at 16c the scheme has only a 38% probability of sufficiency. But in early 2026 the Government **paused** that increase, citing affordability, and launched a "focused review of insurance affordability for households," pausing the NHI Act settings review until it is further advanced. [Cabinet paper ECO-26-SUB-0003, Feb 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- **Evidence of blanket flood *exclusions* or non-renewals at scale.** I found strong data on premium loadings and reducing online availability, but little public, insurer-specific data on flood-*excess* increases (e.g. higher/separate flood excesses) or explicit exclusion clauses. If insurers are already writing flood out via excess or exclusion rather than price, the "retreat is mainly price" framing would need revising. This likely needs the actual policy wordings / renewal notices of the major insurers (IAG, Tower, Suncorp/Vero) — a document review I could not complete here.
- **A named insurer withdrawing cover from a whole area.** RBNZ says this hasn't broadly happened; a confirmed area-wide non-renewal (post-Gabrielle Hawke's Bay, or a repeatedly flooded Auckland catchment) would move "full withdrawal" from forward-risk to fact. I did not find a confirmed example.
- **The 2026 affordability review's findings** could either confirm affordability is deteriorating faster than premiums suggest, or (as the Cabinet paper hedges) show pricing is "not… the most significant issue." Its mid-2026 output should be treated as the next update to this finding.
- **Coastal vs. pluvial confusion.** The headline retreat timeline is coastal/sea-level-rise modelling. If a decision leans on "insurance gone in 15 years," confirm whether the property's dominant hazard is coastal inundation (where the Storey model applies) or rainfall/river flooding (where I found no equivalent public timeline).
- **Could not verify:** insurer-by-insurer underwriting rules, actual excess schedules, current non-renewal rates, and household-level uptake/lapse behaviour — these sit in private insurer and RBNZ supervisory data I cannot access. A person with industry or regulatory access (or an OIA to Treasury/RBNZ for the Finity underlying data) would sharpen the excess/exclusion gap.

## Open follow-up questions

- Do the major NZ insurers' current policy wordings raise or separate **flood excesses**, or add flood **exclusions**, for high-risk homes — distinct from premium loading? (Needs policy-document review.)
- What is the **non-renewal / decline rate** for flood-exposed homes, and has any insurer withdrawn from a defined area post-2023? (Needs RBNZ supervisory or OIA data.)
- Is there an equivalent to the Storey coastal-retreat timeline for **rainfall/river flooding**, which is the peril behind the 2023 Auckland events?
