# Rural digital exclusion has real costs, but NZIER's headline benefit is best treated as an upper-bound model

## Executive answer

- NZIER's Chorus-commissioned 2022 report estimated annual incremental benefits from "unconstrained" rural connectivity of **$1.79 billion for rural households**, **$344 million in rural business time savings**, and **at least $189.5 million in rural business output**, or about **$16.5 billion over ten years**, but the report itself says the benefit streams are not directly additive and are modelled against the rural status quo rather than measured programme outcomes [NZIER, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).
- Independent public indicators support the direction of the cost story: rural households pay more for broadband, report lower satisfaction, and face a larger coverage/performance gap than urban users, with the Commerce Commission reporting in 2026 that rural households paid an average **$13.12 more per month**, or **18% extra**, and rural satisfaction trailed urban satisfaction across all measured service dimensions [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Health and education evidence shows digital exclusion is an access-risk multiplier rather than just an entertainment or convenience issue: the Rural Health Strategy says around **25% of rural general practices were not taking new enrolments in July 2022** and points to telehealth as a possible substitute when clinically appropriate, while Ministry of Education briefings show the Crown allocated tens of thousands of internet connections to learner households during COVID-era distance learning [Ministry of Health, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf), [Ministry of Education, 2022](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2024-03/14.-BN-1293687-Update-on-Key-Issues-relating-to-Rural-Education-003.pdf?VersionId=xiliWIc74xNouHKGL6j_SEBSm1dYaXIW).
- Farm and small-business evidence supports a productivity channel, but not a clean rural-dollar loss estimate: MPI says patchy connectivity remains a significant barrier to farmers adopting agricultural technology, and MBIE's 2024 business digital monitor says some businesses are falling behind on digital opportunities while lack of time, knowledge, affordable tools, and cybersecurity concerns remain barriers [MPI, 2022](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/news/media-releases/boosting-rural-connectivity-aims-to-deliver-sustainable-benefits-to-kiwi-farmers), [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses).
- The best current interpretation is that rural digital exclusion has material costs across households, farms, health, education, and businesses, but the public record does **not** yet prove NZIER's $16.5 billion as an observed cost; it is a scenario estimate that should be triangulated with service-quality, affordability, health-access, education-continuity, and firm-adoption indicators [NZIER, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf), [Flint Global, 2026](https://5437809.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/5437809/Connecting%20Rural%20New%20Zealand%20-%20Final.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — multiple official and sector sources support the channels of harm, but only NZIER publishes a national dollar estimate for rural household and business benefits, and a later Flint Global report commissioned by non-fibre network owners challenges its counterfactual.

## Evidence

### What NZIER actually estimated

NZIER's report was commissioned by Chorus to assess potential benefits from closing the urban-rural digital divide, and its executive summary says constrained capacity and reliability discourage digital-technology use even where some access exists [NZIER, 2022](https://www.nzier.org.nz/publications/rural-connectivity-economic-benefits-of-closing-the-rural-digital-divide), [NZIER, 2022 PDF](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf). NZIER estimated annual incremental benefits of $1.79 billion for rural households, $344 million in business time savings, and at least $189.5 million in rural business output from "connectivity with unconstrained capacity"; it also stated that the benefit estimates are not directly additive because they capture different factors [NZIER, 2022 PDF](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

That makes NZIER useful for identifying benefit channels, but not enough on its own to state a realised national loss: a March 2026 Flint Global report prepared for One New Zealand, Spark, 2degrees, Fortysouth, and Connexa argues that economic assessment needs a realistic dynamic baseline, full cost assessment, and separation between the benefits of connectivity generally and the incremental benefits of fibre specifically [Flint Global, 2026](https://5437809.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/5437809/Connecting%20Rural%20New%20Zealand%20-%20Final.pdf). The same Flint report says NZIER's estimates were applied across 280,000 rural households and 140,000 rural businesses, assume improvement to 100% availability and take-up, and should be considered an upper bound because many rural users already have some adequate non-fibre connectivity [Flint Global, 2026](https://5437809.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/5437809/Connecting%20Rural%20New%20Zealand%20-%20Final.pdf).

### Households: the strongest independent cost signal is price and quality, not total disconnection

The 2025 Commerce Commission telecommunications monitoring report says 95% of rural addresses were within at least one mobile network operator's 4G fixed-wireless coverage area as of June 2025, while rural 5G fixed-wireless coverage reached 16% of rural addresses and WISP coverage reached 71% of rural addresses [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report shows why coverage is not the same as equal service: average 4G mobile coverage reached 99.0% of population but only 54.6% of landmass in 2025, which matters for geographically dispersed rural work, travel, emergency, and farm use [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Affordability is a measurable household cost. The Commerce Commission reports that rural households paid more than urban households in every region in its price table, with a weighted average rural price of $85.77 per month versus $72.65 urban, a $13.12 monthly difference or 18% rural premium [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). Service experience also differs: the Commission says rural consumers rated broadband lower across all service dimensions, with pricing and customer service the weakest areas at 50% satisfaction [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The 2023 Census is a useful floor for household access but not a rural performance measure. Stats NZ reported that household internet access rose to 90.5% of households in 2023, while its DataInfo+ quality statement says access-to-telecommunication data is household-level, does not show whether each household member has equal access, and had no information for 8.4% of occupied-private-dwelling households [Stats NZ, 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/2023-census-population-dwelling-and-housing-highlights/), [Stats NZ DataInfo+, 2024](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/fbe47f92-4602-4279-858a-bbbb168e53ff). Stats NZ also says no access to the internet is used in the New Zealand Deprivation Index and can indicate ability to access services such as social and health care in an emergency, so household internet access has recognised social-risk meaning even when it does not quantify broadband speed, latency, price, or reliability [Stats NZ DataInfo+, 2024](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/fbe47f92-4602-4279-858a-bbbb168e53ff).

### Health access: telehealth can reduce travel pressure only if the patient can actually use it

The Ministry of Health's Rural Health Strategy says rural general-practice access can involve weeks-long waits or closed books, that about 25% of rural practices were not taking new enrolments as at July 2022, and that rural people could use telehealth where clinically appropriate or otherwise need to travel outside their community [Ministry of Health, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf). The strategy also reports that, among rural people enrolled with a general practice, 12% travelled more than 30 minutes to their enrolled practice, with higher travel-time regions including Bay of Plenty, Gisborne, West Coast, and Otago [Ministry of Health, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf).

The telehealth constraint is independently supported. The NZ Telehealth Resource Centre says rural internet connectivity can be an issue for telehealth services and that better connectivity is vital to realise telehealth's potential for equitable health-care access [NZ Telehealth Resource Centre, undated](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/telehealth-resources/technology/technology/internet-connectivity-in-rural-areas). A 2019 NZ Telehealth Leadership Group DHB stocktake found that 15 DHBs cited patients not having access to devices as a barrier and 12 cited patients not having fast internet as a barrier, and it warned that people who would benefit most from closer-to-home telehealth may be least equipped to access it [NZ Telehealth Leadership Group, 2020](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/assets/Uploads/Telehealth-Full-Report-2020-V7-Pages.pdf).

### Education: public records show internet access was treated as essential learning infrastructure

The Ministry of Education's 2022 rural-education briefing says the Ministry provided internet connectivity, digital resources, and other learning equipment in response to COVID-19, and that more than 40,000 internet connections had been allocated to households to support distance learning during school closures or isolation [Ministry of Education, 2022](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2024-03/14.-BN-1293687-Update-on-Key-Issues-relating-to-Rural-Education-003.pdf?VersionId=xiliWIc74xNouHKGL6j_SEBSm1dYaXIW). A separate Ministry briefing says the emergency response contracted internet service providers to connect up to 50,000 learner households for six months, had allocated about 40,000 connections before the August 2021 Alert Level 4 move, and Cabinet approved funding for up to 7,000 new connections at $2 million in August 2021 [Ministry of Education, 2021](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-08/06.-ER-Keeping-households-connected-into-the-2022-school-year-127209.._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=lZsMyfJRRhorLL3A0JQspk4gTzX3Vh0w).

Those education records do not isolate rural households or prove long-run learning loss from rural broadband constraints, but they do show the Crown treated home connectivity as necessary for remote learning continuity [Ministry of Education, 2021](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-08/06.-ER-Keeping-households-connected-into-the-2022-school-year-127209.._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=lZsMyfJRRhorLL3A0JQspk4gTzX3Vh0w). Current Ministry guidance also allows schools and kura to share ultrafast broadband fibre with local communities through digital hubs and names Zero Data as a way to access some government and school websites without mobile data, which is a practical signal that affordability and local access still matter for education-adjacent digital inclusion [Ministry of Education, 2024](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/digital-technology/home-internet-access-students).

### Farms and primary industries: connectivity is a barrier to adopting farm technology, but the public NZ dollar estimate is thin

MPI's Sustainable Food and Fibre Futures announcement for rural LoRaWAN co-investment states that patchy network connection remains a significant barrier to many farmers adopting agricultural technology solutions, and says improving remote rural connectivity would help lift productivity and support sustainability tools for farmers and growers [MPI, 2022](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/news/media-releases/boosting-rural-connectivity-aims-to-deliver-sustainable-benefits-to-kiwi-farmers). AgriTechNZ's 2022 baseline report says digital agriculture has economic, environmental, and social potential for New Zealand's primary industries, but its published summary emphasises adoption motivations and barriers rather than giving a public national loss estimate for poor connectivity [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf).

The OECD's 2022 New Zealand digitalisation working paper gives an independent productivity context: it says digital technologies can help reduce New Zealand's "tyranny of distance", reports that less than 10% of more than 4,000 respondents in the 2017 Survey of Rural Decision Makers used precision agriculture and only 3% indicated uptake of automation or robotics, and cites joint OECD-MBIE work finding that ultra-fast broadband adoption increases the chance that New Zealand firms start exporting, especially where firms make effective use of digital tools [OECD, 2022](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2022/04/boosting-productivity-in-new-zealand-by-unleashing-digitalisation_82b58366/05cc1b5c-en.pdf). This supports NZIER's productivity mechanism, but it does not independently validate NZIER's rural-business dollar totals [NZIER, 2022 PDF](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

### Small businesses: digital capability data supports an opportunity cost, not a rural-specific observed loss

MBIE's 2024 business digital monitor says overall digital-tool usage and confidence were broadly stable but an emerging digital divide meant some businesses were seizing digital opportunities while others were falling behind, with cybersecurity, lack of time and knowledge, affordable tool choice, and digital marketing needs featuring as barriers [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses). MBIE's 2023 monitor found that 52% of businesses believed they would benefit from being more digital and 48% were not as digitally enabled as they could be because of cost and value-for-money concerns [MBIE, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses).

Digital Boost evaluation results show plausible business benefits from digital support: MBIE reported that Wave 3 participants included 17% reporting increased revenue, 26% reporting increased business resilience, and 23% reporting less time spent on existing business tasks [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-boost-evaluation-research). These are programme-participant outcomes rather than rural-connectivity effects, so they support the direction of NZIER's time-saving and revenue/productivity channels without proving the rural size of those channels [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-boost-evaluation-research), [NZIER, 2022 PDF](https://www.nzier.org.nz/hubfs/Public%20Publications/Client%20reports/Rural%20connectivity%20FINAL.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A transparent official cost-benefit analysis using address-level service availability, measured speeds, congestion, data caps, prices, resilience, and actual adoption would change the conclusion most, because it could separate "first adequate connection" benefits from "upgrade to fibre or higher capacity" benefits [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Flint Global, 2026](https://5437809.fs1.hubspotusercontent-na1.net/hubfs/5437809/Connecting%20Rural%20New%20Zealand%20-%20Final.pdf).
- Rural-specific observed outcomes would raise confidence: telehealth completion/failed-consult data by rurality, school remote-learning participation by household connectivity, farm-tech adoption by connection quality, and small-business revenue or time-savings data linked to connectivity quality would test whether NZIER's benefit channels are the right size [Ministry of Health, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2023-07/rural-health-strategy-oct23-v2.pdf), [Ministry of Education, 2021](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-08/06.-ER-Keeping-households-connected-into-the-2022-school-year-127209.._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=lZsMyfJRRhorLL3A0JQspk4gTzX3Vh0w), [MPI, 2022](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/news/media-releases/boosting-rural-connectivity-aims-to-deliver-sustainable-benefits-to-kiwi-farmers), [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses).
- I could not verify a current official NZ estimate of the total annual rural digital-exclusion cost across households, farms, health, education, and SMEs, nor a public dataset that links rural address-level broadband quality to observed health, education, farm, or business outcomes.
- A human rural-sector check is still needed because public data does not show how households trade off price, data caps, latency, travel, device-sharing, and backup connectivity in day-to-day decisions.

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues were opened; this issue was broad but answerable as a triangulation finding.
- Which rural health services have telehealth failure, no-show, or phone-only substitution data that can be linked to local broadband quality?
- Can the Commerce Commission's connectivity map outputs be combined with school, farm, or small-business geographies without exposing personal or commercially sensitive data?
- Are rural premiums larger once total household connectivity is counted, including backup mobile data, satellite hardware, landline retention, and extra data packs?
