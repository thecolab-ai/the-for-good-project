# NZ aged residential care faces severe, quantifiable staffing constraints that link to delayed admissions, bed closures and provider viability — but the causal chain to resident harm remains under-evidenced

## Executive answer

- **Registered nurses are the binding constraint.** The NZ Aged Care Association's 2023 member survey found a 15% RN vacancy rate, 83% of facilities not fully staffed with RNs (up from 59% in 2021), and 33% annual RN turnover. NZACA reports over 1,200 ARC beds closed in the year prior due to RN shortages [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf); [NZACA, pay parity](https://nzaca.org.nz/pay-parity-for-aged-care-nurses-promised-but-not-delivered/).
- **Pay disparity with public hospitals drives the exodus.** ARC RNs are paid up to $20,000 less than Te Whatu Ora hospital nurses; the 2023 hospital pay equity settlement widened the gap to ~10%. In 2023, 36% of RNs/ENs who left ARC went to a Te Whatu Ora hospital, versus 11% to another ARC provider [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).
- **The sector depends heavily on internationally qualified nurses.** 24% of ARC RNs were on visas in 2023 (down from 39% in 2019), drawn mainly from the Philippines and India [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). Nationally, 46.6% of all nurses with practising certificates were internationally qualified as of March 2025, up from 29% in 2019 — giving NZ the second-highest foreign-trained share in the OECD and a "self-sufficiency indicator" near 50% versus an OECD average of 91% [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf).
- **Staffing shortages demonstrably delay admissions and close beds.** Sapere's 2024 review for Te Whatu Ora found high-priority dementia/psychogeriatric residents waited on average nearly six months for ARC admission after assessment, ranging from 92 days (MidCentral) to 219 days (West Coast) [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf); [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review).
- **The link from staffing to audit findings and HDC complaints is directionally supported but not yet causally proven.** The Ngā Paerewa evaluation identified workforce shortage as a common struggle across all stakeholder groups in implementing care standards [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf), and the Aged Care Commissioner says workforce sustainability needs prioritising [Aged Care Commissioner, 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/). HDC received 154 complaints about ARC facilities in 2024/25 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf) — but no published study statistically ties staffing levels to complaint rates or audit non-conformance in NZ ARC.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The existence, magnitude and mechanisms of staffing constraints are strongly evidenced by multiple independent sources (Infometrics/NZNO, Treasury, Nursing Council, NZACA, Sapere). The causal pathway from staffing shortage → delayed admission/bed closure is well evidenced. The pathway from staffing → audit findings/HDC complaints/resident harm is asserted by authoritative bodies but rests on directional and anecdotal evidence, not yet on a published NZ statistical linkage study.

## Evidence

### The workforce: size, mix and what each role does

ARC combines health services with accommodation and daily living support across four care levels — rest home, dementia, continuing/hospital, and psychogeriatric — each with different funding, certification and staffing requirements [Treasury, ARC case study for Productivity Commission, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). Registered nurses (RNs) and caregivers/Kaiāwhina are the critical workforces, operating in different labour markets: RNs are tertiary-qualified, regulated practitioners who carry professional and contractual accountability for ensuring staffing meets resident needs; caregivers/Kaiāwhina are the unregulated workforce performing personal-care tasks, with no training required for entry though providers must give induction training and close supervision [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). Enrolled nurses (low numbers) can perform specific but not all nursing tasks [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf).

A 2022 Treasury case study estimated the ARC workforce at ~36,000 people: 72% caregivers/Kaiāwhina and enrolled nurses, 19% registered nurses, 5% activity coordinators, and the remainder non-care staff (chefs, cleaners, non-clinical managers) [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). The 2023 Infometrics/NZNO estimate, reconciling Nursing Council data with the NZACA survey, puts the direct-care workforce at ~20,347 FTEs: 19% RNs (3,894 FTE), 2% ENs (335 FTE), 79% caregivers/Kaiāwhina (16,118 FTE) [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

Allied-health and manager roles are less separately quantified. The Treasury case study notes clinical managers — the most senior RNs — carry regulatory and contractual accountability, and that only 17% of clinical managers and 5% of RN facility managers were on visas, far lower than the RN/caregiver workforce [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). The Sapere review and NZACA submissions describe managers working extended hours and sleeping over to cover RN gaps, but no public dataset gives an allied-health (physiotherapy, occupational therapy, diversional therapy) vacancy rate for ARC [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). This is an identified gap.

### Registered nurse constraints — the binding constraint

RNs are the hardest roles to fill and the ones whose shortage most directly limits facility operation. The NZACA's 2023 member survey found:

- **15% RN vacancy rate** (vs 6% for ENs, 4% for caregivers/Kaiāwhina), equating to ~687 vacant RN FTEs [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).
- **83% of facilities not fully staffed with RNs** in 2023, up from 59% in 2021 — applied to the 679 certified rest-home facilities, ~564 facilities were RN-understaffed [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).
- **33% annual RN turnover** in 2023 (down from 48% in 2021), meaning facilities lose a third of their RNs each year [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

The Treasury case study recorded a longer-term pattern: sector turnover between 2005 and 2019 ranged from 21–30%, with RN turnover at 33% and caregiver turnover at 23% in December 2019, and a December 2019 survey showing 7.5% of RN and 6.2% of clinical manager positions vacant versus 3.2% for caregivers [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). Of RNs ceasing ARC employment in an October 2018 survey, only 22% stayed in aged care; 49% moved to a DHB [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf).

A 2022 NZNO gerontology nursing leader reported ~900 ARC beds closed nationwide in the first half of 2022 due to staff shortages, tracked through section 31 notices filed to HealthCERT (the certifier under the Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001) — notices rising from ~200–300 in 2020 to 841 in 2021 and over 800 in the first five months of 2022 [Kaitiaki Nursing NZ, 2022](https://kaitiaki.org.nz/article/nurse-leader-pleads-for-funding-as-staffing-shortage-forces-closure-of-900-aged-care-beds/). NZACA subsequently reported over 1,200 beds closed in the year before the Infometrics report [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

### Pay disparity as the primary mechanism

The leading cause of ARC nurse shortages and bed closures, per NZACA, is that ARC nurses are paid up to $20,000 less than Te Whatu Ora hospital nurses, with public hospitals also offering better terms and conditions [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The 2023 Te Whatu Ora pay equity settlement widened the ARC-to-hospital pay difference to around 10%, exacerbating long-standing workforce issues despite a $240m injection (2022–2024) that had narrowed the gap [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

The outflow is visible: 36% of RNs and ENs who left ARC jobs in 2023 went to a Te Whatu Ora hospital, versus 11% to another ARC provider, 16% moved location in NZ, and 15% left the paid workforce [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). Exit rates for ARC nurses rose from 16% in 2015 to 37% in 2023 — higher than primary care (32%), mental health community (27%), and the all-nurse average (8.6%) [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The Treasury case study had earlier noted a median ARC RN salary of $58,240 in December 2017, near the bottom of the DHB range ($52,460–$70,820), and sector claims of a ~$10,000 average shortfall by December 2020 [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). Sapere estimated it would cost ~$89m/year to close the ARC–hospital pay gap [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

### Caregiver/Kaiāwhina and enrolled nurse constraints

Caregivers/Kaiāwhina are easier to recruit from a wider labour pool but still face turnover. The 2023 NZACA survey showed a 4% vacancy rate (vs 15% for RNs) but a 31% turnover rate — similar to RNs — suggesting vacant positions are filled quickly but churn is high [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The 2017 pay-equity settlement introduced a legislated minimum pay scale ($19–$27/hour) and a training-career framework; providers reported no significant increase in NZ applicants as a result, but an increase in migrant applicants, and the beneficial wage gap narrowed as minimum wage rose to $20 (2021) and $21.20 (2022) [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf).

EN numbers in ARC are small (489 headcount / 335 FTE in 2023), with a 6% vacancy rate and a 17% turnover rate last measured in 2019 [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). ACANZ commented that a Nursing Council consultation on EN scope of practice could give ENs more independence, enabling them to perform some tasks currently done by RNs — a potential partial mitigation [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

### Allied-health and manager constraints

Allied-health and manager constraints are qualitatively described but not separately quantified in the public datasets found. The Treasury case study notes that dual-use beds (rest-home or hospital) require an RN workforce flexible across units, and that larger groups introduce clinical and HR overhead roles covering multiple facilities [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). The Kaitiaki article reports clinical and facility managers working extended hours, sleeping over, and covering night shifts to fill RN gaps, with level-4 caregivers (medication-qualified) providing physical care [Kaitiaki Nursing NZ, 2022](https://kaitiaki.org.nz/article/nurse-leader-pleads-for-funding-as-staffing-shortage-forces-closure-of-900-aged-care-beds/). No public source found gives an ARC-specific vacancy or turnover rate for allied-health roles (physiotherapy, OT, diversional therapy) or for facility managers. This is an explicit gap.

### Dependence on internationally qualified workers

The ARC sector is a significant employer of migrants. The Treasury case study estimated that by 2019 ~40% of RNs and ~40% of caregivers/Kaiāwhina in ARC were on visas, with the temporary visa workforce doubling between 2012 and 2019 [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf). Regional reliance was uneven: the share of RNs on visas by DHB region ranged from 11% (Northland) to 68% (Auckland DHB), exceeding 50% in Hawke's Bay and Capital & Coast [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf).

By 2023 the NZACA survey found 24% of ARC RNs were on visas (down from 39% in 2019), with 36% on Skilled Migrant Category Residence Visas and 64% on other visas; 26% of visa-holding RNs originated from the Philippines and 28% from India [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The drop from 39% to 24% coincided with COVID border restrictions; the more recent reopening is visible in national Nursing Council data showing IQN applications peaking at ~7,000 per quarter in late 2023 [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf).

At the whole-of-nursing level, 46.6% of nurses with annual practising certificates were internationally qualified as of 31 March 2025, up from 29% in 2019 — the second-highest foreign-trained share in the OECD (behind Ireland at ~47%), and well above Australia (18.1%), the UK (17.9%) and the OECD average (8.7%) [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf). NZ's "self-sufficiency indicator" (domestically educated share) has dropped to ~50% versus an OECD average of 91%; only Ireland is lower (53%) [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf). India has become the dominant source country since borders reopened, followed by the Philippines, with many having worked in the UK or Ireland before NZ [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf).

A Health NZ briefing released by the Ombudsman in late 2024 said the sector depended on internationally qualified nurses and that a 2022–23 campaign supported 551 overseas-trained RNs into ARC by the end of November 2023 — as quoted in the stream's reviewed framing doc [Aged care framing, analysis/nz-aged-care-quality-and-funding, 2026](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/analysis/nz-aged-care-quality-and-funding-rest-homes-reside-framing.md). The original Ombudsman PDF (OIA-202400209) is no longer reachable at its cited URL (returns 404 as of 2026-07-05); the framing doc's quotation is the verified secondary route.

A recognised risk is that NZ is a "stepping stone" — some IQNs use Trans-Tasman mutual recognition to move on to Australia after gaining NZ registration, undermining workforce planning [Nursing Council of NZ, 2025](https://www.nursingcouncil.org.nz/common/Uploaded%20files/Public/Publications/other_reports/2025-08-international-nurse-migration-nz-report.pdf). However, NZACA survey tenure data does not show IQNs leaving ARC faster than NZ-qualified nurses: IQN RNs averaged 2.3 years at the respondent facility, similar to NZQN RNs with 1–4 years' experience (2 years) [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

### Link to delayed admissions

The most direct, quantified link from staffing shortage to access harm is admission delay. Sapere's 2024 review for Te Whatu Ora found that high-needs dementia and psychogeriatric care residents waited, on average, nearly six months to be admitted to an ARC facility after being assessed as high priority for moving out of a home setting. Waiting times ranged from 92 days in the MidCentral region to 219 days in the West Coast region [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf); [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review). Sapere's Advisory Group heard claims that due to staffing shortages, healthcare assistants often operate outside their training, doing the work of RNs [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

### Link to audit findings / standards implementation

The Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation (Malatest International, 2024) found that "a common struggle with a shortage in the health workforce was identified as a key factor in how well and easily providers were able to action Ngā Paerewa" across all stakeholder groups — large or small, public or private [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf). ARC facilities specifically struggled with new restraint criteria (Section six) [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf). The evaluation also noted that some providers faced barriers including "limited capacity" and that smaller providers with limited resources struggled to implement the standards effectively [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

This is directional evidence that workforce shortage impedes standards implementation, but the evaluation does not present a statistical model linking specific staffing levels to audit non-conformance rates or certification outcomes.

### Link to HDC complaints

HDC received 154 complaints about aged residential care facilities in 2024/25, down from 183–185 in the preceding three years [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). Around 18% of all HDC complaints are about the care of older people [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). Common primary issues in complaints about older people's care include inadequate/inappropriate treatment (13%), delay in treatment (9%), and missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (9%) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). The Aged Care Commissioner makes recommendations on individual complaints — e.g. staff education on communication, dementia care, and clinical practice standards [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The Kaitiaki article reports that sector nurses anecdotally observed more complaints to HDC relating to provision of care and sentinel events, attributing this to staffing problems, but states explicitly: "there is no analytical data yet to support that" [Kaitiaki Nursing NZ, 2022](https://kaitiaki.org.nz/article/nurse-leader-pleads-for-funding-as-staffing-shortage-forces-closure-of-900-aged-care-beds/). No published NZ study was found that statistically links ARC staffing levels to HDC complaint rates.

### Link to provider viability

Sapere's 2024 review found clear evidence the sector was underfunded, with providers building smaller care centres, facilities closing, and a shift to providers taking more revenue from residents through "premium beds" and occupational rights agreements [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). A substantial increase in the price paid by Te Whatu Ora for ARC services is needed to incentivise large-scale new investment; lack of national standardisation has created significant regional funding-rate differences [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The Treasury case study earlier noted sector occupancy below 90% in 2020 and the trend away from single owner-managed rest homes toward larger groups (Ryman, Oceania, BUPA), with EY analysis finding it difficult to earn an adequate return from either small or very large facilities [Treasury, ARC case study, 2022](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-is-case-study-aged-residential-care.pdf).

On 17 June 2026, Ministers announced a 4% 2026/27 funding uplift worth ~$79m for ARC providers, including a 2.66% base increase and targeted investment — current evidence of government response, but not by itself a verdict on long-run viability [Beehive, 2026 funding uplift](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector) (page bot-blocked on fetch; cited via the stream's reviewed framing doc).

### How much staffing would it take?

Applying Australia's mandated minimum ARC care minutes (200 minutes/resident/day including 40 RN minutes, rising to 215/44 from October 2024) to NZ, Infometrics estimated NZ ARC would need an additional 8,232 carer FTEs (38% increase) including 1,550 RN FTEs (34% increase) to meet minimum safe staffing, or 14,224 additional carer FTEs (65% increase) including 6,288 RN FTEs (137% increase) for optimal staffing — on top of the ~1,380 existing funded vacancies [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). Even in Australia, 42% of services did not meet the total care-minutes mandate and 59% did not meet both total and RN minutes in the June quarter 2024 [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published NZ statistical study linking ARC staffing levels (RN hours/resident/day, vacancy rates) to audit non-conformance, HDC complaint rates, or resident outcomes (falls, pressure injuries, medication errors, mortality)** would move the staffing→quality/harm link from "directional" to "proven." This is the single biggest evidence gap. Australia's mandated-care-minutes regime exists precisely because that linkage has been made internationally; NZ has not yet done the equivalent domestic study.
- **Current 2026/27 funding-flow and pay-parity data** showing the ARC–hospital pay gap has materially narrowed (e.g. a dedicated ARC RN pay equity settlement) would weaken the "pay disparity drives exodus" mechanism. The June 2026 $79m uplift is a down payment, not yet evidence of structural closure of the gap.
- **A reversal of the IQN share** (e.g. domestic graduate growth outpacing IQN inflow, or a measured drop in the foreign-trained percentage) would change the self-sufficiency picture. As of March 2025 the trend is still toward higher IQN reliance.
- **An official ARC-specific allied-health and manager workforce dataset** (vacancy, turnover, visa status by role) would fill the identified gap where this finding can only describe those constraints qualitatively.
- **A recovered or relocated copy of the OIA-202400209 Health NZ briefing** (the original Ombudsman URL now 404s) would let the 551-IQN figure be cited to a primary source rather than to the stream's framing doc. A researcher with OIA access could re-request it from Health NZ or the Ombudsman.

What I could not verify:
- The exact current location of the Health NZ OIA briefing (OIA-202400209) — the Ombudsman PDF returns 404 and was not in the Wayback Machine. The 551-IQN figure and the "sector depends on internationally qualified nurses" quote are taken from the stream's reviewed framing doc, which itself cites the dead URL.
- The Sapere review's full text was bot-blocked on direct fetch; the admission-wait figures (92–219 days, ~6 months) are verified via Infometrics' detailed quotation of Sapere, not from the primary PDF.
- No public NZ dataset was found giving ARC-specific allied-health (physio/OT/diversional therapy) vacancy or turnover rates, or a separate manager vacancy rate.
- The causal link from staffing shortage to HDC complaint rates or to sentinel events is acknowledged by sector leaders as anecdotal and unproven by analytical data [Kaitiaki Nursing NZ, 2022](https://kaitiaki.org.nz/article/nurse-leader-pleads-for-funding-as-staffing-shortage-forces-closure-of-900-aged-care-beds/). A human with ARC clinical or audit authority is needed to assess whether the directional evidence understates or overstates the real harm pathway.

## Open follow-up questions

- **research: Does a statistical link exist between ARC RN staffing levels and audit non-conformance, HDC complaints, or resident outcomes in NZ?** This is the core unproven causal claim. Requires linking HealthCERT audit data, HDC complaint data, and provider-level staffing/vacancy data with denominators and resident-acuity adjustment. May need an OIA or a researcher partnership with Health NZ/HDC.
- **research: What is the ARC-specific allied-health and manager workforce profile (vacancy, turnover, visa status, qualifications)?** No public dataset found. Likely requires an NZACA/CANZ member survey or a Health NZ workforce census.
- **research: How does NZ's ARC staffing compare to Australia's mandated care-minutes regime in practice, and what would a NZ care-minutes standard cost and deliver?** Infometrics has done the top-line NZ application; a detailed cost-benefit and implementation study (including rural/small-facility feasibility, dementia acuity, and the EN scope-of-practice mitigation) is the natural next step.
- **research: What is the scale and impact of the "stepping stone" IQN outflow from NZ ARC to Australia?** Nursing Council data shows NZ is a stepping stone; quantifying the ARC-specific loss rate and its effect on workforce planning would test whether IQN dependence is a stable strategy or a leaking bucket.
