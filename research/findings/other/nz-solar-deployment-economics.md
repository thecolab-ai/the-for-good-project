# NZ solar deployment is accelerating, but household benefits depend on ownership, daytime use, and export rules

## Executive answer

- Distributed solar is still a small share of NZ electricity, but its installed base is now material: Electricity Authority EMI registry data shows solar-all distributed generation rising from 2,236 ICPs / 8.63 MW at 31 December 2013 to 81,452 ICPs / 875.9 MW at 31 December 2025, and to 86,919 ICPs / 986.9 MW at 31 May 2026 [Electricity Authority EMI, installed distributed generation trends CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?FuelType=solar_all&Show=Capacity&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4).
- Utility-scale solar has moved from "proposed" into "visible in generation": the Electricity Authority says 247 MW of grid-connected solar has been installed since 2023, and a further 783 MW of grid-connected solar coming to market in 2026/27 would take grid-connected solar capacity to 1,031 MW [Electricity Authority, grid-connected solar](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/grid-connected-solar-is-having-its-time-in-the-sun/).
- For a normal owner-occupier with suitable roof space and daytime load, household solar can stack up: EECA's 2026 public guidance gives typical installed costs of $8,500 for 3 kW, $11,500 for 5 kW, and $20,000 for 10 kW systems, and says 7-10 year payback is possible when the household can use most of the solar electricity it generates [EECA, solar costs and savings](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/).
- The economics are much weaker for households that export most generation at low buy-back rates, cannot shift use into daylight hours, rent their home, lack upfront capital or mortgage-linked green finance, or have an unsuitable roof; EECA says value improves with morning/daytime electricity use and direct self-consumption, while the Electricity Authority says buy-back rates vary by retailer and 2026 rule changes are intended to reward exports at times the grid needs them [EECA, residential solar value](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/eeca-insights/understanding-the-value-of-residential-solar-pv-and-storage-in-new-zealand/), [Powerswitch, solar buy-back rates](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates), [Electricity Authority, new ways to empower consumers](https://www.ea.govt.nz/projects/all/energy-competition-task-force/consultation/new-ways-to-power-electricity-consumers/).
- Equity is the weak point: around one-third of NZ households do not own their dwelling, and MBIE says renters were three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy hardship in the year ended June 2024, so unsubsidised rooftop solar mainly reaches households with property control, credit access and lower hardship risk unless community/shared or public-housing models are deliberately expanded [Stats NZ, home ownership 2023](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/home-ownership-increases-and-housing-quality-improves/), [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the deployment, cost and rule-change evidence is strong and current, but a complete consented-solar-farm register and household payback distribution would require more granular project, retailer, roof, tariff and load-profile data than I verified for this finding.

## Evidence

### Uptake: distributed solar is growing quickly from a low base

The Electricity Authority's EMI installed distributed generation report says it derives installed DG counts and capacity from registry fields populated by distributors, including fuel type and generation capacity, and it covers ICPs with load-and-generation or generation-only installation types from September 2013 onward [Electricity Authority EMI, installed distributed generation trends](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/retail/Reports/guehmt). The same report notes a data caveat: "Solar+Batteries" and "Wind+Batteries" categories were added in November 2023, and prior combinations may have been classified inconsistently as Solar or Other, so the solar series is best treated as a registry trend rather than a perfect engineering census [Electricity Authority EMI, report notes](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/retail/Reports/guehmt).

Using the EMI CSV filtered to `FuelType=solar_all`, the end-of-year solar-all distributed generation series is: 2,236 ICPs / 8.63 MW in 2013; 5,506 / 22.10 MW in 2014; 9,512 / 37.46 MW in 2015; 13,786 / 53.29 MW in 2016; 18,283 / 71.03 MW in 2017; 22,722 / 92.02 MW in 2018; 26,606 / 116.01 MW in 2019; 30,878 / 147.07 MW in 2020; 37,154 / 193.97 MW in 2021; 46,376 / 264.61 MW in 2022; 56,803 / 378.71 MW in 2023; 69,027 / 564.72 MW in 2024; and 81,452 / 875.86 MW in 2025 [Electricity Authority EMI, solar-all CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?FuelType=solar_all&Show=Capacity&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4). The latest available month in that CSV when fetched was 31 May 2026, at 86,919 solar-all ICPs, 3.67577% uptake and 986.896 MW installed distributed solar capacity [Electricity Authority EMI, solar-all CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?FuelType=solar_all&Show=Capacity&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4).

The same EMI source can be split by market segment, but the report warns market segments are not mutually exclusive, so these are descriptive slices rather than additive totals [Electricity Authority EMI, report notes](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/retail/Reports/guehmt). At 31 May 2026, residential solar-all DG was 79,252 ICPs and 445.206 MW, SME was 5,120 ICPs and 114.002 MW, commercial was 3,371 ICPs and 170.342 MW, and industrial was 4,296 ICPs and 371.347 MW [Electricity Authority EMI, residential CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?MarketSegment=Res&FuelType=solar_all&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4), [Electricity Authority EMI, SME CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?MarketSegment=SME&FuelType=solar_all&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4), [Electricity Authority EMI, commercial CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?MarketSegment=Com&FuelType=solar_all&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4), [Electricity Authority EMI, industrial CSV](https://www.emi.ea.govt.nz/Retail/Download/DataReport/CSV/GUEHMT?MarketSegment=Ind&FuelType=solar_all&_rsdr=ALL&_si=v%7C4).

### Utility-scale solar: commissioned capacity and the consent pipeline are now real

MBIE's Energy in New Zealand 2025 describes solar as a relatively new electricity source and distinguishes distributed solar on homes/businesses from utility-scale solar farms feeding the national grid [MBIE, Electricity chapter](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/energy-in-new-zealand/energy-in-new-zealand-2025/electricity). MBIE's renewables chapter lists new solar plant capacity commissioned from November 2023 to December 2024, including Rangitaiki Solar at 32 MWp, Kohira Solar at 33 MWp and Ruawai Solar at 16.7 MWdc [MBIE, Renewables chapter](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/energy-in-new-zealand/energy-in-new-zealand-2025/renewables).

By February 2026, the Electricity Authority said 247 MW of grid-connected solar had been installed in New Zealand since 2023, that grid-connected solar contributed between 1% and 2% of total supply from mid-October 2025 to early February 2026, and that half-hourly solar generation reached 210 MW on 30 January 2026 [Electricity Authority, grid-connected solar](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/grid-connected-solar-is-having-its-time-in-the-sun/). MBIE's March 2026 Energy Quarterly summary provides independent generation evidence for the same direction, reporting solar generation of 373 GWh in the March 2026 quarter, up 50.2% as new capacity came online [MBIE, March 2026 Energy Quarterly summary](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/new-zealand-energy-quarterly/march-2026-summary).

The large consent pipeline includes Lake Benmore/Mackenzie examples under fast-track. The Point Solar Farm page says the project would construct and operate an approximately 670-hectare solar farm on the northern shore of Lake Benmore and that Far North Solar Farm's substantive application was deemed complete on 16 October 2025 [Fast-track, The Point Solar Farm](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/the-point-solar-farm). The Point's application economic assessment describes the proposal as a 450 MWp photovoltaic solar farm on a 678 ha site near Lake Benmore, with consent sought for 35 years [Fast-track, The Point economic assessment](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0009/23013/20260325-The-Point-Solar-Farm-EIA-Final-redacted_Redacted.pdf). Haldon Solar is a separate fast-track project across approximately 320 hectares adjacent to Lake Benmore, with the substantive application by Lodestone Energy deemed complete on 22 September 2025 and a draft decision released by the expert panel on 26 June 2026 [Fast-track, Haldon Solar](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/haldon-solar).

### Household economics: self-consumption, not exports, is the core value

EECA's 2026 solar cost guidance says typical installed residential costs are $8,500 for a 3 kW system, $11,500 for a 5 kW system and $20,000 for a 10 kW system, including standard installation costs and GST [EECA, solar costs and savings](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/). EECA says adding a battery typically costs another $5,000-$15,000 and that solar without a battery will generally give the best returns, while batteries can add value but usually lengthen payback because of their upfront cost [EECA, solar costs and savings](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/).

EECA's rule-of-thumb payback is conditional, not universal: it says a 7-10 year payback is possible if the household can use most of the electricity generated from solar, has average-to-high daytime use, and has electric heating and hot water [EECA, solar costs and savings](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/). EECA's more detailed 2025 modelling found rooftop solar PV is likely to be financially viable for a significant proportion of households, particularly high-energy households, and reported modelled no-battery 5 kW north-facing IRRs of about 7-14% in Queenstown, 6-12% in Auckland and 6-11% in Christchurch, with Wellington lower but still reaching 10% for some households [EECA, residential solar value](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/eeca-insights/understanding-the-value-of-residential-solar-pv-and-storage-in-new-zealand/).

Export rates matter because exported solar is not usually worth the same as avoided retail imports. Powerswitch, a Consumer NZ service, defines the buy-back rate as the amount a retailer pays per kWh of solar electricity injected into the network and says buy-back rates vary by retailer [Powerswitch, solar buy-back rates](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates). EECA says the average New Zealand household uses about 7,000 kWh per year and pays about $2,400 annually, implying an average retail bill value near 34 c/kWh before fixed-charge/detail differences, while Genesis's public FAQ gives a solar buy-back rate of 12.5 c/kWh excluding GST for home and business export connections and Powershop advertises 13 c/kWh flat with 23 c/kWh during defined winter peak times [EECA, plan your solar system](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/), [Genesis, solar buy-back FAQ](https://www.genesisenergy.co.nz/help/faqs/what-is-the-buyback-rate), [Powershop, solar](https://www.powershop.co.nz/solar/). The exact comparison depends on the household's tariff, GST treatment, fixed charges, export timing and self-consumption, so this finding treats low export value as a mechanism rather than a single national rate [Powerswitch, solar buy-back rates](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates), [EECA, residential solar value](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/eeca-insights/understanding-the-value-of-residential-solar-pv-and-storage-in-new-zealand/).

### Barriers: network connection, export caps, upfront capital and split incentives

Grid-connected households must comply with Part 6 of the Electricity Industry Participation Code, contact their retailer, metering provider and lines company, and obtain distributor approval for new solar connections [Electricity Authority, solar power](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/solar-power/). The Electricity Authority says export limits are safeguards used by distributors to prevent overload, instability and safety risks, but also says setting limits too low can reduce distributed-generation investment and reduce local-generation supply [Electricity Authority, export-limit decision paper](https://www.ea.govt.nz/documents/9545/Decision_paper_-_Maximising_benefits_from_local_electricity_generation.pdf).

NZ has recently changed rules to reduce this barrier. On 7 April 2026, the Electricity Authority announced new export-limit rules requiring lines companies to set a default 10 kW export limit for straightforward small-scale DG such as household solar and batteries, while allowing dynamic/flexible export limits where network conditions justify them [Electricity Authority, new export-limit rules](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/press-release/new-rules-encourage-more-solar-to-networks/). The Authority said about 75,000 households with solar and more than 14,700 of those with batteries could feed into local networks at the time of the announcement, and that prior caps were sometimes lower than needed [Electricity Authority, new export-limit rules](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/press-release/new-rules-encourage-more-solar-to-networks/).

Capital and credit access remain core barriers. EECA's household guidance puts a normal small-to-medium system in the $8,500-$11,500 range and explicitly tells households to consider financing, including 0-1% home energy loans [EECA, solar costs and savings](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/). The Electricity Authority also says some lenders, including big banks, offer special low-interest loans for household generation systems, but those products typically require the borrower to own a home and have mortgage or credit capacity [Electricity Authority, solar power](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/solar-power/).

The renter split incentive is structural. Stats NZ reported that 66.0% of households owned their home or held it in a family trust in the 2023 Census, which leaves roughly one-third of households without ownership control over the dwelling [Stats NZ, home ownership 2023](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/home-ownership-increases-and-housing-quality-improves/). MBIE's 2024 energy hardship report says renters were three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy hardship, and says homeowners have greater incentives and autonomy to improve housing quality while renters may have less control over housing quality and appliance efficiency [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). That same logic applies to rooftop solar: the landlord controls the roof asset while the tenant usually pays the electricity bill and receives most direct bill savings [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship report](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [Electricity Authority, solar power](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/solar-power/).

### Levers: NZ is moving toward better export pricing, but equity levers are still narrow

NZ's 2026 rule changes are targeted at export constraints and price signals rather than a direct household solar rebate. The Electricity Authority has confirmed that large retailers with at least 5% market share must offer time-of-use import plans by 1 July 2026 and must also offer plans that reward households supplying power from rooftop solar and batteries when the system needs it [Electricity Authority, new ways to empower consumers](https://www.ea.govt.nz/projects/all/energy-competition-task-force/consultation/new-ways-to-power-electricity-consumers/). The Authority says most existing buy-back rates were fixed regardless of when exported power was valuable, and that time-varying buy-back rates are intended to reflect the value of power when supplied [Electricity Authority, new ways to empower consumers](https://www.ea.govt.nz/projects/all/energy-competition-task-force/consultation/new-ways-to-power-electricity-consumers/).

NZ has also tried targeted community and public-housing renewable-energy funds. MBIE says the Community Renewable Energy Fund committed $28 million to community-based renewable projects that help households access secure, renewable and more affordable energy, including up to 70 community buildings being fitted with solar PV and battery systems after the 2023 North Island severe weather events [MBIE, Community Renewable Energy Fund](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/low-emissions-economy/energy-efficiency-in-new-zealand/community-renewable-energy-fund). MBIE says the Māori and Public Housing Renewable Energy Fund was a $28 million trial of small-scale renewable energy technologies on Māori and public housing, with Kāinga Ora aiming to install solar panels on 750 public homes by 2024 [MBIE, Māori and Public Housing Renewable Energy Fund](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/low-emissions-economy/energy-efficiency-in-new-zealand/maori-and-public-housing-renewable-energy-fund).

Overseas evidence points to two equity-relevant levers: upfront discounts and shared/community solar. Australia's Clean Energy Regulator says Australia has the highest household solar uptake in the world and that its Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme can give eligible household or business systems an upfront discount through small-scale technology certificates [Clean Energy Regulator, rooftop solar](https://cer.gov.au/schemes/renewable-energy-target/small-scale-renewable-energy-scheme/small-scale-renewable-energy-systems/rooftop-solar). The US EPA says community shared solar can expand access for renters, households with shaded roofs, and households unable to install solar because of financial or other barriers [US EPA, community shared solar](https://www.epa.gov/statelocalenergy/community-shared-solar). Those sources do not prove the same policy would work unchanged in NZ, but they identify the policy mechanisms most directly aimed at the barriers NZ households face [Clean Energy Regulator, rooftop solar](https://cer.gov.au/schemes/renewable-energy-target/small-scale-renewable-energy-scheme/small-scale-renewable-energy-systems/rooftop-solar), [US EPA, community shared solar](https://www.epa.gov/statelocalenergy/community-shared-solar).

## What would change this conclusion

- A complete, official project-by-project register of consented, under-construction and commissioned solar farms, with MWac/MWp, grid connection point and expected commercial operation date, would sharpen the utility-scale deployment conclusion; I relied on EA/MBIE summaries and selected fast-track project pages rather than building a full consent census.
- A public household-level dataset linking solar installation, tenure, income, roof suitability, consumption timing, tariff, buy-back rate, finance cost and payback would replace the current "typical household" economics with a distribution of winners and losers.
- Evidence that time-varying buy-back rates materially increase realised household export income after 1 July 2026 would improve the household-economics conclusion; evidence that retailers meet the rule with low-value or hard-to-use offers would weaken it.
- A large public or community-solar programme that reaches renters, Māori/public housing or low-income owner-occupiers at scale would materially change the equity assessment.
- Lived-experience input from renters, budgeting services, installers, lines companies and households in energy hardship is needed to interpret whether solar products are understandable and accessible in practice; public aggregate data cannot answer that.

## Open follow-up questions

- Can EMI or MBIE publish a clean distributed-solar series that separates household, SME, commercial and industrial solar without overlapping market-segment caveats?
- How much do 2026 time-varying buy-back plans actually pay over a year for common solar-only and solar-plus-battery households?
- Which NZ lines companies still impose lower-than-10 kW export limits after the 2026 rule change, and how often are those limits technically justified?
- What community/shared-solar legal and billing model would let NZ renters receive bill credits without taking roof ownership or long contract risk?
