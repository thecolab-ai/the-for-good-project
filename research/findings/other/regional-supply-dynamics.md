# Auckland is the volume engine, Canterbury and Queenstown-Lakes are high-intensity growth markets, and Wellington City is a low-consent capital-city outlier

## Executive answer

- Auckland should be treated as the national volume engine rather than a per-capita outlier: Stats NZ reported 16,862 Auckland new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026, 42% of New Zealand's 39,737, and Auckland's 9.3 consents per 1,000 residents was above the 7.4 national rate but below Canterbury and Otago [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).
- Canterbury is the strongest large-region supply case in this comparison: Stats NZ reported 8,468 Canterbury new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026, 12.1 per 1,000 residents, while Greater Christchurch's 2023 capacity evidence reported a long-term Greater Christchurch urban surplus but a Selwyn long-term shortfall inside that total [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Greater Christchurch HDCA evidence, 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0016/141127/cc921192201686bf6c2c7480034c1ea2c6eda60d.pdf).
- Queenstown-Lakes is a small-volume but extreme per-capita pressure market: Stats NZ's territorial-authority data gives Queenstown-Lakes District 1,909 new dwellings consented and 35.5 per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026, while QLDC's 2025 HBA adoption notice says the district needs 27,100 additional dwellings over 30 years and has a short-term infrastructure-limited shortfall of about 1,000 dwellings [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/).
- Wellington City is the low-per-capita capital-city case: Stats NZ's territorial-authority data gives Wellington City 525 new dwellings consented and 2.5 per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026, and the Wellington City HBA housing update reported a 2021-2051 shortfall of 10,222 dwellings under the operative District Plan and market-realisation modelling [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf).
- The practical answer is not "which region is good or bad"; the four places have different binding questions: Auckland is about converting very large enabled capacity into infrastructure-served, market-realised dwellings; Canterbury is about sustaining high delivery while managing Christchurch/Selwyn/Waimakariri spatial balance; Queenstown-Lakes is about infrastructure and visitor-economy growth pressure; and Wellington City is about turning plan-enabled and feasible capacity into actual consents and completions [Auckland HBA, 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/media/v0jperq1/auckland-council-hba-sept-2023_final_print-part-2.pdf); [Greater Christchurch Spatial Plan, 2024](https://www.greaterchristchurch.org.nz/assets/Documents/Work-Programme/Greater-Christchurch-Spatial-Plan/Greater-Christchurch-Spatial-Plan-2024.pdf); [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/); [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the consent-count comparison is high-confidence because it comes from the current Stats NZ release and downloadable CSV, but the causal interpretation is medium-confidence because public capacity assessments measure enabled, feasible, or realisable capacity rather than completed dwellings.

## Evidence

### One comparable consent baseline

The latest Stats NZ building-consents release available on 5 July 2026 is the May 2026 release, published on 2 July 2026, and Stats NZ says the June 2026 release is scheduled for 3 August 2026 [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/). Stats NZ reported 39,737 new homes consented nationally in the year ended May 2026 and a national rate of 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).

The four-place comparison uses Stats NZ's "Dwelling units / New / Number" actual rows from the May 2026 downloadable monthly CSV for year-ended counts, and the matching annual "new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents" CSV for per-capita rates [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). ### Auckland: high volume, capacity-rich, infrastructure-constrained in places

Auckland's 16,862 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026 were more than Canterbury and Wellington region combined, and apartments/townhouses/units/other dwellings made up 11,084 of Auckland's new dwelling consents [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Auckland Council's 2023 HBA says Auckland had forecast demand of about 200,000 additional dwellings over 30 years to 2052 and more than sufficient "reasonably expected to be realised" housing capacity in aggregate [Auckland HBA, 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/media/v0jperq1/auckland-council-hba-sept-2023_final_print-part-2.pdf).

The same Auckland HBA says the aggregate result masks short-term feasibility challenges, ongoing infrastructure challenges in locations not previously expected to accommodate significant growth, and choices about where scarce infrastructure and funding resources should be allocated [Auckland HBA, 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/media/v0jperq1/auckland-council-hba-sept-2023_final_print-part-2.pdf). Auckland Council's 2024 Future Development Strategy monitoring report says 13,855 dwellings were consented in 2023/24, down 27% from the previous year, and that 82% of those dwellings were in the existing urban area, 13% in future urban areas, and 5% in rural areas [Auckland FDS monitoring, 2024](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/auckland-future-development-strategy-2023-2053-monitoring-report-december-2024/).

**Interpretation:** Auckland's supply dynamic is high market throughput plus a large enabled-capacity cushion, but the public evidence points to infrastructure funding, sequencing, and feasibility as the question to test rather than simple zoning sufficiency [Auckland HBA, 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/media/v0jperq1/auckland-council-hba-sept-2023_final_print-part-2.pdf); [Auckland FDS monitoring, 2024](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/auckland-future-development-strategy-2023-2053-monitoring-report-december-2024/).

### Canterbury and Greater Christchurch: high regional intensity with uneven sub-regional balance

Canterbury's 8,468 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026 made it the second-largest regional volume in the Stats NZ release, and its 12.1 consents per 1,000 residents was higher than Auckland's 9.3 and the national 7.4 rate [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/). Within Greater Christchurch, Stats NZ's territorial-authority data shows Christchurch City consented 4,524 new dwellings at 10.8 per 1,000 residents, Selwyn consented 2,314 at 26.4 per 1,000 residents, and Waimakariri consented 843 at 12.1 per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

Greater Christchurch's 2024 Spatial Plan says more than 700,000 people are projected to live in Christchurch, Selwyn, and Waimakariri by 2050, about 30% more than today, with the population potentially doubling to 1 million within 60 years [Greater Christchurch engagement page, 2023](https://greaterchristchurch.org.nz/urbangrowthprogramme/draft-greater-christchurch-spatial-plan/). Evidence summarising the Greater Christchurch 2023 HDCA reports a long-term 2022-2052 urban feasible-capacity surplus of 54,450 dwellings across Greater Christchurch, but also reports a Selwyn long-term shortfall of 3,250 dwellings inside that total [Greater Christchurch HDCA evidence, 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0016/141127/cc921192201686bf6c2c7480034c1ea2c6eda60d.pdf).

**Interpretation:** Canterbury's supply dynamic is not just "Christchurch grows"; it is a three-council system where Christchurch contributes large intensification capacity, Selwyn contributes very high per-capita greenfield-heavy consenting, and Waimakariri remains materially smaller in consent volume [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [Greater Christchurch HDCA evidence, 2023](https://www.waimakariri.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0016/141127/cc921192201686bf6c2c7480034c1ea2c6eda60d.pdf).

### Queenstown-Lakes: high per-capita consenting under infrastructure pressure

Queenstown-Lakes District consented 1,909 new dwellings in the year ended May 2026, which was far below Auckland's raw volume but far above all comparison places on per-capita intensity at 35.5 new dwellings per 1,000 residents [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). QLDC's adopted 2025 HBA summary says the district population is expected to grow by 80% to 97,500 over 30 years and that the adopted high-growth housing bottom line requires 27,100 additional dwellings over 30 years, including 9,100 by 2033 [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/).

QLDC's 2025 HBA adoption notice says substantial plan-enabled housing capacity already exists, but current infrastructure limitations create a district-wide short-term shortfall of about 1,000 dwellings, especially in Whakatipu, with a medium- and long-term surplus expected as planned infrastructure investment and development opportunities increase [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/). The Queenstown Lakes Spatial Plan was prepared through the Grow Well Whaiora Partnership between central government, local government, and Kai Tahu partners to align long-term urban development and infrastructure planning for Wakatipu and Upper Clutha [Queenstown Lakes Spatial Plan, 2021](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/media/5k3l4whu/2a-attachment-a-queenstown-lakes-spatial-plan-cc.pdf).

**Interpretation:** Queenstown-Lakes' supply dynamic is a pressure-market problem, not a national-volume problem: very high consenting intensity is still consistent with a short-term infrastructure-limited capacity shortfall when population, worker, visitor-economy, and second-home pressures are concentrated in constrained basins [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/).

### Wellington City: low current consenting despite capacity reform evidence

Wellington region had 2,168 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026 and a regional rate of 4.0 per 1,000 residents, while Wellington City had 525 new dwellings consented and a city rate of 2.5 per 1,000 residents [Stats NZ release, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). Wellington City's 2022 HBA housing update reported projected demand for 36,621 additional dwellings between 2021 and 2051, operative-District-Plan enabled capacity of 104,941 dwellings, economically feasible capacity of 37,875 dwellings, realisable capacity of 26,399 dwellings, and a resulting shortfall of 10,222 dwellings [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf).

The Wellington City HBA says the city's Spatial Plan is the strategy for managing growth over 30 years and is intended to direct new houses, transport networks, infrastructure, and services into areas that benefit the city most [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf). Later Wellington City district-plan evidence reported that under the proposed District Plan a realisable-capacity scenario materially met long-term expected demand, but that evidence is a capacity assessment rather than evidence that consents or completions had already increased [Wellington PDP evidence, 2022](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/district-plan/proposed-district-plan/files/hearing-streams/01/council-reports-and-sup-ev/statement-of-evidence-of-philip-osborne-on-behalf-of-wellington-city-council.pdf).

**Interpretation:** Wellington City's supply dynamic is the clearest capacity-to-consents gap in this comparison: public evidence shows a city trying to expand capacity through spatial planning and district-plan reform, while the current Stats NZ consent baseline remains low in both raw and per-capita terms [Stats NZ CSV, 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- Territorial-authority completion data, if published and current, would change the weighting of this finding because Stats NZ consents are a leading indicator and Stats NZ's dwelling-estimates method discounts and lags consents when estimating dwelling stock [Stats NZ DataInfo+, 2026](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/ded8075e-2713-4876-84eb-b2a7b54e8b4a).
- A current 2026 council-by-council Housing Growth Target compliance dataset would change the capacity side of this comparison because the current public documents are not all from the same year and do not all use the same post-reform planning settings [HUD Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf); [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/).
- A current infrastructure-ready capacity audit for Auckland, Greater Christchurch, Queenstown-Lakes, and Wellington on one method would change the causal ranking because public sources repeatedly distinguish plan-enabled capacity from feasibility, infrastructure readiness, and likely realisation [Auckland HBA, 2023](https://knowledgeauckland.org.nz/media/v0jperq1/auckland-council-hba-sept-2023_final_print-part-2.pdf); [QLDC, 2025](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/2025/october/25-10-10-assessment-of-housing-and-business-capacity-in-queenstown-lakes-district-adopted/); [Wellington City HBA, 2022](https://wrlc.org.nz/assets/Documents/Documents/2025/10/HBA-Chapt-2-WCC-with-Appendices.pdf).
- I could not verify a single public, current dataset that aligns consents, completions, infrastructure-ready capacity, and affordability outcomes for all four places on the same definitions.
- I could not verify from public sources how much of Queenstown-Lakes consenting is absorbed by resident households versus visitor accommodation, second homes, or worker accommodation; that distinction would need local HBA microdata or council/market expertise.
- A housing planner, council infrastructure finance specialist, or consenting practitioner should review any policy-facing conclusion drawn from this comparison because this finding uses only public aggregate sources.
