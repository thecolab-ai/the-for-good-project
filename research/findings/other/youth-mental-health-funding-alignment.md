# Youth mental-health funding is shifting toward access and crisis supports, but public data cannot yet show that spend is aligned to youth need or outcomes

## Executive answer

- The largest clearly mapped public youth-relevant funding stream is Access and Choice: Budget 2019 allocated $664 million over five years, with $516.4 million for service delivery, $99.73 million for workforce development, and $48.15 million for enablers; Youth services were allocated 15% of service-delivery funding, Kaupapa Maori 20%, Pacific 7-8%, and IPMHA 57-62%, depending on whether allocated, committed, or people-seen shares are used. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)
- Suicide-prevention funding is much smaller but more targeted: the 2025-2029 action plan is backed by about $20 million a year of Vote Health suicide-prevention investment plus more than $16 million a year from 2025/26 for crisis cafes, youth acute/respite/crisis recovery, ED peer support, a youth/priority-population campaign, bereavement support, a new community fund, and training. [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf)
- The main contracted NGO/philanthropic-public hybrids are Gumboot Friday/I Am Hope ($24 million over four years; $6 million per year in the 2026/27 Vote Health estimates), the $9.72 million Mental Health and Addiction Community Sector Innovation Fund, and named or matched NGO/community providers such as Youthline, Sir John Kirwan Foundation, Rotorua Community Youth Centre Trust, Mental Health Foundation, and Le Va-administered Pasifika suicide-prevention community funds. [Treasury, Budget 2024 Summary of Initiatives](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-06/b24-sum-initiatives.pdf); [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf); [Le Va, 2025](https://www.leva.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Empowering-Pasifika-Communities-web.pdf)
- Directionally, the money is moving toward evidence-consistent features: earlier support, low-barrier access, youth-friendly settings, crisis alternatives, postvention, Pacific and Maori community approaches, and a target that 25% of mental-health and addiction investment goes to prevention/early intervention. [Health NZ, 2026](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/mental-health-and-addiction-targets); [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf)
- Alignment is still not proven: specialist youth access remains a bottleneck, public outcome data for Youth Access and Choice and other youth supports is incomplete, school guidance and school-based-health funding have weak accountability data, and provider-level contract/funding amounts are not consistently published by age, ethnicity, disability, rainbow status, region, acuity, or intervention type. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - High for named public funding streams and targets; Medium for alignment judgements because the public record shows direction and reach, not enough outcome, regional, acuity, or priority-population spend data.

## Evidence

### Public mental-health money is large, but youth-specific lines are only partly visible

Vote Health was $33.031 billion in the 2026/27 Estimates, including $15.995 billion for hospital and specialist services, $10.348 billion for primary/community/public/population health, and $811 million for hauora Maori services; those appropriations include mental-health services but do not isolate youth mental-health spending. [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf)

The Suicide Prevention Action Plan says implementation is part of more than $2.6 billion spent annually by government on mental-health and addiction services, but it identifies only about $20 million a year as existing Vote Health suicide-prevention investment and more than $16 million a year from 2025/26 as the new mental-health and suicide-prevention support package. [Ministry Suicide Prevention Action Plan, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-09/suicide-prevention-action-plan-2025-2029-v2.pdf)

The new $16 million-plus package is explicitly directed to crisis recovery cafes and locally designed crisis services, improved acute/respite/crisis recovery services for young people, ED peer support, a youth and priority-population wellbeing campaign, bereavement support, a new community fund for higher-need populations such as maternal, youth and rural communities, and suicide-prevention training. [Ministry Cabinet paper, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-06/Cabinet%20material%20-%20Suicide%20Prevention%20Action%20Plan%202025-2029%20Redacted.pdf)

The first quarterly action-plan update for July-September 2025 reported crisis recovery cafes/hubs/services being established, existing postvention agreements renewed to 30 June 2026, procurement of new postvention services planned, and work underway on a community fund focused on maternal, youth and rural groups to complement Maori and Pacific funds. [Ministry quarterly update, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/Suicide-Prevention-Action-Plan-2025%E2%80%932029-Quarterly-update-July%E2%80%93September-2025_0.pdf)

### Access and Choice is the main scaled youth-relevant early-support investment

Access and Choice was funded from Budget 2019 with $664 million over five years, including $455 million over 2019/20-2022/23 and $209 million in 2023/24, with later inflationary uplifts. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

Te Hiringa Mahara reported that, by 30 June 2024, $628 million of the Access and Choice funding had been committed, all funding was committed in the final roll-out year, and the programme had delivered 676,261 sessions to 207,606 people in 2023/24 against a 325,000-person annual reach aim. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

The same report shows the allocation design: of the $664.29 million, $516.40 million funded service delivery, $99.73 million funded workforce development, and $48.15 million funded enablers; by service type, Youth services were set at 15% of service-delivery funding, Kaupapa Maori 20%, Pacific 7%, and IPMHA 58% of allocated funding as at 30 June 2024. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

Youth Access and Choice had 24 services in all 20 districts by 2023/24, and Youth services saw 10,932 people in that year; however, 54% of 12-24-year-olds using Access and Choice were seen through IPMHA rather than the Youth stream. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

The 2026/27 Vote Health estimates set Access and Choice performance standards of 325,000 people accessing primary mental-health and addiction services through the programme and 80% being seen within one week, while the 2025/26 estimated actual was 263,000 people and 82% seen within one week. [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf)

### Contracted NGO and philanthropic/public streams are visible by name, but not consistently by need or outcome

Budget 2024 funded Gumboot Friday to deliver free mental-health counselling for young people aged 5-25 through I Am Hope; the 2026/27 Vote Health estimates show $6 million a year for Gumboot Friday across 2025/26-2029/30. [Treasury, Budget 2024 Summary of Initiatives](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-06/b24-sum-initiatives.pdf); [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf)

The Ministry's 2025 Gumboot Friday briefing says Budget 2024 made $24 million available over four years, the contract reimburses $150 per eligible session, the first-year contract target was at least 30,000 sessions for 10,000 young people, and the proposed year-two variation was at least 40,000 sessions for 15,000 clients aged 5-25. [Ministry Gumboot Friday briefing, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-07/H2025067783%20Briefing_0.pdf)

The Auditor-General did not assess Gumboot Friday service quality, but found the $24 million direct-supplier funding process was unusual and inconsistent with good procurement practice because the provider and amount were decided without an open, transparent, competitive assessment of service type, provider options, and price. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/gumboot-friday)

Budget 2024 also created a $9.72 million Mental Health and Addiction Community Sector Innovation Fund by reprioritising existing baseline funding, and public announcements named Youthline, Sir John Kirwan Foundation, Mental Health Foundation, Wellington City Mission, MATES in Construction, and Rotorua Community Youth Centre Trust among first-round recipients. [Treasury, Budget 2024 Summary of Initiatives](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-06/b24-sum-initiatives.pdf); [Beehive, 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/boost-rotorua%E2%80%99s-youth-mental-health-services)

The public recipients list describes Youthline funding as expansion of its Counselling Your Way programme, an eight-session structured intervention for young people aged 16 and over, but the public list does not provide a full by-recipient funding table in the source text captured for this finding. [Health NZ recipients page, 2026](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/workforce-development/scholarships-and-grants/apply/recipients)

Le Va's Pasifika Suicide Prevention Community Fund report says Le Va awarded $3.55 million from 2020 to 2023 across 78 community initiatives, with more than 19,000 participants directly engaged and more than 2.4 million social-media reach, but the report also says outcome reporting was variable and not independently verified. [Le Va, 2025](https://www.leva.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Empowering-Pasifika-Communities-web.pdf)

The Mental Health Foundation's 2024/25 annual impact report shows a mixed public/philanthropic model, with 2025 revenue including $3.18 million contract income, $3.12 million donations/legacies/sponsorship, and $1.16 million grants and other contract income, and it describes youth-focused activity including Rangatahi Rise, Pink Shirt Day-funded school tools, and suicide-prevention/postvention resources. [Mental Health Foundation, 2025](https://mentalhealth.org.nz/media/e1ndqi5d/annual-impact-report-2024-2025-mhf.pdf)

Youthline's latest annual report found in this scan was 2022/23 rather than current 2025/26; it reported supporting 14,917 young people, managing 260,000 text conversations, providing 11,930 counselling sessions across Auckland, and suicide risk as the top presenting issue over text, so it is useful context for contracted/philanthropic youth reach but not a current funding baseline. [Youthline, 2023](https://youthline.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/youthline_annual_report_fy23.pdf)

### Education and school/community programmes are substantial but hard to connect to outcomes

The Auditor-General reported that the Ministry of Education spends about $95 million annually on secondary-school guidance entitlement staffing, but it does not require schools to report how they spend that entitlement and cannot tell how many services it funds, whether needs are met, or whether the funding is used for its intended purpose. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

The Auditor-General also reported that Health NZ-funded nurse-led school-based health services cover about 300 decile 1-5 secondary schools, about 35% of Year 9-13 students, while most secondary students are in decile 6-10 schools where the Health NZ-funded service is not available. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

Budget 2026 continues $3.137 million a year for Extending School Based Health Services and $3.5 million a year for Piki, an integrated primary mental-health and addiction support service for young people in Greater Wellington. [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf)

Youth One Stop Shops are a key community model for 12-24-year-olds, but the Auditor-General found they received about $19 million of combined government funding in 2021/22 and still had no consistent national funding, objectives, outcomes data, or performance measures. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

### Alignment with need and evidence is directional, not demonstrated

The strongest alignment is strategic: the funding map emphasises early intervention, youth-friendly access, crisis alternatives, postvention, community funds, Maori and Pacific approaches, and prevention/early intervention targets, which broadly matches the Ministry evidence synthesis and Te Hiringa Mahara's youth early-intervention review. [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf)

The Ministry evidence synthesis says large population-level suicide reductions are unlikely from isolated, unsustained interventions that are not connected to an integrated multi-level strategy, and it calls for sustained outcomes frameworks, Te ao Maori integration, health-economic evaluation, and real-time self-harm/suicide surveillance. [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf)

Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 youth early-intervention review found that many low-intensity and "light touch" interventions had at least modest effectiveness, but the evidence base was uneven and stronger evaluation is needed to identify what works best, for whom, especially for communities facing access barriers. [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf)

The main misalignment risk is acuity: public funding visibly expanded mild-to-moderate primary/community access, while the Auditor-General found that funding for specialist child/adolescent services rose 24% from 2011/12 to 2019/20 but the number of children and young people accessing ICAMHS rose 30%, and Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 access summary found 0-18-year-olds had the longest specialist waits and highest declined-referral rate. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara access summary, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

The 2026/27 Vote Health estimates acknowledge the specialist youth-access issue by setting a mental-health target for 78% of young people to be seen within three weeks from referral in 2026/27, against a 2030 target of 80%; that target does not by itself show whether funding is sufficient for higher-acuity youth need. [Treasury, Vote Health Estimates 2026/27](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-05/est26-v5-health.pdf)

The equity picture is also only partly aligned: Access and Choice reached Maori and Pacific people above their population shares and Youth services saw many Maori young people, but Te Hiringa Mahara says outcomes data is incomplete, disability status is difficult to identify, and Kaupapa Maori, Pacific and Youth services are not required to submit the same national data as IPMHA. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Health NZ funding table that separates child/youth mental-health spend by Access and Choice stream, ICAMHS/CAMHS, crisis, postvention, school-based services, Youth One Stop Shops, Gumboot Friday, Innovation Fund recipients, region, age, ethnicity, disability, rainbow status where safe, and acuity would change this finding from a funding-stream map into a real equity-allocation assessment. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)
- A public outcome dataset linking funded youth supports to consistent outcome measures, wait harms, referrals accepted/declined, crisis escalation, repeat ED use, school engagement, and self-harm/suicide indicators would change the conclusion from "directionally aligned" to "effective" or "misallocated", depending on results. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf); [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf)
- Publication of the Child and Youth Mental Health Study could change the need baseline, especially for 5-14-year-olds, disabled young people, rainbow young people, high-deprivation households, Maori, Pacific and Asian youth, and young people outside secondary-school survey frames. [Ministry child and youth mental health study page, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/surveys/child-and-youth-mental-health-study)
- A current annual report or audited accounts for major youth providers such as Youthline, Sir John Kirwan Foundation/Mitey, and I Am Hope that cleanly separates public contracts, philanthropy, programme spend, age group, geography, and outcome results would improve the philanthropic/NGO map. [Youthline, 2023](https://youthline.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/youthline_annual_report_fy23.pdf); [Mental Health Foundation, 2025](https://mentalhealth.org.nz/media/e1ndqi5d/annual-impact-report-2024-2025-mhf.pdf)
- Human review by rangatahi, whanau, Maori, Pacific, rainbow, disabled, rural, youth-worker, school, crisis and clinical stakeholders is needed before this stream makes G1 decisions, because public funding presence does not prove practical access, cultural safety, trust, or suitability of adult-service pathways for 18-24-year-olds. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara early-intervention review, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Early-intervention-and-secondary-prevention-for-young-people/MHWC-early-intervention-effectiveness-review-April-2026.pdf)
- I could not verify a complete current provider-level list of all youth mental-health and suicide-prevention contracts, philanthropic grants, contract values, subcontractors, regional coverage, or outcomes; I also could not verify a current Youthline audited annual report newer than FY2023 in this scan. [Health NZ recipients page, 2026](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/health-professionals/workforce-development/scholarships-and-grants/apply/recipients)

## Open follow-up questions

- Which youth mental-health contracts are still short-term, small or duplicated across agencies, and does consolidation improve continuity without reducing kaupapa Maori, Pacific, rainbow, disabled, rural or youth-led choice? [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)
- How much of the 25% prevention/early-intervention target reaches youth-specific services rather than all-age prevention activity, and how much reaches Maori, Pacific, disabled, rainbow, care-experienced and high-deprivation youth? [Health NZ, 2026](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/mental-health-and-addiction-targets)
- Which public/philanthropic youth supports have enough outcome and cost data to compare value for money against specialist wait-list reduction, distress reduction, crisis escalation, or school/work participation outcomes? [Ministry evidence synthesis, 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-07/report-evidence-synthesis-10jul24.pdf)
