# NZ plastic recycling is economically hard because small, distant, mixed streams need quality, demand and producer-funded infrastructure at the same time

## Executive answer

- The economic barrier is structural, not mainly household effort: New Zealand has a small domestic market, unevenly distributed plastic reprocessing, Cook Strait and long-haul transport costs, incomplete MRF capability, contamination costs, and weak or volatile end-market demand for some recycled plastics [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).
- Scale is the central constraint: PPPS says New Zealand needs Australia/regional alignment because domestic scale is small, while the national infrastructure stocktake says onshore plastic reprocessing was only 32,584 tonnes per year and concentrated around Auckland, the lower North Island and Canterbury [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf).
- Quality is the second constraint: Auckland Council reported nearly a quarter of kerbside recycling was contaminated in January 2024 and cost ratepayers about $3 million per year in sorting and disposal, while RLF says contamination creates re-transportation, landfill, equipment-damage and lost-capacity costs at MRFs [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/01/new-standards-to-recycle-right/); [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).
- Polymer mix matters: rigid PET, HDPE and PP have value pathways, but coloured or lined PET, soft plastics, lids/caps and mixed films often need extra sorting, different collection channels, subsidies, or better design before recycling is economic [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf); [Soft Plastics Recycling Scheme report, 2024](https://www.packagingforum.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Soft-Plastic-Recycling-Scheme-Annual-Accreditation-Report-2024.pdf).
- Highest-leverage post-G1 investigations for a small volunteer team are: producer-funded PPPS/EPR evidence, recycled-content demand/procurement, MRF/transport bottleneck maps, packaging-design rules for high-loss formats, and soft-plastics/lids trial economics; these are investigations only, not ideation issues [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [OECD recycled-content requirements, 2024](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/05/plastics-recycled-content-requirements_c6de64b7/b311ee60-en.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of the barriers is well supported across official, scheme and operator-facing sources, but exact dollar impacts by polymer, district, MRF and contract are not public.

## Evidence

### Scale and distance make every tonne work harder

PPPS says lack of scale and lack of circularity are core New Zealand constraints, says New Zealand should avoid duplicating EPR networks, and says alignment with Australia is critical because New Zealand's onshore scale is small [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). The same report says only about 29,000 tonnes of plastic packaging were reprocessed onshore in 2022, about 16,000 tonnes were exported for reprocessing, and around 16,000 tonnes of onshore capacity were expected to be added [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

The national infrastructure stocktake reports 32,584 tonnes per year of onshore plastic reprocessing, with capacity uneven across polymers: LDPE/LLDPE 35%, HDPE 27%, clear PET 18%, PP 9%, PVC under 1%, and export markets 14% [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf). It also says plastic reprocessing facilities are concentrated around Auckland, the lower North Island and Canterbury; post-consumer plastic often travels further than post-commercial plastic; some South Island clear PET travels to the North Island; and Cook Strait shipping can cost more than exporting or landfilling [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf).

That geography means transport is not a side issue: the stocktake gives an indicative waste transport cost range of $0.30-$0.60 per tonne-kilometre for 20-tonne aggregated loads, with a 100 km haul at $0.40 per tonne-kilometre costing $40 per tonne [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf). PPPS's CBA also treats shipping as a major cost line: Option 3 includes $93.1 million present value for shipping, alongside $111.7 million collection, $54.8 million sorting and $147.6 million reprocessing [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

### Contamination and MRF capability convert potential value into cost

Contamination is a real council cost, not only a messaging problem: Auckland Council reported in July 2023 that its kerbside contamination rate had reached 22%, up from an 11% decade-long pre-2020 level, and that the higher contamination cost ratepayers an extra $3 million per year [Auckland Council, 2023](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/07/double-trouble-for-recycling/). Auckland Council repeated in January 2024 that nearly a quarter of its kerbside recycling was contaminated and that the contamination cost about $3 million per year, while its standardised list excluded plastics 3, 4, 6 and 7, all lids, aerosol cans, liquid paperboard, foil/trays, items under 50 mm and items over 4 litres [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/01/new-standards-to-recycle-right/).

RLF gives the operator-level mechanism: non-recyclable material and general waste in kerbside recycling create extra disposal, handling and transport costs, and material that enters MRFs or recycling facilities but cannot be recovered ultimately goes to landfill [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf). RLF also says contamination creates re-transportation costs, landfill fees, damage to MRF equipment and lost system capacity [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).

MRF capability is uneven by material and site. RLF says lids and caps were removed from kerbside because they are often under 5 cm, too small to hand-sort or fall through sorting lines, but MRF operators still see lids and caps in the recycling stream [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf). RLF's lids-and-caps subgroup recommends extra MRF lines and reprocessor equipment to sort PET, HDPE and PP caps, but says separate lids-and-caps recovery is not commercially viable without government funding at first [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).

### Polymer mix, colour and design change the economics

PPPS's fee categories show a practical hierarchy: rigid PET, HDPE and PP are Category A recyclable materials; HDPE/PP film, LDPE, EPS and other rigid plastics are conditional; PVC, compostable and other packaging with no pathway are Category C, with Option 3 modelled fees of $208/t for Category A, $416/t for Category B and $1,731/t for Category C [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). That fee spread is direct evidence that product design and polymer choice are economic levers, because the recommended scheme would make hard-to-recycle packaging pay much more than recyclable packaging [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

Coloured PET is a specific example of a material that can be nominally accepted but economically weak. PPPS classifies clear/light-blue mono-polymer PET food and non-food containers as "recyclable with value", but classifies PET trays with liners, coloured non-food PET trays/pots/punnets, and opaque or solid-colour PET bottles/flasks/trays as Category C material accepted at kerbside but not by reprocessors [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf). RLF adds that Pact NZ warned poor cap separation could reduce clear PET value by around 3%, which shows how small design/sortation details can affect the highest-value plastic stream [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).

PP is a counterexample where demand exists but capture and sorting are weak. Plastics NZ says demand for recycled PP is strong in New Zealand, manufacturers still import rPP, pelletised rPP was worth about NZ$1,300-$1,700/t at the time of its 2022 report, and seven NZ reprocessors existed, but only 1,200-1,500 tonnes of post-consumer rPP were being reprocessed from an estimated 8,000-10,000 tonnes of post-consumer PP packaging [Plastics NZ PP report, 2022](https://www.plastics.org.nz/images/documents/Environment/Circular_Design_Economy/Polypropylene_Progress_Report__August_2022.pdf). The same report says further work was needed to improve MRF sorting, prevent sorted or partly sorted PP being sold overseas where domestic markets existed, and develop consistent good-quality feedstocks [Plastics NZ PP report, 2022](https://www.plastics.org.nz/images/documents/Environment/Circular_Design_Economy/Polypropylene_Progress_Report__August_2022.pdf).

Soft plastics are hard because collection, processing and end-product demand must all line up. The 2024 Soft Plastics Recycling Scheme report says the scheme collected 982 tonnes and processed 737 tonnes in 2024, had supply agreements with Future Post in Waiuku and Blenheim and saveBOARD in Hamilton, and budgeted to reprocess 1,000 tonnes [Soft Plastics Recycling Scheme report, 2024](https://www.packagingforum.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Soft-Plastic-Recycling-Scheme-Annual-Accreditation-Report-2024.pdf). The same report says the collection-processing gap reflected bale movement timing and reduced processor demand, including economic pressure on Future Post's wine and viticulture customer base, and says mandatory stewardship is needed to fund expansion, higher processing costs, compliance and reporting [Soft Plastics Recycling Scheme report, 2024](https://www.packagingforum.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Soft-Plastic-Recycling-Scheme-Annual-Accreditation-Report-2024.pdf).

### Virgin-plastic economics and recycled-content demand are market-design problems

The infrastructure stocktake identifies the low price of virgin resin relative to recycled resin, the low price of imported virgin-plastic products, scrap-price competition with offshore buyers, poor recovered-material quality, high capital costs for recycling equipment, and recycled-content willingness by manufacturers and brands as key dynamics affecting recycler viability [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf). OECD similarly says secondary plastics are mainly treated as substitutes for primary plastics, leaving secondary markets small and vulnerable, and recommends combining EPR-style supply "push" policies with recycled-content-target "pull" policies [OECD Global Plastics Outlook, 2022](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2022/02/global-plastics-outlook_a653d1c9.html).

Recycled-content mandates can pull demand but are not sufficient alone. OECD's recycled-content review says such policies can stimulate demand and investment, but limited quality supply can raise prices; it also says demand policies need complementary supply policies such as EPR, collection, separation, decontamination and design-for-recycling improvements [OECD recycled-content requirements, 2024](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/05/plastics-recycled-content-requirements_c6de64b7/b311ee60-en.pdf). This matters for New Zealand because OECD notes smaller markets distant from recycling supply chains may get less local benefit from content requirements than large markets with mature supply-side systems, while PPPS explicitly frames Australia/regional alignment as important for New Zealand scale [OECD recycled-content requirements, 2024](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/05/plastics-recycled-content-requirements_c6de64b7/b311ee60-en.pdf); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).

Comparable-country evidence points in the same direction. The EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation entered into force in February 2025 and aims to make all EU-market packaging recyclable in an economically viable way by 2030, safely increase recycled plastics in packaging, and decrease virgin material use [European Commission, 2026](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/waste-and-recycling/packaging-waste_en). Australia's APCO targets are voluntary/co-regulatory rather than a direct NZ model, but APCO reports only 19% progress for plastic packaging recycling/composting against a 70% target and says its next plan includes high-quality collection/recycling support and eco-modulated member fees [APCO National Packaging Targets, 2026](https://apco.org.au/national-packaging-targets).

## What would change this conclusion

- A live regulated PPPS with actual fee schedules, contracts, MRF/PRF investments, producer reporting and measured recovery results would replace this finding's reliance on design-stage modelling [MfE priority product stewardship, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/waste/product-stewardship/priority-product-stewardship/); [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf).
- Contract-level council, MRF, exporter and reprocessor data could change the intervention ranking because public sources expose broad costs and constraints but not local gate fees, bale rejection rates, contamination by polymer, stockpiling, processor yields or commercial margins [PPPS Scheme Design Report, 2025](https://ppps.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Plastic-Packaging-Product-Stewardship-Scheme-Design-Report-2025.pdf); [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf).
- Updated post-2024 national data on kerbside contamination, MRF outputs and reprocessor intake after standardisation could move confidence from Medium to High, because Auckland's contamination figures are strong for Auckland but not a national rate [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/01/new-standards-to-recycle-right/); [RLF progress report, 2025](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Recycling-Leadership-Forum-Term-1-Annual-Progress-Report-and-Recommendations-v2.pdf).
- I could not verify national current prices for virgin resin, recycled resin, MRF bale grades, freight rates or processor gate fees from public sources; the finding therefore uses official/sector descriptions of market dynamics rather than a current commodity-price model [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf); [OECD recycled-content requirements, 2024](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/05/plastics-recycled-content-requirements_c6de64b7/b311ee60-en.pdf).
- Human validation from council waste managers, MRF operators, reprocessors, producers, retailers, community-resource-recovery groups and iwi/regional infrastructure planners is needed before turning these ranked investigations into solutions, because the sources do not capture lived operational constraints or Treaty/local-governance implications consistently [MfE infrastructure stocktake, 2023](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/Waste/Waste-and-resource-recovery-infrastructure-and-services-stocktake-Project-summary-report.pdf).

## Open follow-up questions

- What contract-level data would councils, MRFs and reprocessors be willing to publish in aggregated form so PPPS fees can be tested against real net costs?
- Which NZ buyers could create durable demand for rPET, rHDPE, rPP and soft-plastic products through procurement without encouraging low-value downcycling?
- Which MRF upgrades have the strongest tonne-per-dollar effect for PET colour separation, caps/lids recovery, PP capture and soft-plastic trial handling?
- What packaging-design changes would remove the largest avoidable losses from the kerbside 1/2/5 stream without shifting costs to households or councils?
