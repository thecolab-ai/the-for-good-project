# New Zealand's main energy-hardship interventions leave structural gaps for renters and working low-income households

## Executive answer

- MBIE's latest national energy-hardship reporting says 6.7% of households, about 132,000 households, could not afford to keep their homes adequately warm in the year ended June 2024, and the groups most exposed include renters, low-income households, households in material hardship, Māori and Pacific households, crowded households, and one-parent families. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- Warmer Kiwi Homes is well targeted to cold-home quality among eligible owner-occupiers, but it structurally excludes renters because EECA requires applicants to own and live in the home and says rentals are excluded. [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/)
- The Winter Energy Payment reaches many beneficiaries and superannuitants automatically, but it is not targeted to cold or inefficient housing and excludes people who are energy-poor but not receiving a main benefit, pension, or Jobseeker Support Student Hardship. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html)
- Retailer, EnergyMate, Power Credits, Work and Income emergency assistance, MoneyTalks, CAB, council, and community channels create a patchwork backstop, but public evidence shows these are conditional on retailer contact, specific participating retailers, referral pathways, regional availability, urgent-need or income/asset tests, or community-service capacity. [Electricity Authority, consumer care](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-youre-finding-it-hard-to-pay-your-bill/); [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf); [Work and Income, urgent power bills](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/living-expenses/heating-and-power-bills.html)
- The clearest gap in the interventions mapped here is therefore not "no help exists"; it is that no major intervention in this mapped set cleanly covers a renter or working low-income household whose home is cold, whose power costs are unaffordable, and whose situation is not yet an urgent arrears/disconnection case. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024); [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/); [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the eligibility boundaries are clear from current official sources, but public uptake data is uneven and I could not verify a current national dataset that cross-tabs intervention use by tenure, income source, ethnicity, and household composition.

## Evidence

### The hardship population is broader than any one intervention's eligibility gate

MBIE defines energy wellbeing as people, households, and whānau being able to obtain and afford adequate energy services for wellbeing in their home or kāinga, and defines energy hardship as the opposite of that situation. [MBIE, defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship)

MBIE's current monitoring uses five Household Economic Survey measures, including inability to pay electricity, gas, rates or water bills on time more than once, inability to afford to keep a dwelling adequately warm, putting up with feeling cold a lot, major damp or mould problems, and major heating problems. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

In the year ended June 2024, MBIE reported that 6.7% of households, about 132,000 households, could not afford to keep their homes adequately warm, while renters were three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy-hardship measures. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

MBIE also reported that households with Māori and Pacific peoples were two to three times more likely than the general population to experience energy-hardship measures, and that households that did not think they had enough income for everyday needs were three to five times more likely to report energy hardship than the general population. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

MBIE reported that households with a DEP-17 material-hardship score of 6 or more were four to seven times more likely than the general population to experience energy-hardship measures, which means any intervention map needs to distinguish general poverty relief, housing-quality remediation, and direct electricity-bill support. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

### Warmer Kiwi Homes reaches eligible owner-occupiers, not renters

EECA's current Warmer Kiwi Homes page says an insulation grant requires the person to own and live in a home built before 2008, have a Community Services Card or SuperGold Combo card or live in an area identified as low-to-middle income, and have a home that does not already have ceiling and underfloor insulation. [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/)

EECA's current heating-grant criteria are narrower: the person must own and live in a pre-2008 home, have a Community Services Card or SuperGold Combo card or live in an area identified as low income, have ceiling and underfloor insulation to EECA standards, and lack an operational fixed heater in any living area. [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/)

EECA says the grant can be used only for the home the applicant owns and lives in, and not for a bach, Airbnb, holiday home, rental, or home owned by a business. [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/)

EECA's 2024/25 annual report says Warmer Kiwi Homes completed 24,868 retrofits in 2024/25, made up of 17,755 insulation and 7,113 heating retrofits, and says contracted service providers have completed more than 175,000 insulation and heating installs since 2018. [EECA Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/assets/EECA-Resources/Corporate-documents/Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf)

EECA's annual report also states that low-income homeowner eligibility is determined by health referral, deprivation index, or Community Services Card, and that the applicant must be an owner-occupier of a house built before 2008. [EECA Annual Report 2024/25](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/assets/EECA-Resources/Corporate-documents/Annual-Report-2024-25.pdf)

That makes Warmer Kiwi Homes a strong overlap with low-income owner-occupiers in pre-2008 homes, but a structural non-overlap with the renter group MBIE identifies as three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy-hardship measures. [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024); [EECA, Warmer Kiwi Homes](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/co-funding-and-support/products/warmer-kiwi-homes-programme/information/)

### The Winter Energy Payment reaches income-support categories, not housing-quality need

Work and Income says the Winter Energy Payment is an extra payment for winter heating costs and that a person must be getting a main benefit, pension, or Jobseeker Support Student Hardship to receive it. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html)

Work and Income says eligible people do not need to apply and the payment is paid automatically from 1 May to 1 October every year; current weekly rates are $20.46 for single people with no dependent children and $31.82 for couples and people with dependent children. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html)

The 2025/26 Supplementary Estimates put the Winter Energy Payment appropriation at $600.696 million and describe it as paid to eligible people under Social Security Act criteria. [Budget 2026, Vote Social Development Supplementary Estimates](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/suppestimates/suppest26socdev.pdf)

This creates broad overlap with superannuitants, veterans' pension recipients, main-benefit recipients, and Jobseeker Support Student Hardship recipients, but it likely leaves gaps for working low-income households that are not in those categories, including some that are already associated with MBIE energy-hardship indicators. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html); [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)

The Winter Energy Payment also does not distinguish a warm, efficient owner-occupied dwelling from a cold rental or an uninsulated home, because public eligibility is tied to benefit or pension status rather than dwelling quality. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html); [MBIE, defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship)

### Retailer and community schemes are real, but fragmented and conditional

The Electricity Authority says that if a customer tells their power company they are having trouble paying, the company must help resolve payment difficulties by advising on power use, plans, payment options, payment support plans, and support services. [Electricity Authority, consumer care](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-youre-finding-it-hard-to-pay-your-bill/)

The Electricity Authority says power companies must take specified steps before considering disconnection for non-payment, and says a household with a registered or applying medically dependent consumer cannot be disconnected for non-payment. [Electricity Authority, consumer care](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-youre-finding-it-hard-to-pay-your-bill/)

EECA's public consumer page says most power companies have teams or funds for households in difficulty, says electricity providers must offer alternative payment options when they know a customer is struggling, and points consumers to the $110 Power Credits Scheme for some low-use-plan households. [EECA, help paying energy bills](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/save-on-your-energy-bills/need-help-paying-your-energy-bill/)

ERANZ's 2025 consumer hardship resource says EnergyMate households at risk of energy hardship can be referred by their power company, a participating budgeting support service, or participating Healthy Homes Initiative support services, but that in-home visits are available only in certain regions and centres. [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)

ERANZ reported in October 2024 that EnergyMate had helped more than 2,500 households since 2019, operated in 12 locations, and was testing a phone-based model with Debtfix because national in-person coverage was difficult. [ERANZ, EnergyMate and Debtfix, 2024](https://www.erganz.org.nz/news/energymate-and-debtfix-join-forces-to-tackle-energy-hardship)

ERANZ's 2025 resource describes the Power Credits Scheme as a retailer-and-lines-company scheme for hardship caused by removal of low fixed charges, available only through participating retailers, with a $110 credit and a maximum of two credits in 12 months where the second requires budgeting support from a financial mentor. [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)

Retailer-specific schemes can be substantial but are customer-base specific: Meridian says it committed $5 million to support 5,000 Meridian and Powershop households in hardship and had helped more than 3,000 households, while Contact says its Customer Wellbeing team provides personalised support and that its Good Initiative committed $5 million in the current year to direct and community support. [Meridian Energy, Energy Wellbeing](https://www.meridianenergy.co.nz/sustainability/customers/energy-wellbeing); [Contact Energy, Customer Wellbeing](https://contact.co.nz/wellbeing)

Work and Income's urgent power, gas, water, and heating help is broader than the Winter Energy Payment because a person does not have to be on a benefit, but it is an urgent-need pathway with citizenship/residence, income, and asset tests, usually requires talking to the power or water company first, and generally pays up to $200 for an outstanding bill or reconnection. [Work and Income, urgent power bills](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/living-expenses/heating-and-power-bills.html)

ERANZ's resource points households to MoneyTalks for free financial advice and connection to local financial mentoring services, CAB for rights and budgeting-service connections, curtain banks, Community Energy Network advice, and local council assistance, which confirms the existence of a community support layer but not a single national entitlement. [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A public dataset cross-tabbing Warmer Kiwi Homes, Winter Energy Payment, EnergyMate, Power Credits, MSD urgent electricity grants, and retailer hardship programme use by tenure, income source, ethnicity, household composition, region, and dwelling condition would change the confidence from Medium to High if it showed that the apparent gaps are being filled in practice. [MBIE says its energy-hardship measures are intended to help monitor targeting](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024)
- Evidence that landlords, Healthy Homes enforcement, or a rental-facing retrofit programme now reaches most cold rental households in energy hardship would narrow the renter-gap conclusion; I found tenant rights and Healthy Homes standards references, but not national uptake evidence showing cold rentals in energy hardship are being retrofitted at scale. [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)
- Current official Winter Energy Payment recipient counts by household type and tenure would sharpen the overlap map; I found current eligibility, rates, and appropriation data, but not a current public recipient table cross-tabbed against energy-hardship groups. [Work and Income, Winter Energy Payment](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/winter-energy-payment.html); [Budget 2026, Vote Social Development Supplementary Estimates](https://budget.govt.nz/budget/pdfs/suppestimates/suppest26socdev.pdf)
- Retailer-level hardship programme disclosures with consistent definitions, acceptance rates, wait times, debt outcomes, disconnection outcomes, and demographic/tenure breakdowns would change the patchwork conclusion if they showed national, equitable coverage across hardship groups. [Electricity Authority, consumer care](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-care-obligations/if-youre-finding-it-hard-to-pay-your-bill/)
- I could not verify a current, public, national count of unique households helped across retailer schemes without double-counting households that also received EnergyMate, MoneyTalks, Work and Income assistance, or community support. [ERANZ hardship resource, 2025](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)
- A human reviewer with frontline financial-mentoring, tenancy, Māori/Pacific community provider, or retailer hardship experience should test whether the public eligibility map matches how households actually navigate support. [FinCap/MoneyTalks is listed as a support route in ERANZ's consumer resource](https://www.erganz.org.nz/assets/documents/250214-ERANZ-Having-trouble-paying-your-power-bill-2025.pdf)

## Open follow-up questions

- What current, aggregate data do financial mentors, MoneyTalks, CAB, EnergyMate, and retailer hardship teams hold on energy-hardship households by tenure, region, and ethnicity, and can it be shared safely without identifying households?
- How many rental households experiencing MBIE energy-hardship measures are in homes that fail, barely meet, or cannot practically benefit from current Healthy Homes standards?
- What would be the highest-leverage small-team intervention for structurally excluded renters: eligibility navigation, landlord/tenant standards enforcement support, referral triage, or a local retrofit/curtain-bank partnership map?
