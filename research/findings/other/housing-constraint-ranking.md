# Infrastructure funding and servicing is the hardest national housing-supply constraint, with land rules close behind and consent processing less binding

## Executive answer

- **Rank 1: infrastructure funding and servicing capacity binds hardest at national policy level.** HUD's Going for Housing Growth programme makes development capacity depend on being infrastructure-ready, and its infrastructure-funding RIS says the current development-contributions system was designed for predictable growth and has lost cost-recovery effectiveness as growth patterns became less predictable [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [HUD, GfHG infrastructure-funding RIS](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Going-for-Housing-Growth/Proactive/DOC-7-Regulatory-Impact-Statement-Improving-Local-Government-Infrastructure-Funding-Settings-_Redacted.pdf).
- **Rank 2: zoned/developable land is a binding upstream constraint, but it is being directly targeted by current reforms.** HUD's land-use RIS says the key barrier has been an ongoing shortage of developable urban land, with Auckland fringe urban land costing 4.4 times nearby rural land, while current GfHG policy requires Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible capacity using high population-growth projections [HUD/MfE, GfHG land-use RIS](https://www.regulation.govt.nz/assets/RIS-Documents/ris-hud-gfhg-jun24.pdf); [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- **Rank 3: development finance and demand are currently a market-cycle constraint, not the deepest structural one.** MBIE's 2025 pipeline says building costs remain well above pre-pandemic levels and affect project viability, while the same report forecasts residential consent growth from 33,500 new dwellings in 2025 to 40,000 by 2030 as the sector recovers [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025); [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report).
- **Rank 4: construction capacity is material but not the current national bottleneck.** MBIE forecasts total construction activity troughing at $55.7b in 2025 before rising to $65.4b by 2030, and it describes workloads as tight because consent activity is down from the 2021-22 highs rather than because the sector is already over capacity [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report); [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025).
- **Rank 5: building-consent processing is real friction but not the hardest national constraint on current public data.** MBIE's Q1 2026 monitoring says all BCAs had median processing times under 20 working days, 95.5% of all applications were processed within statutory timeframes, and 95.9% of residential applications were processed within statutory timeframes, although total elapsed time remains longer where RFIs stop the clock [MBIE, Building Consent System Performance Monitoring](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/building-system-insights-programme/building-consent-system-performance-monitoring).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high that infrastructure funding/servicing, land-use rules, finance/demand, construction capacity, and consent processing are all real constraints; medium for the ranking because public evidence is stronger at national-policy level than for causal weight by council, developer type, and project stage.

## Evidence

### Ranking method

I interpreted "binds hardest" as the constraint most likely to stop consented or enabled capacity becoming buildable housing across New Zealand in 2026, not the constraint that annoys applicants most on an individual project [analysis framing for stream #435](../../../analysis/nz-housing-supply-vs-actual-building-consents-why-framing.md). I weighted a constraint higher where public evidence showed it affects both greenfield and brownfield capacity, appears in current central-government reform design, and explains why planning capacity can fail to become serviced, financeable, consented, and completed homes [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan](https://media.umbraco.io/te-waihanga-30-year-strategy/zsunnlry/national-infrastructure-plan-03032026.pdf).

### Infrastructure funding and servicing ranks first

HUD's current GfHG page says development capacity must be infrastructure-ready: short-term capacity requires adequate existing infrastructure, medium-term capacity can rely on funding for adequate infrastructure identified in a long-term plan, and long-term capacity can rely on infrastructure identified in a council infrastructure strategy [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). The same page says Pillar 2 is intended to give councils and developers a flexible funding and financing toolkit for infrastructure needed to support land zoned for housing development [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).

The infrastructure-funding RIS explains why this is not just a finance-admin problem: to recover development contributions accurately, councils need to anticipate development location and size, fund non-growth costs, finance growth costs, predict asset costs, and amend their development-contributions policy before the consent or connection application is received [HUD, GfHG infrastructure-funding RIS](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Going-for-Housing-Growth/Proactive/DOC-7-Regulatory-Impact-Statement-Improving-Local-Government-Infrastructure-Funding-Settings-_Redacted.pdf). The RIS says changes in the operating environment and governing legislation have eroded development contributions' ability to recover costs, and the preferred levy option would let councils recover a larger share of aggregate infrastructure growth costs across an area [HUD, GfHG infrastructure-funding RIS](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Going-for-Housing-Growth/Proactive/DOC-7-Regulatory-Impact-Statement-Improving-Local-Government-Infrastructure-Funding-Settings-_Redacted.pdf).

Te Waihanga's National Infrastructure Plan reinforces the same constraint from outside HUD: it says infrastructure bottlenecks are most likely when rapid population growth and urbanisation move faster than existing networks, and it recommends publishing spatial models of the long-run infrastructure costs of servicing growth plus water-asset information on condition and growth capacity [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan](https://media.umbraco.io/te-waihanga-30-year-strategy/zsunnlry/national-infrastructure-plan-03032026.pdf). That makes infrastructure funding and servicing the first-ranked national constraint because it can invalidate otherwise-zoned capacity and because both central-government housing policy and independent infrastructure planning are reforming around it [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Te Waihanga, National Infrastructure Plan](https://media.umbraco.io/te-waihanga-30-year-strategy/zsunnlry/national-infrastructure-plan-03032026.pdf).

Council material is consistent with the national diagnosis. Auckland Council's 2025 development-contributions policy says growth-related infrastructure costs should be shared between developers and ratepayers, while the adopted policy supports a 30-year growth-infrastructure plan in investment-priority areas [Auckland Council, new Development Contributions Policy](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/05/new-development-contributions-policy-approved/). QLDC's development-contributions page shows that development contributions can be triggered by land-use consent, subdivision consent, building consent, or utility-service connection, which illustrates how growth infrastructure costs attach across the delivery chain rather than only at zoning [QLDC, Development contributions](https://www.qldc.govt.nz/services/resource-consents/before-you-apply/development-contributions/).

### Zoned/developable land ranks second

HUD/MfE's land-use RIS says GfHG is intended to create more competitive urban land markets by making land supply and development capacity more responsive, and it identifies an ongoing shortage of developable urban land as a key barrier to increasing housing supply [HUD/MfE, GfHG land-use RIS](https://www.regulation.govt.nz/assets/RIS-Documents/ris-hud-gfhg-jun24.pdf). The same RIS cites Te Waihanga evidence that each square metre of urban land at Auckland's fringe costs 4.4 times nearby rural land and says that evidence suggests the zoning premium doubled between 2011 and 2021 [HUD/MfE, GfHG land-use RIS](https://www.regulation.govt.nz/assets/RIS-Documents/ris-hud-gfhg-jun24.pdf).

Current GfHG policy responds directly to land constraints by requiring Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity using high population-growth projections, prohibiting rural-urban boundary lines in planning documents, requiring appropriate density across Tier 1 urban areas, and requiring capacity offsets where capacity is lost for reasons such as special character [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). I rank land below infrastructure because HUD's own definition of capacity requires infrastructure-readiness and because a zoned capacity surplus can still fail if the network funding and staging are not in place [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [HUD, GfHG infrastructure-funding RIS](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Going-for-Housing-Growth/Proactive/DOC-7-Regulatory-Impact-Statement-Improving-Local-Government-Infrastructure-Funding-Settings-_Redacted.pdf).

### Development finance and demand rank third

MBIE's 2025 National Construction Pipeline Report says residential building-cost inflation cooled to 2.5% in 2024 from 14% in 2021 and 13.1% in 2022, but also says building costs remain well above pre-pandemic levels and continue to affect project viability where building values have not kept up [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025). The same report says lower interest rates and special home-loan rates under 5% had improved access to finance, while migration had slowed sharply through 2024 before being forecast to increase through the forecast period [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025).

This constraint is more cyclical than infrastructure or land. MBIE's summary forecasts total construction activity decreasing to $55.7b in 2025, down from $58.1b in 2024 and $63b in 2023, then rising to $65.4b by 2030 as residential activity recovers [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report). MBIE also forecasts new dwelling consents increasing from 33,500 in 2025 to 40,000 by 2030, with 115,000 of 215,000 new dwellings over 2025-2030 being multi-unit consents [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/supporting-a-skilled-and-productive-workforce/national-construction-pipeline-report).

### Construction capacity ranks fourth

Construction capacity matters because MBIE's pipeline is explicitly intended to help the sector plan skills, capital equipment, and procurement, and MBIE says boom-bust cycles have harmed productivity, innovation, employment, skill levels, and quality in the construction sector [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025). However, the 2025 report describes construction workloads as tight with consent activity well down from the 2021-22 highs, and it forecasts a gradual recovery rather than reporting that residential construction is already blocked by a present national labour ceiling [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025).

Stats NZ's construction-industry note also points to a softer labour market rather than a binding national labour shortage at the time of writing: it says around 87% of the fall in filled construction jobs was from men, and that construction prices were up but slower than prices in all industries [Stats NZ, Building New Zealand: Focus on the construction industry](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/building-new-zealand-focus-on-the-construction-industry/). I treat this as a secondary source for direction only because the page did not expose detailed tables through the quick web extractor during this run; the primary ranking relies more heavily on MBIE's pipeline report [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025).

### Consent processing ranks fifth

Building-consent processing is measurable and imperfect, but current MBIE data does not support ranking it as the hardest national constraint. MBIE's Q1 2026 monitoring says the overall median statutory processing time was nine working days, all BCAs had median processing time under 20 working days, and 95.5% of all applications were processed within the statutory period [MBIE, Building Consent System Performance Monitoring](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/building-system-insights-programme/building-consent-system-performance-monitoring). For building-consent applications specifically, MBIE says 94.5% were processed within statutory timeframes with a median processing time of 13 working days; for residential applications, 95.9% were processed within statutory timeframes [MBIE, Building Consent System Performance Monitoring](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/building-system-insights-programme/building-consent-system-performance-monitoring).

This does not mean consenting is harmless. MBIE says total elapsed time includes both BCA processing time and applicant response time when an RFI is made, and Q1 2026 overall median elapsed time was 16 working days compared with a nine-working-day statutory processing median [MBIE, Building Consent System Performance Monitoring](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/building-system-insights-programme/building-consent-system-performance-monitoring). MfE's 2024/25 National Monitoring System summary says councils increased use of RMA section 37 waiver/extension powers, decreased use of section 92 further-information requests, and decreased median statutory processing time for new resource consents, which indicates resource-consent administration remains material but mixed rather than plainly dominant [MfE, National Monitoring System](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/rma/national-monitoring-system/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current council-by-council dataset linking enabled capacity, infrastructure-ready capacity, consent applications, approved consents, RFIs, starts, completions, and sale/rent outcomes could change the ranking from a national-policy ranking to a stronger causal ranking by place [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Stats NZ, building-consents CSV catalogue result found by stats-nz skill](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).
- Evidence that the new development-levy, IFF, Local Water Done Well, and council-incentive changes quickly unlock serviced capacity would lower infrastructure funding/servicing in the ranking [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).
- Evidence that high-growth councils meet infrastructure-ready capacity targets but still do not get viable development would move development finance/demand above infrastructure [HUD, GfHG infrastructure-funding RIS](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Going-for-Housing-Growth/Proactive/DOC-7-Regulatory-Impact-Statement-Improving-Local-Government-Infrastructure-Funding-Settings-_Redacted.pdf); [MBIE, National Construction Pipeline Report 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31693-national-construction-pipeline-report-2025).
- Evidence that residential building-consent or resource-consent elapsed time is the main reason viable, serviced, zoned projects do not proceed would move consent processing higher; MBIE's current national building-consent monitoring does not show that [MBIE, Building Consent System Performance Monitoring](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/building-system-insights-programme/building-consent-system-performance-monitoring); [MfE, National Monitoring System](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/rma/national-monitoring-system/).
- I could not verify a current public national dataset of actual dwelling starts or completions by project and council, nor a public dataset tying development-finance rejections to specific infrastructure, land, or consent-processing reasons; those gaps likely require council/developer data, an OIA, or a human with housing-development finance expertise.
