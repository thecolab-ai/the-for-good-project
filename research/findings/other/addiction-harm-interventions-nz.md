# Alcohol price and availability controls, stimulant contingency management, and gambling supply controls are the strongest evidence-backed harm levers for New Zealand

## Executive answer

- **Strongest overall evidence:** alcohol price controls, especially excise/minimum-pricing/discount controls, have the strongest cross-population evidence among the interventions reviewed; WHO describes pricing and taxation as among the most effective and cost-effective alcohol measures, and Scotland's minimum unit pricing evaluation estimated reductions in alcohol-attributable deaths, hospital admissions, population sales and health inequality markers. [WHO SAFER pricing](https://www.who.int/initiatives/SAFER/pricing-policies); [Public Health Scotland MUP evaluation summary](https://publichealthscotland.scot/news/2023/june/minimum-unit-pricing-reduces-alcohol-related-harm-to-health/)
- **Most realistic alcohol lever in New Zealand now:** local alcohol policies are more immediately available than a national pricing reform because the 2023 community-participation amendments removed appeals against provisional local alcohol policies, widened objections and removed cross-examination in licensing hearings. [Beehive, 24 Aug 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Community Participation) Amendment Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/60/en/latest/)
- **Strongest methamphetamine treatment lever:** contingency management should be treated as the first candidate for any NZ service expansion or pilot because a systematic review of reviews found sufficient evidence for contingency management in stimulant-use disorder, a methamphetamine-specific systematic review supported adoption in outpatient programmes, and the ASAM/AAAP stimulant guideline makes contingency management a primary treatment component with high-certainty/strong recommendation. [Ronsley et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0234809); [Brown and DeFulio, 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0376871620304725); [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf)
- **Least settled methamphetamine lever:** stimulant prescribing or "substitution" pathways are not ready as a general NZ policy without specialist clinical governance because review evidence remains mixed or low-certainty, and the ASAM/AAAP guideline limits psychostimulant prescribing for stimulant-use disorder to addiction-trained physicians with close monitoring. [Ronsley et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0234809); [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf)
- **Strongest gambling direction:** shift from voluntary individual tools toward supply, product, advertising and enforcement controls; gambling reviews find weaker evidence for individual harm-minimisation tools than the policy debate often implies, while DIA's 2023 strengthened class 4 rules and the 2026 online-casino regime give New Zealand implementable regulatory hooks. [McMahon et al., 2019](https://knowledge.lancashire.ac.uk/id/eprint/25299/); [Blank et al., 2021](https://www.drugsandalcohol.ie/33679/); [DIA, Minimising gambling harm](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Minimising-Gambling-Harm); [DIA, Online casino gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - confidence is High for alcohol pricing evidence and stimulant contingency management, Medium for alcohol local-availability controls and gambling supply controls, and Low-Medium for stimulant prescribing, drug-checking effects on methamphetamine harm, and online-casino licensing effects before the regime is fully operational in 2027. [WHO SAFER pricing](https://www.who.int/initiatives/SAFER/pricing-policies); [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf); [DIA online provider timeline](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers)

## Evidence

### Scope and grading method

I answered the issue as a comparative implementation question: which interventions have credible evidence of reducing alcohol, methamphetamine or gambling harm, and which could plausibly be used in New Zealand under current or near-current policy settings. [Issue #513](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/513); [stream framing](../../../analysis/nz-addiction-and-substance-harm-alcohol-methamphet-framing.md)

The grades below use **High / Medium / Low** for effectiveness and equity evidence, **Low / Medium / High** for implementation cost, **High / Medium / Low** for political feasibility, and **Fast / Medium / Slow** for time-to-first-useful-result. These are evidence-weighted judgements, not modelled benefit-cost estimates, because the reviewed sources use different outcomes, time periods and implementation contexts. [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728); [Blank et al., 2021](https://www.drugsandalcohol.ie/33679/)

I checked the vendored NZ data skills before broad web search: `data-govt-nz` surfaced the Ministry of Health gambling-harm intervention-services dataset and DIA class 4 machine/venue datasets, but those datasets describe service activity and licensed gambling infrastructure rather than intervention-effectiveness evidence, so they inform feasibility/context rather than the effectiveness grades. [data.govt.nz gambling intervention-services dataset](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/gambling-harm-intervention-services-data); [data.govt.nz class 4 venue and machine dataset](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/class-4-gambling-venue-and-gaming-machine-numbers-quarterly-lists)

### What this means for New Zealand prioritisation

If a national decision-maker wants the largest evidence-backed harm reduction, alcohol price controls should be first on the alcohol list, but political feasibility is weaker than evidence strength because I found no current central-government MUP or excise-rise proposal in the 2026-2029 substance-harm action plan. [WHO SAFER pricing](https://www.who.int/initiatives/SAFER/pricing-policies); [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029)

If a council, iwi/community coalition or public-health agency wants an actionable alcohol pathway now, local alcohol policies are the more realistic route because the 2023 law removed appeal and hearing barriers that the Government identified as blocking community influence over where and when alcohol is sold. [Beehive, 24 Aug 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Sale and Supply of Alcohol (Community Participation) Amendment Act 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2023/60/en/latest/)

For methamphetamine, the strongest service-design conclusion is to test and scale contingency management inside addiction services rather than wait for a pharmacotherapy breakthrough, because the treatment-review evidence and clinical guideline agree that contingency management has the best support while stimulant prescribing remains off-label/specialist and lower-certainty. [Ronsley et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0234809); [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf)

Drug checking is realistic and already legal in New Zealand, but it should be framed as a fast harm-reduction and early-warning layer rather than the primary answer to methamphetamine dependence, because the Ministry describes drug checking as testing, interpretation and harm-reduction information rather than treatment. [Ministry of Health drug checking](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/drug-checking); [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029)

For gambling, the practical near-term NZ path is enforcement and improvement of existing class 4/casino harm-minimisation duties plus careful implementation of online-casino licensing, but the stronger long-term policy direction is structural regulation of supply, advertising and product risk rather than relying on self-exclusion, signage or voluntary limit-setting alone. [DIA, Minimising gambling harm](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Minimising-Gambling-Harm); [DIA, Online casino gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling); [McMahon et al., 2019](https://knowledge.lancashire.ac.uk/id/eprint/25299/); [Blank et al., 2021](https://www.drugsandalcohol.ie/33679/)

A small volunteer project should not try to deliver clinical methamphetamine treatment, prescribe stimulants, regulate alcohol prices, or police online casinos, because those require statutory authority, clinical governance, or regulator powers; a volunteer project could more safely support evidence translation, service navigation, public data dashboards, local alcohol policy submissions, drug-checking/early-warning communications, or evaluation tooling that accountable agencies and community providers choose to use. [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728); [DIA online provider timeline](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers)

## What would change this conclusion

- A current NZ Cabinet decision to pursue alcohol excise reform, minimum unit pricing, or national discount controls would raise the political-feasibility grade for alcohol price policy; I did not verify such a proposal in the current 2026-2029 substance-harm action plan. [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029)
- NZ outcome data showing local alcohol policies reduce hospitalisation, assaults, family harm or hazardous drinking after the 2023 amendments would raise the local-alcohol-policy effectiveness grade from Medium-High to High. [Beehive, 24 Aug 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [PHCC LAP briefing](https://www.phcc.org.nz/briefing/local-alcohol-policies-whats-opportunity-reduce-harm)
- A New Zealand contingency-management pilot with audited outcomes by ethnicity, region, retention, abstinence, whānau impact and adverse events would raise confidence in NZ implementation and equity; I could not verify public NZ contingency-management outcome data at scale. [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf); [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029)
- A high-quality NZ or Australia/NZ trial of psychostimulant prescribing with strong retention, safety monitoring, diversion controls and sustained outcomes would change the stimulant-prescribing grade; present sources support only cautious specialist/off-label consideration. [Ronsley et al., 2020](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0234809); [Sharafi et al., 2023](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/add.16347); [ASAM/AAAP guideline, 2024](https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/156927/cdc_156927_DS1.pdf)
- Post-2027 evidence that the Online Casino Gambling Act reduces harm without shifting high-risk gamblers to unlicensed operators would raise the online-casino grade; pre-2027 claims about harm reduction should remain provisional. [DIA, Online casino gambling](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Casino-Gambling); [DIA online provider timeline](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Online-Gambling-for-Providers)
- Human review is needed before G1 prioritisation because lived-experience, Māori health, Pacific health, clinical-addiction, council, enforcement and gambling-harm provider knowledge could change feasibility and equity judgements that public documents cannot settle. [Ministry of Health substance harm action plan](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/action-plan-to-prevent-and-reduce-substance-harm-2026-2029); [Ministry of Health gambling harm strategy](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/strategy-to-prevent-and-minimise-gambling-harm-202526-to-202728)
