# NZ Super is broad and low-earner-protective, not an OECD generosity outlier for average earners

## Executive answer

- **The corrected generosity claim holds:** OECD 2025 puts New Zealand's modelled net replacement rate for an average earner at 43.8%, well below the OECD average of 63.2%; the same table puts New Zealand higher for low earners at 67.0%, but still below the OECD low-earner average of 75.4% [OECD, net pension replacement rates](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html).
- **New Zealand is unusual in design, not in average-earner generosity:** OECD classifies New Zealand as having a residence-based basic first-tier pension and no mandatory second-tier pension, while the Retirement Commission describes NZ Super as a non-means-tested cash pension for people aged 65+ with residence-based eligibility criteria [OECD, structure of retirement-income provision](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/structure-of-retirement-income-provision_14694649.html); [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super).
- **Australia is the closest comparator but a different policy bargain:** Australia combines a targeted Age Pension at age 67 with mandatory private superannuation; OECD gives Australia a higher average-earner net replacement rate than New Zealand, 53.0% vs 43.8%, and lower latest public cash pension expenditure, 3.4% of GDP vs New Zealand's 5.1% [Services Australia, Age Pension](https://www.servicesaustralia.gov.au/age-pension); [OECD, net pension replacement rates](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html); [OECD, public expenditure on pensions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/public-expenditure-on-pensions_ddc9a2dd.html).
- **The poverty-protection story is mixed:** NZ Super is explicitly a tier-1 poverty-protection benefit, but OECD 2025 reports New Zealand's over-65 relative income poverty at 33.7%, compared with 14.8% across the OECD and 22.6% in Australia; OECD also warns that country differences in older people's housing and other wealth may not be reflected in income-poverty rates [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super); [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html).
- **New Zealand is not a current public-cost outlier, but unchanged settings still matter:** OECD's latest public cash old-age and survivor benefit table puts New Zealand at 5.1% of GDP, below the OECD average of 8.1%, while OECD's long-term projection table puts New Zealand at 5.3% in 2025 and 6.5% in 2050, below OECD32 averages of 9.0% and 10.0% [OECD, public expenditure on pensions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/public-expenditure-on-pensions_ddc9a2dd.html); [OECD, long-term projections](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - OECD 2025 and official NZ/Australian sources strongly support the comparative direction, but the poverty conclusion is only medium confidence because OECD's relative-income measure does not fully capture housing wealth, housing costs, material hardship, or in-kind support.

### Generosity and adequacy

OECD replacement-rate modelling compares future pensions for a single worker entering the labour market at age 22 in 2024 under legislated rules, with common economic assumptions across countries, so it is a pension-system design comparison rather than a forecast of actual future household income [OECD, gross pension replacement rates](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/gross-pension-replacement-rates_95e3eed6.html).

On that measure, New Zealand is not generous for average earners: its modelled 43.8% net replacement rate is below Australia at 53.0% and below the OECD average of 63.2% [OECD, net pension replacement rates](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html). New Zealand's flat-rate design is more protective at the bottom than the middle or top: OECD gives New Zealand a 67.0% low-earner net replacement rate and a 23.7% high-earner rate, while Australia is 82.7% for low earners and 38.1% for high earners [OECD, net pension replacement rates](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/net-pension-replacement-rates_a7a9e376.html).

The Retirement Commission's framing fits the OECD taxonomy: NZ Super is a tier-1 benefit intended to protect against poverty in old age, while tier 2 aims at retirement-income adequacy and tier 3 at raising individual replacement rates [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super). OECD's structure table makes the design gap explicit: only Ireland and New Zealand lack mandatory second-tier pensions, and New Zealand appears as a residence-based basic system rather than an earnings-related mandatory public or private system [OECD, structure of retirement-income provision](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/structure-of-retirement-income-provision_14694649.html).

### Targeting and the Australia comparison

New Zealand's first-tier pension is broad: the Retirement Commission says NZ Super is not means-tested, and Work and Income's 2026 rate table pays standard fortnightly rates by living situation and couple status rather than by income or assets [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super); [Work and Income, 2026 benefit rates](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/benefit-rates/benefit-rates-april-2026.html).

Australia's Age Pension is targeted: Services Australia says applicants must be 67 or older, under income and assets test limits, and normally an Australian resident for at least 10 years [Services Australia, Age Pension](https://www.servicesaustralia.gov.au/age-pension). Australia also has a mandatory private pillar: the Australian Taxation Office says the super guarantee rate increased to 12% from 1 July 2025, while OECD's 2025 table still reports Australia's 2024 mandatory contribution rate at 11.5% [Australian Taxation Office, super guarantee](https://www.ato.gov.au/tax-rates-and-codes/key-superannuation-rates-and-thresholds/super-guarantee); [OECD, mandatory pension contributions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/finances-of-retirement-income-systems_a2470cd2.html).

The tradeoff is visible in OECD public and private spending tables: Australia has lower latest public cash old-age and survivor spending than New Zealand, 3.4% vs 5.1% of GDP, but materially higher private pension-benefit expenditure, 4.5% of GDP for Australia while OECD records no comparable private-pension expenditure value for New Zealand in Table 8.3 [OECD, public expenditure on pensions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/public-expenditure-on-pensions_ddc9a2dd.html); [OECD, private pension-benefit expenditures](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/private-expenditure-on-pensions_76511fb3.html).

### Pension age and work after 65

New Zealand's current normal pension age is 65, below Australia's 67 and close to the OECD 2024 average of 64.7 for men and 63.9 for women [OECD, current retirement ages](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_e40274c1-en/full-report/current-retirement-ages_0f63b747.html). The OECD table records no early-retirement option for New Zealand's basic pension, while Australia has no early option for the targeted Age Pension but does have private funded superannuation access from age 55 in the 2024 table [OECD, current retirement ages](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_e40274c1-en/full-report/current-retirement-ages_0f63b747.html).

New Zealand also has relatively high labour-market attachment after 65: OECD reports a 2024 effective labour-market exit age of 66.9 for both New Zealand men and women, compared with OECD averages of 64.7 for men and 63.6 for women [OECD, effective age of labour market exit](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/effective-age-of-labour-market-exit_30d6a384.html). This does not prove NZ Super is adequate, because some people work longer by choice while others work because income or housing costs require it; the finding did not separate those reasons [OECD, effective age of labour market exit](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/effective-age-of-labour-market-exit_30d6a384.html).

### Poverty protection

OECD's old-age poverty measure is relative income poverty: income below 50% of median equivalised household disposable income in that country [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html). On that measure, New Zealand looks weak despite universal NZ Super: OECD reports over-65 poverty at 33.7% in New Zealand, 22.6% in Australia, and 14.8% on average across the OECD [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html).

This is a surprising result and should not be overread as a complete material-hardship ranking. OECD explicitly says income data may not reflect considerable country differences in older people's housing or other wealth, and Stats NZ's household income release is a national income/housing-cost source rather than an OECD-harmonised old-age poverty ranking [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html); [Stats NZ, household income and housing-cost statistics 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).

The practical interpretation is that NZ Super's broad floor protects many people from having no public pension, but it does not guarantee that older households sit above a moving relative-income poverty line, especially where rent, single-person household costs, disability costs, or lack of private savings matter [Retirement Commission, NZ Super](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/nz-super); [NZ Council of Christian Social Services, Older Persons Poverty Monitor engagement document](https://nzccss.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Older-Persons-Poverty-Monitor-Engagement-Document-Published-Version-1-1.pdf).

### Fiscal cost

New Zealand's current public pension cost is below the OECD average in the OECD cash-benefit table: latest public cash old-age and survivor spending is 5.1% of GDP for New Zealand, 3.4% for Australia, and 8.1% for the OECD [OECD, public expenditure on pensions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/public-expenditure-on-pensions_ddc9a2dd.html). OECD's long-term public-pension expenditure projection also puts New Zealand below the OECD32 average, at 5.3% of GDP in 2025 and 6.5% in 2050, compared with OECD32 averages of 9.0% and 10.0% [OECD, long-term projections](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html).

That below-average OECD fiscal position does not mean there is no sustainability issue. The #440 framing separately records Treasury's current-policy pressure path, and this finding's narrower OECD comparison only shows that New Zealand is not already a high-spending OECD pension outlier [analysis #440 framing](../../../analysis/nz-superannuation-fiscal-sustainability-how-much-i-framing.md); [OECD, long-term projections](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/long-term-projections-of-public-pension-expenditure_af4ed734.html).

## What would change this conclusion

- A newer OECD pensions release or corrected StatLink workbook that materially changes New Zealand's replacement rates, old-age poverty rate, or pension-expenditure tables would change the comparative conclusion [OECD, Pensions at a Glance 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4.html).
- A harmonised old-age material-hardship or after-housing-cost poverty comparison across New Zealand, Australia, and the OECD would improve the poverty-protection section; OECD's relative-income measure is useful but incomplete for countries where home ownership and housing costs are central to older people's living standards [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html); [Stats NZ, household income and housing-cost statistics 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).
- A Treasury or Retirement Commission reconciliation of NZ Super's net-of-tax cost, the NZ Super Fund contribution path, and OECD's gross public-expenditure series would sharpen the fiscal comparison; this finding uses OECD's cross-country definitions rather than re-estimating New Zealand's net fiscal burden [OECD, public expenditure on pensions](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/public-expenditure-on-pensions_ddc9a2dd.html).
- I could not verify a current independent cross-country source using the same 2025 OECD old-age poverty definition, so that claim should be cited specifically as an OECD indicator rather than as a complete judgement on material hardship [OECD, old-age income poverty](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html).
- This finding did not include interviews with older people, disability advocates, renters, Maori or Pacific organisations, or retirement-income officials; lived experience and administrative microdata are needed before using the OECD profile to choose a reform path [Retirement Commission, 2025 Review of Retirement Income Policies](https://retirement.govt.nz/policy-and-research/2025-review-of-retirement-income-policies).
