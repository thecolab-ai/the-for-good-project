# NZ energy hardship evidence supports multiple interacting drivers, not a clean percentage split

## Executive answer

This answers issue #327 by comparing the NZ evidence for four proposed drivers of household energy hardship: poor housing stock, electricity pricing and tariffs, income inadequacy, and the landlord/tenant split incentive [issue #327](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/327).

- I did **not** find NZ-specific public evidence that apportions energy hardship into shares such as "housing stock explains X%, prices Y%, income Z%"; MBIE's framework and official measures instead treat energy hardship as multi-dimensional and interacting across household resources, dwelling characteristics, household circumstances, energy supply, energy prices, and related factors [MBIE, defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship), [MBIE, components of the energy wellbeing framework](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/21645-components-of-the-energy-wellbeing-framework-pdf), [Stats NZ, investigating energy hardship measures](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Retirement-of-archive-website-project-files/Reports/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand/Download-data/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand.pdf).
- **Income inadequacy looks like the broadest cross-cutting constraint**, because MBIE says low-income households are more likely to experience energy hardship, income determines ability to afford both adequate housing and sufficient energy, and 2024 HES measures show affordability indicators such as not being able to keep the dwelling adequately warm and paying utility bills late are nationally material [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).
- **Poor housing stock is strongly evidenced, especially for renters**, because BRANZ's tenure comparison found rental houses in poorer condition than owner-occupied houses, Stats NZ/Environmental Health Intelligence NZ 2023 Census analysis found dampness and mould more common in rented dwellings across all health districts, and insulation interventions have causal evidence for warmer/drier homes and health benefits [BRANZ, 2015 House Condition Survey tenure report](https://d39d3mj7qio96p.cloudfront.net/media/documents/SR370_2015_House_Condition_Survey.pdf), [EHINZ, dampness and mould by tenure](https://www.ehinz.ac.nz/assets/Surveillance-reports/Released_2025/Dampness-and-mould-in-New-Zealand-households-a-tenure-based-analysis.pdf), [He Kāinga Oranga, Housing, Insulation and Health Study](https://www.healthyhousing.org.nz/our-research/past-research/housing-insulation-and-health-study).
- **Electricity pricing and tariff structure matter, but the current NZ public evidence is stronger on mechanisms and vulnerable subgroups than on total attribution**: Consumer NZ found prepay customers could pay materially more than comparable postpay customers, the Electricity Authority says prepay may limit access to plan types such as time-of-use plans, and official dashboards now track prepay and postpay disconnections because disconnection patterns are consumer-care evidence [Consumer NZ, prepay customers paying more](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/home-energy/prepay-customers-paying-much-more-for-power), [Electricity Authority, consumer access and choice](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-access-and-choice/), [Electricity Authority, disconnections dashboard](https://www.ea.govt.nz/data-and-insights/charts-and-dashboards/disconnections-for-non-payment/).
- **The landlord/tenant split incentive is a real mechanism, but I found no NZ evidence quantifying its marginal contribution separately from the renter housing-quality gap**; NZ legal/economic literature describes the principal-agent problem directly, and rental-sector minimum standards partly respond to that problem, but it should be treated as a mechanism behind rental housing quality rather than as an independently measured share of hardship [Barton, Energy Efficiency and Rental Accommodation](https://www.otago.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0026/329264/-energy-efficiency-and-rental-accommodation-dealing-with-split-incentives-055632.pdf), [Motu, Warming Up New Zealand metered energy use](https://www.motu.nz/assets/Documents/our-work/urban-and-regional/housing/Warming-Up-New-Zealand-Impacts-of-the-New-Zealand-Insulation-Fund-on-Metered-Household-Energy-Use.pdf), [Tenancy Services, healthy homes compliance](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/healthy-homes-compliance/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction and mechanisms are well supported by official NZ sources and peer-reviewed/research evidence, but the central "relative contribution" question cannot be answered with a clean NZ apportionment from the public evidence found here.

## Evidence

### The best NZ answer is "interacting drivers", not a percentage split

MBIE defines energy hardship as the opposite of energy wellbeing: when individuals, households and whanau are not able to obtain and afford adequate energy services to support wellbeing in their home or kainga [MBIE, defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship). MBIE's framework says people and dwellings sit at the centre and are surrounded by factors that affect energy wellbeing, with factors interacting so individual households can experience hardship differently [MBIE, components of the energy wellbeing framework](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/21645-components-of-the-energy-wellbeing-framework-pdf).

MBIE's current monitoring uses five Household Economic Survey measures rather than a single causal model: paying electricity/gas/rates/water bills late more than once, inability to afford adequate warmth, putting up with feeling cold a lot to keep costs down, dampness and/or mould as a major problem, and trouble heating or keeping accommodation warm in winter [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). MBIE says there is no single measure because energy hardship has multiple dimensions and experiences, and it uses subjective HES responses because lived experience cannot be captured by only looking at incomes or energy costs [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

Older Stats NZ work used the same conceptual triad: energy hardship occurs through a relationship between housing quality, energy prices, and household incomes, and the report then examined prices, income shares, and changing use rather than assigning causal percentages to each driver [Stats NZ, investigating different measures of energy hardship](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Retirement-of-archive-website-project-files/Reports/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand/Download-data/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand.pdf). MBIE's 2022 submissions summary also shows why a single weighting is contested: submitters variously argued that income, dwelling characteristics, energy prices, energy companies, and geography deserved more weight [MBIE, defining energy hardship submissions summary](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/defining-energy-hardship-summary-of-submissions.pdf).

**Confidence: High** that public NZ sources support multiple interacting drivers and **Low** that they currently support a clean apportionment across the four issue categories.

### Income inadequacy is the broadest constraint

In the year ended June 2024, MBIE reported that 6.7% of households, or 132,000 households, could not afford to keep their homes adequately warm; 6.3%, or 126,000 households, put up with feeling cold a lot to keep costs down; 6.0%, or 118,000 households, paid utility bills late more than once; 5.2%, or 102,000 households, had a major problem heating or keeping accommodation warm in winter; and 3.7%, or 74,000 households, had dampness and/or mould as a major problem [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

MBIE explicitly identifies income as a key determinant of a household's ability to afford adequate housing and sufficient energy, and says low-income households are more likely to experience energy hardship measures [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024). The public-health literature reaches the same directional conclusion from expenditure measures: PHCC researchers estimated from 2018/19 HES data that 16.37% of households were in energy poverty before housing costs under a "twice median proportion of income spent on energy" measure, rising to 30.54% after housing costs, and they stated that a better measure would include the thermal performance of homes [PHCC, energy poverty and lowest-income households](https://www.phcc.org.nz/briefing/energy-poverty-lowest-income-households-pay-more-aotearoa).

Income is therefore likely to be the most general limiting factor for a volunteer-team intervention choice: a housing-quality tool, tariff tool, or tenant-rights resource all still has to deal with households that cannot absorb upfront costs, higher fixed charges, inspection costs, or the cash-flow timing of bills [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [PHCC, energy poverty and lowest-income households](https://www.phcc.org.nz/briefing/energy-poverty-lowest-income-households-pay-more-aotearoa).

**Confidence: Medium** that income inadequacy is the broadest driver, because the evidence is strong directionally but does not quantify its marginal contribution separately from rent, housing quality, household size, climate, appliance efficiency, or tariff exposure.

### Poor housing stock is strongly evidenced, especially in rented homes

BRANZ's 2015 House Condition Survey tenure report was designed to compare owner-occupied and rental properties across New Zealand and weighted its sample to represent owner-occupied and rented houses [BRANZ, 2015 House Condition Survey tenure report](https://d39d3mj7qio96p.cloudfront.net/media/documents/SR370_2015_House_Condition_Survey.pdf). BRANZ found rental stock was in poorer condition than owner-occupied stock across a range of building components, and its damp/mould findings are relevant because MBIE uses dampness/mould and heating difficulty as energy-hardship measures [BRANZ, 2015 House Condition Survey tenure report](https://d39d3mj7qio96p.cloudfront.net/media/documents/SR370_2015_House_Condition_Survey.pdf), [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

The 2023 Census-based EHINZ tenure analysis found that dampness and mould were more commonly reported in rented dwellings than owner-occupied dwellings across health districts, and it notes that tenants typically have limited ability or legal rights to install heat pumps, add insulation, or improve ventilation [EHINZ, dampness and mould by tenure](https://www.ehinz.ac.nz/assets/Surveillance-reports/Released_2025/Dampness-and-mould-in-New-Zealand-households-a-tenure-based-analysis.pdf). MBIE's 2024 monitoring also found renters had higher energy-hardship rates than owner-occupiers: renters reporting damp/mould as a major problem fell from 10.2% in 2019 to 6.7% in 2024, while homeowners fell from 2.5% to 2.0%; renters reporting heating as a major problem fell from 15.9% to 8.5%, while homeowners fell from 4.0% to 3.3%; and renters putting up with feeling cold a lot fell from 14.8% to 10.9%, while homeowners rose from 3.5% to 3.8% [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

The intervention evidence supports housing quality as more than a correlate. He Kāinga Oranga's Housing, Insulation and Health Study randomly insulated half of about 1,400 eligible households after baseline winter data collection, and reported that insulated homes were drier and slightly warmer, occupants reported significantly warmer homes, and adults and children reported improved self-rated health compared with households not yet insulated [He Kāinga Oranga, Housing, Insulation and Health Study](https://www.healthyhousing.org.nz/our-research/past-research/housing-insulation-and-health-study). The Warm Up New Zealand: Heat Smart evaluation used matched treatment/control households and found insulation retrofits were associated with lower annual metered energy use and health-service cost savings, while the programme-level cost-benefit analysis estimated a favourable benefit-cost ratio under its preferred scenario [He Kāinga Oranga, WUNZ:HS evaluation](https://www.healthyhousing.org.nz/our-research/past-research/evaluation-warm-new-zealand-heat-smart-programme), [Motu, WUNZ:HS cost-benefit analysis](https://motu.nz/our-research/urban-and-regional/housing/cost-benefit-analysis-of-the-warm-up-new-zealand-heat-smart-programme).

**Confidence: High** that poor housing stock materially contributes to energy hardship and **Medium** that it should be ranked near income for a renter-focused volunteer product, because strong causal evidence exists for insulation/retrofit benefits but not for a whole-population attribution share.

### Electricity pricing and tariff structures matter, but subgroup evidence is stronger than whole-population attribution

Stats NZ's earlier energy-hardship report found that domestic energy prices, especially gas and electricity prices, rose faster than average weekly earnings from 1989 to 2016, and it treated energy prices as one of the three core parts of the housing-quality / energy-price / income relationship [Stats NZ, investigating different measures of energy hardship](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Retirement-of-archive-website-project-files/Reports/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand/Download-data/Investigating-different-measures-of-energy-hardship-in-New-Zealand.pdf). The Electricity Authority's regional power-prices dashboard explains that low-user plans have lower fixed charges and higher variable charges than standard plans, and that the low fixed charge regulations are being phased out from April 2022 to April 2027 [Electricity Authority, regional power prices](https://www.ea.govt.nz/data-and-insights/charts-and-dashboards/regional-power-prices/).

Prepay is the clearest tariff/access subgroup. Consumer NZ's 2023 Powerswitch-based research found most prepay customers could pay about 15% more for power, with estimated premiums of about 15% in Wellington, 11% in Auckland, and 17% in Christchurch compared with pay-monthly counterparts [Consumer NZ, prepay customers paying more](https://www.consumer.org.nz/home-and-living/home-energy/prepay-customers-paying-much-more-for-power). The Electricity Authority says power companies turned down around 46,900 applications for postpay electricity for credit-related reasons in 2025, says prepay can help people connect and manage debt, but also says prepay may not offer the same range of plans as postpay and can limit access to newer plans such as time-of-use plans [Electricity Authority, consumer access and choice](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-access-and-choice/).

Disconnection evidence also supports pricing/access risk, but not apportionment. The Electricity Authority's disconnections dashboard covers monthly disconnections for non-payment, distinguishes prepay and postpay, and defines prepay disconnection as supply being disconnected when the credit balance reaches zero or an approved arrears limit [Electricity Authority, disconnections dashboard](https://www.ea.govt.nz/data-and-insights/charts-and-dashboards/disconnections-for-non-payment/). The Consumer Care Obligations require retailers to monitor prepay disconnection frequency and duration and to contact prepay customers when they frequently run out of credit or experience relatively long disconnections, which means the regulator treats prepay disconnection patterns as a consumer-care signal [Electricity Authority, Consumer Care Obligations](https://www.ea.govt.nz/code-and-compliance/the-code-electricity-industry-participation-code-2010/part-11a-consumer-care-obligations/schedule-11a1-consumer-care-obligations/33-failure-to-disconnect-within-timeframe/).

**Confidence: Medium** that pricing and tariffs are a material driver, and **Low-to-Medium** that a tariff-comparison tool alone would address the largest share of hardship, because plan choice is constrained for some households by credit checks, prepay availability, metering, digital capability, tenancy constraints, and low disposable income [Electricity Authority, consumer access and choice](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/consumer-access-and-choice/), [MBIE, components of the energy wellbeing framework](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/21645-components-of-the-energy-wellbeing-framework-pdf).

### The renter split incentive is real, but it is best treated as a mechanism behind the rental housing gap

Barton describes the landlord/tenant problem as a principal-agent gap: the landlord is responsible for the building fabric and main appliances, while the tenant usually pays energy bills and experiences the heating and ventilation performance; the paper says the landlord lacks direct incentive to invest in extra insulation or better appliances when benefits are reaped by the tenant without direct influence on rent [Barton, Energy Efficiency and Rental Accommodation](https://www.otago.ac.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0026/329264/-energy-efficiency-and-rental-accommodation-dealing-with-split-incentives-055632.pdf). Motu's WUNZ:HS metered-energy report makes the same implementation point: owner-occupiers have more incentive to enter retrofit schemes because they receive benefits after bearing costs, while landlords bear installation capital costs and benefits go to tenants and may not be fully reflected in rents [Motu, WUNZ:HS metered energy use](https://www.motu.nz/assets/Documents/our-work/urban-and-regional/housing/Warming-Up-New-Zealand-Impacts-of-the-New-Zealand-Insulation-Fund-on-Metered-Household-Energy-Use.pdf).

The policy response is now partly regulatory rather than voluntary. Tenancy Services says all private rentals must comply with healthy homes standards from 1 July 2025; the standards cover heating, insulation, ventilation, moisture ingress and drainage, and draught stopping, and landlords who do not meet obligations breach the Residential Tenancies Act and may face penalties [Tenancy Services, healthy homes compliance](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/healthy-homes-compliance/). HUD's monitoring page says it commissioned annual renter/landlord surveys to track awareness and compliance, and its September 2025 news item says a June 2025 survey found about nine in ten landlords indicated their properties already met or would meet the standards by the 1 July 2025 deadline [HUD, healthy homes standards](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/healthy-homes-standards), [HUD, 2025 monitoring news](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/new-survey-shows-progress-made-on-healthy-homes-standards).

The compliance evidence still has limits. The HUD survey is self-reported landlord/tenant monitoring, while Renters United argued to MBIE in 2023 that Healthy Homes enforcement relied heavily on renters self-enforcing complex standards and that monitoring was not strong enough to make concrete claims about sector compliance; that is an advocacy submission rather than a representative official audit, so it should be used as a risk signal rather than a prevalence estimate [HUD, 2025 monitoring news](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/new-survey-shows-progress-made-on-healthy-homes-standards), [Renters United submission to MBIE energy hardship panel](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/28456-renters-united-energy-hardship-the-challenges-and-the-way-forward-discussion-paper-submission-pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that split incentive is a major mechanism in rental housing quality, but **Low** that current public NZ evidence quantifies it as an independent percentage of energy hardship.

## What would change this conclusion

- A representative NZ causal model linking dwelling condition, metered energy need, actual energy use, tariff/plan type, income after housing costs, tenure, household composition, climate zone, and self-reported hardship would change the central "no apportionment" conclusion.
- Retail microdata from the Electricity Authority's retail data monitoring programme, linked only in aggregate and privacy-preserving form to income/tenure/housing-quality indicators, could move the pricing/tariff contribution from Medium confidence to High [MBIE, 2024 energy hardship measures](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024), [Electricity Authority, disconnections dashboard](https://www.ea.govt.nz/data-and-insights/charts-and-dashboards/disconnections-for-non-payment/).
- A current independent audit of Healthy Homes compliance, rather than self-reported landlord/tenant monitoring, could change the split-incentive and rental housing-quality ranking [HUD, healthy homes standards](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/healthy-homes-standards), [Tenancy Services, healthy homes compliance](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/healthy-homes/healthy-homes-compliance/).
- Lived-experience input from renters, prepay customers, household-budget advisers, Maori and Pacific providers, and medically dependent electricity users is needed before choosing a volunteer product, because the sources here show aggregate patterns but not which intervention would be usable or trusted in practice [MBIE, defining energy hardship](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship).
- I could not verify the unmerged discover framing file named in the issue in this checkout or via GitHub contents API, so this finding relies on issue #327 and public sources rather than that framing document [issue #327](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/327).
- The vendored `household-hardship-nz` CLI was checked first, but data.govt.nz CKAN requests returned invalid JSON in this environment on 3 July 2026; I therefore used MBIE, Stats NZ PDFs, and other primary web sources for the cited HES-related claims rather than citing failed local CLI output.
