# Overseas flood-adaptation reward schemes work best when they pair a visible risk signal with certified works and funded delivery capacity

## Executive answer

- **Best answer:** the most transplantable design pattern for NZ is not a single overseas programme, but a three-part machine: (1) property-level risk information, (2) a recognised certificate or standard for works, and (3) a premium/grant/rates pathway that actually pays the owner back for reducing risk. FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0 improved individual risk-based pricing but is politically softened by statutory annual increase caps; CRS discounts reward councils but GAO says FEMA is redesigning CRS because some discount activities are not directly actuarial; Flood Re's Build Back Better gives a clear post-claim cap but uptake is only about one-third of eligible policyholders; and the QLD/NSW funds show that construction, assessment and relocation capacity can dominate programme delivery. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977); [FEMA CRS FAQ, 2024](https://agents.floodsmart.gov/sites/default/files/media/document/2025-07/fema-nfip-community-rating-system-discount-faq-09-2024.pdf); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties); [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about); [NSW Audit Office, 2026](https://www.audit.nsw.gov.au/our-work/reports/the-resilient-homes-and-resilient-lands-programs-0).
- **What worked:** transparent discounts or grants changed the owner's arithmetic. CRS offers eligible NFIP policyholders 5%-45% premium reductions by community class; Flood Re BBB gives up to GBP10,000 for property flood resilience during reinstatement; QLD offered up to AUD50,000 for retrofit and up to AUD150,000 for raising or relocation; Alabama's FORTIFIED model shows why a third-party standard matters, with statutory/regulated insurance discounts and post-Hurricane Sally evidence of materially lower wind losses. [FEMA CRS FAQ, 2024](https://agents.floodsmart.gov/sites/default/files/media/document/2025-07/fema-nfip-community-rating-system-discount-faq-09-2024.pdf); [Flood Re BBB](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/); [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about); [Alabama Code section 27-31D-2](https://law.justia.com/codes/alabama/title-27/chapter-31d/section-27-31d-2/); [ALDOI, 2025](https://www.aldoi.gov/currentnewsitem.aspx?ID=1321).
- **What failed or limited impact:** caps and subsidies protected affordability but weakened pure risk signals; BBB's offer was simple but hard to convert into completed works; NSW's audit found persistent delays, late evaluation planning and slow resilient-measures delivery; and both Australian schemes show that buyback, raise, retrofit and relocation are different operating businesses, not one generic "adaptation fund". [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties); [NSW Audit Office, 2026](https://www.audit.nsw.gov.au/our-work/reports/the-resilient-homes-and-resilient-lands-programs-0); [NSW Government, 2026](https://www.nsw.gov.au/ministerial-releases/more-time-for-flood-impacted-homeowners-to-raise-retrofit-or-relocate).
- **NZ transplant assessment:** the National Adaptation Framework already has the right policy hook - "investment in risk reduction" and a New Zealand Flood Map intended to let property, insurance and other markets function well - but it does not yet specify a homeowner reward instrument. The closest low-regret transplant is an NZ flood-resilience certificate tied to the national flood map, council LIM/update processes, insurer pricing where legally and commercially feasible, and targeted grants for households that cannot self-fund. [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the programme mechanics are well supported by official sources, but comparable outcome evidence is uneven. The strongest quantified performance evidence found is for Alabama FORTIFIED wind loss reduction, not flood retrofits; Flood Re and the Australian schemes are still learning through delivery.

## Evidence

### United States: RR2.0 and CRS

FEMA began implementing Risk Rating 2.0 in October 2021 to align NFIP premiums more closely with individual property flood risk, and GAO concluded that the new methodology "substantially improves ratemaking" while leaving non-risk-based charges and affordability problems unresolved. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977). The design lesson is that property-level risk pricing can create a mitigation signal, but US politics still caps annual premium increases for most policyholders at 18%, which GAO says perpetuates a premium shortfall, poorly targets affordability support and delays the move to full-risk premiums. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977).

RR2.0's most relevant adaptation mechanism is not just higher prices. FEMA has mitigation tools that show potential premium reductions for actions such as flood vents and elevating machinery and equipment, and NFIP's Increased Cost of Compliance coverage can provide up to USD30,000 after substantial or repetitive flood damage to help rebuild in ways that reduce future flood damage. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977); [FloodSmart, ICC](https://www.floodsmart.gov/recover/rebuild-better). This makes RR2.0 partly transplantable to NZ as a "show me the premium effect of this work" tool, but less transplantable as a public affordability model because GAO's central critique is that untargeted premium caps hide the cost and undercut actuarial pricing. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977).

The Community Rating System is the council-level counterpart: FEMA assigns CRS classes from 10 to 1, and eligible policies receive up to 0%, 5%, 10% and onward to 45% premium reductions depending on the community class. [FEMA CRS FAQ, 2024](https://agents.floodsmart.gov/sites/default/files/media/document/2025-07/fema-nfip-community-rating-system-discount-faq-09-2024.pdf). CRS worked as a simple, legible community incentive, but GAO recommended that FEMA adjust CRS so community ratings are based only on activities that reduce flood risk and so discounts are incorporated into full-risk premiums based on actuarial evaluation of risk reduction; FEMA told GAO in September 2025 that it was finalising a new CRS design for implementation expected by December 2027. [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977).

### Alabama FORTIFIED as the certification comparator

Alabama is not a flood scheme, but it is the cleanest comparator for certification-linked insurance recognition. Alabama law requires insurers to provide a premium discount or rate reduction for properties retrofitted to specified IBHS FORTIFIED standards, and the IBHS/FORTIFIED public incentive material says Alabama homeowners can receive up to 55% off the wind portion of property insurance for FORTIFIED designations. [Alabama Code section 27-31D-2](https://law.justia.com/codes/alabama/title-27/chapter-31d/section-27-31d-2/); [FORTIFIED Alabama incentives](https://fortifiedhome.org/incentives-alabama/). Alabama's Department of Insurance reported a University of Alabama / IBHS study finding FORTIFIED reduced Hurricane Sally loss frequency by 55%-74% and loss ratio by 51%-72%, with IBHS reporting the same results. [ALDOI, 2025](https://www.aldoi.gov/currentnewsitem.aspx?ID=1321); [IBHS, 2025](https://ibhs.org/ibhs-news-releases/study-shows-ibhss-fortified-program-reduced-hurricane-sally-damage/).

The transplantable feature is not the wind standard itself. It is the regulated chain: a technical standard, third-party designation, insurer obligation or clear insurer practice, and post-event empirical checks that the certified houses actually lose less. [Alabama Code section 27-31D-2](https://law.justia.com/codes/alabama/title-27/chapter-31d/section-27-31d-2/); [ALDOI, 2025](https://www.aldoi.gov/currentnewsitem.aspx?ID=1321). NZ would need a flood-specific standard and evidence base before copying the discount mandate, because flood resistance, recoverability and safe retreat are more site-specific than a roof/wind standard. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).

### United Kingdom: Flood Re BBB and Flood Performance Certificates

Flood Re's Build Back Better offers householders up to GBP10,000 for property flood resilience measures when repairing after a flood, and Flood Re's own page describes the purpose as avoiding reinstatement that leaves homes open to the next flood. [Flood Re BBB](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/). The 2025 FloodReady review says BBB launched in April 2022, is offered by over 70% of the UK insurance market to high-risk customers, and is internationally recognised as a resilience model. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).

The failure mode is uptake and operational complexity. The same FloodReady review says providing PFR through BBB has been more complex than expected because only around one-third of eligible policyholders accept the offer, with barriers including long and complex rebuilds, limited awareness, procurement delays, aesthetics concerns and limited guidance on recoverability options. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties). The review also identifies supply-side constraints: public procurement can be overly complex, specialist PFR suppliers are often SMEs, local access can be difficult, and products need testing and certification to protect consumers and build trust. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).

Flood Performance Certificates are the UK's proposed visibility layer. FloodReady says an FPC would show how resilient a property is to flooding and suggest improvements, that Flood Re piloting is welcomed by Defra, and that financial stakeholders see FPCs as potentially helping insurers and lenders make more informed decisions as Flood Re moves toward its 2039 end date. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties). The lesson for NZ is strong but conditional: a certificate is useful only if it is cheap enough to get, technically trusted, visible in insurance/lending/LIM workflows, and backed by installers who can deliver the recommended works. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).

### Australia: QLD and NSW resilient-homes funds

Queensland's Resilient Homes Fund is the clearest post-flood funded-delivery model. The Queensland Government says the AUD741 million fund covered 39 local government areas affected by the 2021-22 flood event and let eligible homeowners apply for funding to repair or retrofit, raise, demolish and rebuild, relocate, or use voluntary buyback for the most severely impacted and highest future-risk homes. [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about). The same official page gives capped pathways: up to AUD50,000 with co-contribution for repair/retrofit, up to AUD150,000 with co-contribution for raising, and up to AUD150,000 with no co-contribution for demolish-and-relocate or extend, with later eligibility expansion for some home-raise cases. [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about).

QLD's design strengths were menu clarity and flood-level linkage: retrofit, raising and relocation were separate streams, and habitable floors had to be raised to meet or exceed the assessed flood level and the 2021-22 flood event level. [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about). The main limit found in public official material is that delivery is still time-bound and participant-specific: registrations closed on 30 July 2023, grant applications closed on 1 December 2025, and building-contract completion dates must not exceed 31 March 2027. [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about).

NSW shows the implementation risk more sharply. The Northern Rivers Resilient Homes Program reached AUD880 million after a further AUD180 million was added, and the NSW Government said in December 2024 that 836 offers had been approved, 708 buyback offers accepted and 564 settlements completed; the same release said eligibility for resilient measures was being revised to include hardship waivers and upfront design/planning funding. [NSW Government, 2024](https://www.nsw.gov.au/ministerial-releases/northern-rivers-resilient-homes-program-boosted-to-880-million). A 2026 NSW Audit Office report found the programme was not effectively planned before implementation, that the buyback stream would not meet its original funded target, that funding was re-profiled in August 2025 from 1,345 buybacks/420 resilient measures toward 1,000 buybacks/600 resilient measures, and that the first resilient-measures payment was not made until December 2023, 21 months after the February 2022 flood. [NSW Audit Office, 2026](https://www.audit.nsw.gov.au/our-work/reports/the-resilient-homes-and-resilient-lands-programs-0). NSW later extended resilient-measures completion to December 2028, explicitly citing time needed to secure builders, finalise designs and respond to capacity constraints in the Northern Rivers building market. [NSW Government, 2026](https://www.nsw.gov.au/ministerial-releases/more-time-for-flood-impacted-homeowners-to-raise-retrofit-or-relocate).

### NZ transplant assessment, kept separate from overseas facts

NZ's National Adaptation Framework has the right enabling language but not the operating machinery. MfE says the Framework has four pillars: risk and response information sharing, roles and responsibilities, investment in risk reduction, and cost-sharing pre- and post-event; it also says the New Zealand Flood Map will unify national, local and regional flood data so people and businesses can make informed decisions and property, insurance and other markets can function well. [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/). That is a necessary platform for reward machinery, not a reward scheme in itself. [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/).

The most transplantable features are:

- **A Flood Performance Certificate analogue:** high transplantability, because it would translate the New Zealand Flood Map and site works into a property-level statement usable by owners, councils, insurers and lenders. Its success would depend on national technical standards, quality assurance and avoiding a certificate that becomes another stigma without a reward. [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).
- **A BBB-style post-claim resilience top-up:** medium-high transplantability, because NZ private insurers already manage flood building claims and reinstatement choices. The UK evidence says the cap is easy to explain, but one-third uptake means NZ would need claimant advice, installer panels and default scoping during reinstatement rather than relying on opt-in awareness. [Flood Re BBB](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).
- **A CRS-style council reward:** medium transplantability, because NZ has no national public flood-insurance premium to discount. The equivalent would have to be central-government co-funding, rates relief, infrastructure-funding priority, or insurer-recognised community risk reduction; the CRS lesson is to link the reward to demonstrable risk reduction, because GAO found non-actuarial discounts create reform pressure. [FEMA CRS FAQ, 2024](https://agents.floodsmart.gov/sites/default/files/media/document/2025-07/fema-nfip-community-rating-system-discount-faq-09-2024.pdf); [GAO, 2023](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105977).
- **A QLD-style pre/post-event resilience grant menu:** medium transplantability, because caps and streams are clear, but QLD/NSW show the need for assessment, design, consenting, builder capacity, co-contribution hardship rules and land supply before launch. A NZ programme that announces grants without delivery capacity would reproduce NSW's delay pattern. [QLD Resilient Homes Fund](https://www.qld.gov.au/housing/buying-owning-home/home-owners-financial-help/resilience-to-floods-and-cyclones/resilient-homes-fund/overview/about); [NSW Audit Office, 2026](https://www.audit.nsw.gov.au/our-work/reports/the-resilient-homes-and-resilient-lands-programs-0); [NSW Government, 2026](https://www.nsw.gov.au/ministerial-releases/more-time-for-flood-impacted-homeowners-to-raise-retrofit-or-relocate).
- **A FORTIFIED-style statutory discount:** lower immediate transplantability for flood, but high conceptual value. NZ would first need an accepted flood-resilience standard, verified installation, and evidence that certified measures reduce losses enough for insurers to price; otherwise a statutory discount could become a cross-subsidy rather than a risk-based reward. [Alabama Code section 27-31D-2](https://law.justia.com/codes/alabama/title-27/chapter-31d/section-27-31d-2/); [ALDOI, 2025](https://www.aldoi.gov/currentnewsitem.aspx?ID=1321); [IBHS, 2025](https://ibhs.org/ibhs-news-releases/study-shows-ibhss-fortified-program-reduced-hurricane-sally-damage/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A published NZ insurer commitment to recognise specified flood-resilience works in premiums, excesses or underwriting would raise the transplantability of certificate and statutory-discount models. Without that, a certificate risks becoming disclosure without reward. [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties); [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/).
- Final evaluation results from Queensland's Resilient Homes Fund, especially completed retrofit/raise counts, owner outcomes, avoided-loss estimates and cost per risk-reduced home, would materially change the Australian comparison. Natural Hazards Research Australia says the QLD evaluation is in progress and is assessing buyback, retrofit and house-raising across physical, financial, social and emotional resilience dimensions. [Natural Hazards Research Australia](https://www.naturalhazards.com.au/research/research-projects/evaluating-resilient-homes-fund).
- Better public data on Flood Re BBB completion, claim outcomes and renewal premiums would change confidence in the BBB model. I found official evidence for the cap, market coverage and low uptake, but not a public, independently verified avoided-loss estimate for completed BBB homes. [Flood Re BBB](https://www.floodre.co.uk/buildbackbetter/); [GOV.UK FloodReady, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/floodproof-an-action-plan-to-build-resilience/floodready-an-action-plan-to-build-the-resilience-of-people-and-properties).
- The introduced NZ Climate Change Response amendment bill, future National Adaptation Plan update, or any Budget decision creating a standing adaptation fund could change the NZ assessment from "policy hook without machinery" to a live instrument-design question. MfE says further Framework decisions will follow over time. [MfE National Adaptation Framework](https://environment.govt.nz/what-government-is-doing/areas-of-work/climate-change/adapting-to-climate-change/national-adaptation-framework/).
- A planner, insurer, lender and flood engineer should review any move from this research into ideation. The legal and actuarial feasibility of insurer discounts, LIM changes and council rates relief is outside this finding's authority.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would an NZ flood-resilience certificate need to include so that councils, insurers, lenders and buyers trust it without turning it into an unrewarded stigma?
- Which NZ insurers, if any, would recognise certified property-level flood works through premiums, excesses, coverage availability or claims handling?
- Could the New Zealand Flood Map support a public mitigation-discount calculator comparable to FEMA's mitigation tool, without exposing personal or address-level data?
- What assessment and installer capacity would NZ need before launching a QLD-style grant menu for retrofit, raising and relocation?
