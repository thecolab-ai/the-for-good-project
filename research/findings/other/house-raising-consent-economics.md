# House-raising in flood overlays has a real consent cost stack, but public records do not yet show frequent refusals

## Executive answer

- **The clearest conclusion:** raising an existing dwelling in a flood overlay is not just a building job; in the councils checked it can require building consent, resource consent or both, plus professional flood/structural evidence and possible title/LIM consequences. The evidence supports H4's "costly/difficult to consent" limb as a process-and-cost claim, not yet as a high-refusal-rate claim. [MBIE Building Performance](https://www.building.govt.nz/projects-and-consents/apply-for-building-consent/building-consent-process); [Hutt City Council natural hazards](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/building-consents/before-you-start/natural-hazard-risks); [Hastings District Council building consents](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents-information/building-consents/)
- **Construction cost dwarfs council fees, but council fees are material and open-ended.** NIWA/WT Partnership's residential elevation matrix puts a 2 m lift at about **$131,874-$357,453** for typical residential examples, excluding facade closure and several hidden costs; consent deposits/fees then add from low thousands to tens of thousands if notification is triggered, before private engineer/planner/surveyor costs. [NIWA elevation pamphlet](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf); [Hutt City Council resource fees](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/resource-consents/info-portal/resource-consent-charges-and-fees); [Hastings District Council resource consent fees](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/fees-and-costs/); [Christchurch City Council resource consent fees](https://ccc.govt.nz/the-council/plans-strategies-policies-and-bylaws/plans/long-term-plan-and-annual-plans/fees-and-charges/fees-resource-consents)
- **Time is at least two statutory clocks, not one queue.** Building consents generally have a 20-working-day statutory processing period once a complete application is accepted, and non-notified resource consents are usually around 20 working days, but RFIs stop the clock and notified resource consent can move the resource-consent limb into months. [MBIE Building Performance](https://www.building.govt.nz/projects-and-consents/apply-for-building-consent/building-consent-process); [Hastings non-notified consent process](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/notified-and-non-notified-consents/); [Hutt City resource consent timeframes](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/resource-consents/before-you-start/building-consent-time-frames)
- **Refusal/decline evidence is the weak link.** Auckland publicly says a Category 2P mitigation can fail if building or resource consent is declined, and Hastings/Hutt explain refusal/decline powers, but I did not find public aggregate data for "house raising in flood overlays: lodged / granted / refused / withdrawn" in the four-council record checked. [OurAuckland Category 2P deep dive](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/); [Hastings resource consent process](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/process/); [Hutt consents issued lists](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/property/consents-issued); [Christchurch resource consents received and issued](https://ccc.govt.nz/consents-and-licences/resource-consents/resource-consent-process/resource-consents-received-and-issued)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the fee schedules, statutory clocks and natural-hazard gateways are well supported, but the finding cannot quantify refusal rates without OIA/LGOIMA data or a council consent-register extract that classifies house-raising/flood-overlay applications.

## Evidence

### Baseline: house lifting is a six-figure construction decision before consent costs

NIWA's Future Coasts Aotearoa material says WT Partnership, with advice from Auckland House Lifting, estimated the cost to raise existing residential and non-residential buildings by up to 2 m, with typical residential examples ranging from **$131,874** for a 2/3-storey timber-framed dwelling with a 52 m2 ground floor footprint to **$357,453** for a single-storey brick/concrete dwelling with a 106 m2 footprint. [NIWA elevation pamphlet](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf). NIWA's accompanying article says timber-and-pile buildings were found more cost-effective to lift than rebuild, while concrete construction may not be financially viable to lift, and it warns that hidden expenses such as temporary accommodation, storage, roads, water and wastewater services were not included. [NIWA news, 2023](https://niwa.co.nz/news/higher-and-drier-cost-raising-homes).

This matters for consent economics because the consent decision sits on top of a high base project cost: even a $2,000-$3,000 non-notified land-use deposit or a few hundred dollars of Building Act natural-hazard/title processing is not the main cost, but engineering reports, RFIs, repeated inspections, notification and delay can make the consenting limb material to whether the owner proceeds. [Hutt City Council resource fees](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/resource-consents/info-portal/resource-consent-charges-and-fees); [Hastings building fees](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents-information/fees-and-costs/); [Christchurch building-fee guide](https://ccc.govt.nz/consents-and-licences/building-consents/building-consent-forms-guides-fees/fees-and-charges-building-consents).

### What "real cost/time" means here

The advertised council-fee stack is only a lower-bound estimate, because councils repeatedly state that applicants pay actual costs above deposits, extra specialist costs, extra processing, extra inspections or external expertise. [Hutt building fees](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/building-consents/before-you-start/building-consent-charges-and-fees); [Hutt resource fees](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/resource-consents/info-portal/resource-consent-charges-and-fees); [Hastings building fees](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents-information/fees-and-costs/); [Hastings resource-consent fees](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/fees-and-costs/).

For an owner-funded lift in the NIWA residential range, a plausible public-fee lower bound is therefore: building-consent processing/inspection/CCC charges at council hourly or banded rates; national/council levies tied to project value; any Building Act natural-hazard title certificate/notice process if the work is treated as construction or major alteration on hazard land; resource consent if the raised dwelling/floor-level/site works do not fit permitted standards; and private technical evidence such as structural design, floor-level survey, flood report or planner/AEE. [NIWA elevation pamphlet](https://niwa.co.nz/sites/default/files/inline-images/Elevating%20Buildingsv2.pdf); [MBIE natural-hazard guidance](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); [Hastings building consents](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents-information/building-consents/); [Hutt natural hazards](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/building-consents/before-you-start/natural-hazard-risks).

Time has the same lower-bound problem. A clean building consent and a clean non-notified resource consent could each sit near a 20-working-day statutory processing frame after acceptance, but the sources explicitly allow for stopped clocks, allocation delays, further-information requests, and longer notified paths. [MBIE Building Performance](https://www.building.govt.nz/projects-and-consents/apply-for-building-consent/building-consent-process); [Hastings notified/non-notified consents](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/notified-and-non-notified-consents/); [Hutt resource timeframes](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/resource-consents/before-you-start/building-consent-time-frames); [Christchurch resource consents](https://portal.zero.govt.nz/0690ef3293449a51183263516d3a4f39/consents-and-licences/resource-consents).

### Refusal/decline data: obtainable in principle, not public enough in this pass

The strongest refusal signal is Auckland's Category 2P rule: if mitigation is not feasible because a resource or building consent has been declined, the property changes to Category 3 and there are no additional grants for rescoping. [OurAuckland Category 2P deep dive](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/). That is a significant gate, but it does not reveal how often applications are actually declined. [OurAuckland Category 2P deep dive](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/04/a-deep-dive-into-category-2p/).

Hastings and Hutt explain that councils can refuse/decline where statutory tests are not met, and Hastings says restricted discretionary, discretionary and non-complying activities can be granted or refused depending on the plan category and assessment. [Hastings resource consents](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/planning-and-resource-consents/resource-consents/); [Hastings building consents](https://www.hastingsdc.govt.nz/services/building-consents-information/building-consents/); [Hutt natural hazards](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/building-consents/before-you-start/natural-hazard-risks). The public registers I found are mainly received/issued lists, so a robust refusal-rate answer needs LGOIMA requests or downloadable consent-management data including activity description, flood-overlay flag, outcome and withdrawal status. [Hutt consents issued](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/property/consents-issued); [Christchurch resource consents received and issued](https://ccc.govt.nz/consents-and-licences/resource-consents/resource-consent-process/resource-consents-received-and-issued).

## What would change this conclusion

- A council extract or LGOIMA response for Auckland, Christchurch, Hutt and Hastings listing **house lift / floor-level raise / relocated or raised dwelling** applications inside flood/coastal-inundation overlays, with lodged date, accepted date, RFIs, notification status, final invoice, private specialist report requirements if recorded, decision date and outcome, would change this from a fee-schedule finding to a true observed-cost finding.
- A refusal/decline table showing that many flood-overlay house-raising applications are declined, withdrawn after RFI, or converted to Category 3 because consent failed would strengthen H4's "difficult to consent" limb; a table showing most complete applications are granted quickly with predictable conditions would weaken it.
- I could not verify actual final invoices or actual elapsed calendar time for anonymised house-raising consents, and I did not contact councils or inspect property-identifying files because the issue guardrail excludes personal/property-identifying data.
- This needs review by a council consenting practitioner, planner or resource-management lawyer before being used for policy design, because the legal classification of "raise an existing dwelling" can change by district plan rule, hazard overlay, earthworks, floor-level standard, scale of alteration and whether the work is treated as a major alteration under Building Act natural-hazard provisions.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would LGOIMA data show for 2021-2026 actual cost, RFI count, notification status and outcome for house-raising/floor-level-raise applications in flood overlays?
- Could councils publish non-identifying consent outcome data by natural-hazard overlay and work type, rather than only received/issued registers?
- Do insurers or banks recognise a completed council-consented house raise differently from a flood-overlay property with no recorded mitigation?
