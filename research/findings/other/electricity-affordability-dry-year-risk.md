# NZ electricity affordability and dry-year risk need five focused research tracks

## Executive answer

- The household-facing problem is not one simple "power companies put prices up" story: bills combine generation, transmission, distribution, retail, metering, GST, and levies, and the Electricity Authority says the generation share of the average household bill has increased over the last decade as wholesale prices rose [Electricity Authority, "Your power bill"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/bill/).
- Dry-year risk is a real system driver because New Zealand's electricity generation capacity is more than half hydro, dry years are periods when low rainfall keeps hydro lake levels below average, and the Electricity Authority says declining hydro reservoirs lead hydro generation to be priced for water scarcity while more thermal generation often comes online, lifting average spot prices [Electricity Authority, 2024](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/).
- The spot market should be described as nodal, not as a single national price: prices are calculated every half-hour and vary by location, with about 12,000 final prices each day, one for each trading period at each node [Electricity Authority, "Spot market"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/industry/wholesale/spot-market/).
- The market-structure question is legitimate but not answered by this baseline: the Electricity Authority says most generation is from Contact, Genesis, Meridian, and Mercury, while the wholesale market includes about 80 generators, 62 retailers, and four gentailers [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/).
- The stream should fan out into five research questions: real residential price trends, measured dry-year price effects, gentailer concentration and margins, energy hardship/disconnections, and the new generation/flexibility pipeline [#277](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/277), [#279](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/279), [#281](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/281), [#282](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/282), [#283](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/283).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - official sources support the framing and the research split, but this discover finding deliberately does not quantify real household price changes, spot-price spikes, or gentailer margins.

## Evidence

### Problem statement

New Zealand households need a plain-English account of which parts of electricity prices are driven by wholesale generation conditions, regulated network costs, retail competition, and consumer vulnerability, because the Electricity Authority describes the average monthly household bill as a bundle of generation, distribution, transmission, retail, GST, metering, and levies rather than a single charge [Electricity Authority, "Your power bill"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/bill/).

The Electricity Authority says generation is the cost of producing electricity and that generation's share of the average power bill has increased over the last decade, reflecting higher wholesale prices [Electricity Authority, "Your power bill"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/bill/). The same page identifies increased peak demand, relatively dry weather, gas supply uncertainty, generation-investment delays including COVID-19 delays, and uncertainty around Tiwai Point as contributors to price rises [Electricity Authority, "Your power bill"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/bill/).

This means the stream should not start by assuming retail margin is the main driver, because the regulator's published bill explanation points to generation, dry weather, gas, investment timing, and network-cost resets as distinct mechanisms that need to be separated [Electricity Authority, "Your power bill"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/bill/).

### Dry-year mechanics

The Electricity Authority distinguishes winter peak capacity from dry-year risk: winter peak is about enough capacity during short high-demand periods, while dry-year risk is about enough fuel when rainfall into hydro lakes is below average for an extended period [Electricity Authority, 2024](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/).

The Electricity Authority says New Zealand's electricity generation capacity is over 50% hydro, many hydro schemes have reservoirs, and dry-year risk occurs when low rainfall causes hydro lake levels to stay below average for an extended period [Electricity Authority, 2024](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/). MBIE's Energy in New Zealand 2025 reports that New Zealand generated 43,879 GWh of electricity in 2024, that low hydro inflows reduced hydroelectric generation, and that the drop was covered by new renewable capacity and increased non-renewable generation from gas, coal, and oil [MBIE, Energy in New Zealand 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/energy-in-new-zealand/energy-in-new-zealand-2025/electricity).

The price mechanism is scarcity, not a single national auction price: the Electricity Authority says that during hydro-reservoir decline, hydro generation is priced to reflect increasingly scarce water, and more thermal generation often comes online in dry years, resulting in higher average spot prices [Electricity Authority, 2024](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/).

The spot market operates at specific places on the grid: generators offer electricity at 52 grid injection points, retailers and major users bid at 196 grid exit points, prices differ by trading period and location, and final settlement prices total about 12,000 per day [Electricity Authority, "Spot market"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/industry/wholesale/spot-market/). A household-facing explainer should therefore say dry years lift average spot prices and hedge costs, not that every generator everywhere receives one national half-hourly price [Electricity Authority, "Spot market"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/industry/wholesale/spot-market/).

### Recent Lake Pukaki decision

RNZ reported on 3 July 2026 that Meridian received final approval from the government's fast-track panel to access contingent storage at Lake Pukaki for three years, and the article says Meridian framed the decision as a buffer for dry years and as downward pressure on fixed rates through winter 2028 [RNZ, 3 July 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/658613/meridian-gets-go-ahead-to-access-reserve-water-promises-price-hikes-will-end-soon). The official Fast-track project page describes the Lake Pukaki Hydro Storage and Dam Resilience Works project as seeking resource consents for water takes from Lake Pukaki contingent storage over three consecutive winters from winter 2026 at levels lower than the normal permitted minimum, plus rock armouring on Pukaki Dam [Fast-track, Lake Pukaki project](https://www.fasttrack.govt.nz/projects/lake-pukaki-hydro-storage-and-dam-resilience-works).

Meridian's claim that access to contingent storage will put downward pressure on prices is a proponent claim, not independent proof of household-price effects, because the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment described the proposal as a trade-off between electricity system costs and security of supply and said that trade-off would best be informed by an energy strategy [Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment, 2026](https://pce.parliament.nz/publications/comment-on-lake-pukaki-hydro-storage-and-dam-resilience-works-fast-track-application/).

### Market structure

The Electricity Authority says most electricity generation is produced by four companies, Contact Energy, Genesis Energy, Meridian Energy, and Mercury Energy, while the wholesale market includes about 80 generation companies, 62 retailers, and four gentailers [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/).

The same Electricity Authority page says retailers and large industrial users buy electricity through the spot market and can use financial hedges to smooth some or all spot-price volatility by fixing electricity prices for a period [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/). For households, that means wholesale spot-price spikes do not automatically appear on the next monthly bill, but they can affect the fixed rates and hedge costs that retailers face over time [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/).

The market-structure research issue should test whether gentailer concentration changes wholesale margins, hedge availability, or retail pass-through, rather than treating "four gentailers dominate" as enough to prove excess margins [#281](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/281).

### Who bears the cost

MBIE defines energy wellbeing as being able to obtain and afford adequate energy services for wellbeing in the home or kainga, and defines energy hardship as the opposite of that situation [MBIE, "Defining energy hardship"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship). MBIE's framework includes household income, payment methods, dwelling efficiency, energy prices, retail plans, climate, and household circumstances as interacting factors, so price rises are only one part of hardship [MBIE, "Defining energy hardship"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-hardship/defining-energy-hardship).

The Electricity Authority published a disconnections dashboard in December 2025 and reported January-October 2025 insights: postpay accounts averaged 810 disconnections per month, while prepay accounts averaged 27,000 disconnections per month affecting about 10,000 prepay customers, or about 35% of prepay customers [Electricity Authority, 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/general-news/strengthening-visibility-of-disconnections-in-the-retail-electricity-market/). The dashboard page says it reports monthly domestic and small-business disconnections for non-payment, including counts, duration, and repeated disconnections [Electricity Authority, disconnections dashboard](https://www.ea.govt.nz/data-and-insights/charts-and-dashboards/disconnections-for-non-payment/).

The hardship child issue should quantify which households face the highest burden as a share of income and how disconnection risk differs by payment type, because aggregate average bills can hide the effect on households with low income, inefficient homes, medical needs, or prepay arrangements [#282](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/282).

### What is already changing

MBIE reports that in 2024 renewable generation capacity increased by 556 MW, while lower hydro inflows and lower gas supply increased coal use for electricity generation [MBIE, Energy in New Zealand 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/energy-and-natural-resources/energy-statistics-and-modelling/energy-publications-and-technical-papers/energy-in-new-zealand/energy-in-new-zealand-2025/electricity). The Electricity Authority says the electricity system is shifting toward more intermittent renewable generation such as wind and solar, distributed energy resources such as rooftop solar and batteries, and two-way power flows [Electricity Authority, "New Zealand's electricity sector"](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/new-zealands-electricity-sector/).

The dry-year solution question should not assume that more solar and wind automatically solve multi-week hydro shortages, because the Electricity Authority says batteries can store some excess solar and wind energy but only for a few hours, which is not enough by itself when rainfall is below average for weeks to months [Electricity Authority, 2024](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-difference-between-winter-peak-capacity-and-dry-year-risk/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A full CPI-adjusted residential electricity price series by region and retailer could change how strongly the stream frames the problem as recent household price inflation rather than long-run affordability stress [#277](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/277).
- Wholesale spot and hedge-market data comparing wet, normal, and dry years could change the weight given to dry-year mechanics versus network-cost resets or retail margins [#279](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/279).
- Audited gentailer margin, internal transfer-pricing, and hedge-market evidence could change the assessment of whether market structure is a primary driver or a secondary amplifier [#281](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/281).
- Household-level or decile-level expenditure evidence could change the framing of who is most harmed, especially if low-income or prepay households face materially different effective prices or disconnection patterns [#282](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/282).
- A current, official project pipeline for generation, storage, demand response, and grid connections could change whether the dry-year problem looks temporary through winter 2028 or structurally persistent [#283](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/283).
- I could not verify household price pass-through from the Lake Pukaki decision; Meridian's statement is useful as a proponent claim, but independent market data is needed before treating it as a household-price forecast.
- A human with electricity-market expertise should review the market-design wording before this stream moves to synthesis, because nodal pricing, hedge contracts, water values, and retail pass-through are easy to oversimplify.
