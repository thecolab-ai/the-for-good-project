# NZ hazard overlays are usually sub-parcel maps, but LIM disclosure can turn partial exposure into a whole-property warning

## Executive answer

- The founder's H2 is **partly supported, with an important correction**: council hazard information is generally drawn as mapped flood/coastal/land-instability extents, not as a binary parcel-only "high risk" tag. Auckland, Hamilton, Whangarei and Dunedin all describe mapped hazard areas, overlays, flood extents, or GIS-derived paths rather than whole-parcel risk polygons. [Auckland Council, 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2026/03/understanding-auckland-s-regional-flood-maps/), [Hamilton City Council, 2025](https://hamilton.govt.nz/property-rates-and-building/district-plan/flood-mapping/), [Whangarei District Council PC1](https://www.wdc.govt.nz/Services/Planning/District-Plan/District-Plan-changes/Current-plan-changes/PC1), [Dunedin 2GP Natural Hazards](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/716560/11.-Natural-Hazards.pdf)
- The overbreadth risk appears when those mapped extents are converted into **LIM/property disclosures**. A LIM is issued for the land/property requested; LGOIMA requires hazards that affect, or may affect, "the land concerned"; and the 2025 LIM regulations require natural-hazard sections, maps or map links where held, but do not require a fresh risk assessment or further site-specific analysis. [LGOIMA s44B](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/0174/latest/DLM122242.html), [Local Government (Natural Hazard Information in LIMs) Regulations 2025](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2025/0068/16.0/whole.html)
- Auckland is the clearest large-council example: the LIM uses regional/catchment flood maps overlaid on the requested property boundary, is not based on individual property data, and does not include building/home mitigation. [Auckland Council, 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2026/03/understanding-auckland-s-regional-flood-maps/)
- Waikato Regional Council shows the main failure mode: some regional hazard information provided for LIMs is not designed for property-scale use and, if included, may not be applicable to a particular parcel. [Waikato Regional Council, 2025](https://www.waikatoregion.govt.nz/assets/WRC/RegionalHazardsPortalPositionStatementProvisionNaturalHazardInformationLIMs.pdf)
- Consent rules are more granular than LIM stigma in the sample. Auckland, Hamilton, Whangarei and Dunedin all frame controls around activities, buildings, development, or site-specific reports in mapped hazard areas. That means the policy problem is best stated as: **partial-parcel exposure can become whole-property disclosure and market stigma, even where the engineering/planning control should be applied only to the affected part or activity.** [Auckland Unitary Plan E36](https://unitaryplan.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/Images/Auckland%20Unitary%20Plan%20Operative/Chapter%20E%20Auckland-wide/5.%20Environmental%20Risk/E36%20Natural%20hazards%20and%20flooding.pdf), [Whangarei District Council PC1](https://www.wdc.govt.nz/Services/Planning/District-Plan/District-Plan-changes/Current-plan-changes/PC1), [Dunedin 2GP Natural Hazards](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/716560/11.-Natural-Hazards.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the pattern is consistent across the official sample, but I did not inspect actual property-specific LIMs or run a GIS parcel-intersection audit, because that would risk property-identifying data and requires council datasets or anonymised samples.

## Evidence

### The legal unit is the LIM land/property, not the hazard polygon

LGOIMA s44B requires a LIM to include natural-hazard information, to the extent known, for each hazard or potential hazard that affects or may affect "the land concerned" [LGOIMA s44B](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/0174/latest/DLM122242.html). The 2025 LIM regulations then standardise how that information is presented: each LIM must include a natural-hazard section, district-plan hazard information must be noted, and maps or map links must be included where the map helps the recipient understand the hazard [Local Government (Natural Hazard Information in LIMs) Regulations 2025](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2025/0068/16.0/whole.html).

The same regulations also limit what councils must do when issuing a LIM. They do not require a territorial authority to prepare a risk assessment, undertake further analysis, or search beyond information required under s44B [Local Government (Natural Hazard Information in LIMs) Regulations 2025](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2025/0068/16.0/whole.html). That matters for granularity: the LIM can carry the best held map/record for the requested land without resolving whether a house, driveway, building platform, or unaffected half of the parcel is actually exposed.

### Consent controls are often activity-specific, not whole-parcel bans

Auckland's E36 chapter repeatedly frames standards as applying to buildings, habitable rooms, fences, parking, roads/accessways, storage and other activities **in** the relevant floodplain or coastal inundation area, and the plan background says available hazard information varies in detail and quality [Auckland Unitary Plan E36](https://unitaryplan.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/Images/Auckland%20Unitary%20Plan%20Operative/Chapter%20E%20Auckland-wide/5.%20Environmental%20Risk/E36%20Natural%20hazards%20and%20flooding.pdf). That is not a simple whole-parcel prohibition.

Whangarei is even more explicit about the distinction. It says PC1 rules are triggered when new subdivision and development occurs in mapped natural-hazard overlays, and then a site-specific assessment is required to understand the nature and extent of the hazard risk and any mitigation; it also describes optional site-specific report pathways for applicants [Whangarei District Council PC1](https://www.wdc.govt.nz/Services/Planning/District-Plan/District-Plan-changes/Current-plan-changes/PC1). Dunedin likewise says site-specific factors such as topography, elevation and soil should be considered for a particular proposal, even though the plan identifies hazard-prone areas through overlays and mapped areas [Dunedin 2GP Natural Hazards](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/716560/11.-Natural-Hazards.pdf).

This distinction is the main policy answer to #404: **the maps are finer than parcels, the consent rules can be finer than parcels, but the LIM and property-record channel can still make the whole property look hazard-affected to buyers, insurers, lenders or owners unless the disclosure clearly shows and explains the affected area.**

## What would change this conclusion

- A de-identified GIS audit across several councils showing the percentage of parcels where only a small share of the parcel intersects a hazard layer, plus how those same parcels are described in LIM text, would turn this from a source-pattern finding into a quantified finding.
- A sample of actual LIMs, with all addresses/title references removed, could show whether councils clearly state "part of the property" and include useful maps, or whether the text reads as a whole-property high-risk warning.
- A council practitioner could clarify how automated LIM generation decides when a mapped hazard "affects" a property and whether thresholds, buffers, layer confidence, or minimum intersection areas are used.
- A resource-management planner or lawyer should review any proposal based on this finding, because the right fix may differ by layer: map display, LIM wording, district-plan rule drafting, title notice, or insurer/lender interpretation.
- Evidence that councils routinely distinguish affected and unaffected portions in buyer-facing LIM summaries would weaken the "whole-property stigma" conclusion. Evidence that property records only say "this property is affected" without maps or qualifications would strengthen it.
