# NZ can identify learners through multi-evidence assessment, but the 2026 reset is more clearly a measurement system than an automatic support pathway

## Executive answer

- **Yes, New Zealand can identify learners who need help without relying on a single one-size-fits-all benchmark, but the strongest pathway is classroom assessment-for-learning plus professional judgement, not SMART alone.** Tāhūrangi describes assessment for learning as gathering, analysing, interpreting and using evidence of learner progress, with ākonga and whānau involved in next-step conversations, and SMART itself says tools should be used alongside other evidence rather than replacing teacher judgement. [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p); [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)
- **The 2026 assessment reset does identify need, but it does not create a published automatic entitlement when a learner is below expectation.** Budget 2025 says the assessment tool will support next steps for individual students and consistent assessment for about 540,000 learners, while Treasury's SMART advice explicitly treated the link from twice-yearly standardised assessment to better learning support arrangements as an intervention-logic question still needing clearer articulation. [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment); [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- **Funded support exists, but it sits beside the assessment reset rather than being triggered mechanically by a SMART/progress descriptor result.** Public sources point to classroom adjustments, learning support teams, LSCs, RTLB, ORS, teacher aides, PLD, targeted literacy/maths supports, accelerated tutoring, and equity-index funding as the response architecture. [Ministry learning support parent guide](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/learning-support/supporting-your-child-if-they-need-extra-help-their-learning); [Budget 2025 learning support at a glance](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-05/Budget%202025%20At%20a%20Glance.pdf?VersionId=1czcm8qU8x7ICWr.Whd9SWb6ZGXRyRge); [Ministry Foundation Learning Action Plan](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/foundational-learning-action-plan)
- **For Māori-medium, the public reporting design is culturally more specific than a simple English-medium benchmark, but Treasury identified unresolved tensions between standardised assessment and aromatawai principles.** Kauwhata Reo's Tohu Aromatawai use mokopuna evidence to identify achievement and what is needed to progress, while Treasury warned that a te reo Māori item bank might not provide enough adaptability and that data ownership/use would need engagement with Māori-medium providers. [Kauwhata Reo TMoA reporting guidance](https://kauwhatareo.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/guidance-for-kaiako-reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau-on-tmoa/5637271336.p); [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- **Bottom line: on current public evidence, the new system is a better measurement-and-signalling mechanism than a guaranteed support mechanism.** It can help teachers, parents, whānau and the Ministry see need earlier, but I found no public rule saying a below-expectation result automatically brings a specified funded intervention, staffing entitlement, specialist service, tutoring place, or school-level resourcing change. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p); [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment); [Budget 2025 learning support at a glance](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-05/Budget%202025%20At%20a%20Glance.pdf?VersionId=1czcm8qU8x7ICWr.Whd9SWb6ZGXRyRge)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the policy architecture and funding lines are well evidenced in official sources, but the absence of an automatic support trigger is based on a public-document review rather than internal Ministry operating rules or school-level implementation data.

## Evidence

### The best non-benchmark pathway is formative practice plus professional judgement

Assessment-for-learning guidance already gives New Zealand a non-mechanical way to identify need: it frames assessment as evidence gathered and used in timely ways to support further learning, and says assessment information should be used to plan next learning opportunities, adjust strategies, and identify additional support needs sooner rather than later. [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p)

The 2026 SMART guidance does not present SMART as a standalone decision-maker: it says SMART gives clear and consistent information for next learning steps, but also says tools should not replace teacher judgement and should be used alongside other assessment sources to determine progress and achievement. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

The reporting framework likewise uses a mixed-evidence design rather than a pure test-score design: parent reports can include teacher comment, assessment results, evidence of learning and Progress Markers, and the narrative should include how parents and whānau can support next learning steps. [Tāhūrangi reporting to parents and whānau](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p)

That means the practical alternative to a one-size-fits-all benchmark is not "no common evidence"; it is common progress language plus local professional judgement and support planning. [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p); [Tāhūrangi reporting to parents and whānau](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau/5637359076.p)

### SMART creates information and signalling, not a guaranteed intervention

SMART is a national information system: it is a bilingual digital tool for twice-yearly assessment and aromatawai in Years 3-10, it is free for schools and kura, and in 2026 Year 3-8 schools can use SMART, e-asTTle or PATs for twice-yearly monitoring. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

The Ministry's Budget 2025 page says the new assessment tool has $75.8m operating funding and $9.8m capital funding, supports consistent assessment and reporting for about 540,000 students, and includes training teachers to administer assessments and determine appropriate next steps for individual students. [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment)

The SMART page also says aggregated data can help the Ministry identify where additional resources or targeted investment may be required, but that is a system-level targeting claim rather than an individual entitlement rule. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

Treasury's released advice is the strongest evidence that the support chain was not fully resolved at funding-decision time: it said the benefit of more school-level data was unclear, that twice-yearly standardised assessment needed to be linked to better learning support arrangements and policy, and that a tagged contingency could require clearer intervention logic in the Māori-medium context. [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### Funded supports are real, but separate from the benchmark

For classroom and school-level response, the Ministry's Foundation Learning Action Plan names a package that includes targeted supports for learners, PLD and resources for teachers, structured teaching approaches, consistent assessment, and specialist literacy and numeracy staff. [Ministry Foundation Learning Action Plan](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/attendance-and-achievement/foundational-learning-action-plan)

For learning support, the parent-facing Ministry page says schools and kura have learning support resources and tools, that teachers or doctors can help families decide whether more support is needed, and that support can include early intervention teachers, teacher aides, learning support coordinators, RTLB, speech-language therapists, psychologists, occupational therapists and Kaitakawaenga. [Ministry learning support parent guide](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/learning-support/supporting-your-child-if-they-need-extra-help-their-learning)

Budget 2025 funded a multi-tiered learning support system with universal, targeted and highest-needs tiers, including LSC access for all Year 0-8 schools by 2028, over 2 million additional teacher-aide hours per year from 2028, targeted maths intervention teachers for Years 0-6, extension of tier 2 targeted intervention to Year 6, and a demand-based ORS model for verified high-and-complex-needs learners. [Budget 2025 learning support at a glance](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-05/Budget%202025%20At%20a%20Glance.pdf?VersionId=1czcm8qU8x7ICWr.Whd9SWb6ZGXRyRge)

RTLB are a specialist pathway, but their public description is broad learning, wellbeing and behaviour support across schools and kura, not an automatic response to a reading, writing or maths benchmark result. [Ministry RTLB page](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/learning-support/resource-teachers-learning-and-behaviour)

PLD is another support route, but it is teacher-capability support rather than an individual learner entitlement: structured literacy and te reo matatini PLD supports teachers in structured approaches and prioritises schools and kura with highest need, while Maths PLD consists of four days available in 2025 and 2026 for Year 0-8 staff in state and state-integrated schools. [PLD structured literacy and te reo matatini](https://pld.education.govt.nz/structured-literacy-and-maths-pld/structured-literacy-approaches/); [Maths PLD](https://pld.education.govt.nz/structured-literacy-and-maths-pld/maths-pld/)

Tutoring is present but targeted by programme design rather than automatically by SMART: the 2025 Year 7-8 Maths Trial tested accelerated Maths and Pāngarau programmes for students needing targeted support, and the Ministry says its findings informed national rollout from 2026 through the Year 7-8 Maths and Pāngarau Acceleration Programme and Hāpai Pāngarau Tau 7 & 8. [Tāhūrangi Accelerated Maths Tutoring Trial](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/accelerated-maths-tutoring-trial/5637248834.p)

The resource-allocation route is also mostly population and school-level: the Equity Index replaced decile funding from January 2023 and estimates socioeconomic barriers to educational achievement, while operational equity funding is based on the school Equity Index rather than on a particular child's SMART result. [Ministry Equity Index](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/changes-in-education/equity-index); [Ministry equity funding](https://www.education.govt.nz/education-professionals/schools-year-0-13/funding-and-financials/operational-funding/equity-funding)

### Māori-medium reporting is not just English-medium benchmarking, but aromatawai fit remains a live risk

Kauwhata Reo guidance says Tohu Aromatawai use mokopuna evidence for learning to identify where mokopuna are achieving and what is needed to progress, and the TMoA common reporting components include Tohu Aromatawai, a narrative on next learning steps, aromatawai results, attendance and progress over time. [Kauwhata Reo TMoA reporting guidance](https://kauwhatareo.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/guidance-for-kaiako-reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau-on-tmoa/5637271336.p)

The same guidance says the Tohu Aromatawai are common components that kura are expected to use, with descriptors including E kairangi ana, E mātau ana, E ū haere ana, E whanake haere ana and E maiangi ana. [Kauwhata Reo TMoA reporting guidance](https://kauwhatareo.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/guidance-for-kaiako-reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau-on-tmoa/5637271336.p)

SMART's formal 2026 requirement is delayed for kura kaupapa Māori, Ngā Kura ā-Iwi and specified hapū/iwi-based special-character schools until 1 January 2027, which gives a policy buffer but does not itself solve the design question. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

Treasury warned that e-asTTle use in Māori-medium settings was low, that standardised assessment may not fit neatly with aromatawai principles of ākonga participation, that a te reo Māori item bank might not be adaptable enough, and that Māori-medium providers would likely want consultation on data ownership and use. [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### What support follows when a learner is below expectation?

The public answer is "it depends on pathway and local judgement." Classroom formative practice should adjust teaching and identify extra support needs; reporting should tell parents and whānau next steps; SMART should inform teacher judgement; learning support can bring LSC, RTLB or specialists; tutoring and targeted intervention programmes can support selected learners; PLD can improve teacher practice; and school/system resourcing can be informed by aggregate data and equity indicators. [Tāhūrangi assessment for learning](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/assessment-for-learning/5637190483.p); [Ministry learning support parent guide](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/learning-support/supporting-your-child-if-they-need-extra-help-their-learning); [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p); [Budget 2025 learning support at a glance](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-05/Budget%202025%20At%20a%20Glance.pdf?VersionId=1czcm8qU8x7ICWr.Whd9SWb6ZGXRyRge)

I found no public rule that a learner below a SMART/progress-marker expectation automatically receives a named funded support, and the strongest contrary evidence is Treasury's warning that the intervention logic still needed to distinguish how twice-yearly standardised assessment would improve learning support arrangements compared with more flexible assessment arrangements. [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment)

The practical risk is therefore not that no support exists, but that measurement can outpace response capacity: the Ministry's 2025 Annual Report says demand for specialist learning support grew 26% since 2017/18, creating longer waits, while Budget 2025 responds with expanded learning-support investment and a changed ORS model. [Ministry of Education Annual Report 2025](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/Annual%20Report%202025.pdf?VersionId=btvOLyb4FgtZo8WcTQoaT8W.0BIHhWHb); [Budget 2025 learning support at a glance](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-05/Budget%202025%20At%20a%20Glance.pdf?VersionId=1czcm8qU8x7ICWr.Whd9SWb6ZGXRyRge)

## What would change this conclusion

- A published Ministry operating model showing that a defined SMART, progress-marker, Tohu Aromatawai or other below-expectation signal automatically triggers funded tutoring, RTLB input, LSC action, specialist assessment, teacher-aide hours, PLD, or school-level resourcing would shift the conclusion toward "support mechanism." I did not find that trigger in the public sources reviewed. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p); [Ministry Budget 2025 curriculum and assessment](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/corporate-documents/budget-2025/curriculum-and-assessment)
- Evidence from 2026 implementation showing that schools consistently convert assessment evidence into timely, funded support for below-expectation learners would weaken the "mainly measurement" conclusion. I did not find a 2026 outcomes evaluation of SMART-to-support conversion, and the first SMART detailed reports were only released on 3 July 2026. [Tāhūrangi SMART overview](https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)
- Māori-medium provider evidence showing that SMART/aromatawai data governance, item design, whānau reporting and intervention pathways are accepted and useful would change the Māori-medium risk assessment. Treasury's released advice identifies this as a live design and engagement issue, not as resolved evidence. [Treasury SMART OIA 20250889](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [Kauwhata Reo TMoA reporting guidance](https://kauwhatareo.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/guidance-for-kaiako-reporting-to-parents-and-wh-nau-on-tmoa/5637271336.p)
- A human with school leadership, learning-support, RTLB, Māori-medium, disability, whānau or student experience should review whether this finding underweights informal support pathways that are real in schools but not visible in national policy documents. The public sources show formal programmes and funding lines, but not whether individual children receive help quickly after a below-expectation result. [Ministry learning support parent guide](https://www.education.govt.nz/parents-and-caregivers/schools-year-0-13/learning-support/supporting-your-child-if-they-need-extra-help-their-learning)
