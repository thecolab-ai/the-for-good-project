# Universal light-vehicle RUC would shift cost toward high-kilometre efficient-vehicle users, with the sharpest hardship risks for low-income, rural, disabled, and digitally excluded drivers

## Executive answer

- **The clearest cash losers are not simply "low-income drivers"; they are petrol drivers who travel many kilometres in efficient petrol or non-plug-in hybrid vehicles.** At current published rates, petrol excise duty is 70.024 cents per litre and the ordinary light-vehicle RUC rate is $76 per 1,000km including GST, so a simple consumer-price break-even is about 9.4 L/100km if petrol excise is fully removed and the light RUC rate is used unchanged; drivers below that fuel-use threshold would pay more per kilometre under distance charging, while drivers above it could pay less [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges).
- **Low-income households are most exposed to hardship and compliance friction even where the per-kilometre tax change is ambiguous.** Ministry advice on vehicle licence fees says the poorest households spend about 30% of their household budgets on transport compared with 8% for the wealthiest households, and the RUC RIS says road users may face extra cost and effort during transition [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf); [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).
- **Rural and isolated drivers are likely to be over-represented among people with high kilometres and few alternatives.** The Ministry's distributional transport report shows rural households in 2018/19 were more likely than major-urban households to report fuel expenditure and had higher mean petrol-only and diesel-only expenditure, and Ministry advice separately says rural or isolated residents have fewer alternative transport options [Ministry of Transport distributional report, 2022](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/The-Distributional-Impacts-of-Transport-final-report-004-with-cover.pdf); [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf).
- **Disabled people face a different risk: dependence and access, not just kilometres.** Stats NZ's 2023 Household Disability Survey found 851,000 people living in New Zealand households were disabled, Whaikaha reports 53% of disabled people had not enough or only just enough income to meet basic needs, and Ministry transport material says Total Mobility exists for disabled people who cannot use buses, trains or ferries independently some or all of the time [Stats NZ, Disability statistics 2023](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/disability-statistics-2023/); [Whaikaha summary of HDS 2023](https://www.whaikaha.govt.nz/news/news/17-percent-of-new-zealanders-are-disabled); [Ministry of Transport Total Mobility consultation, 2026](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Consultation/Total-Mobility/Total-Mobility-Discussion-Document-Final.pdf).
- **Digital access is a first-order implementation risk for electronic RUC.** The RUC RIS explicitly says lack of digital skills or limited access to digital services may disadvantage some users, and Digital.govt.nz's digital-inclusion evidence identifies people in social housing, disabled people, Maori, Pasifika, larger country towns, older people, unemployed people and people not seeking work as groups prone to lower internet access [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf); [Digital.govt.nz, Digital inclusion and wellbeing in New Zealand](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/161~digital-inclusion-and-wellbeing-in-new-zealand/html).

**Overall confidence: Medium.** The direction of risk is well supported by official Ministry, Stats NZ, Digital.govt.nz and public-submission evidence, but no public source found here directly models universal light-vehicle RUC by household income, rurality, disability status, digital access, vehicle efficiency and vehicle kilometres travelled in one dataset.

## Evidence

### What changes when petrol vehicles move from fuel excise to RUC

Petrol excise charges litres of petrol, while RUC charges distance and vehicle class; the Ministry's published 2020/21 ordinary rates list petrol excise duty at 70.024 cents per litre and a light-vehicle RUC distance licence at $76 per 1,000km including GST [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges). If the full petrol excise component and its GST disappear from the pump price and the ordinary $76/1,000km light RUC rate applies unchanged, the consumer-price break-even is approximately `76 / (0.70024 * 1.15 * 10) = 9.4 L/100km`; below that fuel-consumption level a petrol driver pays less fuel excise today than the RUC charge, and above it a petrol driver pays more fuel excise today than the RUC charge [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges).

That break-even is illustrative rather than a forecast because the Government has not set a final full-transition date or final universal light-vehicle RUC design in the sources reviewed; the Bill's policy is to enable a future transition from fuel excise duty to RUC, and the active Bill was at Committee of the whole House stage after second reading on 30 June 2026 [New Zealand Parliament, Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/d31385e9-dd9d-4cbd-c52d-08de22297aa7); [Select Committee report, 18 May 2026](https://selectcommittees.parliament.nz/download/SelectCommitteeReport/21247aa4-d7ce-4527-7b02-08deb491c3a4). The Ministry's RUC RIS also treats alternative payment schemes as implementation details still to be worked through, including monthly or estimated periodic payments, odometer reporting intervals, missed-payment consequences, unpaid-RUC debt and provider roles [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

The shift therefore has two distributional channels: a **rate channel** that moves burden from fuel consumption to kilometres, and an **administration channel** that moves users from automatic payment at the pump to licence, account, billing, reporting or provider relationships [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). The Ministry's RIS explicitly says its analysis did not assess horizontal fairness or equity, so the public policy case is missing the household-level fairness test needed to say who is worse off with high confidence [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

### Income: cost burden is clear; net tax direction is not

Low-income households are more vulnerable to any added transport cost because Ministry advice says the poorest households already spend 30% of household budgets on transport, compared with 8% for the wealthiest households [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf). The same advice says households in higher-deprivation areas tend to be more car-dependent, have more limited access to services such as healthcare, social services and supermarkets, and are underserved by public transport alternatives [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf).

The Ministry's distributional transport report makes the net-income story more complex: it says transport-fuel price impacts are often weakly progressive or proportional in developed-country studies, and its New Zealand analysis found the highest expenditure group often spent the lowest share of total budget on fuel, while lower-income households have less ability to adapt to price increases [Ministry of Transport distributional report, 2022](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/The-Distributional-Impacts-of-Transport-final-report-004-with-cover.pdf). For universal RUC, that means the poorest households are not automatically the largest **per-kilometre** losers, but they are the households least able to absorb prepayment, transaction fees, debt penalties, provider fees or unexpected high-distance bills [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf); [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf).

Current RUC purchasing already creates a small-block penalty: NZTA's public RUC pages list transaction fees, and the AA submission says current RUC buyers pay $12.44 online or $13.71 at an agent each time RUC is purchased, making compliance more expensive for people who cannot afford to buy large kilometre blocks in advance [NZTA, RUC rates and transaction fees](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/road-user-charges/ruc-rates-and-transaction-fees); [AA submission on the Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill, 2025](https://www.aa.co.nz/content/dam/nzaa/01-brand/governance---reporting/governance/government-submissions/2025/AA%20submission%20on%20the%20Land%20Transport%20(Revenue)%20Amendment%20Bill.pdf). The AA supports the RUC amendments but says market success is not guaranteed, private-sector fees are unknown, and NZTA should remain a fallback option until market pricing is at least comparable with NZTA's fees [AA submission, 2025](https://www.aa.co.nz/content/dam/nzaa/01-brand/governance---reporting/governance/government-submissions/2025/AA%20submission%20on%20the%20Land%20Transport%20(Revenue)%20Amendment%20Bill.pdf).

### Rurality: high travel exposure plus fewer alternatives

Rural households appear more exposed to distance-based charging because the Ministry's 2018/19 HES-based table reports rural households had a 65.2% any-fuel-expenditure share, mean petrol-only expenditure of $3,183 and mean diesel-only expenditure of $379, compared with major-urban households at 53.5%, $2,468 and $112 respectively [Ministry of Transport distributional report, Table 4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/The-Distributional-Impacts-of-Transport-final-report-004-with-cover.pdf). In the same report's regression, each additional minute from the nearest Territorial Authority Ward centre was associated with about $14 higher annual fuel expenditure, which supports distance-to-services as a distributional risk for fuel or distance charges [Ministry of Transport distributional report, 2022](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/The-Distributional-Impacts-of-Transport-final-report-004-with-cover.pdf).

Ministry vehicle-licence-fee advice makes the rural vulnerability explicit: for people in rural or isolated areas, alternative transport options are harder to access, private-vehicle use is not optional in some situations, and extra vehicle costs can add barriers to social, cultural and economic participation [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf). Infrastructure New Zealand's submission independently warns that usage-based charging may disproportionately affect rural communities, low-income households and people without viable transport alternatives, and recommends a social-impact assessment and income bands for people required to use a vehicle for work, school or important medical care [Infrastructure New Zealand submission, 2026](https://infrastructure.org.nz/http-infrastructure-org-nz-wp-content-uploads-2026-01-7-1-26-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill-submission-pdf/).

This does not mean every rural driver pays more under universal RUC. Rural drivers with high-consumption petrol vehicles could pay less per kilometre if petrol excise is fully replaced by a flat light RUC at the current rate, while rural drivers with efficient petrol or non-plug-in hybrid vehicles and long annual kilometres would pay more [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges). The rural equity question is therefore a combined question of kilometres, fuel efficiency, income, essential-trip purpose and availability of alternatives, not rural residence alone [Ministry of Transport distributional report, 2022](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/The-Distributional-Impacts-of-Transport-final-report-004-with-cover.pdf); [Infrastructure New Zealand submission, 2026](https://infrastructure.org.nz/http-infrastructure-org-nz-wp-content-uploads-2026-01-7-1-26-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill-submission-pdf/).

### Disability: highest risk where accessible travel is car-dependent or digitally mediated

Stats NZ's 2023 Household Disability Survey found that 17% of people living in New Zealand households, or 851,000 people, were disabled [Stats NZ, Disability statistics 2023](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/disability-statistics-2023/). Whaikaha's summary of the same survey says 53% of disabled people reported having not enough income or only just enough income to meet basic needs, compared with 33% of non-disabled people [Whaikaha, 17 percent of New Zealanders are disabled](https://www.whaikaha.govt.nz/news/news/17-percent-of-new-zealanders-are-disabled).

Transport-system evidence shows why a private or subsidised door-to-door option can be essential for some disabled people: the Ministry's 2026 Total Mobility consultation says Total Mobility helps disabled people travel when they cannot take buses, trains or ferries independently some or all of the time, and that users had been paying 25% of fares after the Community Connect subsidy before changes from 1 July 2026 reduce the subsidy from 75% to 65% [Ministry of Transport Total Mobility consultation, 2026](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Consultation/Total-Mobility/Total-Mobility-Discussion-Document-Final.pdf). Ministry vehicle-licence-fee advice also says some disabled people are less likely than non-disabled people to find it easy to access doctors, supermarkets, parks and public transport, and that private vehicles can materially affect access to needed places [Ministry of Transport vehicle-licence-fee RIA, 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Regulatory-Impact-Assessment-Increasing-annual-vehicle-licence-fees-2024..pdf).

The disabled-user risk under universal RUC is therefore not just whether an individual disabled driver travels more kilometres than average. It is whether a disabled person, caregiver, whānau vehicle, modified vehicle, Total Mobility provider or support service faces higher unavoidable travel costs, added digital/account administration, or penalties for missed readings or payments in a context where alternatives are already constrained [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf); [Ministry of Transport Total Mobility consultation, 2026](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Consultation/Total-Mobility/Total-Mobility-Discussion-Document-Final.pdf).

### Digital access and privacy: e-RUC convenience can become exclusion

The RUC reform is explicitly digital-facing: the RIS discusses alternative payment schemes through websites or apps, estimated or monthly billing, odometer reporting at intervals, automated payments and software-based solutions [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf). The same RIS says road users may face additional cost and effort during transition, and that lack of digital skills or limited digital-service access may disadvantage some users [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).

Digital.govt.nz's digital-inclusion evidence says groups prone to lower internet access include people in social housing, disabled people, Pasifika, Maori, people in larger country towns, older people over 75, unemployed people and people not actively seeking work; it also says people in social housing and disabled people appear particularly disadvantaged for internet access [Digital.govt.nz, Digital inclusion and wellbeing in New Zealand](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/161~digital-inclusion-and-wellbeing-in-new-zealand/html). Motu's source paper reports that only 71% of people with disabilities had internet access in the 2017 NZES and that 17% of people with disabilities reported no internet access in the 2018 NZCVS, compared with 5% in the full sample [Motu, Digital inclusion and wellbeing in New Zealand](https://motu-www.motu.org.nz/wpapers/19_17.pdf).

Submitters also treat digital RUC as a privacy and consumer-protection issue rather than a purely technical improvement. Infrastructure New Zealand supports digital RUC but calls for robust privacy safeguards, transparent auditing and accessible dispute mechanisms [Infrastructure New Zealand submission, 2026](https://infrastructure.org.nz/http-infrastructure-org-nz-wp-content-uploads-2026-01-7-1-26-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill-submission-pdf/). The New Zealand Initiative supports RUC modernisation but recommends privacy-protective defaults, data minimisation, purpose limitation, security standards and interoperability across RUC, tolling and time-of-use systems [New Zealand Initiative submission, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/submissions/submission-land-transport/document/911).

### What submissions add to the public-data picture

Public submissions mostly support modernising RUC but differ on safeguards. Infrastructure New Zealand explicitly names rural communities, low-income households and people without viable alternatives as groups at risk from usage-based charging [Infrastructure New Zealand submission, 2026](https://infrastructure.org.nz/http-infrastructure-org-nz-wp-content-uploads-2026-01-7-1-26-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill-submission-pdf/). The AA supports the proposed RUC Act changes but warns that current RUC purchasing is harder and more expensive for people who cannot buy large volumes, that private-sector RUC fees are unknown, and that compliance improvement is essential before petrol-fleet transition [AA submission, 2025](https://www.aa.co.nz/content/dam/nzaa/01-brand/governance---reporting/governance/government-submissions/2025/AA%20submission%20on%20the%20Land%20Transport%20(Revenue)%20Amendment%20Bill.pdf). The New Zealand Initiative supports road pricing but says equity concerns should be addressed through targeted measures such as direct rebates and improved public transport alternatives rather than broad exemptions [New Zealand Initiative submission, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/submissions/submission-land-transport/document/911).

Drive Electric adds an environmental-equity caution: it supports digital RUC modernisation but opposes a purely distance- and weight-based long-term regime without an emissions component because it says that would penalise EVs for battery weight and remove efficiency incentives embedded in fuel excise [Drive Electric submission page, 2026](https://driveelectric.org.nz/policy-submissions/drive-electric-submission-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill/). That point matters for distribution because a flat distance charge makes efficient petrol cars and hybrids pay more per kilometre than they do under petrol excise, but it also weakens the fuel-saving incentive that currently rewards all efficient vehicles [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges); [Drive Electric submission page, 2026](https://driveelectric.org.nz/policy-submissions/drive-electric-submission-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Ministry/NZTA microsimulation that joins household income, rurality, disability status, digital access, vehicle type, fuel efficiency, annual vehicle kilometres travelled, provider fees, compliance penalties and likely RUC rates would replace this risk-ranking with a proper distribution table [Ministry of Transport RUC RIS, 2025](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/RUC-RIS-updated.pdf).
- Final regulations that guarantee low-fee manual options, post-pay options, hardship plans, accessible dispute processes, privacy-minimising defaults and capped provider fees would reduce the digital, cashflow and disability-access risks identified here [Ministry of Transport RUC regulations consultation, 2026](https://consult.transport.govt.nz/policy/proposed-regulations-for-road-user-charges/user_uploads/road-user-charges-regulations-consultation-document.pdf).
- A final universal light-vehicle RUC rate materially below $76/1,000km, a retained fuel-efficiency or emissions component, or targeted rebates for essential high-kilometre users would change the cash-loser ranking [Ministry of Transport, PED/RUC rates](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/rates-of-petrol-excise-duty-and-road-user-charges); [Drive Electric submission page, 2026](https://driveelectric.org.nz/policy-submissions/drive-electric-submission-land-transport-revenue-amendment-bill/).
- I could not verify a public official submission bundle with every select-committee submission in easily searchable form; I used the Parliament report page plus submitters' public pages/PDFs that could be fetched or extracted [New Zealand Parliament, Land Transport (Revenue) Amendment Bill](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/d31385e9-dd9d-4cbd-c52d-08de22297aa7).
- I could not verify a current public dataset that cross-tabs light-vehicle fuel efficiency, household income, disability status, rurality and digital access in one file; that is the main reason confidence is Medium rather than High.
- Human review is needed from disabled people, rural communities, low-income driver advocates and digital-inclusion providers because public administrative data cannot decide what level of monitoring, prepayment, privacy risk, hardship process or manual-service burden is acceptable.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would a RUC hardship design look like if it targeted essential high-kilometre travel rather than income alone?
- Should universal RUC retain an emissions or fuel-efficiency component, and how would that affect low-income efficient-vehicle owners versus high-income EV owners?
- What minimum non-digital service standard should NZTA or approved providers be required to offer before petrol vehicles enter RUC?
- How should RUC treat modified vehicles, caregiver-driven vehicles, Total Mobility providers and disability-support travel?
