# New Zealand is not an OECD high-consumption outlier, but its alcohol harm burden remains large and pattern-sensitive

## Executive answer

- **The corrected OECD consumption claim holds:** OECD Health at a Glance 2025 puts New Zealand's recorded alcohol consumption at 8.2 litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+ in 2023, slightly below the OECD37 average of 8.5 and below the United Kingdom at 9.3 and Australia at 10.5, although the OECD workbook marks Australia's value as the latest available from 2022. [OECD New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025-country-notes_2f94481e/new-zealand_38876760/2154f240-en.pdf); [OECD Figure 4.10 StatLink workbook](https://stat.link/files/8f9e3f98-en/gb5xzw.xlsx)
- **New Zealand's domestic supply trend points down, not up:** Stats NZ's year-ended December 2025 release reports total alcoholic beverage available for consumption fell 8.3% to 442 million litres and pure alcohol available fell 7.6%, while the underlying CSV gives 7.19 litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+ in 2025 after 7.85 litres in 2024 and 8.22 litres in 2023. [Stats NZ 2025 alcohol release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025/); [Stats NZ alcohol CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Alcohol-available-for-consumption/Alcohol-available-for-consumption-Year-ended-December-2025/Download-data/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025.csv)
- **Drinking pattern still matters:** the New Zealand Health Survey 2024/25 reports 74.9% of adults drank alcohol in the previous year, 16.6% were hazardous drinkers on the AUDIT measure, and 8.2% drank six or more drinks on one occasion at least weekly; those rates are lower than 2019/20 for past-year drinking, hazardous drinking, and weekly heavy sessions, but they still describe a large current risk population. [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)
- **International harm rankings depend on the measure:** WHO modelled 2019 alcohol-attributable all-cause death rates put New Zealand at 18.6 deaths per 100,000, close to Australia at 18.2 and below the United Kingdom at 21.9, while WHO modelled alcohol-attributable DALY rates put New Zealand higher than both Australia and the United Kingdom. [WHO GHO alcohol-attributable death-rate indicator](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/alcohol-attributable-all-cause-deaths--age-standardized-death-rates-%28all-ages%29--per-100-000-population); [WHO GHO API, alcohol-attributable deaths](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001832); [WHO GHO API, alcohol-attributable DALYs](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001833)
- **Policy comparison should use instruments, not one "harm" proxy:** OECD identifies price, availability, drink-driving enforcement, advertising restrictions, brief intervention, and dependence treatment as separable policy levers; New Zealand has excise and a health levy plus local licensing reforms, Scotland has minimum unit pricing with evaluated reductions in deaths and hospital admissions, and Australia and England/Scotland publish narrower administrative harm series that are not directly interchangeable with New Zealand's broad attributable-burden report. [OECD harmful alcohol policy chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preventing-harmful-alcohol-use_6e4b4ffb-en/full-report/component-13.html); [New Zealand alcohol levy review](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-08/alcohol-levy-review-final-report-aug24.pdf); [Beehive alcohol law changes](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws); [Public Health Scotland MUP evaluation](https://publichealthscotland.scot/news/2023/june/minimum-unit-pricing-reduces-alcohol-related-harm-to-health/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the consumption and current New Zealand survey conclusions are strong because they come from OECD, Stats NZ, and the Ministry of Health, but cross-country harm comparisons are only medium confidence because WHO modelled attributable burden, New Zealand's Health NZ burden report, AIHW Australian administrative series, and UK/Scotland alcohol-specific series use different definitions and reference years.

## Evidence

### 1. Recorded consumption

The OECD 2025 country note says New Zealand's 2023 alcohol consumption was 8.2 litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+, compared with an OECD average of 8.5 litres. [OECD New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025-country-notes_2f94481e/new-zealand_38876760/2154f240-en.pdf)

The OECD StatLink data behind Figure 4.10 gives New Zealand at 8.2 litres, OECD37 at 8.5 litres, the United Kingdom at 9.3 litres, and Australia at 10.5 litres; the OECD workbook footnote marks Australia's value as the latest available from 2022 rather than 2023. [OECD Figure 4.10 StatLink workbook](https://stat.link/files/8f9e3f98-en/gb5xzw.xlsx)

The OECD methods note says the consumption indicator is annual sales of pure alcohol in litres per person aged 15+, and it warns that conversion methods vary and that the indicator generally does not adjust for tourist consumption or unrecorded consumption except for some countries. [OECD alcohol consumption chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/alcohol-consumption_f009f222.html)

WHO's SDG 3.5.2 total per-capita alcohol-consumption series gives a similar ordering for 2024, with New Zealand at 8.9 litres, Australia at 10.5 litres, and the United Kingdom at 10.4 litres. [WHO GHO API, total alcohol per-capita consumption](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001688)

Stats NZ's 2025 domestic-availability series is lower again than the OECD 2023 country-note value because it is newer and domestic-supply based: Stats NZ reports 31.294 million litres of pure alcohol available in 2025 and the CSV series `ALCA.SAL1D` gives 7.1865 litres of pure alcohol per person aged 15+ in the year ended December 2025. [Stats NZ 2025 alcohol release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025/); [Stats NZ alcohol CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Alcohol-available-for-consumption/Alcohol-available-for-consumption-Year-ended-December-2025/Download-data/alcohol-available-for-consumption-year-ended-december-2025.csv)

### 2. Heavy episodic, hazardous, and weekly heavy drinking

The New Zealand Health Survey 2024/25 reports 74.9% of adults drank alcohol in the previous year, down from 81.6% in 2019/20. [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)

The same survey reports 16.6% of adults were hazardous drinkers in 2024/25, using an AUDIT score of 8 or more, down from 21.3% in 2019/20. [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)

The same survey reports 8.2% of adults drank six or more drinks on one occasion at least weekly in 2024/25, down from 12.0% in 2019/20. [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)

WHO's heavy episodic drinking indicator is not the same as the New Zealand Health Survey hazardous-drinking measure because WHO defines heavy episodic drinking as at least 60 grams of pure alcohol on one occasion in the past 30 days among adults aged 15+, while NZHS hazardous drinking uses the AUDIT risk score. [WHO heavy episodic drinking indicator](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/alcohol-heavy-episodic-drinking-%28population%29-past-30-days-%28-%29); [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)

WHO's modelled 2020 heavy episodic drinking rate for the whole adult population is 41.4% for New Zealand, 39.4% for Australia, and 33.3% for the United Kingdom, but this should not be used as a current 2024/25 New Zealand rate because it is older, modelled, includes abstainers in the denominator, and uses the WHO 60-gram past-30-days definition. [WHO GHO API, heavy episodic drinking population](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001739); [WHO heavy episodic drinking indicator](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/alcohol-heavy-episodic-drinking-%28population%29-past-30-days-%28-%29)

OECD's 2025 heavy episodic drinking figure does not include a New Zealand value, so it cannot be used to rank New Zealand against the OECD heavy episodic drinking average. [OECD alcohol consumption chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/alcohol-consumption_f009f222.html); [OECD Figure 4.11 StatLink workbook](https://stat.link/files/8f9e3f98-en/b5meiy.xlsx)

### 3. Alcohol-attributable mortality, hospitalisation, and burden

WHO's alcohol-attributable all-cause death-rate indicator estimates deaths per 100,000 population that would not have occurred if alcohol exposure had been removed, based on alcohol-attributable fractions and country mortality data. [WHO alcohol-attributable death-rate indicator](https://www.who.int/data/gho/data/indicators/indicator-details/GHO/alcohol-attributable-all-cause-deaths--age-standardized-death-rates-%28all-ages%29--per-100-000-population)

WHO's 2019 all-sexes, all-ages alcohol-attributable all-cause death-rate series gives New Zealand 18.6 deaths per 100,000, Australia 18.2, and the United Kingdom 21.9. [WHO GHO API, alcohol-attributable deaths](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001832)

WHO's 2019 all-sexes, all-ages alcohol-attributable DALY-rate series gives New Zealand 1,637 DALYs per 100,000, Australia 1,317, and the United Kingdom 1,355. [WHO GHO API, alcohol-attributable DALYs](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001833)

Health NZ's published 2024 burden report estimates that alcohol caused 901 deaths, 1,250 cancers, 29,282 hospitalisations, 49,742 DALYs, and 128,963 ACC claims in New Zealand in 2018. [Health NZ Figshare article metadata](https://api.figshare.com/v2/articles/27048892); [Health NZ alcohol burden report PDF](https://ndownloader.figshare.com/files/49256578); [University of Otago report summary](https://www.otago.ac.nz/news/newsroom/health-burden-of-alcohol-highlighted-in-report)

The Otago summary of the Health NZ-published report says the 2018 burden numbers are underestimates because the report estimates harms to the drinker and excludes harms to others such as people injured by a drink driver. [University of Otago report summary](https://www.otago.ac.nz/news/newsroom/health-burden-of-alcohol-highlighted-in-report)

AIHW reports that alcohol use was responsible for 4.1% of total burden of disease and injury in Australia in 2024, 84,318 alcohol-related hospitalisations in 2023/24 at a rate of 312.8 per 100,000, and 1,765 preliminary alcohol-induced deaths in 2024 at an age-standardised rate of 5.9 per 100,000. [AIHW alcohol page](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/alcohol/alcohol-tobacco-other-drugs-australia/contents/drug-types/alcohol)

The UK Office for National Statistics reports 9,809 alcohol-specific deaths in the United Kingdom in 2024 at an age-standardised rate of 14.8 per 100,000, and it defines alcohol-specific deaths as deaths from conditions wholly attributable to alcohol. [ONS alcohol-specific deaths 2024](https://www.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/healthandsocialcare/causesofdeath/bulletins/alcoholrelateddeathsintheunitedkingdom/registeredin2024)

England's alcohol profile reports 339,916 alcohol-specific admissions, 280,747 narrow alcohol-related admissions, and 1,018,986 broad alcohol-related admissions in 2023/24, and it reports 8,274 alcohol-specific deaths and 22,644 alcohol-related deaths for England in 2023. [GOV.UK alcohol profile February 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/alcohol-profile-february-2025-update/alcohol-profile-short-statistical-commentary-february-2025)

Scotland reports 29,430 hospital admissions wholly attributable to alcohol in 2024/25 and 1,185 alcohol-specific deaths in 2024, with the deaths rate at 20.9 per 100,000. [Public Health Scotland hospital statistics 2024/25](https://publichealthscotland.scot/publications/alcohol-related-hospital-statistics/alcohol-related-hospital-statistics-scotland-financial-year-2024-to-2025/); [National Records of Scotland alcohol-specific deaths 2024](https://www.nrscotland.gov.uk/publications/alcohol-specific-deaths-2024/)

### 4. Policy settings

OECD's harmful-alcohol policy review separates interventions across information and education, pricing, availability, drink-driving enforcement, advertising regulation, health-care counselling, and pharmacological treatment for dependence. [OECD harmful alcohol policy chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preventing-harmful-alcohol-use_6e4b4ffb-en/full-report/component-13.html)

OECD reports that taxation, minimum unit pricing, sobriety checkpoints, reduced outlet opening hours, and alcohol counselling in primary care can reduce harmful alcohol use, and it reports the largest health-expenditure savings for minimum unit pricing in its modelled policy set. [OECD harmful alcohol policy chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preventing-harmful-alcohol-use_6e4b4ffb-en/full-report/component-13.html)

New Zealand's alcohol policy setting includes excise and a Pae Ora alcohol levy: the 2024 levy review says alcohol excise accounted for 20.7% to 55.9% of alcoholic-beverage prices and that the 2023/24 levy fund was $11.5 million. [New Zealand alcohol levy review](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-08/alcohol-levy-review-final-report-aug24.pdf)

New Zealand also changed licensing participation rules in 2023 by removing appeals against provisional local alcohol policies, removing cross-examination at licensing hearings, allowing any person or group to object to a licence application, and extending objection timeframes. [Beehive alcohol law changes](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/huge-win-communities-passing-new-alcohol-laws)

New Zealand has considered minimum unit pricing through an official Ministry of Justice report that modelled $1.00 and $1.20 per standard drink options, estimated ten-year net savings from those options, and stated that comparable excise increases would deliver larger modelled savings while affecting all alcoholic beverages. [Ministry of Justice minimum pricing report](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/key-initiatives/key-initiatives-archive/sale-and-supply-of-alcohol/alcohol-minimum-pricing-report/)

Scotland is the most useful UK policy comparator for minimum unit pricing because Public Health Scotland's evaluation estimated that minimum unit pricing reduced deaths wholly attributable to alcohol by 13.4%, reduced alcohol-related hospital admissions by 4.1%, and reduced population alcohol consumption by 3.0%. [Public Health Scotland MUP evaluation](https://publichealthscotland.scot/news/2023/june/minimum-unit-pricing-reduces-alcohol-related-harm-to-health/)

## What would change this conclusion

- A newer OECD or WHO cross-country release that gives New Zealand higher recorded alcohol consumption than the OECD average, Australia, or the United Kingdom would overturn the consumption conclusion. [OECD Figure 4.10 StatLink workbook](https://stat.link/files/8f9e3f98-en/gb5xzw.xlsx); [WHO GHO API, total alcohol per-capita consumption](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001688)
- A current cross-country survey that applies the same hazardous-drinking or heavy episodic drinking definition to New Zealand, Australia, the United Kingdom, and the OECD would improve the pattern comparison; OECD's 2025 heavy episodic drinking figure omits New Zealand, and WHO's comparable HED series is modelled for 2020. [OECD Figure 4.11 StatLink workbook](https://stat.link/files/8f9e3f98-en/b5meiy.xlsx); [WHO GHO API, heavy episodic drinking population](https://ghoapi.azureedge.net/api/SA_0000001739)
- A current New Zealand alcohol-attributable burden update using 2024/25 consumption and health data would change the harm conclusion, because the Health NZ burden report is based on 2018 outcomes and the NZHS shows declines in hazardous and weekly heavy drinking since 2019/20. [Health NZ alcohol burden report PDF](https://ndownloader.figshare.com/files/49256578); [Ministry of Health NZHS 2024/25](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/annual-update-of-key-results-202425-new-zealand-health-survey)
- A harmonised administrative dataset that maps New Zealand hospitalisations, Australian alcohol-related hospitalisations, England broad/narrow alcohol-related admissions, and Scotland wholly attributable admissions to one denominator and one definition would change the system-demand comparison. [AIHW alcohol page](https://www.aihw.gov.au/reports/alcohol/alcohol-tobacco-other-drugs-australia/contents/drug-types/alcohol); [GOV.UK alcohol profile February 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/alcohol-profile-february-2025-update/alcohol-profile-short-statistical-commentary-february-2025); [Public Health Scotland hospital statistics 2024/25](https://publichealthscotland.scot/publications/alcohol-related-hospital-statistics/alcohol-related-hospital-statistics-scotland-financial-year-2024-to-2025/)
- I could not verify a single official OECD "alcohol harm" ranking that combines consumption, hazardous drinking, mortality, hospitalisation, and policy settings into one measure, and this finding should not be used as if such a ranking exists. [OECD alcohol consumption chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/alcohol-consumption_f009f222.html); [OECD harmful alcohol policy chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preventing-harmful-alcohol-use_6e4b4ffb-en/full-report/component-13.html)
- Human and policy-specialist review is needed before turning this comparison into a recommended intervention package, because cross-country benchmark evidence does not decide which New Zealand communities, services, licensing settings, or equity impacts should be prioritised. [For Good Project streams and human gates](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/STREAMS.md)
