# Supermarket shelf and online representations can be matched to price histories with a privacy-safe evidence pack

## Executive answer

- A regulator- or public-facing supermarket example should not rely on price-history data alone: it needs a captured consumer-facing representation, the exact product, store, date/time, and the later matched price-history rows, because New Zealand pricing law turns on representations such as displayed price, advertised price, "special", "was/now", unit price, and checkout price rather than on high prices or price rises by themselves [Consumer Protection, misleading prices or advertising](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising); [Commerce Commission, pricing guidance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/buying-goods-and-services/pricing/); [existing pricing-law finding](nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md).
- The safest minimum artifact is an **evidence pack**: original capture file, redacted publishable copy, capture metadata, product/store identifiers, displayed claim fields, Grocer/retailer price-history extracts, matching notes, checksums, and a short confidence assessment [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md); [Digital Preservation Coalition, fixity and checksums](https://www.dpconline.org/handbook/technical-solutions-and-tools/fixity-and-checksums).
- Privacy risk is manageable if capture is deliberately narrow: photograph only the label, product, shelf bay, screen, or checkout display needed to evidence the claim; avoid faces, bodies, loyalty-card details, receipts, payment cards, order numbers, addresses, account names, and geolocation beyond the store/date needed for the claim, because photos and recordings can be personal information when they identify a living person [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, recording people in public places](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/recordings-of-people-in-public-places/); [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act).
- The pack should separate **private originals** from **public evidence**: keep the original file and metadata for reviewer/regulator verification, but publish only a redacted image or cropped screenshot plus non-personal metadata and derived metrics, because the Privacy Act obligations for agencies are about collection, storage, use, and disclosure of personal information [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, recording people in public places](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/recordings-of-people-in-public-places/); [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act).
- Confidence should stay **Medium**, not High, unless a human reviewer reproduces the product/store/date match, checks legal wording, and confirms the displayed representation, checkout price, and history rows all refer to the same product and store; Grocer history is useful but its documented history rows are leaner than current rows and do not by themselves prove what shoppers saw on a shelf or screen [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md); [existing reference-pricing method finding](supermarket-pricing-method.md).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the legal need for exact representations is strongly supported by official NZ sources, and the technical matching fields are already documented in project findings and the `grocer-nz` skill. Confidence is not High because I did not run a live in-store capture, test retailer terms for screenshot reuse, or obtain Commerce Commission, retailer, privacy-law, or evidence-law review of the proposed evidence-pack protocol.

## Evidence

### Why a captured representation is required

The Fair Trading Act framing for supermarket pricing is representation-based: Consumer Protection says advertised or displayed prices must not mislead, special offers and discounts must be genuine, and a trader that displays a price cannot charge more than the displayed price without likely Fair Trading Act risk [Consumer Protection, misleading prices or advertising](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising). Commerce Commission consumer guidance likewise treats special offers, limitations, qualifications, and price display as the consumer-facing facts that matter [Commerce Commission, pricing guidance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/buying-goods-and-services/pricing/).

The Commerce Commission says its grocery work includes pricing and promotional transparency for consumers and that pricing and promotional practices remain among its grocery-sector priorities [Commerce Commission, Grocery](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/). The Commission's December 2024 supermarket-charges announcement described alleged inaccurate pricing and misleading specials, and the 2025 Annual Grocery Report described ongoing Fair Trading Act matters concerning alleged pricing inaccuracies and misleading specials [Commerce Commission, supermarket charges announcement](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/criminal-charges-on-the-way-for-major-supermarkets/); [Commerce Commission, Annual Grocery Report 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Annual-Grocery-Report-2025-2-June-2026.pdf).

Unit pricing adds another display-specific evidence need. The Commerce Commission's unit-pricing guideline says the Consumer Information Standards (Unit Pricing for Grocery Products) Regulations 2023 require qualifying grocery retailers to display unit prices for regulated grocery products, with in-store compliance from 31 August 2024 and online compliance from 31 August 2025 [Commerce Commission, unit-pricing guideline](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0024/347154/Unit-pricing-regulations-a-guide-for-grocery-retailers-Guideline-March-2024.pdf); [Consumer Information Standards (Unit Pricing for Grocery Products) Regulations 2023](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2023/0185/latest/whole.html). That means an evidence pack should record the unit price, unit used, purchase price, and their relative placement when the issue is unit-pricing display, not only the product's total price [Commerce Commission, unit-pricing guideline](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0024/347154/Unit-pricing-regulations-a-guide-for-grocery-retailers-Guideline-March-2024.pdf).

### What the evidence pack should contain

The pack should preserve the **raw observation**: original photo, screenshot, screen recording, or archived page; capture date/time; capture method; store banner and store name; online URL or app/page route where applicable; product name, brand, size, unit and barcode if visible; displayed purchase price; displayed unit price; displayed sale, club, multibuy, "was/now", "price dropped", "everyday", limitation, or qualification text; and whether the observed price was shelf, online, checkout, receipt, or app/cart evidence [Consumer Protection, misleading prices or advertising](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising); [Commerce Commission, unit-pricing guideline](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0024/347154/Unit-pricing-regulations-a-guide-for-grocery-retailers-Guideline-March-2024.pdf).

The pack should preserve the **price-history match**: Grocer product ID, store ID, vendor/banner, product redirect status and barcode checks where available, current-price fields on or near the capture date, and per-product history rows for the same product and store over the lookback window [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md); [Grocer API notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/references/api-notes.md). The documented Grocer current-price fields include `updated_at`, `store_id`, `product_id`, original, sale, club, online, and multibuy price fields, while the documented history fields are `updated_at`, `store_id`, and `price_cent`; this difference means history can support a price path but not, by itself, reconstruct the exact shelf or online wording [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).

The pack should preserve **integrity metadata**: SHA-256 checksum for each raw file, checksum timestamp, capture-device clock source if known, file size, filename, recorder initials or pseudonymous recorder ID, redaction log, and reviewer log [Digital Preservation Coalition, fixity and checksums](https://www.dpconline.org/handbook/technical-solutions-and-tools/fixity-and-checksums). This does not prove a photo is true, but a checksum can detect whether a stored file has changed after capture, which is the narrower integrity function described in digital-preservation guidance [Digital Preservation Coalition, fixity and checksums](https://www.dpconline.org/handbook/technical-solutions-and-tools/fixity-and-checksums).

The pack should preserve **matching uncertainty**: whether the barcode was visible, whether product name/brand/size/unit match exactly, whether Grocer `redirected_to` was followed, whether the history has enough observations, whether club/member-only or multibuy prices were separated, whether the observation was in store or online, and whether the checkout/receipt price was actually tested [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md); [existing reference-pricing method finding](supermarket-pricing-method.md).

### Privacy-safe capture rules

For in-store shelf captures, the default should be close-crop photos of the shelf ticket and product facing, taken at an angle that excludes shoppers, staff, checkout screens unrelated to the product, trolley contents, and payment areas [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, recording people in public places](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/recordings-of-people-in-public-places/); [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act). If a person, loyalty number, account name, order number, address, phone number, email, or payment detail appears in the raw capture, the public copy should be redacted and the original should be access-limited to reviewers or a regulator if needed [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act).

For online captures, the default should be a logged-out browser where possible, or a crop that excludes account identity, saved addresses, loyalty status, personalised offers, cart identifiers, and delivery-slot details [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act). If a logged-in account is necessary to see a checkout, club, substitution, delivery, or pickup price, the pack should record that the observation may be account-, store-, fulfilment-, or location-specific and should not publish account-linked screenshots [Foodstuffs online-shopping terms, cited in existing Grocer audit](grocer-nz-data-audit.md); [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act).

For checkout captures, the privacy-safe minimum is a cropped display or receipt section containing product description, price, discount, date/time, store, and receipt transaction time if present, with payment card, loyalty account, cashier ID if identifying, basket items outside the example, and receipt barcode/QR code redacted before publication [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act). A checkout mismatch claim should show both the pre-checkout representation and the charged price, because official guidance distinguishes displayed or advertised price from the price actually charged [Consumer Protection, misleading prices or advertising](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising).

### Matching workflow

First, capture the display before querying history, because the display text anchors the legal and factual question [Consumer Protection, misleading prices or advertising](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/common-consumer-issues/misleading-prices-or-advertising). Second, identify the product through barcode where possible, then product name, brand, size, unit, and Grocer product ID, because the Grocer API notes document barcode and redirect fields and the existing method finding warns that product identity should not rely on product ID alone for publication-ready examples [Grocer API notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/references/api-notes.md); [existing reference-pricing method finding](supermarket-pricing-method.md).

Third, identify the store through store name, banner, address/location if needed, Grocer store ID, and online fulfilment store where available, because Grocer prices are store-specific and the current-price files are per store [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md). Fourth, join the captured representation to the same product/store history and run the already-defined reference-pricing metrics: immediate prior price, modal and median lookback price, days at displayed reference price, promotion-duty cycle, multibuy-duty cycle, and sensitivity over 30/60/90/180-day windows when coverage permits [existing reference-pricing method finding](supermarket-pricing-method.md).

Fifth, assign confidence before publication. **High** should require a captured display, unambiguous product/store/date, barcode or equivalent identity check, sufficient history density, component price fields where relevant, checksumed originals, redacted public copies, second-reviewer reproduction, and legal-sensitivity review [existing reference-pricing method finding](supermarket-pricing-method.md); [Digital Preservation Coalition, fixity and checksums](https://www.dpconline.org/handbook/technical-solutions-and-tools/fixity-and-checksums). **Medium** should cover clear captures and price-history matches with some remaining limits, such as no barcode, no checkout test, sparse but usable history, or uncertainty about club/member-only pricing [Grocer skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md). **Low** should cover history-only signals, screenshots without exact store context, captures with unresolved personal-data redactions, or product identity based only on a loose name match [Consumer Protection, Privacy Act](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/privacy-act); [Grocer API notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/references/api-notes.md).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Commerce Commission, court, or MBIE evidence template for supermarket pricing complaints would supersede this proposed schema and could add or remove required fields [Commerce Commission, Grocery](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/).
- Retailer, Grocer, Grosave, PricePulse, or regulator permission for screenshot/data reuse could change what can safely be published, while restrictive terms could force the project to publish only methods, aggregate signals, and private regulator handoffs [existing Grocer data audit](grocer-nz-data-audit.md).
- A privacy lawyer, Commerce Commission staff member, or experienced consumer advocate might set a stricter rule for retaining originals, redacting receipts, capturing checkout screens, or collecting logged-in online examples [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, recording people in public places](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/recordings-of-people-in-public-places/).
- I could not verify whether stores permit in-store photography under their entry terms, whether a court or regulator would accept this exact pack, or whether checkout screenshots from logged-in accounts can be shared under retailer terms; those questions need human/legal review before external publication.
- I did not conduct a live in-store or online checkout capture, so the workflow is a cited protocol rather than a tested field procedure.

## Open follow-up questions

- Can Consumer NZ, the Commerce Commission, or a legal reviewer provide a preferred public complaint/evidence template for shelf-price, checkout-price, "special", and unit-pricing concerns?
- Which redaction workflow best preserves evidential value while reliably removing faces, loyalty details, receipts, QR codes, order numbers, and addresses?
