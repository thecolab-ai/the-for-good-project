# NZ should benchmark rural connectivity against usable-service standards, not headline coverage

## Executive answer

- New Zealand should compare itself with Australia, Canada, and Nordic countries using a **benchmark basket**, not a single coverage percentage: fixed broadband service standard, mobile service standard, mapping method, affordability, subsidy design, satellite treatment, and measured rural performance [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html), [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Canada is the clearest service-standard comparator because its public target is **50/10 Mbps with unlimited data**, with a target path to 100% household availability by 2031 and public progress reporting that separates rural and urban access [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [ISED progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet).
- Australia is the clearest comparator for mobile-map implementation details and satellite policy because it is introducing good/moderate/basic/no-coverage mobile-map categories from 30 June 2026, is legislating a Universal Outdoor Mobile Obligation from 1 December 2027, and explicitly expects direct-to-device low-earth-orbit satellite to help fill outdoor mobile gaps [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps), [Australian Department of Infrastructure, 2026](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/modernising-universal-telecommunications-services).
- The Nordic comparison should not be treated as one model: Norway has very high measured household availability at 100 Mbps and 1 Gbps, Sweden has a 2025 tiered gigabit strategy, and Finland's legal universal service floor remains a basic 5 Mbps obligation even while wider strategy and commercial coverage go beyond it [Nkom, 2025](https://nkom.no/rapporter-og-dokumenter/internett-i-norge-arsrapport-2025/_/attachment/download/e7d63a75-f7df-4b12-bae0-0b6d666b8343%3Abcaf40fd4e57707ed4742d1c2b6bb623fbd668f3/Internet%20in%20Norway%202025%20-%20Nkom%20Annual%20Report.pdf), [European Commission Sweden profile](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-connectivity-sweden), [Traficom, 2026](https://traficom.fi/en/telephone-and-broadband/telephone-and-broadband-users/guidelines-problems-functionality-and-availability-telephone-and-broadband-subscriptions).
- NZ's present public evidence is strong on technology mix, prices, performance, and map caveats. I verified a broad Digital Strategy measure that high-speed internet should be available to all New Zealanders, and TSO arrangements for local residential telephone service, but not a Canada-style quantified broadband floor for speed, upload, data allowance, and target date in the NZ sources checked [Digital Strategy for Aotearoa](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/237~the-digital-strategy-for-/html), [MBIE telecommunications service obligations](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/our-role-in-the-ict-sector/telecommunications-service-obligations), [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the comparator framework is well supported by official regulator and government sources, but country-to-country equality is limited because the sources use different geographies, technologies, and definitions of "available", "rural", "mobile coverage", and "universal service".

## Evidence

### The comparison should start from OECD's rural-performance warning

OECD's 2025 broadband-divides report says policy should first measure connectivity gaps and uses third-party broadband indicators with harmonised spatial definitions across the urban-rural continuum [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html). OECD's regional speed evidence shows why NZ should avoid headline coverage alone: in Q4 2024, fixed download speeds in OECD regions far from metropolitan areas were on average **23.5 percentage points below** metropolitan regions, while mobile download speeds showed a larger **35.0 percentage point** average gap [OECD, state of connectivity](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en/full-report/state-of-connectivity-between-and-within-countries_e8df092b.html).

OECD also reports that Canada had one of the largest absolute fixed-speed gaps between metropolitan and far-from-metropolitan regions in Q4 2024, at **71.2 Mbps**, while Norway was among countries with small fixed-broadband regional differences of five percentage points or less [OECD, state of connectivity](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en/full-report/state-of-connectivity-between-and-within-countries_e8df092b.html). That makes Canada useful as a large-country rural catch-up benchmark and Norway useful as a high-performing Nordic benchmark, while Australia is useful because OECD identified it among countries with mobile regional gaps above 40 percentage points [OECD, state of connectivity](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en/full-report/state-of-connectivity-between-and-within-countries_e8df092b.html).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ should publish a rural comparison table that separates nominal availability, measured speed, latency, service orderability, price burden, mobile landmass/road coverage, and resilience, because OECD treats performance gaps and spatial definitions as central to broadband-divide analysis and Ofcom's Connected Nations infrastructure reporting separately tracks coverage, performance, usage, take-up, and speeds [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html), [Ofcom Connected Nations reports](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/phones-and-broadband/coverage-and-speeds/infrastructure-research).

### NZ's baseline: useful monitoring, broad strategy goals, and emerging mobile-map standards

The Commerce Commission's 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report splits fixed-location broadband into urban areas where local fibre company fibre is available and rural areas where it is not available, and says this is its standard approach even though it may not align with other agencies' urban-rural definitions [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report says the Commission's map and report use data provided by telecommunications providers as at June 2025 and are not a live view of connectivity [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

NZ's public rural evidence already includes performance and price indicators: the Commerce Commission reports that rural ADSL users averaged **10 Mbps** in April 2025 MBNZ testing, and its regional price table shows rural households paid a weighted average **$85.77** per month compared with **$72.65** for urban households, or **18% extra** for rural households [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The Commission also states that affordability is generally the key barrier to broadband connectedness in the regional stories it examined [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The NZ connectivity map is useful but conservative for benchmark claims: providers supply the data, coverage is a point-in-time 30 June 2025 view, wireless parcel coverage is assigned when at least 50% of a land parcel falls within a provider coverage area, satellite is excluded because local obstacles can block a nominally nationwide signal, the map is not inclusive of all providers, and it may differ from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). Those caveats mean NZ should compare map **method quality** and **map caveats**, not only the resulting coverage percentages [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

NZ does have an official mobile coverage-map standardisation path. The Commerce Commission's January 2025 Mobile Coverage Maps Guidelines say mobile network operators should standardise coverage-level descriptions, signal-strength thresholds, displayed services, and supporting technical terms, with Outcome 1 implementation expected by 5 September 2025 [Commerce Commission mobile coverage-map guidelines, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/364154/Product-Disclosure-Mobile-Coverage-Maps-guidelines-30-January-2025.pdf). In October 2025, the Commission said providers were using the same coverage descriptors and thresholds - good, moderate, and limited - and had adopted exit rights for material gaps between map claims and real-world coverage [Commerce Commission, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/mobile-providers-deliver-clearer-coverage-maps-and-exit-rights-for-consumers/).

NZ's public strategy is less directly comparable to Canada's quantified universal-access target in the sources checked. The Digital Strategy for Aotearoa measures inclusion partly by whether high-speed internet is available to all New Zealanders and affordable quality connections/devices are available, while MBIE's TSO page describes current TSO providers as Chorus for network service supporting local residential telephone service and Spark for local residential telephone service to end users [Digital Strategy for Aotearoa](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/237~the-digital-strategy-for-/html), [MBIE telecommunications service obligations](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/our-role-in-the-ict-sector/telecommunications-service-obligations).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ can already report rural broadband by technology, price, and measured performance, and it now has standardised public mobile coverage descriptors. The remaining Canada-style benchmark gap I could verify is narrower: a public broadband target stated as a quantified service floor, such as speed, upload, data allowance, geography, and target date, rather than the broader "high-speed internet" and affordability measures in NZ's digital strategy [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [Digital Strategy for Aotearoa](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/237~the-digital-strategy-for-/html).

### Canada: benchmark against a clear 50/10 unlimited objective and addressable funding maps

Canada's CRTC reports a national broadband availability target of 90% of households with **50/10 Mbps unlimited** by 2021, which has been met, and a target of **100% of households with 50/10 unlimited by 2031**, which it describes as on track [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm). ISED's public progress dashboard says Canada's goal is for all Canadians to have access to at least **50 Mbps download and 10 Mbps upload**, and it reports rural household access rising from **38.1% in 2017** to **87.4% in 2024**, compared with urban access of **99.8% in 2024** [ISED progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet).

Canada's Universal Broadband Fund eligibility is tied to ISED's Availability Map, where eligible areas are those having less than 50/10 Mbps service, and ISED says the map represents available broadband services down to the **250 metre road segment** level using Statistics Canada demographics, road files, and ISP information [ISED UBF application guide](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/universal-broadband-fund-application-guide). ISED's national broadband data page also publishes 25 km2 hexagonal layers for 50/10 coverage, 5/1 coverage, and programme-supported areas, based on detailed coverage information from ISPs to CRTC and ISED and evaluated against a pseudo-household demographic distribution from the 2021 Census [ISED national broadband data information](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/universal-broadband-fund/national-broadband-data-information).

Canada treats satellite dependence explicitly rather than folding it into normal coverage: the CRTC Broadband Fund includes satellite-dependent community projects, including operating costs for satellite capacity, earth-station upgrades, access infrastructure, and transport projects that connect a satellite-dependent community to terrestrial networks [CRTC Broadband Fund](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/internet/fnd/fnd.htm). The CRTC's 2026 mapping guide defines eligible satellite-dependent communities as communities relying on satellite transport capacity for one or more telecommunications services, and separately identifies funded communities expected to lose that dependence when terrestrial transport is built [CRTC mapping guide](https://www.crtc.gc.ca/eng/internet/fnd/appl/cartograph.htm).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ should copy the **clarity** of Canada's 50/10 unlimited benchmark, road-segment/household-oriented eligibility logic, and separate satellite-dependent category, while noting that Canada's large rural/remote geography differs materially from NZ's scale and terrain [ISED progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet), [CRTC mapping guide](https://www.crtc.gc.ca/eng/internet/fnd/appl/cartograph.htm).

### Australia: benchmark mobile-map transparency, regional subsidy design, and satellite integration

Australia's ACMA announced that mobile operators must publish standardised mobile network coverage maps by **30 June 2026**, with 4G and 5G coverage shown in four categories: good, moderate, basic, or no coverage [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps). ACMA says the maps will use predictive modelling, plain-English descriptions, at least quarterly updates, and like-for-like comparison across carriers, and that it may later enhance maps with in-field measurement and crowd-sourced coverage data [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps).

Australia is also expanding universal-service thinking from fixed/voice service toward outdoor mobile service: the Department of Infrastructure says the proposed Universal Outdoor Mobile Obligation would require Optus, Telstra, and TPG Telecom to provide reasonable and equitable access to baseline outdoor mobile coverage across Australia from **1 December 2027** [Australian Department of Infrastructure, 2026](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/modernising-universal-telecommunications-services). The same department says operators are expected to use existing mobile coverage and direct-to-device low-earth-orbit satellites, and says LEO satellites can in time fill some phone-coverage gaps in remote and sparsely populated areas [Australian Department of Infrastructure, 2026](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/modernising-universal-telecommunications-services).

Australia's subsidy design is a mixed portfolio rather than a single rural broadband fund: the Regional Connectivity Program funds place-based telecommunications infrastructure in regional, rural, and remote Australia and had awarded **A$370.3 million** across up to **298** projects under rounds 1 to 3, including mobile voice/data coverage, fixed wireless, fibre, and backhaul [Australian Regional Connectivity Program](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/internet/regional-connectivity-program). The Regional Broadband Scheme is a levy-style mechanism to fund NBN Co's fixed wireless and satellite network losses, with NBN Co paying around 95% of the cost and competing NBN-comparable fixed-line networks paying the remaining 5% [Australian Regional Broadband Scheme](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/internet/regional-broadband-scheme-rbs).

Australia's satellite treatment is also practical rather than theoretical: NBN's Sky Muster satellite service supplies NBN broadband to homes and businesses in regional and remote Australia, and NBN says it has signed a contract with Amazon to offer a new LEO satellite service for customers in NBN's satellite footprint [NBN Sky Muster](https://www.nbnco.com.au/learn/network-technology/sky-muster-explained). The ACCC's Measuring Broadband Australia work found in March 2025 that Starlink and NBN fixed wireless delivered busy-hour download speeds above 100 Mbps, with Starlink averaging **170.2 Mbps** busy-hour download and NBN fixed wireless Home Fast and Superfast medians of **166.2 Mbps** and **283.5 Mbps** [ACCC, 2025](https://www.accc.gov.au/media-release/wireless-networks-provide-high-speed-alternatives-to-remote-and-regional-households).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ should use Australia to test whether public mobile coverage maps go beyond common descriptors into update cadence, outdoor-use assumptions, and emergency/rural travel usefulness, and should compare whether satellite is treated as a funded universal-service input, a performance-tested competitive option, or an excluded line-of-sight caveat [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps), [Australian Department of Infrastructure, 2026](https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-communications/modernising-universal-telecommunications-services), [Commerce Commission mobile coverage-map guidelines, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/364154/Product-Disclosure-Mobile-Coverage-Maps-guidelines-30-January-2025.pdf).

### Nordic countries: benchmark ambition, but keep legal floors separate from strategy goals

Norway is the strongest Nordic performance benchmark found: Nkom's 2025 annual report says that at the end of 2024, **99.1%** of Norwegian households were offered internet access of at least **100 Mbps** and **96.2%** were offered at least **1 Gbps**, while basic household coverage for 4G and 5G was estimated at **100%** and **99.7%** respectively [Nkom, 2025](https://nkom.no/rapporter-og-dokumenter/internett-i-norge-arsrapport-2025/_/attachment/download/e7d63a75-f7df-4b12-bae0-0b6d666b8343%3Abcaf40fd4e57707ed4742d1c2b6bb623fbd668f3/Internet%20in%20Norway%202025%20-%20Nkom%20Annual%20Report.pdf). OECD separately identifies Norway as one of the countries with small fixed-broadband regional differences in 2024, which independently supports using Norway as a high-performing spatial-equity comparator [OECD, state of connectivity](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en/full-report/state-of-connectivity-between-and-within-countries_e8df092b.html).

Sweden is the clearest Nordic strategic-target comparator: the European Commission's Sweden profile says the 2025 strategy implies **98%** of households and businesses with at least **1 Gbps**, the remaining **1.9%** with at least **100 Mbps**, and **0.1%** with at least **30 Mbps**, with reliable high-quality mobile services across Sweden no later than 2023 [European Commission Sweden profile](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-connectivity-sweden). The same profile identifies the Swedish Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) as the regulator monitoring and regulating electronic communications, and says Bredbandsforum supports cooperation to improve digital connectivity access across the country [European Commission Sweden profile](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-connectivity-sweden).

Finland shows why "Nordic" should not be simplified into one high-speed legal obligation: Traficom's 2026 guidance says users have a right to basic communications services at home or place of business, and that universal services include a fixed or wireless telephone connection and a **5 Mbps** basic broadband connection at reasonable quality and affordable price [Traficom, 2026](https://traficom.fi/en/telephone-and-broadband/telephone-and-broadband-users/guidelines-problems-functionality-and-availability-telephone-and-broadband-subscriptions). Finland's Ministry of Transport and Communications announced in 2021 that the universal-service broadband speed was raised from 2 Mbps to 5 Mbps, confirming that the current universal-service floor is a basic safety-net obligation rather than a gigabit-ambition target [Finnish Government, 2021](https://valtioneuvosto.fi/en/-/speed-of-the-universal-service-broadband-set-at-5-mbit-s).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ should compare separately against Nordic **coverage achievements** (especially Norway), Nordic **strategic targets** (especially Sweden), and Nordic **legal safety nets** (Finland), because these are different policy instruments with different meanings for rural households [Nkom, 2025](https://nkom.no/rapporter-og-dokumenter/internett-i-norge-arsrapport-2025/_/attachment/download/e7d63a75-f7df-4b12-bae0-0b6d666b8343%3Abcaf40fd4e57707ed4742d1c2b6bb623fbd668f3/Internet%20in%20Norway%202025%20-%20Nkom%20Annual%20Report.pdf), [European Commission Sweden profile](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/digital-connectivity-sweden), [Traficom, 2026](https://traficom.fi/en/telephone-and-broadband/telephone-and-broadband-users/guidelines-problems-functionality-and-availability-telephone-and-broadband-subscriptions).

### EU/BEREC material gives NZ two useful checks: state-aid discipline and mobile-map quality

The EU's broadband state-aid guidelines distinguish aid for fixed access networks, mobile access networks, and backhaul networks, and assess whether aid creates an incentive effect, contributes to economic development, complies with EU law, and balances positive and negative effects on competition [EUR-Lex broadband state-aid summary](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/EN/legal-content/summary/guidelines-on-state-aid-for-broadband-networks.html). OECD's funding comparison shows European programmes often state measurable targets such as 100 Mbps with gigabit upgrade paths, gigabit coverage for businesses or socio-economic drivers, 5G road coverage, and calculated investment gaps or state-aid amounts [OECD funding chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en/full-report/national-broadband-targets-public-funding-programmes-and-cost-studies-quantifying-the-cost-of-universal-connectivity_bdd6f37a.html).

BEREC's common position on consumer information for mobile coverage says national regulators use mobile coverage information to assess coverage obligations and provide independent reliable information to consumers, policymakers, governments, the European Commission, industry, and the wider public [BEREC, 2018](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/document-categories/berec/regulatory-best-practices/common-approachespositions/berec-common-position-on-information-to-consumers-on-mobile-coverage). BEREC's four common positions cover technical specifications for comparable mobile coverage information, signal predictions, accuracy assurance, and availability/presentation of coverage information, and they are for outdoor mobile-service coverage [BEREC, 2018](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/document-categories/berec/regulatory-best-practices/common-approachespositions/berec-common-position-on-information-to-consumers-on-mobile-coverage).

**Benchmark implication:** NZ should judge rural subsidies against both **additionality** and **competition effects**, and should judge mobile maps against published technical assumptions, accuracy checks, update cadence, and consumer-comparable categories [EUR-Lex broadband state-aid summary](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/EN/legal-content/summary/guidelines-on-state-aid-for-broadband-networks.html), [BEREC, 2018](https://www.berec.europa.eu/en/document-categories/berec/regulatory-best-practices/common-approachespositions/berec-common-position-on-information-to-consumers-on-mobile-coverage).

### A practical comparator dashboard for NZ

NZ's rural-connectivity dashboard should have at least eight columns, because each comparator country exposes a different policy risk [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html):

| Dimension | Why it matters for NZ | Strong comparator source |
|---|---|---|
| Quantified fixed broadband service floor | NZ's broad "high-speed internet" strategy measure is useful, but Canada gives a more directly testable floor for "what service is good enough?" | Canada's 50/10 unlimited objective [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), NZ Digital Strategy measure [Digital Strategy for Aotearoa](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/237~the-digital-strategy-for-/html) |
| Rural progress by urban-rural geography | Headline national coverage can hide rural catch-up gaps. | Canada's rural/urban 50/10 progress table [ISED progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet) |
| Map granularity and challenge process | Provider-supplied coverage needs a correction path. | ISED road-segment eligibility and challenge guidance [ISED UBF application guide](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/universal-broadband-fund-application-guide) |
| Satellite category | Satellite can be high-performing, obstructed, capacity-constrained, or a transitional dependency. | CRTC satellite-dependent communities and NZ map satellite caveat [CRTC mapping guide](https://www.crtc.gc.ca/eng/internet/fnd/appl/cartograph.htm), [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/) |
| Mobile coverage-map standard | Rural safety and work depend on mobile where people travel, not just where homes are. | NZ good/moderate/limited descriptors and Australia's good/moderate/basic/no-coverage categories [Commerce Commission, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/mobile-providers-deliver-clearer-coverage-maps-and-exit-rights-for-consumers/), [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps) |

| Affordability | A service that is available but unaffordable is not usable digital inclusion. | NZ rural price premium and Canada affordability/price monitoring [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [CRTC 2026 market report](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/2026/ctmr.htm) |

## What would change this conclusion

- A current NZ government policy that defines a rural universal broadband service objective by speed, latency, data allowance, reliability, and affordability would change the conclusion that Canada is clearer on quantified benchmark design [CRTC current trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [Digital Strategy for Aotearoa](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/237~the-digital-strategy-for-/html), [MBIE telecommunications service obligations](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/our-role-in-the-ict-sector/telecommunications-service-obligations).
- Evidence that NZ's good/moderate/limited mobile coverage descriptors include the same implementation details as Australia's quarterly update cadence, modelling assumptions, and proposed measurement/crowdsourcing path would reduce the remaining Australia comparison to satellite and outdoor universal-service policy [ACMA, 31 March 2026](https://www.acma.gov.au/articles/2026-03/new-rules-mobile-phone-coverage-maps), [Commerce Commission mobile coverage-map guidelines, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/364154/Product-Disclosure-Mobile-Coverage-Maps-guidelines-30-January-2025.pdf).
- Address-level NZ evidence showing that rural households inside provider-reported footprints can reliably order service at an agreed speed, latency, data, and price standard would weaken the recommendation to add orderability and challenge-process measures [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [ISED UBF application guide](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/universal-broadband-fund-application-guide).
- I could not verify an official NZ rural affordability threshold comparable to Canada's 50/10 unlimited availability target or Finland's "affordable price" universal-service language; NZ's public affordability evidence is currently stronger as price-premium monitoring than as a household affordability standard [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Traficom, 2026](https://traficom.fi/en/telephone-and-broadband/telephone-and-broadband-users/guidelines-problems-functionality-and-availability-telephone-and-broadband-subscriptions).
- I could not verify directly comparable rural latency, outage, and failed-install datasets across NZ, Australia, Canada, Norway, Sweden, and Finland; these would require regulator data requests, provider data, or a harmonised measurement project rather than public document review [OECD, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/closing-broadband-connectivity-divides-for-all_d5ea99b2-en.html).
- A human rural-sector review is still needed because public comparator documents do not show how rural households, farms, iwi, marae, health providers, and schools trade off price, latency, data caps, mobile coverage, satellite hardware, power resilience, and support availability in daily use [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues were opened; the comparator scope was broad but answerable as a single methods finding.
- What minimum NZ rural service standard would be credible for health, education, farm operations, remote work, and emergency use: 50/10 unlimited, 100/20, latency-bounded, or technology-specific?
- Could NZ publish a map-challenge process for communities, councils, marae, schools, and providers without exposing commercially sensitive provider data or household-level personal information?
- How should NZ count direct-to-device satellite voice/SMS once it is available: mobile universal service, emergency resilience, or a separate safety layer?
