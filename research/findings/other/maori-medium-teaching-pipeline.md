# Māori-medium and te reo Māori teacher demand is growing faster than the qualified pipeline can prove it will cover

## Executive answer

- The current public evidence points to a **qualified, te reo Māori-proficient kaiako constraint**, not just a generic lack of ITE places: Māori-medium schooling had 28,382 students in 362 schools on 1 July 2025, while first-time Māori-medium ITE enrolments reached 300 in 2025 and first-time Māori-medium ITE completions fell to 135 in 2024. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)
- The pipeline is improving at the entry point but remains narrow at the exit point: first-time Māori-medium ITE enrolments rose 71% to 300 in 2025, yet completions were 135 in 2024, 89% of those completions were in primary-sector programmes, and only 10 were secondary Māori-medium graduates. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)
- The demand signal is broader than immersion schooling because 266,199 students were in Māori language in English-medium schooling in 2025, and the main workforce programmes explicitly target both Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings and English-medium secondary te reo Māori roles. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)
- LAT reliance is a real shortage signal, but the public data is not granular enough to isolate Māori-medium LAT reliance: the EBITE programme is explicitly built for LAT holders in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium and te reo Māori roles, while PPTA's 2026 secondary staffing survey found 8% of advertised positions with known outcomes were filled by LAT holders and listed Te Reo Māori among LAT-filled, unfilled and cancelled/transferred areas. [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award); [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/teacher-shortage-easing-but-how-its-easing-is-concerning/document/2656)
- The most likely binding constraints are **te reo Māori/cultural proficiency, secondary subject specialisation, and geography**, with retention as a local risk rather than a clearly quantified national Māori-medium attrition problem. The Ministry's 2025 TDS report names te reo Māori, Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium, rumaki reo and reo rua as areas where national/regional projections can hide ongoing recruitment and retention difficulty, and it says language and cultural expertise make Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium recruitment and retention especially challenging. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium -- the learner-demand and ITE counts are official and current, but no public source found here directly matches Māori-medium/te reo Māori vacancies, LAT use, ITE completions, placement region, and retention by school setting.

## Evidence

### Scope and answer

This finding answers issue #505 by comparing current Māori-medium/Kaupapa Māori and te reo Māori learner demand with the public teacher-training and stop-gap workforce pipeline; it does not estimate the exact number of teachers required, because the public sources found here do not provide a current Māori-medium/te reo Māori vacancy-by-region-by-subject census. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf); [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling)

Māori-medium education means students are taught through Māori for at least 51% of the time, covering Māori language immersion levels 1 and 2; Māori language in English-medium education covers students learning te reo Māori as a subject or receiving curriculum teaching through Māori for up to 50% of the time, covering levels 3 to 5. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling)

### Learner demand is growing and is not confined to immersion kura

As at 1 July 2025, Māori-medium schooling enrolled 28,382 students, equal to 3.3% of the total school population and up 4.6% from July 2024; the same page records 362 schools with at least one Māori-medium student, 18 more than in July 2024. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling)

The Māori-medium total grew from 22,391 in 2020 to 28,382 in 2025, a 5,991-student increase over five years; that comparison is arithmetic from Education Counts' 2020-2025 table and should be read as roll growth, not as a direct teacher-demand count. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling)

English-medium Māori-language demand is much larger in headcount: 266,199 students were in Māori language in English-medium education in 2025, 5,879 more than in 2024, across 1,403 schools. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling)

That English-medium denominator matters because the same targeted programmes cover both immersion/Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium roles and English-medium secondary te reo Māori teaching roles: Te Tipu Whakarito scholarships are for aspiring teachers in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings or English-medium secondary te reo Māori, and EBITE eligibility includes LAT holders teaching or intending to teach in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings or as te reo Māori subject teachers in English-medium settings. [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)

Early learning adds an upstream continuity signal, although this finding does not count it as school-sector demand: in 2025, 8,263 Māori children attended kōhanga reo and 1,743 Māori children attended other te reo Māori bilingual or immersion ECE services. [Education Counts, language use in ECE](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/language-use-in-ece)

### The measured ITE pipeline is small after completions, especially for secondary

Overall domestic first-time ITE enrolments increased from 3,600 in 2024 to 4,290 in 2025, including primary rising to 1,990 and secondary rising to 1,105. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)

Within that overall recovery, first-time Māori-medium ITE enrolments increased by 71% to 300 in 2025, and 78% of those first-time Māori-medium ITE students were in primary-sector programmes. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)

Completions show the tighter near-term supply signal: first-time Māori-medium ITE graduates decreased from 165 in 2023 to 135 in 2024, 74% of the 2024 graduates were from immersion programmes, 89% were in primary-sector programmes, and only 10 were secondary Māori-medium graduates. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)

As an order-of-magnitude stock/flow comparison, 300 first-time Māori-medium ITE entrants in 2025 is about one entrant per 95 current Māori-medium school students, and 135 first-time Māori-medium ITE graduates in 2024 is about one graduate per 210 current Māori-medium students; these are my calculations from Education Counts figures and are not an official staffing ratio. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)

If the English-medium Māori-language cohort is included, the combined 2025 learner base is 294,581 students, making 300 first-time Māori-medium ITE entrants roughly one entrant per 982 students; this broader ratio should be treated only as evidence of competition for the same te reo Māori-capable workforce, because Education Counts' English-medium category includes lower-immersion levels and is not a direct count of dedicated te reo Māori teacher vacancies. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)

### Targeted programmes are material but not yet proof of enough qualified supply

Te Tipu Whakarito offers 130 annual scholarships for students aspiring to teach in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings or English-medium secondary te reo Māori, covering course fees and a $10,000 allowance over the period of study. [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship)

Te Waka Whakarei offers 90 annual career-changer scholarships for people changing career to teach in Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings or English-medium secondary te reo Māori, covering course fees and a $30,000 per-year allowance for up to three years. [Education Workforce, Te Waka Whakarei Career-Changer Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-waka-whakarei-career-changer-scholarship)

The Kaupapa Māori/Māori Medium EBITE funding award has 65 funding awards available for LAT holders wanting to start teacher training in 2027, funds LAT holders into a two-year ITE programme while employed, and funds schools/kura for release time and mentoring. [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)

The Ministry's TDS projection treats EBITE as a growing supply initiative, saying EBITE expands from 47 places in 2025 to 70 in 2026 to grow qualified Kaupapa Māori and Māori-medium teachers. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)

For existing teachers, the Teachers' Kaupapa Māori/Māori-Medium Education Study Award offers 42 annual study awards to improve knowledge and proficiency in te reo Māori and tikanga and develop bilingual or immersion teaching skills. [Education Workforce, Teachers' Kaupapa Māori/Māori-Medium Education Study Award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/current-teachers/sabbaticals-study-awards-and-study-grants/teachers-kaupapa-maorimaori-medium-education-study-award)

These programmes are directionally targeted at the right constraint, but public place counts alone do not show utilisation, completion, registration, placement into shortage settings, or retention after bonding; the ITE completion count of 135 Māori-medium graduates in 2024 is therefore the stronger current near-term output measure than the published scholarship/award capacity. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)

### LAT reliance shows schools are already bridging a qualified-teacher gap

A Limited Authority to Teach enables schools and kura to employ people where specialist skills or skills are in short supply, is not a practising certificate, and is not used for permanent employment. [Education Workforce, employing someone on a Limited Authority to Teach](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/principals-and-schools/local-employment-and-recruitment-support/employing-someone-limited-authority-teach)

The Teaching Council's public "Become a teacher" page reported 3,109 people holding Limited Authority to Teach, or 3% of certificated teachers and LAT holders, when accessed on 5 July 2026; that total is not broken down publicly on the page by Māori-medium, subject or region. [Teaching Council, Become a teacher](https://teachingcouncil.nz/en/become-a-teacher)

The PPTA 2026 secondary staffing survey found that 20% of advertised positions with known outcomes could not be filled and a further 8% were filled by LAT holders, and it lists Te Reo Māori among the roles that could not be filled and among the roles filled by LAT holders. [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/teacher-shortage-easing-but-how-its-easing-is-concerning/document/2656)

PPTA also reported that 47% of respondent principals employed untrained or unqualified teachers because they could not find trained and qualified teachers, and that 47% had teachers working outside specialist areas; the same report lists Te Reo Māori among cancelled or transferred courses/classes. [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/teacher-shortage-easing-but-how-its-easing-is-concerning/document/2656)

The PPTA survey is sector evidence rather than an official census, but it is a useful triangulation because the Ministry's TDS report itself says shortages in te reo Māori cannot be offset by surplus in other subjects, and that some regions increasingly rely on LAT holders while Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium kura face persistent shortages of qualified kaiako. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf); [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/teacher-shortage-easing-but-how-its-easing-is-concerning/document/2656)

### Which constraint is binding?

The strongest evidence points to **language/cultural proficiency** as the binding constraint: the Ministry says demand for te reo Māori-proficient kaiako will continue to grow across English-medium and Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium settings, and it says language and cultural expertise make recruitment and retention especially challenging for Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium kura. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)

Language proficiency is also a formal gate: the Teaching Council says all applicants studying to be a teacher or applying for registration and certification must meet English or te reo Māori language competency requirements. [Teaching Council, Language requirements to teach in NZ](https://teachingcouncil.nz/en/become-a-teacher/overseas-trained-teacher-coming-to-Aotearoa/language-competency-requirements)

The secondary/wharekura constraint is also strong: only 10 Māori-medium ITE graduates were in secondary ITE in 2024, Te Tipu Whakarito requires wharekura candidates to have specialist secondary subject content at 300 level and English-medium secondary te reo Māori candidates to have at least one 300-level Māori studies/language/culture paper, and PPTA lists Te Reo Māori, Pāngarau and Pātaiao among hard-to-staff or affected areas. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [PPTA, Secondary School Staffing Survey report 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/teacher-shortage-easing-but-how-its-easing-is-concerning/document/2656)

Training capacity is improving but not yet proven sufficient: first-time Māori-medium ITE enrolments rose to 300 in 2025, Te Tipu Whakarito has 130 scholarships, Te Waka Whakarei has 90 career-changer scholarships, and EBITE has 65 published 2027 awards, but the latest public completion figure is 135 first-time Māori-medium ITE graduates in 2024. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, Te Waka Whakarei Career-Changer Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-waka-whakarei-career-changer-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)

Geography is a compounding constraint: the Ministry projects 2026 primary shortages in Bay of Plenty, Northland, Taranaki and Waikato, says those regions are often characterised by smaller or rural primary schools and more Kaupapa Māori and/or Māori-medium kura, and says domestic training pipelines are concentrated in urban regions while smaller regions such as West Coast and Gisborne produce fewer than 20 new primary teachers. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)

Retention is a local risk but not quantified enough to rank above proficiency and pipeline output: the Ministry projects 2024 national retention at 90.1% for primary and 88.5% for secondary and says retention is not a significant issue from a national secondary perspective, while also warning that Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium kura face persistent recruitment and retention challenges because of language and cultural expertise. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)

Competition with English-medium te reo Māori roles is real because English-medium Māori-language schooling has a much larger student headcount than Māori-medium schooling, and the same Te Tipu Whakarito, Te Waka Whakarei and EBITE settings include English-medium secondary te reo Māori as eligible or intended destinations. [Education Counts, Māori language in schooling](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/maori-language-in-schooling); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, Te Waka Whakarei Career-Changer Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-waka-whakarei-career-changer-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)

## What would change this conclusion

- An official current dataset matching Māori-medium/Kaupapa Māori/te reo Māori vacancies, LAT-filled roles, non-specialist teaching, region, school setting, year level and subject would change the constraint ranking from inference to direct measurement. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection 2025](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/251494/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf)
- Evidence that the 2025 cohort of 300 first-time Māori-medium ITE entrants converts into substantially higher completions, registration and placement in Māori-medium/te reo Māori shortage roles would weaken the "pipeline still too narrow" conclusion. [Education Counts, initial teacher education statistics](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/initial-teacher-education-statistics)
- Programme administrative data showing EBITE, Te Tipu Whakarito and Te Waka Whakarei are under-subscribed because provider places or candidates are not available would point to a different bottleneck than data showing they are over-subscribed but not scaled. [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award); [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship)
- Placement and retention data showing graduates remain in hard-to-staff Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium and English-medium te reo Māori roles for several years would reduce the concern that scholarships and EBITE places do not translate into durable supply. [Education Workforce, Te Tipu Whakarito Scholarship](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/te-tipu-whakarito-scholarship); [Education Workforce, EBITE funding award](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/kaupapa-maori-maori-medium-ebite-programme-funding-award)
- A Māori-medium, wharekura, iwi education, or kaupapa Māori workforce expert could reasonably reweight these constraints because this is public desk research and does not include lived or professional authority from kura.
