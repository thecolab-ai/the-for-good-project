# NZ's Building Act s73/s74 natural-hazard title-tag population is not publicly countable, and removals need official aggregate data

## Executive answer

- I could not verify a public national count of New Zealand Records of Title carrying Building Act 2004 s73/s74 natural-hazard entries, nor a public count of entries ever removed under s74(4). LINZ's open "NZ Property Titles" layer has 2,447,940 title features and excludes ownership, but its published fields are title-level metadata such as `title_no`, `status`, `type`, `land_district`, `issue_date`, `guarantee_status`, `estate_description`, `number_owners`, `spatial_extents_shared`, and geometry; it does not expose memorial/instrument text that would identify s73/s74 natural-hazard entries [LINZ Data Service, NZ Property Titles layer](https://data.linz.govt.nz/layer/50804-nz-property-titles/).
- The only public aggregate count I found is Auckland-specific: Auckland Council says LINZ estimated in August 2023 that 4,089 property titles in the Auckland Council area had Section 74 notices, less than 1% of properties in the region [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/09/explainer-section-74-notices/). I found no public equivalent for other territorial authorities or for New Zealand as a whole.
- The pathway is not legally a one-way door: s74 allows removal when the building consent authority determines the entry is no longer required, and MBIE guidance says removal may follow evidence that the hazard no longer exists or that adequate protection/restoration provision has been made [MBIE Natural Hazard Provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2024/053](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-053.pdf).
- In practice it appears hard to remove without technical evidence or changed circumstances: MBIE Determination 2024/045 upheld a council refusal where the owner had not provided evidence addressing the relevant hazard, while Determinations 2024/053 and 2026/010 show entries can be removed or treated as no longer required in specific facts [MBIE Determination 2024/045](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-045.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2024/053](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-053.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2026/010](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2026/2026-010.pdf).
- The strongest next step is an aggregate, de-identified OIA to LINZ asking for counts by territorial authority/land district, hazard type where recorded, year entered, and year removed under s74(4), including predecessor entries under Building Act 1991 s36(2) and Local Government Act 1974 s641A if LINZ can distinguish them [NHC section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/); [LINZ Data Service, NZ Property Titles layer](https://data.linz.govt.nz/layer/50804-nz-property-titles/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the legal pathway and insurance relevance are well supported by official sources, but the central quantitative question cannot be answered from public data without LINZ and/or council aggregate extracts.

## Evidence

### What the notice is and why it matters

Sections 71 to 74 of the Building Act 2004 apply when new building work or major alterations are proposed on land subject, or likely to be subject, to listed natural hazards; MBIE summarises the available outcomes as refusal under s71(1), ordinary grant where adequate provision is made under s71(2), or grant under s72 with s73 conditions [MBIE Determination 2024/025](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-025.pdf). The listed hazards are erosion, falling debris, subsidence, inundation, and slippage [MBIE Determination 2024/025](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-025.pdf); [NHC section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/).

When a consent is granted under s72, the Registrar-General of Land records on the title that the consent was granted under s72 and records particulars identifying the natural hazard [MBIE Determination 2024/053](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-053.pdf). NHC describes the practical record as a "section 72 notice" on the Record of Title, while public discussion often calls it a "section 74 notice"; NHC also says predecessor Building Act 1991 s36(2) and Local Government Act 1974 s641A notices have the same effect on a Record of Title [NHC section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/); [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/09/explainer-section-74-notices/).

The title entry matters for insurance and lending because NHC says a section 72 notice may result in full or partial claim decline if the later damage is caused by the same type of hazard specified in the notice, and because the notice is intended to alert people with an interest in the property, including potential buyers, banks, lenders, and insurers [NHC section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/). ICNZ's consumer note similarly says EQC/NHC cover may be declined in part or whole where a title has a Section 74 notice, and that private top-up cover may depend on whether EQC/NHC accepts the claim [ICNZ, 2024](https://www.icnz.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/March-2024-The-Implications-of-Section-74-Notices-on-your-Property-Title.pdf).

### What can be counted publicly

I checked the vendored `linz-data-service` skill before generic search. It surfaced the public LINZ "NZ Property Titles" layer, but that layer's metadata shows no field for memorials, instruments, section notices, hazard type, or title encumbrance text; it provides title metadata and geometry only, with a feature count of 2,447,940 at the 2026-07-03 published version [LINZ Data Service, NZ Property Titles layer](https://data.linz.govt.nz/layer/50804-nz-property-titles/). Searches of the LINZ Data Service catalogue for "Building Act s74" and "natural hazard" did not surface a public layer of s73/s74 title notices; the relevant public title layer therefore cannot itself answer how many titles carry the notice [LINZ Data Service API search for Building Act s74](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/?q=Building+Act+s74); [LINZ Data Service API search for natural hazard](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/?q=natural+hazard).

Auckland is the only aggregate territorial count I found: Auckland Council says "In August 2023, LINZ estimated that there were 4089 property titles in the Auckland Council area with Section 74 notices applied" and that this was less than 1% of the region's properties [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/09/explainer-section-74-notices/). Because this is a council publication reporting a LINZ estimate rather than a LINZ dataset or OIA release, confidence in the exact Auckland number is Medium, not High [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/09/explainer-section-74-notices/).

I found no public national total, no public territorial-authority distribution, and no public count of removals under s74(4). The most likely holders of the aggregate data are LINZ, because it operates the title register and records/removes entries after notification, and territorial authorities, because they decide whether a consent is granted under s72 and whether an entry is no longer required under s74(3) [MBIE Natural Hazard Provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2026/010](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2026/2026-010.pdf).

### Removal: legally possible, but not publicly quantifiable

The Building Act pathway is not absolutely irreversible. MBIE guidance says s74 provides for removal of entries when they are no longer required, and says a building owner usually starts the request to the building consent authority; it also says evidence will be needed that the hazard no longer exists or that adequate provision has been made to protect the land, building work, or other property, or to restore damage [MBIE Natural Hazard Provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf). NHC also states that section 72 notices can be removed from a Record of Title by the council in some situations, while NHC has no input into that process [NHC section notices](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/section-notices/).

Public MBIE determinations show the removal test is real but fact-specific. In 2024/045, MBIE confirmed a council's refusal to remove a natural-hazard entry because there was insufficient evidence that the entry was no longer required, and MBIE said the owner needed evidence that the land was no longer subject to, or likely to be subject to, the relevant hazard [MBIE Determination 2024/045](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-045.pdf). In 2024/053, MBIE determined that a title entry was not required by the Act and that the Registrar-General must amend records and remove the entry under s74(4) [MBIE Determination 2024/053](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-053.pdf). In 2026/010, MBIE reversed a council refusal, finding an entry on a current title was no longer required because the building work and land to which the original entry related were not on the current owners' property after subdivision; MBIE left it to the authority to communicate that decision to the Registrar-General under s74(4) [MBIE Determination 2026/010](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2026/2026-010.pdf).

That public case pattern supports a cautious conclusion: removal is not impossible, but a homeowner should expect to need site-specific evidence, a council decision, and LINZ title-register action; the public record does not reveal how often that process succeeds in ordinary council administration without an MBIE determination [MBIE Natural Hazard Provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2024/045](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-045.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2026/010](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2026/2026-010.pdf).

### Suggested OIA wording

The core quantitative request should go to LINZ first, with a fallback to territorial authorities if LINZ cannot classify the entries. The request should ask for aggregate counts only and explicitly exclude title numbers, addresses, owners, and any cell small enough to identify a property [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).

Suggested LINZ OIA:

> Please provide aggregate counts, as at the latest practicable date, of Records of Title that currently contain entries recorded under Building Act 2004 s74(1)(b) following notification under s73 for consents granted under s72, grouped by territorial authority, land district, hazard type where recorded, and year of entry where available. Please also provide aggregate counts of entries removed or amended following notification under s74(4), grouped by year of removal/amendment, territorial authority, land district, and predecessor/current statutory basis where available. Please include comparable predecessor entries under Building Act 1991 s36(2) and Local Government Act 1974 s641A where they can be separately identified. Please do not provide title numbers, addresses, owner names, or any other property-identifying data; suppress or combine small cells if necessary.

## What would change this conclusion

- A LINZ aggregate extract showing the current national count, distribution by territorial authority/land district, hazard-type mix, and s74(4) removals would replace the current "not publicly countable" conclusion with a quantified one [LINZ NZ Property Titles layer](https://data.linz.govt.nz/layer/50804-nz-property-titles/).
- Council OIA responses showing many routine removals without MBIE determinations would weaken the "effectively sticky" conclusion; council responses showing zero or near-zero removals would strengthen it [MBIE Determination 2024/045](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-045.pdf); [MBIE Determination 2026/010](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2026/2026-010.pdf).
- I could not verify whether LINZ stores hazard type in a consistently searchable structured field or only as title-entry text; that matters because a national count by hazard type may require text search and manual classification rather than a simple database query [MBIE Determination 2024/053](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/resolving-problems/determinations/2024/2024-053.pdf); [LINZ NZ Property Titles layer](https://data.linz.govt.nz/layer/50804-nz-property-titles/).
- I could not verify how often councils decide an entry is "no longer required" without an MBIE determination, or how often LINZ actually removes the title entry after a council notification under s74(4); those are official-record questions for LINZ and councils [MBIE Natural Hazard Provisions guidance, 2023](https://www.building.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/projects-and-consents/Planning-a-successful-build/Scope-and-design/natural-hazard-provisions-guidance.pdf).
- A resource-management lawyer, council building-control practitioner, or LINZ title-register specialist should review any policy proposal built from this finding, because the observed friction may come from statutory design, council risk practice, title-system mechanics, insurance underwriting, or all of them [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).
