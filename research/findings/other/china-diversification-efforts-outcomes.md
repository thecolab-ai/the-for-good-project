# Diversification away from China is policy-active in trade, education, and tourism — but measured share change is real only in goods trade, and much of that is China's slowdown rather than policy

Part of #438 (child question 4 of the stream framing, `analysis/nz-export-dependency-on-china-what-s-the-actual-ec-framing.md`). The question: which diversification efforts are underway, and are they changing **actual** trade, student, or visitor shares — as opposed to being market-access announcements?

**Narrowing:** this finding measures diversification at the national share level (export shares by destination, enrolment shares by source country, visitor-arrival shares by source country). It does not attempt firm-level FTA utilisation rates, foreign-investment diversification, or substitutability of specific products — sibling issues #499 (decade share movement and its causes) and #502 (where China exposure is hardest to substitute) cover the adjacent ground.

## Executive answer

- **Goods trade is the one channel where shares have measurably moved.** China's share of NZ goods exports fell from a peak of about 32% in calendar 2021 [MFAT, overview of NZ trade in 2021](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/an-overview-of-new-zealands-trade-in-2021) to 25% in 2024 [VUW/Jason Young, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics) and about 24.1% in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx). Post-FTA growth to the EU (exports up 28% to $7.88b in the year to June 2025) and the UK (primary exports up 25% in FTA year two) is real and fast [MFAT NZ-EU FTA overview](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/new-zealand-european-union-free-trade-agreement/nz-eu-fta-overview); [MFAT NZ-UK FTA primary exports update](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/nz-uk-free-trade-agreement-primary-exports-update).
- **But a large part of the China share fall is China-side demand weakness, not policy-driven redirection.** NZ goods exports to China contracted in both 2023 and 2024 (from a $20.1b peak in 2022 to $17.8b in 2024) [VUW/Jason Young, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics), before a modest 3.0% recovery in the year to March 2025 [MFAT trade and economic update Q1 2025](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/trade-and-economic-update-q1-2025-new-zealand-exports-show-growth-and-resilience-despite-global-trade-uncertainty). A denominator that shrinks by itself is not diversification.
- **Education shows no measured diversification at all.** China was 34% of the 92,580 international enrolments in 2025 and India 14% — top-two concentration of 48% [Education NZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments) — which is at least as concentrated as pre-COVID (derived 2019 China share ≈33%, India 15% — see load-bearing claims). This is despite market diversification having been an explicit goal of the International Education Strategy since 2018 [NZ International Education Strategy 2018–2030 (PDF)](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2549-ENZ-NZ-Int-Strategy-A4-cutdown-1-LB-singles2.pdf). Current policy (the Going for Growth plan to double the sector to $7.2b by 2034) is growth-first, not diversification-first [Ministry of Education](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/tertiary-and-further-education/international-education).
- **Tourism shares changed passively, and current policy is working to re-grow China, not diversify from it.** China fell from 11% of March-month visitor arrivals in 2019 to 7% in March 2026 because Chinese arrivals never recovered post-COVID, while Australia (36%→39%) and the US (13%→15%) grew [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/). Meanwhile the Tourism Growth Roadmap funds China as a "core market" ($13.5m marketing across Australia/China/US, vs $6m for India and Southeast Asia) [MBIE Tourism Growth Roadmap](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism/tourism-growth-roadmap), and Immigration NZ's November 2025 trial makes visits by Chinese passport holders from Australia visa-free [Immigration NZ, Nov 2025](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/) — it is measurably lifting Chinese arrivals already [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals up in March](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-up-in-march/).
- **The newest market-access instruments are announcements, not yet outcomes.** The UAE CEPA has been in force only since 28 August 2025 [MFAT FTAs in force](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force), the GCC agreement is concluded but not in force [MFAT GCC trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/nz-gulf-cooperation-council/key-facts-on-new-zealand-gcc-trade), and the India FTA was signed on 27 April 2026 and is not in force [MFAT NZ-India FTA](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement). None can yet have changed shares.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the trade and tourism share trends are High confidence (Stats NZ and MFAT primary data, multiple sources), but the pre-COVID education comparison rests on a derived figure from a single source chain, and attributing the goods-trade share shift between policy and China-side demand weakness is interpretation, not measured fact.

## Evidence

### What is actually underway (the effort inventory)

**Trade.** The government's stated goal is to double export value in 10 years, pursued through FTAs, trade missions, non-tariff-barrier (NTB) work, and investment attraction [MBIE, Promoting global trade and investment](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/going-for-growth/promoting-global-trade-and-investment); [MFAT annual report 2024/25](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/about-us/mfat-annual-reports/mfat-annual-report-2024-25/deliver-an-intensive-targeted-programme-of-trade-and-economic-diplomacy). The recent market-access timeline: NZ–UK FTA in force 31 May 2023 [MFAT NZ-UK FTA overview](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/new-zealand-united-kingdom-free-trade-agreement/new-zealand-united-kingdom-free-trade-agreement-overview); NZ–EU FTA in force 1 May 2024 [MFAT FTAs in force](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force); NZ–UAE CEPA in force 28 August 2025 [MFAT FTAs in force](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force); GCC agreement concluded but not in force [MFAT GCC trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/nz-gulf-cooperation-council/key-facts-on-new-zealand-gcc-trade); NZ–India FTA signed 27 April 2026, not in force [MFAT NZ-India FTA](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement). MBIE reports 18+ completed ministerial trade missions (Vietnam, Saudi Arabia, China, India, Latin America) and resolution of NTBs affecting about $600m in annual trade [MBIE, Promoting global trade and investment](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/going-for-growth/promoting-global-trade-and-investment). Note the missions list includes China itself — the trade agenda is "more markets", not "less China".

**Education.** Market diversification has been an explicit strategy goal since the International Education Strategy 2018–2030, which named reliance on China and India (then roughly half of enrolments) as a long-term risk [NZ International Education Strategy 2018–2030 (PDF)](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2549-ENZ-NZ-Int-Strategy-A4-cutdown-1-LB-singles2.pdf). The current instrument is the International Education Going for Growth Plan (July 2025), which targets doubling the sector's economic contribution from $3.6b in 2024 to $7.2b by 2034 [Ministry of Education](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/strategies-policies-and-programmes/tertiary-and-further-education/international-education). Education NZ says growth in newer markets (Sri Lanka, Nepal) has been incorporated into its 2026/27 investment framework [Education NZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments) — but those markets were each only 3–4% of 2025 enrolments.

**Tourism.** The Tourism Growth Roadmap (May 2025) aims to double 2023 tourism exports ($9.9b) by 2034 ($19.8b) and to return international visitors to at least 2019 levels (3.89m) by 2026. Its 2025/26 marketing spend is $13.5m for the core markets of Australia, **China**, and the US, and $6m for India and Southeast Asia [MBIE Tourism Growth Roadmap](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism/tourism-growth-roadmap). On visitor settings, Immigration NZ's 12-month trial from 3 November 2025 lets Chinese passport holders with an eligible Australian visa visit NZ from Australia visa-free (NZeTA only, up to 3 months) [Immigration NZ, Nov 2025](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/). So tourism policy treats China as a growth market to win back, not an exposure to reduce.

### Trade outcomes: shares are moving, but read the attribution carefully

**The China share has fallen roughly 8 percentage points from its peak.** China took 32% of NZ goods exports in calendar 2021 [MFAT, overview of NZ trade in 2021](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/an-overview-of-new-zealands-trade-in-2021); the share fell to 25% in 2024 [VUW/Jason Young, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics); and the stream's framing analysis computed 24.1% ($19.885b of $82.665b) for the year ended May 2026 from the Stats NZ merchandise trade workbook [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx). That returns the share roughly to its pre-2019 level — VUW notes 25% is comparable to 2018 [VUW/Jason Young, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics).

**Much of the fall is a shrinking China numerator.** NZ exports to China peaked at $20.1b in 2022, fell to $18.4b in 2023 and $17.8b in 2024 [VUW/Jason Young, Feb 2025](https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/news/2025/02/nz-should-be-worried-about-chinas-trade-statistics) (single source — flagged below), with only a modest recovery (goods and services exports to China up 3.0% to $21b) in the year ended March 2025 [MFAT trade and economic update Q1 2025](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/trade-and-economic-update-q1-2025-new-zealand-exports-show-growth-and-resilience-despite-global-trade-uncertainty). A share decline caused by weak Chinese demand is exposure reduction of a sort, but it is not evidence that exporters *chose* new markets — and it can reverse if Chinese demand recovers.

**Post-FTA growth in the UK and EU is the strongest evidence that policy is moving shares.**
- **EU (FTA in force 1 May 2024):** total exports up 28% to $7.88b in the year to June 2025 (goods $5.93b, services $1.95b), with dairy up 64% to $557m, horticulture up 38% to $1.17b (kiwifruit up 42% to $1.04b), industrial products up 44% to $1,191m, seafood up 28% to $363m [MFAT NZ-EU FTA overview](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/new-zealand-european-union-free-trade-agreement/nz-eu-fta-overview); [MFAT key facts on NZ-EU trade](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/new-zealand-european-union-free-trade-agreement/key-facts-on-nz-eu-trade). Note both citations are MFAT publications of Stats NZ data — one origin family; the EU-side Access2Markets page publishes only the pre-FTA forecast ("trade expected to increase by 30%"), not measured results [European Commission, Access2Markets](https://trade.ec.europa.eu/access-to-markets/en/content/eu-new-zealand-free-trade-agreement).
- **UK (FTA in force 31 May 2023):** primary industry exports grew 25% in FTA year two, from $1.06b to $1.33b (year to June 2025). Dairy went from $2m (year to June 2022, pre-FTA) to $157m (year to June 2025); beef from under $15m (June 2023) to $120m (June 2025); apples up 69% and honey up 50% [MFAT NZ-UK FTA primary exports update](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/nz-uk-free-trade-agreement-primary-exports-update). MFAT's own caveat: the largest UK export, sheep meat ($492m, up 37%), sits outside the FTA quota story and its growth was "driven almost entirely by price" [MFAT NZ-UK FTA primary exports update](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/nz-uk-free-trade-agreement-primary-exports-update).
- **Cross-market picture (year ended March 2025):** EU up 22.6% to $7.2b, UK up 21.2% to $3.8b, US up 6.4% to $16.8b, China up 3.0% to $21b (all goods+services). MFAT frames this as FTAs "helping to diversify our export markets" [MFAT trade and economic update Q1 2025](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/trade-and-economic-update-q1-2025-new-zealand-exports-show-growth-and-resilience-despite-global-trade-uncertainty). Two caveats from the same source: the US March-quarter surge (+17.2%) may partly be tariff front-loading, and the US grew without any new FTA — market shifts are not all policy.

**Scale check.** Even after two years of >20% growth, the EU ($7.88b) and UK ($3.8b) combined are still smaller than China ($21b, goods+services, year to March 2025) [MFAT trade and economic update Q1 2025](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/mfat-market-reports/trade-and-economic-update-q1-2025-new-zealand-exports-show-growth-and-resilience-despite-global-trade-uncertainty). The GCC — the largest concluded-but-pending agreement — was 2.9% of NZ's global exports in the year to June 2025 [MFAT GCC trade facts](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/nz-gulf-cooperation-council/key-facts-on-new-zealand-gcc-trade). New-market growth is changing shares at the margin, not displacing China's position as largest goods-export destination.

### Education outcomes: seven years of diversification strategy, no measured share change

The 2025 baseline: 92,580 international enrolments (up 11% on 2024, 80% of the pre-COVID peak), with China 34%, India 14%, Japan 9%, South Korea 4%, Sri Lanka 4%, Germany/USA/Nepal/Philippines 3% each [Education NZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

The pre-COVID comparison: 2019 had over 115,000 enrolments, with India at about 17,300 (≈15%) [ICEF Monitor, Nov 2024](https://monitor.icef.com/2024/11/new-zealand-international-student-enrolments-up-67-in-2023-amid-growing-public-support/). ICEF reports 2023 Chinese enrolments were 64% of their 2019 level, and 2023 data put China at roughly a third of the 69,000 enrolments [ICEF Monitor, Nov 2024](https://monitor.icef.com/2024/11/new-zealand-international-student-enrolments-up-67-in-2023-amid-growing-public-support/); [Education NZ, 2024](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/69000-international-student-enrolments-in-2023) — which implies a 2019 China share of roughly 33% (≈24,200 ÷ 0.64 ≈ 37,800, of 115,705). That is a derived figure from a single source chain (flagged below).

Reading: China's enrolment share in 2025 (34%) is essentially the same as — or slightly above — its pre-COVID share, India's is essentially flat (15%→14%), and the top-two concentration (≈48%) matches the "half of enrolments" the 2018 strategy set out to reduce [NZ International Education Strategy 2018–2030 (PDF)](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2549-ENZ-NZ-Int-Strategy-A4-cutdown-1-LB-singles2.pdf). Growth in Sri Lanka and Nepal is real but small (≈4% and 3% of 2025 enrolments) [Education NZ, 2026](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments). On the measured record, education diversification has not happened; the sector re-grew along its old concentration lines, and the current policy target (doubling value by 2034) is about growth, with China the largest engine.

### Tourism outcomes: passive share change, active re-concentration policy

The share shift since 2019 is real: of 358,900 March 2026 visitor arrivals, 39% were from Australia (36% in March 2019), 15% from the US (13%), 7% from China (11%), and 3% from India (2%) [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/). On an annual basis, China was 407,100 of 3.89m arrivals in calendar 2019 (≈10.5%) [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/) versus 297,000 of 3.63m in the year ended March 2026 (≈8.2%) [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/).

But this is recovery-lag, not diversification policy: Chinese arrivals were already falling in 2019 (down 9% that year) [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/), collapsed during COVID, and have not recovered — while Australia and the US rebounded past their 2019 levels. Policy is now pulling the other way: China sits inside the roadmap's $13.5m core-market marketing spend [MBIE Tourism Growth Roadmap](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism/tourism-growth-roadmap), and the NZeTA trial is measurably lifting the China channel — in the five months from November 2025 to March 2026 there were 189,200 Chinese-passport visitor arrivals (vs 129,300 a year earlier), 40% arriving via Australia (vs 25%) [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals up in March](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-up-in-march/). Chinese arrivals rose 48,000 year-on-year to 297,000 [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/). The dedicated emerging-market spend ($6m for India + Southeast Asia) is less than half the core-market spend [MBIE Tourism Growth Roadmap](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism/tourism-growth-roadmap).

### Bottom line against the question

| Channel | Diversification effort underway? | Shares actually changing? | Attribution to policy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goods trade | Yes — UK/EU/UAE FTAs in force; GCC, India signed; NTB work, missions | Yes — China 32% (2021) → ~24% (2026); EU/UK growing 20%+ post-FTA | Partial — FTA-linked growth is real, but China's own demand contraction did much of the share work |
| Education | On paper since 2018; current plan is growth-first | No — China 34% and top-two ≈48%, at or above pre-COVID concentration | n/a — no outcome to attribute |
| Tourism | Mixed — small emerging-market spend; core policy re-grows China | Yes, but passively — China 11%→7% of March arrivals via non-recovery | No — the share shift happened despite policy, which is now lifting China arrivals again |

## What would change this conclusion

- **Product-level redirection evidence.** If Stats NZ product-by-destination data showed the *same products* (e.g. dairy, logs) shifting volume from China to the EU/UK/UAE at comparable prices, the goods-trade story would upgrade from "partly passive" to "active diversification". Conversely, if EU/UK growth is mostly new product lines while China-bound volumes held, the FTA growth is additive, not diversifying. Sibling #499 (decade share movement) and #502 (substitutability) should test this.
- **A published 2019 enrolment-by-country table.** My pre-COVID education comparison rests on a derived 2019 China share (≈33%) from one source chain. An Education Counts or ENZ table for 2019 could confirm or overturn "no education diversification" — if China's 2019 share was actually well above 34% (some sector commentary suggests university-level China shares were much higher), the sector has diversified more than this finding credits.
- **China demand recovery.** If Chinese demand rebounds strongly (the year-to-March-2025 data already shows +3.0%), the China share could climb back toward 30% within a couple of years, revealing how little of the share fall was structural. Watching the next two Stats NZ annual merchandise trade releases would settle this.
- **UAE/GCC/India utilisation data from 2027+.** These agreements are too new (or not in force) to judge. First-year utilisation reports like MFAT's UK/EU updates would convert them from announcements to measurable outcomes — or not.
- **Spend-based (not arrivals-based) tourism shares.** I measured tourism by arrivals. MBIE International Visitor Survey spend-by-market data could show China's *spend* share diverging from its arrivals share (Chinese visitors historically spent more per capita), changing the exposure picture.

**What I could not verify:** the exact 2019 China enrolment count (derived only); the calendar-year exports-to-China contraction values (single source, VUW); any independent (non-MFAT/Stats NZ-origin) measurement of post-FTA EU/UK export growth — the EU-side Access2Markets page publishes forecasts only. I found no NZ government document that sets a measurable *diversification target* (e.g. a ceiling on any single market's share) in any of the three channels — the targets that exist are growth targets. Absence of evidence is not proof no such target exists internally, but none is published on the MFAT, MBIE, or Ministry of Education pages reviewed here.
