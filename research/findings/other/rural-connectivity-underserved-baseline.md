# Rural broadband underservice has measured access and price gaps, but no public household count by cause

## Executive answer

- A Stats NZ 2023 Census aggregation gives an estimated **285,045 households** in rural settlements or other rural areas; the comparable majority-rural SA2 estimate is **285,162 households**, of which **222,300 reported internet access**, leaving **29,658 households without internet among stated responses** and **62,862 households either without internet access or not stated** in those majority-rural SA2s [Stats NZ household SA1 feature service](https://services2.arcgis.com/vKb0s8tBIA3bdocZ/arcgis/rest/services/2023_Census_totals_by_topic_for_households_by_SA1/FeatureServer), [Stats NZ household SA2 feature service](https://services2.arcgis.com/vKb0s8tBIA3bdocZ/arcgis/rest/services/2023_Census_totals_by_topic_for_households_by_SA2/FeatureServer), [Stats NZ SA1 higher-geographies concordance](https://services.arcgis.com/yz3Bly07x1dczMks/arcgis/rest/services/statistical_area_1_higher_geographies_2023_generalised/FeatureServer). This is a household-access floor, not a coverage, speed, reliability, or affordability measure [Stats NZ household dataset metadata](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/content/items/7d8a5993db144f95bf9be2f64b14e467?f=json).
- The public **no-terrestrial-service** count is not available. The Commerce Commission says **95% of rural addresses** were inside at least one mobile network operator's 4G fixed-wireless footprint in June 2025, so roughly **5% of rural addresses** sat outside that specific footprint, but the same source separately reports rural coverage for 5G FWA, WISP/NC FWA, copper, HFC, and other technologies; the 5% figure therefore must not be read as a count outside all terrestrial service [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). MBIE's 2023 ministerial briefing expected **up to 3,800 households** to require bespoke solutions after government connectivity programmes, but that is a programme-residual estimate, not a current household count of no terrestrial service [MBIE 2023 incoming-minister briefing](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf).
- **Nominal-but-not-orderable service is real but uncounted publicly.** The Commerce Commission map is provider-supplied, annual, parcel-threshold coverage data rather than a live orderability tool, and NIFF says rural tower congestion has caused stop-sells where no new users can be supported [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).
- **Poor performance is concentrated in legacy copper and some 4G fixed-wireless contexts.** In April 2025 MBNZ data, rural ADSL averaged **10 Mbps**, rural 4G fixed wireless averaged **56 Mbps**, and Starlink Residential averaged **226 Mbps**; ADSL could reliably support one UHD Netflix stream for only **12%** of measured households and had upstream-load latency of **1,290 ms** [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission MBNZ programme page](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/).
- **Rural service has a measured price premium, but not a measured unaffordability count.** The Commission's June 2025 weighted price table shows rural households paid **$85.77/month** on average versus **$72.65/month** for urban households, an **18% rural premium**, and rural 4G FWA plans cost **64% to 94% more** than comparable urban 4G FWA tiers; the same report treats affordability as a key barrier in regional connectedness, but does not publish a household-level count that separates unaffordability from preference, coverage, or skills barriers [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - official sources support the measured household-access baseline, rural coverage-footprint limits, performance gaps, orderability concerns, and price premiums, but exact household counts by "no terrestrial service", "not orderable", "poor performance", and "unaffordable" require provider address-level orderability, plan, income, and performance data that is not public.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This finding treats "rural" as Stats NZ's Urban Rural Indicator categories **Rural settlement** and **Rural other**, which EHINZ describes as rural/other areas under 1,000 residents, including rural settlements with 200-999 residents or at least 40 dwellings and other rural areas [EHINZ urban-rural profile](https://www.ehinz.ac.nz/indicators/population-vulnerability/urbanrural-profile/). I aggregated Stats NZ's 2023 Census household feature service by SA1 using the Stats NZ SA1 higher-geographies service, then used the SA2 household service for the internet-access field because many SA1 telecommunications cells are suppressed or marked not available; the resulting majority-rural SA2 total, **285,162 households**, differs by only **117 households** from the direct SA1 rural total of **285,045 households** [Stats NZ household SA1 feature service](https://services2.arcgis.com/vKb0s8tBIA3bdocZ/arcgis/rest/services/2023_Census_totals_by_topic_for_households_by_SA1/FeatureServer), [Stats NZ household SA2 feature service](https://services2.arcgis.com/vKb0s8tBIA3bdocZ/arcgis/rest/services/2023_Census_totals_by_topic_for_households_by_SA2/FeatureServer), [Stats NZ SA1 higher-geographies concordance](https://services.arcgis.com/yz3Bly07x1dczMks/arcgis/rest/services/statistical_area_1_higher_geographies_2023_generalised/FeatureServer).

The 2023 Census majority-rural SA2 aggregation found **222,300 households with access to the internet**, **251,958 total stated telecommunications responses**, and **285,162 households in occupied private dwellings**; that implies **29,658 stated-response households without internet access** and **62,862 households without internet access or without a stated internet-access response** [Stats NZ household SA2 feature service](https://services2.arcgis.com/vKb0s8tBIA3bdocZ/arcgis/rest/services/2023_Census_totals_by_topic_for_households_by_SA2/FeatureServer). This is an imperfect but useful household baseline because Stats NZ warns that 2023 Census counts use confidentiality rules, fixed random rounding, suppression, and placeholder values, and its household dataset notes that percentages should be calculated using "Total stated" where applicable [Stats NZ household dataset metadata](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/content/items/7d8a5993db144f95bf9be2f64b14e467?f=json).

### Coverage footprint gaps and residual bespoke solutions

The Commerce Commission's rural coverage data says **95% of rural addresses** were within at least one MNO 4G FWA coverage area in June 2025, rural **5G FWA** reached **16%** of rural addresses, and **71%** of rural addresses were within at least one WISP network, although WISP coverage was based on maps from 25 WISPs and actual WISP coverage may be higher [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). That evidence supports a narrow statement about the 4G FWA footprint, not an estimate of households outside all terrestrial broadband, because the same report separately discusses copper, WISP/NC FWA, HFC, 5G FWA, satellite, and other technologies [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). MBIE's 2023 briefing says UFB reached **87% of the population**, RBI had improved broadband available to **81,904 rural homes and businesses**, and **up to 3,800 households** were expected to require bespoke solutions after programmes completed; this indicates a remaining hard-to-serve group but does not provide a current national no-terrestrial-service count [MBIE 2023 incoming-minister briefing](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf).

The Remote Users Scheme confirms that a residual underserved group exists but does not publish a current count of unconnected households: NIFF says RUS targeted rural and remote households where broadband was not available, only voice/text service existed, or internet coverage was poor, and offered either network-extension work or a one-off grant of up to **$2,000** for eligible households still unable to receive coverage [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/). A 2022 Beehive release says **$15 million** was allocated to RUS and that some communities then had only voice and text or extremely slow internet, while the 2023 MBIE briefing records RUS as the programme for the remaining bespoke-solution households [Beehive, 17 November 2022](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/govt-delivers-connectivity-rural-and-remote-households), [MBIE 2023 incoming-minister briefing](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf).

### Nominal but not orderable

The public map cannot prove orderability at an address: ComCom says providers supply the map data, the data is as at **30 June 2025**, coverage is updated annually rather than live, wireless parcel coverage is assigned when at least **50%** of the parcel falls inside a provider coverage area, satellite is excluded because obstacles can block a theoretically national service, not all providers are included, and the map may differ from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). NIFF's RCU page gives the clearest orderability evidence: increased demand on rural broadband towers has caused some towers to enter **stop sell**, meaning no new users can be supported, and stop-sells and congestion restrict work from home, home schooling, e-commerce, whānau connection, events information, and entertainment [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).

The right conclusion is therefore negative: public sources support that nominal coverage can overstate orderable service, but I could not find a public national count of rural households in stop-sell, capacity-constrained, obstructed-satellite, or otherwise non-orderable areas [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).

### Poor performance

The Commission's rural MBNZ analysis covers ADSL, VDSL, 4G FWA, and LEO satellite, but not enough rural NC FWA data, so WISP performance is under-measured in the public baseline [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). In April 2025 testing, rural **ADSL** averaged **10 Mbps**, rural **4G FWA** averaged **56 Mbps**, Starlink Residential averaged **226 Mbps**, and Starlink Residential Lite averaged **200 Mbps**; the Commission's own speed guide says **0-5 Mbps** is basic browsing/email/lower-resolution video and **5-40 Mbps** is only "possibly" enough for one UHD stream or multiple HD streams [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same MBNZ section reports that only **12%** of ADSL households could reliably stream one UHD Netflix stream, **0%** could reliably stream two or more, and ADSL upstream-load latency was **1,290 ms**, which the Commission said likely affects most online activity beyond basic web browsing [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Satellite improves the performance baseline for many rural users but is not a universal fix: the Commission says LEO satellite is subject to heavy rain, snow, trees, buildings, terrain, satellite handovers, high demand, ground-station congestion, radio-frequency interference, and solar activity, while the connectivity map excludes satellite because local obstacles can block line of sight [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

### Price premiums and affordability limits

The Commission's weighted June 2025 regional table shows rural households paid **$85.77/month** on average for broadband versus **$72.65/month** for urban households, or **$13.12/month** and **18%** extra for rural households, with every listed region showing a rural premium [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The largest rural-urban premiums in that table were **Taranaki at 27%**, **Waikato at 26%**, **Northland at 22%**, and **Wellington at 19%**, while the issue's named regions still showed premiums in Bay of Plenty (**16%**), Gisborne/Hawke's Bay (**13%**), Otago (**15%**), Southland (**11%**), Canterbury (**11%**), and Tasman/Nelson/Marlborough/West Coast (**16%**) [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Rural 4G FWA shows a sharper price gap than the all-technology average: the Commission found rural 4G FWA prices were **64%** higher than urban for plans up to 120 GB, **72%** higher for 121-299 GB, **94%** higher for 300 GB-plus, and **71%** higher for unlimited plans [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The Commission's regional-connectivity chapter states its general view that affordability is the key barrier to broadband connectedness across the regions it profiled, but it is still commissioning more work on mobile-only households without broadband, so the unaffordability count cannot yet be separated from preference, coverage, or skills barriers [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- Address-level provider orderability data showing low rural stop-sell and failed-install rates would weaken the finding that nominal coverage overstates usable service [Commerce Commission connectivity map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).
- A public ComCom or MBIE table that reconciles rural **addresses**, Census **households**, **connections**, **premises passed**, and RUS **eligible applicants** would replace the current unquantified no-terrestrial/residual-bespoke gap with a defensible point estimate [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [MBIE 2023 incoming-minister briefing](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27989-briefing-for-the-incoming-minister-for-media-and-communications-proactiverelease-pdf), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- Household-level price-to-income analysis for rural broadband would sharpen the unaffordability count, because the Commission's price tables show rural premiums but do not classify which households cannot afford service [Commerce Commission 2025 monitoring report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- More MBNZ rural WISP/NC FWA volunteers or published WISP performance data would change the performance conclusion for places where WISPs are the main practical alternative to copper, cellular FWA, or satellite [Commerce Commission MBNZ programme page](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/).
- I could not verify a current public count of RUS applications, approved grants, still-unserved eligible households, provider stop-sell addresses, or failed satellite installs; those require agency/provider data or a human-held official information request [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
