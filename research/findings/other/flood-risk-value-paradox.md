# NZ flood-risk homes show a reported discount-and-faster-growth signal, but the public evidence does not yet independently reproduce Cotality's paradox

## Executive answer

- **Best answer:** the paradox is credible as a reported Cotality finding, but I could not independently reproduce it from public aggregate data. RNZ reported on 3 July 2026 that Cotality combined monthly sale prices since 2020 with its flood score and found high-flood-score homes selling at a discount while rising faster than no-risk homes after the 2023 severe weather events; ODT republished the same RNZ story, so it corroborates access and wording but is not an independent analysis [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties) ([Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260702191030/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties)); [ODT/RNZ, 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz) ([Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703010558/https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz)).
- **Where strongest:** Auckland is the strongest quantified example in the public material: RNZ reports Cotality compared Auckland flood-zoned houses with unaffected properties in a 500m buffer, found a $70,000-$100,000 discount, and found 18% value growth since 2020 versus 13% for the nearby unaffected comparison group [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [ODT/RNZ, 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz).
- Hawke's Bay is named by RNZ as another place where the pattern held, but the public article gives no Hawke's Bay discount or appreciation number; that makes Auckland the only publicly quantified "strongest" location found in this pass [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/).
- The reported method is plausible but proprietary: Cotality says its NZ FloodMap/FloodScore products provide nationwide property-level flood scores, modelled flood extents by flood type and return period, and address positions generally placed on the main dwelling; the public page does not publish the scores, the transaction panel, the matching rules, or the output tables needed to audit RNZ's numbers [Cotality FloodMap, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.cotality.com/nz/products/floodmap) ([Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260212030517/https://www.cotality.com/nz/products/floodmap)).
- Treat the headline as **Medium confidence** for "Cotality found this in its data" and **Low-Medium confidence** for "the market truly rewards flood risk" until a non-identifying repeat-sales or hedonic analysis is published. This caution matters because Auckland's official public guidance makes flood-risk information visible to buyers, Treasury says risk-based insurance pricing is already lifting costs for some flood-exposed households, and international flood-risk literature more commonly finds discounts, overvaluation, or slower appreciation rather than a clear premium for high-risk homes [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/09/searching-for-a-home-here-s-what-you-need-to-look-out-for/); [Treasury, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf); [Cotality Miami case study](https://www.cotality.com/insights/articles/the-impact-of-flood-risk-on-property-values-a-case-study-in-miami); [Hino and Burke, PNAS, 2021](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2003374118).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the direct Cotality/RNZ result is clearly reported and Cotality's flood-score product exists, but the key sale-price-by-risk aggregates are not public, the Auckland figure uses a 2020 baseline rather than a clean post-2023 event window, and the Hawke's Bay strength is unquantified in public.

## Evidence

### What RNZ/Cotality actually reported

RNZ's 3 July 2026 article says Cotality supplied the data exclusively to RNZ, and says the dataset combined average monthly sale prices since 2020 with Cotality's flood score, which assigns each New Zealand property a risk score from zero to five using property history, council data, and other information [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [ODT/RNZ, 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz). RNZ quotes Cotality head of data Craig Dargusch saying Cotality expected the highest-risk properties to have the lowest growth rate after the 2023 severe weather events, but instead found the highest flood-risk properties had the highest growth rate; because that quote and the underlying result are from the same Cotality/RNZ source chain, I treat the paradox itself as single-source public evidence rather than independently replicated evidence [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [ODT/RNZ, 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz).

The strongest public numeric result is Auckland: RNZ says Cotality compared flood-zoned Auckland houses with unaffected properties in a 500m buffer, found a $70,000-$100,000 discount for flood-zoned houses, and found 18% value growth since 2020 versus 13% for other properties [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [ODT/RNZ, 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/flood-risk-homes-gaining-value-faster-others-data-shows-rnz). The same article says the pattern also held in Hawke's Bay, but it does not publish Hawke's Bay growth, discount, sample-size, confidence interval, or neighbourhood-matching numbers [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties).

The headline question asks whether homes have appreciated faster since the 2023 Auckland Anniversary floods and Cyclone Gabrielle, but the public Auckland number is "since 2020"; without the underlying time series, I cannot tell how much of the 18% versus 13% gap accumulated before January-February 2023 versus after those events [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/). This timing caveat is important because Cotality's June 2026 Home Value Index says Auckland values were soft in the current market, with all Auckland sub-markets down over three-month and 12-month horizons and peak-to-current falls around 20% or more, so a 2020-to-2026 growth comparison can reflect the pandemic boom, the 2022 downturn, and later recovery rather than only the flood-risk signal [Cotality HVI, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.cotality.com/nz/insights/articles/property-values-feel-the-conflict-pinch-in-june).

### Why the method is plausible but not auditable from public data

Cotality's public FloodMap page says its NZ flood-risk tools assess flood likelihood and impact at broad geographic and individual-property level, include modelled extents by flood type and return period, provide a property-level flood score for every address, and generally position addresses on the main dwelling [Cotality FloodMap, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.cotality.com/nz/products/floodmap). Those product claims support the plausibility of a property-level flood-score analysis, but they do not expose the score definitions, version history, matching algorithm, transaction sample, exclusion rules, or confidence intervals needed to verify the RNZ/Cotality result [Cotality FloodMap, accessed 2026-07-03](https://www.cotality.com/nz/products/floodmap); [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties).

Public hazard data exists for the two named regions, but it is not a substitute for Cotality's proprietary score-and-sale panel. Auckland Council directs buyers to Flood Viewer as a free map for understanding flood risks at and around a property, and says the viewer identifies flood plains, flood-prone areas, overland flow paths, and coastal inundation [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/09/searching-for-a-home-here-s-what-you-need-to-look-out-for/). Hawke's Bay Regional Council publishes a Cyclone Gabrielle flood-extent map, but says the map shows observed flood extent rather than depth, was assembled from photos, videos, satellite photos, local-council building inspection information, and ground contours, and should not be treated as a modelled future flood-hazard map [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/).

Because the issue forbids publishing address-level or identifying property data, a proper open check would need to aggregate before release: for example, by comparing repeat-sales or sale-price indexes for risk strata within non-identifying meshblock/SA1/suburb groups, suppressing small cells, and reporting only aggregate coefficients or index paths [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md). I did not publish or derive address-level examples, and I did not use homes.co.nz screenshots because manually sampling visible properties would create selection bias and could drift into identifying-property evidence rather than aggregate analysis [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/09/searching-for-a-home-here-s-what-you-need-to-look-out-for/).

### Why the paradox is load-bearing

The reported pattern is counter-intuitive because Treasury's January 2026 insurance-affordability Cabinet paper says residential insurance premiums have grown faster than CPI, premiums have grown faster for some people in high-risk areas, access is becoming harder in areas with high earthquake and flood risk, and recent industry moves toward risk-based pricing for risks such as flooding have contributed to higher premiums for exposed households [Treasury, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf). Treasury also says residential insurance prices primarily reflect exposure to hazards such as earthquakes and floods, and that the insurance review would consider insurers' use of property-level risk-based pricing [Treasury, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/cabinet-paper-eco-26-sub-0003.pdf).

The reported NZ pattern also runs against common international evidence. Cotality's own Miami case study says a hedonic model found 100-year flood-zone locations reduced home price per square foot by 9%-18% and that, after controlling for factors such as property attributes and location, a property in a flood zone sold for less and appreciated slower over time than a similar property outside the flood zone [Cotality Miami case study](https://www.cotality.com/insights/articles/the-impact-of-flood-risk-on-property-values-a-case-study-in-miami). Hino and Burke's PNAS study says US flood-zone houses were overvalued in aggregate and that disclosure and information frictions affect whether flood risk is capitalised into prices; that supports caution about assuming observed prices efficiently price flood risk [Hino and Burke, 2021](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2003374118).

There is still a plausible market explanation for the NZ signal: RNZ reports Cotality's interpretation that buyers may be accepting flood risk in exchange for a cheaper entry price, and Auckland Council says buyers have public tools to check flood, landslide, and other natural-hazard risks before buying or renting [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Auckland Council, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/09/searching-for-a-home-here-s-what-you-need-to-look-out-for/). That explanation is not proven by the public data, because a relative growth rebound from a discounted base can coexist with a persistent level discount and does not prove buyers are ignoring flood risk [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Cotality Miami case study](https://www.cotality.com/insights/articles/the-impact-of-flood-risk-on-property-values-a-case-study-in-miami).

### Regional read

Auckland is the only region with a public numeric comparison in the RNZ/Cotality article, and Auckland Council's own recovery material says more than 1,200 high-risk Auckland properties are expected to be purchased by the council after the 2023 extreme weather events, which independently confirms that Auckland has a large enough high-risk storm-affected property set for market effects to matter [Auckland Council, 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2025/02/future-land-use/); [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties). Auckland Council's recovery FAQs say Category 3 properties meet the threshold for intolerable risk to life with no feasible intervention and are eligible for voluntary buy-out, which is a materially different subset from every property with a flood-score or flood-zone flag [Auckland Council recovery FAQs, 2026](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2023/08/recovery-faqs/).

Hawke's Bay is a credible candidate for strong effects because Hawke's Bay Regional Council says Cyclone Gabrielle caused widespread river and stream flooding in the region on 13-14 February 2023, and its flood-extent page says some mapped areas had only surface flooding while other areas were flooded metres deep [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/). However, without Hawke's Bay-specific Cotality sale-price aggregates or a public repeat-sales analysis, the public evidence only supports "Hawke's Bay was named as a place where the pattern held", not "Hawke's Bay was the strongest location" [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Cotality technical note or reproducible aggregate table would materially raise confidence: the minimum useful table would include risk-score bands, monthly or quarterly sale-price index paths, sample sizes, filters, matching geography, repeat-sales/hedonic controls, and uncertainty intervals, with no address-level records [Cotality FloodMap](https://www.cotality.com/nz/products/floodmap); [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties).
- A clean post-event window would change the answer: if the 18% versus 13% Auckland gap is mostly pre-January 2023, then it does not answer the issue's "since the 2023 floods" question; if the gap widens after January-February 2023 in a controlled repeat-sales model, the paradox becomes much stronger [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties); [Cotality HVI, 2026](https://www.cotality.com/nz/insights/articles/property-values-feel-the-conflict-pinch-in-june).
- A non-identifying independent analysis using public hazard layers and sales/valuation data would change confidence if it reproduced the Auckland result and published aggregate-only outputs; the design would need to avoid publishing addresses, exact parcels, or small cells because this stream concerns vulnerable property owners and risky household financial decisions [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).
- Hawke's Bay-specific Cotality tables or an independent Hawke's Bay repeat-sales result would change the regional answer; the public evidence currently confirms flood relevance but not the size of the value paradox there [Hawke's Bay Regional Council, 2026](https://www.hbrc.govt.nz/our-council/cyclone-gabrielle-response/cyclone-gabrielle-flooding/); [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties).
- I could not verify homes.co.nz suburb/sales snapshots in a way that was both unbiased and privacy-safe, and I did not use individual listings, property histories, or addresses [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../../CONTRIBUTING.md); [docs/METHOD.md](../../../docs/METHOD.md).

- Does the Auckland 18% versus 13% growth gap persist if the baseline is January 2023 rather than 2020 [RNZ, 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/657275/flood-risk-homes-defy-analysts-expectations-gain-value-faster-than-unaffected-properties)?
