# Iwi commercial governance models reduce some risks through separation, policy and mandate, but public evidence does not prove one best model

## Executive answer

- The clearest public pattern is a layered model: an iwi authority or post-settlement governance entity sets strategic direction, distributions and non-commercial objectives, while a separate commercial arm manages investments under a commercial mandate. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)
- Separation helps clarify accountability, but it is not a substitute for mandate: Te Tari Whakatau says post-settlement governance entities must be representative, transparent, accountable, beneficial to the claimant group and ratified before settlement assets transfer. [Te Tari Whakatau, Red Book, 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf)
- The best-supported risk controls are explicit investment rules, diversification, liquidity discipline, debt limits and protected-land mechanisms, not simply "professional management"; Waikato-Tainui's Puna framework/SIPO model, Ngāi Tahu's Investments Charter, Te Rarawa's 4 Pou model and Waikato-Tainui's Te Wherowhero title all show different versions of that pattern. [Tainui Group Holdings, Puna Whakatupu Taangata](https://www.tgh.co.nz/our-iwi/puna-whakatupu-taangata/); [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report 2024/25](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)
- Distributions are where governance trade-offs become visible: Ngāi Tahu says its FY2025 distribution of $80.8m was 4.6% of net tribal assets but above what the portfolio can sustainably generate long term, while Waikato-Tainui targets a 2.5% of net assets cash distribution and is changing the portfolio to meet it sustainably. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2025 web page](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/opportunities-and-resources/publications/ngai-tahu-annual-reports/annual-report-2025/); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)
- Public evidence does not prove that independent directors or external expertise alone produce better outcomes: Ngāti Awa Group Holdings publicly uses up to five Ngāti Awa directors plus two independent directors, but Ngāti Awa still had a 2025 portfolio concentrated in primary industries and only mid-range returns, while TDB says iwi results diverged mainly with asset mix, valuations, debt and sector exposure. [Ngāti Awa Group Holdings board page](https://www.naghl.co.nz/board-of-directors); [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium -- the structures, policy mechanisms and reported outcomes are well supported by primary iwi reports and TDB's public comparison, but causality is weaker because public sources do not publish board papers, investment committee minutes, member trust data, or counterfactual comparisons.

## Evidence

### Scope and source basis

This finding answers issue #494 by comparing public governance mechanisms rather than re-answering the sibling venture-outcomes question: it uses the 2025 TDB iwi investment comparison, iwi annual reports and governance pages, Te Tari Whakatau settlement guidance, and a peer-reviewed PSGE management article. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf); [Te Tari Whakatau, Red Book, 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf); [Cribb and Mika, MAI Journal, 2023](https://www.journal.mai.ac.nz/system/files/Cribb_FNL_webready.pdf)

The public governance record is strongest for entity design, annual-report controls, distributions, debt, asset allocation and selected risk-management statements; it is weaker for whether members trust the model, whether board composition caused performance, and whether commercial confidentiality hides material failures or good decisions. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf); [Cribb and Mika, MAI Journal, 2023](https://www.journal.mai.ac.nz/system/files/Cribb_FNL_webready.pdf)

### What the models have in common

TDB's 2025 comparison says the ten iwi it reviewed generally have an overarching trust or PSGE responsible for strategic direction, member distributions and non-commercial objectives, alongside a separate commercial arm that manages assets and executes investment decisions under a commercial mandate. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

That model fits the Crown settlement framework: Te Tari Whakatau says a suitable governance entity is required before settlement assets transfer, that the Crown's principles require representative, transparent and accountable governance for the whole claimant group, and that the entity must be ratified by the claimant community. [Te Tari Whakatau, Red Book, 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf)

The same guidance keeps member mandate separate from negotiation mandate: mandated negotiators do not gain control of settlement assets, and post-settlement governance must be approved or ratified by the claimant group. [Te Tari Whakatau, Red Book, 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf)

Cribb and Mika's 2023 PSGE article supports a caution against structure-only conclusions: PSGEs hold and manage collective settlement assets but also serve social, cultural, environmental and other iwi-defined purposes, and the authors find challenges tend to arise from operationalisation and management rather than design alone. [Cribb and Mika, MAI Journal, 2023](https://www.journal.mai.ac.nz/system/files/Cribb_FNL_webready.pdf)

### Risk, distributions and asset protection

The strongest public link between governance and risk is through asset allocation and leverage: TDB says iwi with property-focused portfolios tend to carry more debt, financial-asset-heavy iwi tend to carry little debt, and capital structures remain generally conservative across the cohort. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

Waikato-Tainui's 2025 report makes the governance mechanism concrete: TGH manages four tribal funds as Chief Investment Officer under Puna, says the WRLT fund's FY2025 loss would have been roughly double without global equities, and says WRLT still held 80% of value in local real-estate-related assets. [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

Waikato-Tainui also shows how asset-protection rules can shape commercial structure: its Brookfield Ruakura model uses long-term ground leases so Waikato-Tainui retains ownership of whenua, and its financial statements say Te Wherowhero title land cannot be sold, alienated, mortgaged or gifted without a mandated process and unanimous consent of custodial trustees. [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

Ngāi Tahu shows the distribution-governance tension from the other direction: its FY2025 report says the $80.8m distribution represented 4.6% of net tribal assets, but Te Rūnanga concluded distributions had exceeded what the portfolio could sustainably generate after accounting for whakapapa registration growth and inflation, so it cut the Office's FY2026 budget by about 30% and began work on a longer-term distribution strategy. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2025 web page](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/opportunities-and-resources/publications/ngai-tahu-annual-reports/annual-report-2025/)

Te Rarawa provides a smaller-portfolio example of the same trade-off: Te Waka Pupuri Pūtea reports commercial asset growth from $32.6m in 2016 to $115.2m in 2025, but says FY2025 required slowing new investment, consolidating assets, strengthening liquidity and avoiding decisions that transfer undue risk to future generations. [Te Rarawa annual report 2024/25](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

### Accountability and trust

The public evidence links accountability most clearly to mandate processes, not to financial performance metrics: Te Tari Whakatau says mandate maintenance is helped by audited accounts, AGMs and elections, and warns that representatives can lose mandate confidence if they do not keep the claimant group informed or follow the Deed of Mandate/constitution. [Te Tari Whakatau, Red Book, 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf)

The Auditor-General's 2025 settlement report reinforces that commercial governance operates inside a wider relationship: it says PSGEs hold and manage settlement redress for iwi and hapū, and warns that Crown delivery failures can cause iwi and hapū to miss economic and social opportunities from redress. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf)

The Auditor-General also records PSGEs saying public organisations often treat settlements as transactions rather than a broader strategic relationship, which matters because governance models can protect iwi assets internally but cannot fully protect settlement durability if Crown obligations are late, fragmented or poorly coordinated. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A public or iwi-approved dataset linking governance features to venture-level outcomes, member trust, distributions, debt, liquidity, write-downs and exits would allow stronger causal conclusions than this case-study synthesis.
- Board papers, investment committee minutes, SIPO compliance reports, distribution-policy reviews, and post-investment reviews would show whether governance controls actually changed decisions before losses occurred.
- Member surveys or iwi-approved accountability reviews could overturn the assumption that transparent reporting and mandate mechanisms are the main public proxies for trust.
- Māori commercial lawyers, iwi governors, PSGE practitioners and members of affected iwi should review this before it informs any policy or funding design; public desk research cannot judge whether a model is tika for a particular iwi.
- I could not verify comparable public detail for every iwi's board appointment rules, conflicts policy, director independence, member voting turnout, investment committee powers, or protected-asset consent thresholds.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which iwi publish enough SIPO or investment-charter detail to compare risk appetite, liquidity thresholds, distribution rules and debt limits directly?
- What member-accountability metrics would be useful without undermining rangatiratanga, commercial confidentiality or Māori data sovereignty?
- After failed or stressed ventures, which iwi publicly report changes to investment approval, risk appetite, board composition or member reporting?
