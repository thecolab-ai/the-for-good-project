# NZ's June 2025 group classification of unscheduled peptides remains deferred and unenacted as of the 76th MCC meeting (April 2026)

**The question answered:** which named peptides are currently prescription medicines vs unscheduled under the Medicines Act 1981, and exactly what happened to Medsafe's June 2025 "Classification of Unscheduled Peptides" submission after the 74th Medicines Classification Committee (MCC) deferred it on 23 July 2025 — enacted, gazetted, or still pending, and what changes on the ground either way.

## Executive answer

- **The June 2025 group-classification proposal is still deferred and unenacted.** The 74th MCC (23 July 2025) agreed classification "would help to reduce this risk of harm" but deferred the decision "pending additional information" about impact on external parties such as the cosmetics sector [Medsafe, 74th MCC minutes, 23 Jul 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm). I verified directly against the two subsequent public MCC records — the 75th meeting minutes (19 November 2025) and the 76th meeting agenda (for a meeting held 22 April 2026, agenda published 10 March 2026) — and **peptides do not appear on either**: the 75th meeting's only "matters arising" were objections to unrelated Lithium and Vitamin D recommendations, and the 76th agenda states plainly that "no public submissions were received for this meeting, or the 75th MCC meeting held in November 2025" [Medsafe, 75th MCC minutes, 19 Nov 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/75mccMin19Nov2025.htm); [Medsafe, 76th MCC agenda, published 10 Mar 2026](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen76/agen76.htm). No Gazette notice for a peptide group entry appears on Medsafe's Gazette-notices record either (most recent entry: 29 August 2025) [Medsafe, Recent Gazette Notices, revised 4 Sep 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp). Minutes for the 76th meeting itself are not yet published as of this research (no 2026 folder exists yet on Medsafe's minutes index) — so the meeting's discussion, if any, is unconfirmed.
- **Medsafe itself has already cleared the objection the MCC deferred for.** A Secretariat's Note appended to the 74th minutes (published 30 October 2025 — three months after the meeting) records that Medsafe consulted cosmetics-industry professional bodies, "no issues were raised", and "Medsafe therefore recommends the peptides outlined in this proposal are classified as prescription medicines" [Medsafe, 74th MCC minutes, Item 5.7 Secretariat's Note](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm). Despite that recommendation being on the public record since October 2025, no MCC meeting in the nine months since has put a peptide-classification decision back on the agenda for a vote. The reform is stalled in an administrative gap, not blocked by unresolved objections.

- **A specific correction to this stream's framing premise: growth-hormone secretagogues are not confirmed as "already prescription medicines" — and the primary source that speaks to this exact class is internally unresolved on the question.** The framing document for this stream (PR #711) and the parent discover issue both group CJC-1295, ipamorelin and tesamorelin with BPC-157/SARMs/TB-500 as "already scheduled". Medsafe's own 16-page June 2025 submission — which is exhaustive about what is and isn't currently scheduled, down to individual peptides like PTD-DBM and B7-33 — **never mentions growth-hormone secretagogues at all**, either as already-scheduled background or as a proposed addition. The 2013 Medsafe submission on peptide-based performance and image-enhancing drugs (PIEDs) does address this exact class, under its "GROWTH HORMONE RELEASING PEPTIDES" heading, which individually discusses tesamorelin, sermorelin, CJC-1295, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin and ipamorelin — and that section is internally split three ways: (1) its narrative conclusion states "GHRH and Ghrelin mimicking substances appear to act on the pituitary gland mediating growth hormone. As these substances cause GH release from the pituitary, they are captured under the hypothalamic releasing factors regulatory entry (in Schedule I of the Medicines Regulations)" — i.e. treats the whole class as already covered by an existing entry; (2) its own Appendix II substance-by-substance table nonetheless marks Tesamorelin, CJC-1295, GHRP-2, GHRP-6 and Ipamorelin individually as **"Not scheduled"** (only Sermorelin is marked "Prescription Medicine in New Zealand"), listing "Hypothalamic Releasing Factors" only as the candidate entry, not a confirmed one; and (3) the submission's final recommendations then hedge explicitly, proposing "scheduling growth hormone releasing peptides under the term 'Human Growth Hormone Secretagogues', if the committee considers these substances not to be included under the current scheduled term 'Hypothalamic Releasing Factors'" — i.e. Medsafe itself had not resolved the question in 2013 [Medsafe, Classification Status of Peptide-based PIEDs, 2013, "GROWTH HORMONE RELEASING PEPTIDES" section, Appendix II table, and Final Summary and Recommendations](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/consultations/MCC-Submission-PIEDs.doc). (A separate section of the same document, "GROWTH HORMONE VARIANTS", covers a different substance class — AOD9604 and hGH Frag 176-191 — and its "unknown mode of action… do not appear to be able to be classified" conclusion does not speak to CJC-1295/ipamorelin/tesamorelin at all; an earlier version of this finding mis-cited that section for this claim.) That is a 12-year-old document and cannot confirm 2026 status, but between its unresolved internal tension and the June 2025 submission's total silence on the class, the "GH secretagogues = already scheduled" claim rests on no primary source that actually confirms it — Sport Integrity NZ's anti-doping cases naming these substances are WADA Prohibited List violations, not Medicines Act prosecutions, so they don't establish Medicines Act classification either [Sport Integrity NZ, 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/rugby-player-gets-anti-doping-ban-for-importing-banned-substances). This should be treated as an open, and specifically *contested*, question rather than a settled fact, until Medsafe's Classification Database (a JavaScript/Blazor search tool I could not query programmatically) or a direct Medsafe enquiry confirms it either way.
- **The enforcement effect of classification is exactly as the framing described, and I found the primary text confirming both sides of it.** For an unscheduled peptide, Medsafe "does not have the grounds, pursuant to the Medicines Act 1981, to seize them" and instead releases the product to the importer with a high-risk-medicine letter [Medsafe submission, Jun 2025, p.3](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). Once a substance is classified as a prescription medicine, s43 of the Medicines Act 1981 makes it an offence, without reasonable excuse, to "import, procure, receive, store, use, or otherwise have in [one's] possession" it [Medicines Act 1981, s43](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1981/0118/latest/DLM55462.html) (fetched via r.jina.ai text proxy — legislation.govt.nz and nzlii.org both return an AWS WAF bot-challenge to direct and browser-based fetches; this is a tooling block, not a dead link — see Confidence & limits). Medsafe's submission states the intended effect explicitly: "In classifying these substances as prescription medicines, the importation of these products into New Zealand without a prescription will not be permitted, mitigating both the known and unknown clinical risks associated with them" [Medsafe submission, Jun 2025, p.12](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf).

## Evidence

### 1. The statutory mechanism: what "classification" actually changes

The Medicines Classification Committee (MCC) is a statutory advisory committee under s9 of the Medicines Act 1981, whose duty is "to make recommendations to the Minister in respect of the classification of any medicines as prescription medicines or restricted medicines or pharmacy-only medicines under this Act" [Medicines Act 1981, s9](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1981/0118/latest/DLM53809.html) (fetched via r.jina.ai after legislation.govt.nz returned an AWS WAF challenge page to direct curl and to the WebFetch tool — see Confidence & limits). Two other sections matter for peptides specifically:

- **Section 20** restricts the sale, distribution or advertising of a "new medicine" (one without the Minister's consent to distribute) — this is the "unapproved medicine" gate that applies regardless of MCC classification, and is why Medsafe describes any peptide product lacking NZ consent as an "unapproved medicine" [Medicines Act 1981, s20](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1981/0118/latest/DLM53819.html); [Medsafe, Use of Unapproved Medicines](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/riss/unapp.asp).
- **Section 43** restricts *possession* of a "prescription medicine" without reasonable excuse (e.g. a valid prescription) — this is the provision that only bites once a substance is actually classified as a prescription medicine, and its absence for unscheduled peptides is the precise mechanism behind Medsafe's "cannot seize" statement [Medicines Act 1981, s43](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1981/0118/latest/DLM55462.html).
- **Section 29** (the exemption letting a "specified practitioner" obtain or administer an otherwise-restricted medicine for a patient under their care) was substantively replaced on 19 November 2025 by the Medicines Amendment Act 2025 (2025 No 66) — the same Bill Medsafe told the 75th MCC meeting had passed its third reading on 11 November 2025 [Medicines Act 1981, s29 (as amended)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1981/0118/latest/DLM54175.html); [Medsafe, 75th MCC minutes, 19 Nov 2025](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/75mccMin19Nov2025.htm). This is a general Medicines Act update running in parallel to the peptide-classification question, not a peptide-specific change, but it is the only substantive Medicines Act 1981 amendment I found in the same window as the peptide deferral, and it also repealed s42C (a pharmacy-ownership restriction) — neither change touches peptide scheduling directly.

(Fetch note: `curl` to legislation.govt.nz returned an empty 202 response, and both the built-in WebFetch tool and `node scripts/fetch.mjs` returned an AWS WAF ("gokuProps"/challenge.js) bot-challenge page — `fetch.mjs` could not escalate past it because `agent-browser` and `cloakbrowser` are not installed in this environment (exit code 3, classified BLOCKED, not dead). ### 2. Medsafe's own June 2025 submission is the authoritative, current classification map

Medsafe's 16-page submission to the 74th MCC, "Classification of Unscheduled Peptides" (June 2025), is exhaustive: it groups every peptide Medsafe's Investigation & Enforcement Team had intercepted at the border between 1 April and 19 May 2025 (56 parcels) into ten proposed group entries plus six individually-named peptides, and states the *current* (pre-decision) classification status of each as part of its case for change [Medsafe submission, Jun 2025](https://medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen74/5.7Peptides.pdf). That document is the single most authoritative primary source for "what is scheduled today" precisely because it was written by the regulator to justify scheduling everything that is not — see the table above for the full breakdown, all in the Executive Answer with page-level citations.

### 3. The deferral, the Secretariat's cure, and the silence since

The 74th MCC's own discussion is recorded in unusual detail: the Committee agreed peptide classification "would help to reduce this risk of harm" and that patients with legitimate need could still access classified peptides via a prescription, but it could not resolve "whether any of the proposed peptides would have legitimate uses in other contexts such as in the cosmetics industry" and deferred pending more information [Medsafe, 74th MCC minutes, item 5.7](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/74mccMin23July2025.htm). The minutes were not published until 30 October 2025 — over three months after the meeting — and by publication carried a Secretariat's Note stating Medsafe had since consulted cosmetics-industry professional bodies, that "no issues were raised", and recommending the classification proceed [same source, item 5.7 Secretariat's Note].

I checked both subsequent public MCC records directly rather than relying on the framing document's July 2025 search window:

- **75th meeting (held by email, 19 November 2025; minutes published 23 December 2025):** the only "matters arising" item was "Objections to recommendations made at the 74th meeting", and it names exactly two: Lithium (item 5.4) and Vitamin D (item 5.5) — not peptides [Medsafe, 75th MCC minutes](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Minutes/2021-2025/75mccMin19Nov2025.htm). This is expected regardless of Medsafe's cure: a *deferred* item is not a *recommendation*, so it is not eligible for the objection process that surfaced Lithium and Vitamin D. But it also means peptides were not brought back as a fresh submission at the very next meeting after the Secretariat's Note was published.
- **76th meeting (scheduled 22 April 2026; agenda published 10 March 2026):** item 6 states "Medsafe notes that no public submissions were received for this meeting, or the 75th MCC meeting held in November 2025" [Medsafe, 76th MCC agenda](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/Agendas/Agen76/agen76.htm). I read the agenda's full text; the only appearance of the word "peptide" is in an unrelated item (7.3, a proposed group entry for "amylin analogues", a different hormone class entirely). **No peptide item appears.**
- **Minutes for the 76th meeting are not yet published.** Medsafe's minutes index (`minutes.asp`) still lists only "2021 – (66th to 75th meeting)" as its most recent range; there is no 2026 folder yet, even though the meeting itself occurred roughly ten weeks before this research was conducted [Medsafe, MCC Minutes index](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/minutes.asp). Based on the 74th→75th publication gap (about five weeks) and the 73rd→74th gap (about three months), this delay is within Medsafe's normal range and not itself unusual — but it means I cannot confirm what, if anything, was discussed about peptides at the 76th meeting itself, only that it was not a scheduled agenda item going in.
- **No Gazette notice for a peptide group entry exists on Medsafe's own record of Gazette notices relating to classification**, which lists every notice back to 2019 by date; the most recent is 29 August 2025, for an unrelated batch of new prescription medicines (abrocitinib, allopurinol, GLP-1 receptor agonists, etc.) [Medsafe, Recent Gazette Notices](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/recent.asp). That page itself is marked "Revised: 4 September 2025" and has evidently not been updated since, which is a second independent signal (alongside the MCC minutes/agendas) that no classification changes of any kind — peptide or otherwise — have been gazetted since then, as far as Medsafe's own published record shows.

**Net effect:** as of this research, the June 2025 group-classification proposal is neither enacted, gazetted, nor formally back before the Committee. It sits in an administrative gap — Medsafe's own recommendation to proceed has been on the public record since October 2025, but no MCC meeting since has scheduled a vote on it.

## What would change this conclusion

- **Publication of 76th MCC meeting minutes** (not yet available) confirming whether peptides were discussed on 22 April 2026, or a subsequent MCC agenda re-listing "Peptide Groups" as a submission item, would directly update the "still deferred" conclusion — this is the single most important thing to check next, and should be a quick recheck of `https://www.medsafe.govt.nz/profs/class/minutes.asp` for a new 2026 entry.
- **A Gazette notice** adding any of the ten proposed group entries (or the six individual peptides) to Schedule 1 of the Medicines Regulations 1984 would mean the reform has been enacted — checkable at Medsafe's Recent Gazette Notices page or the NZ Gazette directly.
- **Confirmation from Medsafe's Classification Database** (a Blazor/JavaScript search tool at `/DbSearch/InfoSearch` that I could not query programmatically — it requires a session-scoped anti-forgery token and appears to render results client-side) would resolve the one genuine open question in this finding: the current classification status of growth-hormone secretagogues (CJC-1295, ipamorelin, tesamorelin, GHRP-2/6, hexarelin). A direct Medsafe enquiry or a person with browser access to that tool could close this in minutes.
- **A dated Gazette notice or MCC minute specifically for thymosin beta-4/TB-500** would upgrade that claim from Medium to High confidence; currently it rests solely on Medsafe's own June 2025 assertion of current status.
