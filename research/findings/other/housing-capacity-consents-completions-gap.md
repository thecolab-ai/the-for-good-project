# NZ's measurable housing gap is between enabled capacity, consented homes, and net dwelling-stock additions, not a single national delivery target

## Executive answer

- The current central-government baseline is a planning-capacity target, not a national "homes completed" target: Going for Housing Growth says Tier 1 and 2 councils must enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity in district plans using high population-growth projections, while the NPS-UD HBA framework requires councils to quantify plan-enabled, infrastructure-ready, feasible, and reasonably expected-to-be-realised capacity against demand plus competitiveness margins [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [MfE, NPS-UD 2020 updated May 2022](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/National-Policy-Statement-Urban-Development-2020-11May2022-v2.pdf).
- The latest national consent baseline is 39,737 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026, equal to 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents; the same Stats NZ downloadable CSV gives Auckland Region 16,862 and Canterbury Region 8,468 new dwellings consented in that year [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).
- The best official public completion proxy is Stats NZ's private-dwelling estimate, not the consent series itself: Stats NZ estimates private dwelling stock from census bases plus lagged building consents, currently using a 12-month lag and a 0.89 weighting factor for every 100 new homes consented at national level [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).
- On that proxy, the year-ended May 2026 consent pipeline of 39,737 homes would translate to about 35,400 net private-dwelling additions after the lag if the current national 0.89 weighting factor applied unchanged; that derived conversion gap is about 4,400 fewer net dwellings than consented homes, before any local mismatch in location, type, infrastructure, or affordability is considered [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).
- Existing council HBAs show why capacity, consents, and completions must stay separate: Auckland's 2023 HBA estimated about 200,000 additional dwellings of demand over 30 years and found more than sufficient reasonably expected-to-be-realised capacity in aggregate, while Wellington's regional HBA page says over 99,000 houses will be required by 2053 and over 206,000 homes are enabled under current district plans [Auckland Council, Auckland HBA 2023](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/); [Wellington Regional Leadership Committee, Regional HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high for the Stats NZ consent count, dwelling-stock method, and current central-government target design; medium for the cross-metric "gap" because capacity, consents, and stock additions are different measures with different timing and geography.

## Evidence

### The current target is capacity, not delivered homes

HUD's current Going for Housing Growth material says new Housing Growth Targets require Tier 1 and 2 councils to enable 30 years of feasible housing capacity in their district plans using high population-growth projections [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme). The same HUD page says Tier 1 and 2 councils will need to zone for at least 30 years of housing demand, rather than a minimum of three years [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme).

The statutory HBA baseline is more granular than a single capacity number: NPS-UD clause 3.25 requires HBAs to quantify housing development capacity for short, medium, and long terms that is plan-enabled, plan-enabled and infrastructure-ready, and plan-enabled, infrastructure-ready, feasible, and reasonably expected to be realised [MfE, NPS-UD 2020 updated May 2022](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/National-Policy-Statement-Urban-Development-2020-11May2022-v2.pdf). NPS-UD clause 3.27 says sufficiency is assessed by comparing housing demand plus the competitiveness margin with the capacity identified under clause 3.25 [MfE, NPS-UD 2020 updated May 2022](https://environment.govt.nz/assets/publications/National-Policy-Statement-Urban-Development-2020-11May2022-v2.pdf).

This means the measurable national baseline is not "target homes minus homes built"; it is a chain from council-enabled capacity, to consents, to net dwelling-stock additions, with council HBAs providing the capacity side and Stats NZ providing the consent and stock-proxy side [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).

### Existing HBA capacity examples are not yet a current national HGT compliance audit

Auckland Council's 2023 HBA says expected demand under its March 2023 medium projection is approximately 200,000 additional dwellings over 30 years to 2052, with high and low scenarios about 100,000 dwellings either side of the medium scenario [Auckland Council, Auckland HBA 2023](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/). The same HBA says Auckland has more than sufficient reasonably expected-to-be-realised housing capacity in aggregate to meet forecast demand plus the competitiveness margin, while warning that the aggregate result masks short-term feasibility and infrastructure challenges [Auckland Council, Auckland HBA 2023](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/publications/housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment-for-the-auckland-region-national-policy-statement-on-urban-development-2020/).

The Wellington Regional Leadership Committee's 2023 HBA page says the Wairarapa-Wellington-Horowhenua region will require over 99,000 houses by 2053 and that current district plans enable over 206,000 homes [Wellington Regional Leadership Committee, Regional HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/). The WRLC page is evidence of an enabled-capacity surplus, but it is not by itself a current Housing Growth Target compliance finding because HUD's new target uses high growth projections and feasible capacity, while the public page reports enabled homes at a regional summary level [HUD, Going for Housing Growth](https://www.hud.govt.nz/our-work/going-for-housing-growth-programme); [Wellington Regional Leadership Committee, Regional HBA](https://wrlc.org.nz/our-projects/regional-housing-and-business-development-capacity-assessment/).

### Consent baseline

Stats NZ reported 39,737 new homes consented nationally in the year ended May 2026, up 19 percent from the year ended May 2025 [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/). Stats NZ also reported 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026, compared with 6.3 per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2025 [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).

I verified the national year-ended May 2026 consent count by summing the `BLDM.SFTZ1100A1A` "New Zealand / Dwelling units / New / Number / Actual" monthly series in Stats NZ's May 2026 downloadable CSV for June 2025 through May 2026, which totals 39,737 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). The same CSV calculation gives 37,813 new dwellings consented in the year ended March 2026 and 34,062 in the year ended March 2025 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

The same Stats NZ CSV gives Auckland Region 16,862, Canterbury Region 8,468, Waikato Region 3,122, and Otago Region 2,821 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). The per-capita regional series in the same CSV gives Canterbury 12.1, Otago 11.1, Auckland 9.3, and New Zealand 7.4 new dwellings consented per 1,000 residents in the year ended May 2026 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip).

### Completion or dwelling-stock proxy

Stats NZ's March 2026 dwelling and household release says the estimated number of private dwellings was 2,117,500 at 31 March 2026 and the estimated number of households was 2,064,700 [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/). The same release says the revised estimated number of private dwellings was 2,087,300 at 31 March 2025 and 2,110,300 at 31 December 2025, so the private-dwelling stock increased by 30,200 over the year to 31 March 2026 and by 7,200 over the March 2026 quarter [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).

Stats NZ explains that it updates private dwelling estimates from each census base using building consents, with a current 12-month lag to allow for completion and a weighting factor to allow for consents that are not fulfilled and dwellings that are demolished or destroyed [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/). The March 2026 release says the revised weighting factor is 0.89, which implies that for every 100 new homes consented nationally, a net 89 private dwellings will be completed 12 months after consent [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).

Using Stats NZ's current 0.89 factor as a simple national conversion proxy, the 39,737 new dwellings consented in the year ended May 2026 imply about 35,366 net private-dwelling additions after the 12-month lag; this is my calculation and not a Stats NZ forecast [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/); [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/). Using the same proxy on the 34,062 new dwellings consented in the year ended March 2025 gives about 30,315 net dwelling additions after the lag, which is close to the observed 30,200 increase in estimated private dwellings over the year to 31 March 2026 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [Stats NZ, Dwelling and household estimates: March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/dwelling-and-household-estimates-march-2026-quarter/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current official national target for completed homes or consented homes would change the baseline from a capacity-consent-stock chain to a direct target-versus-delivery calculation.
- A current public dataset of actual dwelling completions by territorial authority, dwelling type, and completion date would improve or replace the Stats NZ dwelling-stock proxy.
- Updated council HBAs or Housing Growth Target compliance reports using high population-growth projections could change the capacity side of the baseline, especially where older HBAs used medium projections.
- I could not verify a single current national roll-up of Tier 1 and 2 council Housing Growth Target compliance, so this finding uses HUD's rule design and selected HBA examples rather than claiming a national capacity surplus or deficit.
- I did not assess whether new dwellings are affordable, well-located, accessible, or matched to high-need households; that is outside this baseline issue and needs separate research.
