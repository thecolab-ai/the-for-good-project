# An NZ small-credit help front door needs scenario comparison, provenance, and consented handoff to MoneyTalks-style financial mentoring

## Executive answer

- A credible NZ small-credit front door should show scenario-based total-cost outputs, not ranked product recommendations, because NZ consumer-credit disclosures cover interest, fees, dispute-resolution details, cancellation rights, hardship rights, and continuing disclosure, and Consumer Protection tells borrowers to check total repayment including admin fees, debt collection fees, and interest before signing. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Consumer Protection loans and lenders, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/loans-and-lenders)
- Minimum viable data fields are: product type, provider legal/trading identity, FSPR or certification status where applicable, amount/term/repayment frequency constraints, advertised rate range, all credit/default/service fees, missed-payment treatment, eligibility caveats, security/guarantor requirements, hardship/dispute-resolution routes, source URL/document date, scrape/check date, and confidence in comparability. [Consumer Protection, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Commerce Commission register, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/borrowing-money-and-buying-on-credit/register-of-certified-lenders-and-mobile-traders/); [FSPR DRS help, accessed 2 July 2026](https://fsp-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/dispute-resolution-schemes/choosing-a-scheme/)
- The front door should use public lender/regulator sources for public education, but any reliable automated provider register or bulk identity matching will probably need Companies Office API/data-service access and explicit licence/terms review, because Companies Office says its APIs are designed for automated retrieval and customers who regularly use register data. [Companies Office APIs, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/using-our-data-through-apis/)
- Human help should be presented as a first-class outcome: MSD says MoneyTalks is funded through Building Financial Capability, provides free advice through trained financial mentors, connects people with local BFC providers, and acts as a front door and triage to BFC services; FinCap's MoneyTalks page separately says MoneyTalks connects people with financial mentors and services to help with financial hardship. [MSD MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html); [FinCap MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks)
- A warm handoff should not be shipped until an NFP partner has co-designed it and a privacy review has approved the consent flow, because NZ organisations that collect personal information must follow the Privacy Act, and DPUP guidance says consent establishes what a person is agreeing to when personal information is needed to provide a service. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.privacy.org.nz/responsibilities/your-obligations/); [Digital.govt.nz DPUP consent guidance, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/privacy-security-and-risk/privacy/data-protection-and-use-policy-dpup/use-dpup-in-your-work/resources-for-working-with-dpup/how-to-approach-consent)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the minimum data, disclosure, privacy, and MoneyTalks pathway requirements are well supported by official/public sources, but the exact referral operating model, capacity, product set, and legal advice boundary require human partner validation.

## Evidence

### What the front door needs to show

The first screen should ask for a small number of scenario inputs: purpose category, amount needed, when the money is needed, expected repayment timeframe, whether the user already has a credit card or overdraft, whether the purchase is merchant-linked, and whether the user is currently behind on payments; these inputs map to product differences that NZ sources treat separately, including loans, agreed overdrafts, BNPL accounts, credit cards, and hardship/payment-problem pathways. [Consumer Protection borrowing duties, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do); [Consumer Protection BNPL, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/ways-to-buy-and-pay/buy-now-pay-later); [Commerce Commission consumer credit contracts, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/consumer-credit-contracts/)

The output should separate "publicly comparable cost" from "not comparable or eligibility-dependent" because Consumer Protection says lenders must disclose standard terms and borrowing costs publicly to help borrowers compare, while Commerce Commission affordability guidance says lenders still need reasonable inquiries to be satisfied that payments are likely affordable without substantial hardship. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Commerce Commission affordability, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/assessing-for-affordability/)

Minimum scenario output should include principal, term, repayment frequency, total repayment if paid on time, total interest and fees if paid on time, a missed-payment/default-fee warning where the source discloses it, a "not a quote" caveat, and an "eligibility may change this" caveat; the need to include total repayment, interest, fees, default penalties, and contract changes follows from Consumer Protection's CCCFA and loans guidance. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Consumer Protection loans and lenders, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/loans-and-lenders)

The front door should include debt-stress exits before product comparison, because Consumer Protection directs people with BNPL payment problems to talk to the provider, notes hardship application rights, and says a free financial mentor can help prepare or talk to the lender for them. BNPL also needs current regulatory treatment, not old "unregulated instalment" assumptions: Commerce Commission says BNPL lenders must comply with CCCFA lender obligations from 2 September 2024 unless an exemption applies. [Consumer Protection BNPL, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/ways-to-buy-and-pay/buy-now-pay-later); [Commerce Commission BNPL, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/buy-now-pay-later/)

The tool should avoid a "best product for you" answer unless a qualified financial-advice pathway is deliberately created, because FMA says anyone providing advice to retail clients must hold or operate under a Financial Advice Provider licence, and the Financial Services Council's legal guide says regulated financial advice to retail clients must be given by, or under, an FMA-licensed FAP. [FMA Financial Advice Provider, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/financial-advice-provider/); [Financial Services Council financial advice law guide, accessed 2 July 2026](https://blog.fsc.org.nz/hubfs/FSC%20Corporate/Documents/Website%20Docs/FSC%20Financial%20Advice%20Law%20Guide.pdf)

Existing NZ comparison tools reduce duplication risk but do not remove the need for a support-first front door. Money Compare lists personal-loan filters, rates, amount ranges, terms, payment frequency, and establishment fees across dozens of plans; MoneyHub, Canstar, Finance.co.nz, and Glimp publish personal-loan comparisons, lender summaries, or rate-comparison tables. These are useful market context, but they are comparison surfaces rather than MoneyTalks-style triage, warm handoff, consent, and provenance governance models. [Money Compare personal loans, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.moneycompare.co.nz/compare/borrowings.personal-loans); [MoneyHub personal loans, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.moneyhub.co.nz/personal-loans.html); [Canstar personal loans, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.canstar.co.nz/personal-loans/); [Finance.co.nz personal loans, accessed 2 July 2026](https://finance.co.nz/personal-loans/); [Glimp personal loans, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.glimp.co.nz/personal-loans)

### Public and licensed data requirements

Public regulator/consumer-protection sources are sufficient for general education pages covering disclosure duties, high-cost-credit constraints, hardship applications, payment problems, and complaint routes. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Consumer Protection payment problems, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/payment-problems); [Consumer Protection complaints page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/managing-money/how-to-complain-about-your-financial-service-provider)

Current regulator context needs an explicit date stamp: FMA says regulatory responsibility for the CCCFA transferred from the Commerce Commission to FMA on 1 July 2026, so a live product should treat older Commerce Commission guidance as useful source material while checking current FMA/MBIE/Consumer Protection pages for governance changes. [FMA CCCFA, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/legislation/cccfa/)

Lender-owned product pages and disclosure statements are necessary for current rates, fees, amount ranges, term ranges, and eligibility caveats, because Consumer Protection says lenders must make standard terms and borrowing costs publicly available through their website or clearly displayed notices. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

The Commerce Commission certified-lender register is a public validation source for lenders and mobile traders that require certification, but it should not be treated as a complete product catalogue because the register page describes certification status rather than product-level rates, fees, and criteria. [Commerce Commission certified lenders register, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/borrowing-money-and-buying-on-credit/register-of-certified-lenders-and-mobile-traders/)

The FSPR should be used to verify provider identity and dispute-resolution information where applicable, because FSPR guidance says providers of financial services to retail clients must generally belong to an approved dispute-resolution scheme and record membership details on the register. [FSPR DRS help, accessed 2 July 2026](https://fsp-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/dispute-resolution-schemes/choosing-a-scheme/)

Bulk or automated FSPR/NZBN/Companies Office matching should go through the official API or data-request route rather than scraping as an assumed right, because Companies Office says APIs are designed for automated retrieval and are used by customers who regularly use register data or develop products using that data. [Companies Office APIs, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/using-our-data-through-apis/)

### Provenance and update requirements

Each numeric value should store a source URL, source owner, observed date, and extraction method, because a comparison cannot be defended if a lender changes rates or fees after the tool records them; this is an engineering requirement inferred from the changeable nature of lender disclosures, and it needs validation against actual lender update patterns. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

The statutory requirement is change-based rather than calendar-based: lender disclosures need to stay clear and not misleading, and Consumer Protection describes continuing-disclosure duties for changes such as interest rates, credit fees, repayment changes, and some hardship/guarantor information. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

Each product record should separately have an internal freshness class: regulator/static guidance checked quarterly, lender rates and fees checked at least monthly before publication, and high-risk/default-fee fields checked before each campaign or public release. This is an evidence-informed product-governance recommendation, not a statutory interval; I found no official NZ rule prescribing a monthly update frequency for a third-party comparison tool.

Every scenario output should display its last checked date and should degrade to "needs recheck" when a source is stale, because Consumer Protection says lender disclosures must be clear, likely to be noticed, and not misleading or deceptive about important things. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

Named provider inclusion should require two checks: the product page/disclosure source supports the cost fields, and a register/dispute-resolution source supports the provider identity where applicable; this is a governance recommendation based on the separate roles of lender disclosures, the Commerce Commission certified-lender register, and the FSPR DRS record. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Commerce Commission certified lenders register, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/borrowing-money-and-buying-on-credit/register-of-certified-lenders-and-mobile-traders/); [FSPR DRS help, accessed 2 July 2026](https://fsp-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/dispute-resolution-schemes/choosing-a-scheme/)

### Referral and warm-handoff guardrails

MoneyTalks is the only referral destination I would name at minimum viable stage without further consent work, because MSD says MoneyTalks is funded through BFC, provides free advice, uses trained financial mentors, connects people to local BFC providers, and acts as the front door and triage to BFC services. [MSD MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html)

FinCap can be named as the contracted MoneyTalks provider and as public context for the financial mentoring sector, because MSD says FinCap is contracted to provide MoneyTalks and FinCap says MoneyTalks connects people with financial mentors and services to help with financial hardship. [MSD MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html); [FinCap MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks)

Local financial mentoring services should not be listed as operational referral partners until the service owner, MoneyTalks/FinCap, or a human steward confirms the appropriate public listing and referral terms, because this research did not contact any organisation and the issue instruction forbids listing an organisation as a referral partner unless public material or later consent supports it. [Issue #69, accessed 2 July 2026](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/69)

The safest first release is a "self-directed referral" button that opens MoneyTalks contact channels and clearly says no personal data has been sent; MSD publishes phone, text, email, online chat, and availability details for MoneyTalks. [MSD MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html)

A true warm handoff should require explicit consent, minimal data transfer, a copy-to-user option, and a partner-side intake agreement, because NZ organisations that collect personal information must comply with the Privacy Act and DPUP says consent establishes expectations about what a person agrees to when personal information is needed for service delivery. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.privacy.org.nz/responsibilities/your-obligations/); [Digital.govt.nz DPUP consent guidance, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/privacy-security-and-risk/privacy/data-protection-and-use-policy-dpup/use-dpup-in-your-work/resources-for-working-with-dpup/how-to-approach-consent)

Warm-handoff data should be limited to the user's preferred name, contact method, best contact time, broad issue category, whether contact is urgent, and optional free text; it should not include lender account numbers, bank statements, IDs, or detailed debt lists unless a partner explicitly asks for them through an approved intake process, because the Privacy Act covers any information that can identify a person and agencies must handle personal information under the Act. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.privacy.org.nz/responsibilities/your-obligations/)

### Partner validation needs

Before ideation, a human steward should validate with MoneyTalks/FinCap whether the front door should direct everyone to MoneyTalks first, whether a postcode-based local finder is appropriate, what language avoids shame or urgency traps, what hours/capacity constraints matter, and what information mentors actually want before first contact; MSD describes MoneyTalks as the BFC front door, but public pages do not define an API, intake payload, service-level agreement, or co-branding permission. [MSD MoneyTalks, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html)

Before naming any lender product in a public release, a human or reviewer should check whether the source terms permit comparison, whether the value is current, whether the comparison risks implying advice, and whether the provider identity is verified against register sources where applicable. [Consumer Protection CCCFA page, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act); [Companies Office APIs, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/data-services/ways-to-get-our-data/using-our-data-through-apis/)

Before launching personalised ranking, a qualified NZ financial-services lawyer or FMA-facing adviser should review the advice boundary, because FMA says anyone providing advice to retail clients must hold or operate under a FAP licence. [FMA Financial Advice Provider, accessed 2 July 2026](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/financial-advice-provider/)

## What would change this conclusion

- A written response from MoneyTalks/FinCap or MSD saying third-party warm referrals are unwanted, capacity-constrained, or need a different path would change the referral design; I did not contact MoneyTalks, FinCap, MSD, or local providers.
- Legal advice or FMA guidance saying the proposed scenario outputs still amount to regulated financial advice would require a licensed advice model or a narrower education-only tool; I did not obtain legal advice.
- Lender or Companies Office terms that restrict automated reuse of register/product data would change the data architecture; I verified public API/data-service pages but did not obtain API access or licence terms.
- Evidence from financial mentors or borrowers that comparison-before-support causes harm would move the human-help route ahead of any cost output; I found public support for financial mentoring but no lived-experience testing for this specific front door.
- I could not verify an official MoneyTalks API, formal referral SLA, current intake payload, local-service capacity, or permission to co-brand a handoff.
- I could not verify a complete public, machine-readable dataset of current NZ small-credit product rates, fees, and eligibility criteria; existing comparison pages show some rates and fees, but lender-owned pages remain necessary unless a licensed dataset is obtained.
- I could not verify the Commerce Commission disclosure-guidelines PDF or lender-responsibility-principles page with `agent-browser`; on 2 July 2026 both returned 404 through that browser fetcher after plain HTTP fetches had returned 403 in review. Both sources were removed from load-bearing support and replaced with accessible Consumer Protection, Commerce Commission affordability/BNPL/register pages, FinCap, FMA, FSC, and comparison-tool sources.
