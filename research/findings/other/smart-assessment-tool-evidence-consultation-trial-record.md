# What public evidence, consultation, trial and assurance record supports SMART and the new reporting framework?

## Executive answer

- **There is a real assurance and procurement paper trail for SMART — something National Standards never had.** A Treasury OIA release covering January–February 2025 shows a completed RFI (16 supplier responses to 19 approached), a Single-Stage Business Case, an Investment Management "Gateway" review, and an explicit options analysis (Do Nothing vs. three off-the-shelf tiers vs. a bespoke build). [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) National Standards had none of this: it was introduced without a trial, a business case, or costed options analysis, per NZCER's contemporaneous account. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf)
- **But that same paper trail shows Treasury actively pushing back, not endorsing.** Treasury's out-of-cycle funding advice to the Minister of Finance says verbatim: *"Treasury recommends the Minister of Finance does not approve this out-of-cycle funding request... we are concerned that key building blocks are unlikely to be in place by the time a pre-commitment is considered by Cabinet... Some of these concerns are reflected in the amber/red Gateway review rating for the overall programme."* [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) The subsequent Budget assessment scored the initiative Alignment "Excellent" (4/4) and Value "Good" (3/4), but Delivery only "Partial" (2/4) — Treasury explicitly disagreed the tool could be delivered "within the proposed timeframe." [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- **The Government's central public evidence claim — trialling in 85 schools involving ~12,000 student assessment engagements — is, as far as could be verified, single-sourced.** It appears identically worded across the Beehive release, NZ Herald, Stuff, 1News and Ministry material, all of which trace to the same Government announcement; no independently published trial report, dataset, or third-party evaluation of that trial was located. [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) This is flagged explicitly below as a claim needing a second, independent source that this research could not find.
- **On the specific National Standards-era concerns — moderation, workload, labelling, curriculum narrowing, Māori-medium/aromatawai, and data misuse — the record shows the risks were named in advance but evidence so far shows them recurring in practice, not resolved.** Treasury said in January 2025 the business case was "silent" on Māori-medium data sovereignty; NZEI documented in June 2026 an actual inconsistency in Ministry communications about where SMART data is stored. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf); [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool) PPTA's May 2026 survey of deputy principals and NZEI's reporting both describe AI-marking quality problems (identical marks for writing of visibly different quality) and added workload — the same category of moderation and burden complaint that dogged National Standards. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool)
- **No independent (ERO, NZAI, or academic) evaluation of SMART itself — as opposed to general pre-2026 calls for "better assessment" — was located.** ERO and NZAI are cited by the Government only as having called generally for improved assessment practice before SMART existed, not as evaluators of SMART's validity, reliability, or trial performance. [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning)

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The Treasury/procurement paper trail and its contents are High confidence (primary source, directly quoted). Whether the 85-school trial happened as described is Low confidence (single-sourced, unverified). Whether the National Standards-era concerns have been "answered" is Medium-Low: the early 2026 evidence is consistent, multi-sourced, but still only ~5 months into rollout and not from a formal evaluation.

## Evidence

### The Treasury/procurement record that exists

A Treasury OIA response (reference 20250889, released 10 February 2026, covering documents dated 24 January–3 February 2025) contains the actual out-of-cycle funding request, ministerial consultation briefing, and Treasury's Individual Spending Initiative Assessment for the "Standardised Assessment & Aromatawai Tool for Years 3–10." [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) It documents:

- A procurement process reference (MOE29225) with a real timeline: RFI released 27 November 2024 (16 of 19 approached suppliers responded), RFP released 3 February 2025, evaluation by 4 April 2025, and contract signing targeted for 9 May 2025. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- Four costed options plus "do nothing," with whole-of-life costs ranging from $174.707m (Option B1, the preferred off-the-shelf foundational twice-yearly tool) to $374.213m for a bespoke build. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- Total funding sought: $75.808m operating and $9.788m capital over the forecast period (later documents round this to ~$85.6m), against a 10-year total cost of $176.952m. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- Treasury's own Value-for-Money assessment rated the initiative Alignment "Excellent," Value "Good," and Delivery "Partial" — with Treasury stating explicitly: *"We do not agree that the initiative can be implemented within the proposed timeframe... The agency does not have the capability and capacity to deliver this investment to the proposed timeframe."* [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- A specific, quantified delivery risk: *"19,200 new English language assessment items would need to be created from scratch... There is a high risk of delivery of the te reo Māori assessment items, due to the limited capacity of assessment item developers."* [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)
- Baseline usage data on the tool being replaced, used by Treasury to question the "no one is using existing tools" framing: *"57% of English medium schools use the existing e-asTTle for at least one student... In Māori medium schools, 13% of Kaupapa Māori and 13% of Kura Kaupapa Māori use e-asTTle for te reo Matatini me te Pāngarau."* [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

This is a genuine assurance record, and it is more rigorous than the historical record of National Standards' introduction, which NZCER and Thrupp describe as lacking a trial, moving on an "unrealistic" timeframe, and being driven by political commitment rather than an evidenced business case. [NZCER, 2013](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/NZARE%20National%20Standards%20conference%20paper.pdf); [Thrupp, 2017](https://ojs.victoria.ac.nz/nzaroe/article/download/4142/3661/5380) The important nuance is that the record documents Treasury *resisting* the timeline and Cabinet pre-commitment, not endorsing the evidence base as sufficient — Treasury's preferred recommendation was to approve the Business Case but withhold the Budget pre-commitment, precisely so more assurance work could happen first. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### The consultation and trial claim, and its sourcing

The Government's public case for evidence-based design rests heavily on one sentence, repeated near-verbatim across outlets: the new approach "has been developed following consultation with principals' associations and teachers, and trialling in 85 schools involving around 12,000 student assessment engagements," with "feedback from participating schools" reported as "positive." [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) This exact figure and wording recurs in NZ Herald, Stuff, and 1News coverage of the same announcement, and in Ministry/Tāhūrangi material — but in every case it traces back to the same 2 February 2026 Government announcement rather than to an independently published trial report, dataset, or evaluation. No trial report, survey instrument, or raw results from the 85 schools could be located in this research. This is treated below as a single-sourced, unverified claim.

Separately, a later Beehive release states SMART had been used by 220,000 students across more than 1,300 schools since its February 2026 introduction, with completion rates exceeding 70%, and that the first parent reports were released 3 July 2026 — but again presents adoption figures and ministerial framing, not independent evaluation results. [Beehive, "220,000 students take the SMART test", 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/220000-students-take-smart-test) The Ministry's own quoted response to principal criticism (below) confirms SMART was "not compulsory this year." [RNZ, 26 June 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/625493/principals-call-out-new-testing-as-unfit-for-purpose)

### Whether National Standards-era concerns have been answered

**Moderation and item quality.** No published moderation framework, inter-rater reliability study, or independent item-quality review of SMART was located. In its place, direct sector evidence from actual 2026 use points the other way: PPTA's survey of Year 7–10 deputy principals found teachers reporting that *"AI in the SMART writing tool seemed to mark students higher based on how much they wrote, as opposed to the quality,"* with one educator finding *"several students received the same mark"* despite "significant differences" in quality. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) NZEI separately reported the same failure mode: the tool *"failed to evaluate the quality of writing, instead giving identical marks to completely different student scripts based merely on word count."* [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool) Two independent sector bodies reporting the same specific defect is stronger evidence than either alone, though both are advocacy organisations reporting member feedback, not a scientific psychometric study.

**Workload.** PPTA's survey found the tool "demands substantial additional resourcing, including extra teaching sessions, administrative time, professional learning, and staff backfilling," and identified a concrete design/context mismatch: the Australian-built tool assumes 90-minute assessment blocks, while "most schools in Aotearoa worked to timetables of 60-minute classes." [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) PPTA president Chris Abercrombie is quoted describing the change management process as "woeful" and the tool as "certainly not ready for implementation this year." [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) This echoes, rather than resolves, the workload concerns NZCER documented for National Standards (where only 31% of principals agreed National Standards drove what they did in school, alongside reports of softened/altered reporting language to manage the burden). [NZCER, 2016](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/sites/default/files/downloads/National%20Standards%20in%20their%20seventh%20year.pdf)

**Labelling and curriculum narrowing.** Direct evidence of labelling or narrowing effects from SMART specifically was not found (the rollout is too recent for this to show up in outcome data), but PPTA's survey raises an adjacent equity concern: teachers described SMART as a *"'one size fits all' system that does not cater for students with learning needs,"* and questioned the cultural relevance of set texts (e.g., Dickens, Shakespeare) for "junior students in 21st century Aotearoa." [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) Separately, a New Zealand Initiative opinion piece supporting SMART argues that "continued underperformance" identified by such tools should eventually "trigger a compulsory change in school leadership" — precisely the accountability/high-stakes use that OECD's literature warns turns formative assessment into a curriculum-narrowing, teach-to-the-test instrument. [New Zealand Initiative, 2026](https://www.nzinitiative.org.nz/reports-and-media/opinion/smart-educational-assessment/); [OECD, 2011](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/10/student-standardised-testing_g17a2023/5kg3rp9qbnr6-en.pdf) That the argument for high-stakes use is already being made publicly, five months into a tool that is not even compulsory yet, is a leading indicator worth flagging rather than a resolved question.

**Māori-medium / aromatawai.** Treasury's January 2025 assessment states plainly: *"The benefit of funding in the Māori medium context are unclear... A standardised assessment tool does not fit neatly into an aromatawai principle of ākonga being active participants in the process of ako... The business case is currently silent on [data sovereignty]... Providers will want to understand who 'owns' the data and be consulted on how data about their ākonga will be used."* [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) Treasury also explicitly flagged that "there is insufficient time allocated for stakeholder engagement," calling out Māori-medium providers by name as a group Treasury "expects... to be concerned over data sovereignty." [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf) That prediction appears to have materialised: by June 2026, NZEI documented a live discrepancy in Ministry-issued communications about SMART data storage, discussed next. This research did not find any subsequent published business case addendum or intervention-logic document showing the gap Treasury identified was actually closed.

**Data misuse / data sovereignty.** NZEI's 17 June 2026 release documents an inconsistency across different Ministry-produced information sheets: the school leaders' version states *"all SMART data will be stored securely in Aotearoa, with backups for disaster resilience and recovery stored in Australia,"* while the parents' version states only that information is *"stored in New Zealand,"* and the teachers' version does not mention storage location at all. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool) NZEI Principals' Council Chair Stephanie Madden is quoted: *"Telling parents one thing while informing school leaders that data is actually heading offshore is misleading."* [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool) This is currently a single-source claim (NZEI only); no independent confirmation of the exact wording discrepancy across the three documents was found, though it is directionally consistent with the Treasury OIA's account of the tool being supplied via an offshore ICT services arrangement with Australian backups. [Treasury OIA 20250889, 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-02/oia-20250889.pdf)

### What official evidence base is cited, beyond the trial claim

The Government cites the Education Review Office (ERO) and New Zealand Assessment Institute (NZAI) as having "for years... called for improved assessment practices and higher-quality reporting to parents," and quotes NZAI's general position that "assessment information is essential at all tiers of the education system." [Beehive, 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning) Both of these are pre-existing, general calls for better assessment practice in New Zealand schools — they are not evaluations of SMART's design, validity, or trial performance, and neither ERO nor NZAI is cited as having reviewed SMART itself. No ERO report specifically evaluating SMART, and no NZAI technical assessment of SMART's psychometrics, was located.

### Direct evidence from schools using the tool (2026)

Beyond the union surveys, individual principals interviewed by RNZ in late June 2026 raise substantive, specific criticisms: Gareth Sinton (Douglas Park School, Masterton) said SMART "could not yet" measure achievement against curriculum expectations because "all you get is a series of right or wrong answers," and that the tool was not "ready to use in the way that it's perhaps been sold to us." [RNZ, 26 June 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/625493/principals-call-out-new-testing-as-unfit-for-purpose) Lynda Knight (Glenview Primary School, Porirua) argued the ~$85m programme (including ~$24m to acquire the Australian-owned tool) was "unnecessary" given the existing NZ-developed PAT tool "provided detailed information about children's strengths and weaknesses." [RNZ, 26 June 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/625493/principals-call-out-new-testing-as-unfit-for-purpose) The Ministry's response to this criticism, as reported by RNZ, was that SMART is "not compulsory this year" and operates "alongside teacher judgement," with "ongoing quality assurance processes for assessment questions" — an assertion of an assurance process, but again without a published document describing what that process consists of or has found. [RNZ, 26 June 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/education/625493/principals-call-out-new-testing-as-unfit-for-purpose)

## What would change this conclusion

- Publication of the actual 85-school trial report, survey instrument, or raw results (rather than a summary press-release figure) would either substantiate or undercut the Government's headline evidence claim — this is the single most important missing document identified in this research.
- An ERO or NZAI technical evaluation of SMART's validity, reliability, and moderation arrangements (as opposed to their general pre-2026 calls for better assessment) would materially change the "no independent evaluation" finding.
- A published, dated Ministry document showing the intervention logic Treasury said was missing for Māori-medium assessment, and a single, corrected, consistent statement on where SMART data is stored, would resolve two of the specific unresolved risks documented here.
- A systematic (not anecdotal) study of AI-marking reliability across a representative sample of SMART writing assessments — rather than teacher-reported anecdotes, however corroborated — would confirm or refute the moderation concern at scale.
- If SMART becomes compulsory and is explicitly tied to school-level accountability consequences (as the New Zealand Initiative op-ed argues it should be), that would be strong evidence the standardise-test-rank risk identified in the framing (H4) has materialised rather than been avoided.
- Conversely, if the second (post-July 2026) reporting round shows resolved item-quality issues, a published moderation framework, and a corrected/consistent data-storage disclosure, that would weaken this finding's overall confidence rating from Medium toward High-confidence-resolved.

## What could not be verified

- The independent existence, sample, and results of the claimed 85-school trial (~12,000 assessment engagements) — attempted via web search and the Beehive release; no separate trial report was located.
- The full text of Cabinet Minute CAB-25-MIN-0031 and any subsequent Cabinet paper formally approving the mandate itself (as distinct from the Business Case) — only the Treasury OIA's excerpts of the pre-Cabinet consultation briefing were accessible.
- A Ministry of Education OIA response specifically on SMART security/privacy compliance (a request exists on the FYI.org.nz OIA-tracking site) could not be retrieved — the site did not respond within available tooling (timeout), which per this project's fetch protocol is classified as blocked/inaccessible, not a dead or non-existent source. This should be attempted again with browser tooling in a follow-up.
- Whether the specific wording discrepancy NZEI describes across the three Ministry information sheets is accurately quoted — this research relied on NZEI's characterisation and did not independently locate and compare all three source documents.
- Whether the National Standards-era "policy overriding evidence" critique (Thornley-Quinn, cited in the stream's problem statement) has a direct SMART-era analogue in released Cabinet advice — the Treasury OIA release only covers January–February 2025 correspondence, before any trial would have occurred, so it cannot itself confirm or deny the trial claim.

## Open follow-up questions

- What does the actual 85-school trial report say, if and when it is released — and can it be obtained via a further OIA request to the Ministry of Education specifically (distinct from the Treasury-held documents covered here)?
- Has ERO conducted or scheduled any formal review of SMART specifically (as opposed to general assessment practice) in its work programme?
- What, if any, intervention logic or funded response has the Ministry published connecting a below-expectation SMART result to additional support — this overlaps with sibling issue #643 (identifying and supporting learners without a one-size-fits-all benchmark).
