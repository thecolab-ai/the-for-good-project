# NZ's post-discount EV trajectory is a rebound from a 2024 trough, and official odometer data now shows battery-EVs at about 2 percent of light-vehicle kilometres

> **Re-research note (8 July 2026).** This finding was first written on 5 July 2026 with a Low-confidence gap on fuel-type VKT because two data sources — the MfE Data Service VKT table and its data.govt.nz mirror — could not be fetched, and no observed kilometres-by-fuel split was located. After the 8 July 2026 fetch-ladder upgrade (Jina reader + stealth browser) those two sources now resolve, and the Ministry of Transport's Annual Fleet Statistics open-data spreadsheet was recovered from a live/archived download. The result: the two previously-blocked sources turn out to contain only a stale 2001–2012 *total* VKT series with **no** fuel split, while the actual observed kilometres-by-fuel series lives in the MoT Annual Fleet Statistics (Table 8.2). The VKT revenue-risk section is therefore re-grounded in observed official data rather than flagged as unverifiable.

## Executive answer

- **Battery EV uptake fell sharply after the Clean Car Discount ended, then recovered through early 2026.** In NZTA Motor Vehicle Register light passenger/goods vehicles still on the register, BEV/FCEV first registrations fell from 25,868 in 2023 to 7,588 in 2024, rose to 9,195 in 2025, and reached 11,088 in January–June 2026; NZTA says the Clean Car Discount ended on 31 December 2023 and EVDB's NZTA-based dashboard shows recent new-EV market share running well above its 2025 lows [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023); [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).
- **Plug-in hybrid registrations also rebounded strongly after the 2024 trough.** MVR light passenger/goods PHEV/range-extender first registrations fell from 11,770 in 2023 to 4,530 in 2024, then rose to 7,771 in 2025 and 7,052 in January–June 2026, while industry reporting based on Motor Industry Association data says PHEVs were among the strongest-growth powertrains in 2025 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).
- **Ordinary hybrids, not plug-ins, are doing most of the near-term displacement of petrol/diesel registrations.** In the MVR extract, ordinary hybrids were 33.1 percent of 2023 light passenger/goods first registrations, 34.8 percent in 2024, 38.3 percent in 2025, and 36.1 percent in January–June 2026; EVs & Beyond reports non-plug-in hybrids at 30 percent of all new-vehicle registrations in 2025, up from 27 percent in 2024 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).
- **The stock still turns slowly.** Across MVR light passenger/goods vehicles in 1990–2026 vehicle-year files, petrol was 62.6 percent of stock, diesel 24.0 percent, ordinary hybrid 10.0 percent, BEV/FCEV 2.25 percent, and PHEV/range-extender 1.13 percent; EVDB separately reports about 98,900 fully electric light vehicles plus about 49,600 PHEVs as at 30 June 2026, with total plug-ins at 3.30 percent of the light fleet [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet).
- **The revenue-risk hinge is now grounded in observed data: battery-EVs drove about 987 million light-vehicle km in 2024 (2.16 percent of light VKT), up from 0.17 percent in 2018.** The Ministry of Transport's Annual Fleet Statistics report *observed* (odometer-derived) light pure-electric travel of 986.8 million km in 2024, versus 760.3 in 2023 and 76.4 in 2018 — a fast-growing but still small share of the ~45,800 million km driven by light petrol, diesel and pure-electric vehicles [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). Petrol-hybrid and plug-in-hybrid kilometres are classified under petrol/diesel in that table, so those vehicles still contribute fuel-excise revenue; only pure-EV km sit fully outside excise (and are now under light-EV RUC from 1 April 2024) [NZTA RUC factsheet 38](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/factsheets/38/docs/38-ruc.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — high for registration and stock direction from NZTA MVR data; medium for the observed VKT-by-fuel picture because the MoT Annual Fleet Statistics source is official and strong but I did not find an independent observed VKT-by-fuel cross-check; medium for current-market interpretation because the public MVR extract is a stock snapshot rather than a transaction ledger, and because the observed VKT table isolates pure-EV km but does not separately break out PHEV or ordinary-hybrid km.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

I treated "EV uptake trajectory" as light passenger and light goods vehicles, because these are the cars, SUVs, vans and utes most relevant to the Clean Car Discount, Clean Car Standard, and light EV/PHEV RUC transition; NZTA's MVR page says its open dataset is a monthly point-in-time snapshot of currently registered vehicles, includes first NZ registration year/month and import status, and is based on the Motor Vehicle Register [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). I filtered MVR rows to `VEHICLE_TYPE` values `PASSENGER CAR/VAN` and `GOODS VAN/TRUCK/UTILITY`, then grouped motive power into BEV/FCEV, PHEV/range-extender, ordinary hybrid, petrol, diesel and other; this excludes motorcycles, heavy vehicles, buses, trailers, tractors, and pre-1990 vehicle-year records [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

The MVR counts below are counts of vehicles still present in the public snapshot when the vehicle-year CSV files were downloaded, not a complete historical transaction series; NZTA warns the data is provided "as is", many MVR fields are free text, and the current fleet open data is a point-in-time snapshot updated monthly [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). That means the 2023–2026 first-registration counts can undercount vehicles first registered in those years but deregistered before the snapshot, while still being useful for the current fleet's recent-entry composition [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

For observed kilometres by fuel type I use the Ministry of Transport's Annual Fleet Statistics open-data spreadsheet (`NZVehicleFleet_2024`, published October/November 2025), which the Ministry offers as a complete machine-readable download from its Annual Fleet Statistics page; its travel figures are derived from odometer readings captured at warrant/certificate-of-fitness inspections [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner).

### Policy break

The Clean Car Discount ended on 31 December 2023; NZTA says eligible vehicles registered up to and on 31 December 2023 were subject to CCD fees and rebates, no vehicles were subject to a CCD fee from 1 January 2024, and rebate applications after 11:59pm on 31 December 2023 were not accepted [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023). IRD independently describes the scheme as applying rebates or fees to eligible vehicles registered up to or on 31 December 2023, with emissions-based rebates/fees running from 1 April 2022 until 31 December 2023 [IRD Clean Car Discount scheme](https://www.ird.govt.nz/topics/clean-car-discount-scheme).

Light EVs and plug-in hybrids entered RUC from 1 April 2024; the Ministry of Transport says owners of light EVs and plug-in hybrids under 3.5 tonnes had to buy a RUC licence from 1 April 2024, that RUC are paid by owners of diesel, heavy and electric vehicles as a pre-paid per-kilometre charge, and that the government is working towards transitioning the petrol fleet to RUC; NZTA's April 2024 RUC factsheet lists $76 per 1,000km for powered light vehicles up to 3,500kg and $38 per 1,000km for plug-in petrol hybrids with 2 axles and 1,001–3,500kg RUC weight [Ministry of Transport RUC system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system); [NZTA RUC factsheet 38](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/factsheets/38/docs/38-ruc.pdf). Ministry cabinet material records that Cabinet accepted the select committee's $38 per 1,000km PHEV rate rather than the previously approved $53 rate [Ministry of Transport cabinet material](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Road-User-Charges-Light-Electric-RUC-Vehicles-Amendment-Bill.pdf).

The Clean Car Standard remained in force after the discount ended; NZTA describes it as a supply-side policy that encourages low- and no-emission vehicle imports by charging importers for vehicles above CO2 targets and providing credits for vehicles below targets [NZTA Clean Car Standard](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-standard). Ministry advice on the 2024 Clean Car Importer Standard review says the Standard reduces CO2 emissions and motoring costs through annual CO2 targets that progressively lower, while allowing suppliers to choose their vehicle mix [Ministry of Transport Clean Car Standard review](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Outcome-of-the-review-of-the-Clean-Car-Importer-Standard-11-June-2024-Redacted.pdf).

### Current stock

In the MVR light passenger/goods extract, petrol and diesel vehicles still dominate the stock: petrol was 2,762,062 vehicles, diesel 1,058,318, ordinary hybrid 439,972, BEV/FCEV 99,119, PHEV/range-extender 49,650, and other 680 across 1990–2026 vehicle-year files [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). Those MVR-derived stock shares are 62.6 percent petrol, 24.0 percent diesel, 10.0 percent ordinary hybrid, 2.25 percent BEV/FCEV, 1.13 percent PHEV/range-extender, and 0.02 percent other [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

The Ministry's Annual Fleet Statistics give an independent official cross-check on stock by fuel type. Its Table 8.4 (light fleet by main fuel) shows, for 2024: 2,952,568 petrol, 307,968 petrol hybrids, 923,050 diesel, 78,834 pure electric, 34,287 plug-in petrol hybrids, and 113,715 "electric/plug-in" combined, out of 4,309,833 light vehicles — confirming that pure-electric and plug-in vehicles together were under 3 percent of the light fleet in the 2024 Annual Fleet Statistics [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). EVDB provides a second, independent current-market cross-check from NZTA open data: it reports about 98,900 fully electric light vehicles and about 49,600 plug-in hybrids as at 30 June 2026, with "total plug-ins" at 3.30 percent [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet). All three sources agree that plug-ins remain a small stock share, but EVDB's current dashboard is now above the 3 percent threshold [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet).

### First registrations and import channel

The MVR extract shows a 2024 plug-in trough immediately after the Clean Car Discount ended and EV/PHEV RUC began: BEV/FCEV first registrations were 25,868 in 2023, 7,588 in 2024, 9,195 in 2025 and 11,088 in January–June 2026, while PHEV/range-extender first registrations were 11,770 in 2023, 4,530 in 2024, 7,771 in 2025 and 7,052 in January–June 2026 [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023); [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). In share terms, BEV/FCEV fell from 10.0 percent of light passenger/goods first registrations in 2023 to 3.4 percent in 2024, then rose to 4.2 percent in 2025 and 9.5 percent in January–June 2026; PHEV/range-extender fell from 4.6 percent in 2023 to 2.0 percent in 2024, then rose to 3.5 percent in 2025 and 6.1 percent in January–June 2026 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

Ordinary hybrids were more important than plug-ins in the post-discount substitution pattern: ordinary hybrids were 85,409 first registrations in 2023, 77,557 in 2024, 84,707 in 2025 and 41,918 in January–June 2026, equal to 33.1 percent, 34.8 percent, 38.3 percent and 36.1 percent of light passenger/goods first registrations respectively [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). EVs & Beyond, citing Motor Industry Association data, reports a similar 2025 pattern for all new vehicles: BEVs were 5.6 percent of 2025 registrations, PHEVs 5.0 percent, hybrids 30 percent, and internal-combustion vehicles 59.4 percent [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).

New imports and used imports show different substitution paths. In the MVR extract, new-import BEV share fell from 14.7 percent in 2023 to 5.3 percent in 2024, then rose to 5.6 percent in 2025 and 13.0 percent in January–June 2026; used-import BEV share fell from 4.1 percent in 2023 to 1.1 percent in 2024, then rose to 2.0 percent in 2025 and 4.2 percent in January–June 2026 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). Used imports remain hybrid-heavy: ordinary hybrids were 49.7 percent of used light passenger/goods first registrations in 2023, 42.3 percent in 2024, 47.0 percent in 2025 and 46.8 percent in January–June 2026, while petrol used imports were 39.7 percent, 51.2 percent, 46.1 percent and 43.4 percent across the same periods [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

EVDB's live dashboard reinforces the early-2026 rebound but also shows how volatile monthly market share is, and states that new EVs outsell used-import EVs by a wide margin; because these are live rolling figures, this finding cites the directional new-versus-used relationship rather than a brittle single-month pair [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).

### VKT and revenue relevance (recovered)

Road-funding exposure depends on kilometres, not just vehicle counts. The observed kilometres-by-fuel series for light vehicles is published in the Ministry of Transport's Annual Fleet Statistics (Table 8.2, "Travel (million km) by fuel type and vehicle type"), derived from odometer readings [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). Observed **light pure-electric (BEV) travel** rose from 76.4 million km in 2018 to 131.8 (2019), 175.3 (2020), 237.3 (2021), 432.6 (2022), 760.3 (2023) and 986.8 million km in 2024 [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). Against combined light petrol travel of 32,012.5 million km and light diesel travel of 12,774.8 million km in 2024, pure-EV travel is 2.16 percent of light-vehicle kilometres (from 45,774 million km of light petrol + diesel + pure-electric travel), up from 0.17 percent in 2018 and 1.68 percent in 2023 [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). Total collective distance travelled by all vehicles was 49,746 million km [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 1.3](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner).

Two revenue implications follow. First, the fuel-excise base is being eroded only slowly so far — pure-EV km, which pay no petrol excise, are about 2 percent of light travel — but the erosion is accelerating (roughly a doubling of the pure-EV km share every two years since 2018) [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). Second, the observed table classifies vehicles by *main fuel*, so petrol hybrids, petrol-electric hybrids and plug-in petrol hybrids all fold into "petrol travel"; those vehicles still buy petrol and therefore still contribute fuel excise, which is why the near-term excise loss is concentrated in the pure-EV segment rather than the whole "electrified" segment [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.4](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner). As an order-of-magnitude illustration (my calculation), 986.8 million light-BEV km at the $76 per 1,000km light-EV RUC rate corresponds to roughly $75 million per year of RUC liability once fully charged, though 2024 was only a partial RUC year for light EVs (RUC from 1 April 2024) [NZTA RUC factsheet 38](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/factsheets/38/docs/38-ruc.pdf).

The two sources previously blocked — the MfE Data Service "Vehicle Kilometres Travelled" table and its data.govt.nz mirror — now resolve through the upgraded fetch ladder, and it is clear they are **not** the fuel-split source. The MfE table (ID 52128) is a 12-row *total* VKT series for 2001–2012 (36.168 billion km in 2001 rising to 39.971 billion km in 2012), added 1 December 2014 and last updated 31 March 2015 as part of the 2014 Air Domain Report; it has only `Year` and `kmTravelled` columns and no fuel breakdown [MfE VKT table](https://data.mfe.govt.nz/table/52128-vehicle-kilometres-travelled/). The data.govt.nz record "vehicle-kilometres-travelled1" is a harvested CKAN mirror pointing at that same MfE table; its "modified July 2026" timestamp is the harvest/re-issue date of the distribution records, not new underlying data [data.govt.nz VKT catalogue](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/vehicle-kilometres-travelled1). So the earlier inability to fetch those two links hid nothing about fuel-type VKT — the fuel-type series was in the MoT Annual Fleet Statistics all along, and is now cited above.

The Ministry's Vehicle Fleet Model remains the projection tool for forward VKT scenarios: its documentation says the VFM projects fleet composition, VKT, energy use, emissions, registrations and scrappage by sub-category, with actual/estimated data for 2001–2022 and projections to 2050 — useful for scenario design, complementary to the observed Annual Fleet Statistics history [Ministry of Transport VFM documentation](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Vehicle-Fleet-Model-Documentation_202405_v7.pdf). A 2025 NZTA research report on private motor VKT uses linked IDI, Household Travel Survey and MVR data and models fuel-type relationships, but it is a demographic study rather than a current aggregate observed series [NZTA Research Report 745](https://nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/research/reports/745/745-the-demographics-of-private-motor-vkt-in-nz.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A published PHEV/ordinary-hybrid observed VKT split (rather than folding them into petrol/diesel by main fuel) would sharpen the revenue picture, because it would separate the "electrified but still buying petrol" kilometres from the pure-EV kilometres that sit outside excise [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024, Table 8.2](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner).
- A transaction-level NZTA first-registration series, rather than a current-stock MVR snapshot with first-registration fields, could change the exact 2023–2026 flow counts if many vehicles first registered in those years have already left the fleet [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).
- Final 2026 full-year MVR/MIA data could change the rebound interpretation because the January–June 2026 MVR counts are partial-year counts and monthly market share is volatile [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).
- The 2025 Annual Fleet Statistics update (expected late 2026) would show whether observed pure-EV VKT continued its acceleration into the first full RUC year, which is the single most decisive input for the fuel-excise-versus-RUC transition [MoT Annual Fleet Statistics 2024](https://www.transport.govt.nz/statistics-and-insights/fleet-statistics/annual-fleet-statistics/inner).
- Better public Clean Car Standard compliance data by importer and fuel type could separate supply-side effects from demand-side price/RUC effects; this finding can show the timing of the shift, but it cannot causally isolate CCD repeal, RUC, interest rates, model supply, used-import availability and Clean Car Standard settings [NZTA Clean Car Standard](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-standard); [Ministry of Transport Clean Car Standard review](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Outcome-of-the-review-of-the-Clean-Car-Importer-Standard-11-June-2024-Redacted.pdf).
- I did not interview importers, dealers, EV owners, PHEV owners, rural drivers, fleet managers or low-income households; human domain review could change the interpretation of why buyers shifted toward hybrids and PHEVs after 2023, and how PHEVs are actually driven (electric vs petrol miles).

## Open follow-up questions

- Can the Ministry publish an observed VKT split that separately isolates PHEV and ordinary-hybrid kilometres, rather than classifying them by main fuel, so the fuel-excise base can be measured directly?
- How much of the 2026 plug-in rebound is explained by Clean Car Standard supply incentives versus model availability, exchange rates, discounting, finance costs, or buyer acceptance of RUC?
- Are PHEVs being used mostly as electric vehicles or petrol vehicles in New Zealand conditions, and what does that imply for both emissions and fuel-excise revenue?
