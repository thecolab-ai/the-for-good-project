# NZ's post-discount EV trajectory is a rebound from a 2024 trough, not a straight-line rapid-EV-growth story

## Executive answer

- **Battery EV uptake fell sharply after the Clean Car Discount ended, then recovered through early 2026.** In NZTA Motor Vehicle Register light passenger/goods vehicles still on the register, BEV/FCEV first registrations fell from 25,868 in 2023 to 7,588 in 2024, rose to 9,195 in 2025, and reached 11,088 in January-June 2026; NZTA says the Clean Car Discount ended on 31 December 2023 and EVDB's NZTA-based May 2026 dashboard shows new EV market share at 14.6 percent, versus 4.9 percent in May 2025 [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023); [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).
- **Plug-in hybrid registrations also rebounded strongly after the 2024 trough.** MVR light passenger/goods PHEV/range-extender first registrations fell from 11,770 in 2023 to 4,530 in 2024, then rose to 7,771 in 2025 and 7,052 in January-June 2026, while industry reporting based on Motor Industry Association data says PHEVs were the strongest-growth powertrain in 2025 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).
- **Ordinary hybrids, not plug-ins, are doing most of the near-term displacement of petrol/diesel registrations.** In the MVR extract, ordinary hybrids were 33.1 percent of 2023 light passenger/goods first registrations, 34.8 percent in 2024, 38.3 percent in 2025, and 36.1 percent in January-June 2026; EVs & Beyond reports non-plug-in hybrids at 30 percent of all new-vehicle registrations in 2025, up from 27 percent in 2024 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).
- **The stock still turns slowly.** Across MVR light passenger/goods vehicles in 1990-2026 vehicle-year files, petrol was 62.6 percent of stock, diesel 24.0 percent, ordinary hybrid 10.0 percent, BEV/FCEV 2.25 percent, and PHEV/range-extender 1.13 percent; EVDB separately reports about 95,900 fully electric light vehicles plus 48,200 PHEVs, or 3.20 percent plug-in fleet share as at 31 May 2026 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet).
- **VKT is the unresolved revenue-risk hinge.** The Ministry's Vehicle Fleet Model documentation says the model projects fleet composition, VKT, energy use, emissions, registrations and scrappage by sub-category, but I did not find a current public observed VKT table that cleanly separates BEV, PHEV, ordinary hybrid, petrol and diesel kilometres after EV/PHEV RUC began [Ministry of Transport VFM documentation](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Vehicle-Fleet-Model-Documentation_202405_v7.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - high for registration and stock direction from NZTA MVR data, medium for current-market interpretation because the public MVR extract is a stock snapshot rather than a transaction ledger, and low for fuel-type VKT because the required observed split is not publicly exposed in the sources I could verify.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

I treated "EV uptake trajectory" as light passenger and light goods vehicles, because these are the cars, SUVs, vans and utes most relevant to the Clean Car Discount, Clean Car Standard, and light EV/PHEV RUC transition; NZTA's MVR page says its open dataset is a monthly point-in-time snapshot of currently registered vehicles, includes first NZ registration year/month and import status, and is based on the Motor Vehicle Register [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). I filtered MVR rows to `VEHICLE_TYPE` values `PASSENGER CAR/VAN` and `GOODS VAN/TRUCK/UTILITY`, then grouped motive power into BEV/FCEV, PHEV/range-extender, ordinary hybrid, petrol, diesel and other; this excludes motorcycles, heavy vehicles, buses, trailers, tractors, and pre-1990 vehicle-year records because I could not resolve the pre-1990 blob filename from the extracted NZTA page [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

The MVR counts below are counts of vehicles still present in the public snapshot when I downloaded the vehicle-year CSV files on 5 July 2026, not a complete historical transaction series; NZTA warns the data is provided "as is", many MVR fields are free text, and the current fleet open data is a point-in-time snapshot updated monthly [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). That means the 2023-2026 first-registration counts can undercount vehicles first registered in those years but deregistered before the snapshot, while still being useful for the current fleet's recent-entry composition [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

### Policy break

The Clean Car Discount ended on 31 December 2023; NZTA says eligible vehicles registered up to and on 31 December 2023 were subject to CCD fees and rebates, no vehicles were subject to a CCD fee from 1 January 2024, and rebate applications after 11:59pm on 31 December 2023 were not accepted [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023). IRD independently describes the scheme as applying rebates or fees to eligible vehicles registered up to or on 31 December 2023, with emissions-based rebates/fees running from 1 April 2022 until 31 December 2023 [IRD Clean Car Discount scheme](https://www.ird.govt.nz/topics/clean-car-discount-scheme).

Light EVs and plug-in hybrids entered RUC from 1 April 2024; the Ministry of Transport says owners of light EVs and plug-in hybrids under 3.5 tonnes had to buy a RUC licence from 1 April 2024, and NZTA's April 2024 RUC factsheet lists $76 per 1,000km for powered light vehicles up to 3,500kg and $38 per 1,000km for plug-in petrol hybrids with 2 axles and 1,001-3,500kg RUC weight [Ministry of Transport RUC system](https://www.transport.govt.nz/area-of-interest/revenue/road-user-charges-system); [NZTA RUC factsheet 38](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/factsheets/38/docs/38-ruc.pdf). Ministry cabinet material records that Cabinet accepted the select committee's $38 per 1,000km PHEV rate rather than the previously approved $53 rate [Ministry of Transport cabinet material](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Road-User-Charges-Light-Electric-RUC-Vehicles-Amendment-Bill.pdf).

The Clean Car Standard remained in force after the discount ended; NZTA describes it as a supply-side policy that encourages low- and no-emission vehicle imports by charging importers for vehicles above CO2 targets and providing credits for vehicles below targets [NZTA Clean Car Standard](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-standard). Ministry advice on the 2024 Clean Car Importer Standard review says the Standard reduces CO2 emissions and motoring costs through annual CO2 targets that progressively lower, while allowing suppliers to choose their vehicle mix [Ministry of Transport Clean Car Standard review](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Outcome-of-the-review-of-the-Clean-Car-Importer-Standard-11-June-2024-Redacted.pdf).

### Current stock

In the MVR light passenger/goods extract, petrol and diesel vehicles still dominate the stock: petrol was 2,762,062 vehicles, diesel 1,058,318, ordinary hybrid 439,972, BEV/FCEV 99,119, PHEV/range-extender 49,650, and other 680 across 1990-2026 vehicle-year files [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). Those MVR-derived stock shares are 62.6 percent petrol, 24.0 percent diesel, 10.0 percent ordinary hybrid, 2.25 percent BEV/FCEV, 1.13 percent PHEV/range-extender, and 0.02 percent other [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

EVDB provides an independent current-market cross-check from NZTA open data: it reports about 95,900 fully electric light vehicles and 48,200 plug-in hybrids, with EVs at 2.13 percent, PHEVs at 1.07 percent, and plug-ins at 3.20 percent of the light-vehicle fleet as at 31 May 2026 [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet). The EVDB stock figures are close to the MVR aggregation's 99,119 BEV/FCEV and 49,650 PHEV/range-extender counts, which supports the conclusion that plug-ins remain a small stock share even after recent registration rebounds [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB fleet](https://evdb.nz/fleet).

### First registrations and import channel

The MVR extract shows a 2024 plug-in trough immediately after the Clean Car Discount ended and EV/PHEV RUC began: BEV/FCEV first registrations were 25,868 in 2023, 7,588 in 2024, 9,195 in 2025 and 11,088 in January-June 2026, while PHEV/range-extender first registrations were 11,770 in 2023, 4,530 in 2024, 7,771 in 2025 and 7,052 in January-June 2026 [NZTA Clean Car Discount](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-discount-ended-on-31-december-2023); [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). In share terms, BEV/FCEV fell from 10.0 percent of light passenger/goods first registrations in 2023 to 3.4 percent in 2024, then rose to 4.2 percent in 2025 and 9.5 percent in January-June 2026; PHEV/range-extender fell from 4.6 percent in 2023 to 2.0 percent in 2024, then rose to 3.5 percent in 2025 and 6.1 percent in January-June 2026 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

Ordinary hybrids were more important than plug-ins in the post-discount substitution pattern: ordinary hybrids were 85,409 first registrations in 2023, 77,557 in 2024, 84,707 in 2025 and 41,918 in January-June 2026, equal to 33.1 percent, 34.8 percent, 38.3 percent and 36.1 percent of light passenger/goods first registrations respectively [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). EVs & Beyond, citing Motor Industry Association data, reports a similar 2025 pattern for all new vehicles: BEVs were 5.6 percent of 2025 registrations, PHEVs 5.0 percent, hybrids 30 percent, and internal-combustion vehicles 59.4 percent [EVs & Beyond, 14 Jan 2026](https://evsandbeyond.co.nz/battery-electric-vehicle-market-steady-in-2025/).

New imports and used imports show different substitution paths. In the MVR extract, new-import BEV share fell from 14.7 percent in 2023 to 5.3 percent in 2024, then rose to 5.6 percent in 2025 and 13.0 percent in January-June 2026; used-import BEV share fell from 4.1 percent in 2023 to 1.1 percent in 2024, then rose to 2.0 percent in 2025 and 4.2 percent in January-June 2026 [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets). Used imports remain hybrid-heavy: ordinary hybrids were 49.7 percent of used light passenger/goods first registrations in 2023, 42.3 percent in 2024, 47.0 percent in 2025 and 46.8 percent in January-June 2026, while petrol used imports were 39.7 percent, 51.2 percent, 46.1 percent and 43.4 percent across the same periods [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).

EVDB's current dashboard reinforces the early-2026 rebound but also shows how volatile monthly market share is: for May 2026, EVDB reports 1,586 new EV registrations, 14.6 percent new EV market share, 9.5 percent PHEV market share, EV units up 229.0 percent year-on-year, PHEV units up 184.1 percent, hybrid units up 11.5 percent, petrol down 24.0 percent and diesel down 12.4 percent [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats). EVDB also states that new EVs outsell used-import EVs by about four to one, with about 924 new EV registrations per month versus about 220 used-import EV registrations per month [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).

### VKT and revenue relevance

The public stock and registration data are not enough to measure road-funding exposure by themselves because revenue risk depends on kilometres, not just vehicles; the Ministry's Vehicle Fleet Model documentation says the VFM projects vehicle fleet composition, VKT, energy use, emissions, registrations and scrappage by sub-category, which confirms that official modelling treats VKT as a separate driver [Ministry of Transport VFM documentation](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Vehicle-Fleet-Model-Documentation_202405_v7.pdf). The same VFM documentation says its May 2024 update reports actual and estimated data for 2001-2022 and projections from 2023-2050, so it is useful for scenario design but not a current observed post-RUC VKT split [Ministry of Transport VFM documentation](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Vehicle-Fleet-Model-Documentation_202405_v7.pdf).

I found an MfE/data.govt.nz catalogue record for "Vehicle Kilometres Travelled" sourced from the Ministry of Transport and modified in July 2026, but I could not verify through the available fetch rungs that it exposes a current observed fuel-type split matching BEV, PHEV, ordinary hybrid, petrol and diesel [data.govt.nz VKT catalogue](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/vehicle-kilometres-travelled1); [MfE VKT table](https://data.mfe.govt.nz/table/52128-vehicle-kilometres-travelled/). A 2025 NZTA research report on private motor VKT uses linked IDI, Household Travel Survey and MVR data and models fuel-type relationships, but it is not a public current aggregate table suitable for replacing an official observed BEV/PHEV/hybrid/petrol/diesel VKT series [NZTA Research Report 745](https://nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/research/reports/745/745-the-demographics-of-private-motor-vkt-in-nz.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current Ministry or NZTA observed VKT table split by BEV, PHEV, ordinary hybrid, petrol and diesel would materially improve or change the revenue-risk conclusion, because current stock shares do not prove kilometre shares [Ministry of Transport VFM documentation](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Vehicle-Fleet-Model-Documentation_202405_v7.pdf).
- A transaction-level NZTA first-registration series, rather than a current-stock MVR snapshot with first-registration fields, could change the exact 2023-2026 flow counts if many vehicles first registered in those years have already left the fleet [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets).
- Final 2026 full-year MVR/MIA data could change the rebound interpretation because the January-June 2026 MVR counts are partial-year counts and May 2026 market share is only one month [NZTA MVR open data](https://nzta.govt.nz/resources/new-zealand-motor-vehicle-register-statistics/new-zealand-vehicle-fleet-open-data-sets); [EVDB](https://evdb.nz/ev-stats).
- Better public Clean Car Standard compliance data by importer and fuel type could separate supply-side effects from demand-side price/RUC effects; this finding can show the timing of the shift, but it cannot causally isolate CCD repeal, RUC, interest rates, model supply, used-import availability and Clean Car Standard settings [NZTA Clean Car Standard](https://www.nzta.govt.nz/vehicles/clean-car-programme/clean-car-standard); [Ministry of Transport Clean Car Standard review](https://www.transport.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Outcome-of-the-review-of-the-Clean-Car-Importer-Standard-11-June-2024-Redacted.pdf).
- I did not interview importers, dealers, EV owners, PHEV owners, rural drivers, fleet managers or low-income households; human domain review could change the interpretation of why buyers shifted toward hybrids and PHEVs after 2023.
