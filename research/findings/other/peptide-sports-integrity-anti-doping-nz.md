# What is the sports-integrity status of peptides in NZ, and what does the pattern of anti-doping cases show?

## Executive answer

- **WADA classification, confirmed from the primary source:** BPC-157 sits under **S0 (Non-Approved Substances)** and has been prohibited at all times since the **2022 Prohibited List** (in force 1 January 2022) — it was the first substance ever named by example in S0 [WADA, *2022 Athlete Advisory*](https://www.usada.org/athlete-advisory/key-changes-2022-prohibited-list/). Growth hormone secretagogues (ibutamoren/MK-677, ipamorelin, macimorelin, etc.), GH-releasing peptides (GHRPs), GHRH analogues (CJC-1295, tesamorelin, sermorelin) and TB-500 (thymosin-β4) sit under **S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics)** — all prohibited at all times [WADA, *Prohibited List*, current version, viewed via Wayback snapshot 20 May 2026 (live page blocked by an AWS WAF bot challenge — see Sources)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260520144041/https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list).
- **A genuinely counter-intuitive classification detail, confirmed by four independent sources:** despite sitting in S0 — the class WADA itself describes as reserved for substances "not approved by any governmental regulatory health authority for human therapeutic use" — **BPC-157 is a *Specified* Substance**, not Non-Specified. This matters practically: Specified Substance status opens the door to a reduced sanction if an athlete proves the violation wasn't intentional (which is exactly what happened in two of the three NZ rugby cases below). This is confirmed identically by WADA's own site and by two separate New Zealand tribunal decisions applying two different (2022 and 2024) editions of the Prohibited List — see the table below.
- **Sport Integrity Commission (Te Kahu Raunui)**, NZ's Crown-entity anti-doping regulator (successor to Drug Free Sport New Zealand, launched 1 July 2024), adopts the WADA Prohibited List directly into the Sports Anti-Doping Rules (SADR) and republishes/updates it every 1 January [Sport Integrity Commission, *Prohibited substances*, Wayback snapshot 27 January 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260127024041/https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/substances/prohibited-substances) (live page 403s — see Sources).
- **The full pattern, from an official sweep of every peptide/GH-secretagogue case we could find in NZ's anti-doping case record (Sports Tribunal of New Zealand + New Zealand Rugby's own Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, which hears rugby ADRVs separately from the Tribunal):** four cases across 2021–2025, and **all four originated from a NZ Customs border interception of a personally-imported parcel**, not from a positive drug test in competition. None of the four sanctioned individuals were elite/national-level athletes — three were recreational or amateur club-level, explicitly invoking (or, in one case, having invoked on their behalf by the Tribunal) the WADA Code's 2021-introduced "Recreational Athlete" leniency provision. Sanctions ranged from **4 months** (an amateur footballer who bought MK-677/ibutamoren for gym training, unconnected to his football) to **3 years** (the one trafficking case). See the case table below.
- **Non-elite/gym signal:** the clearest documented evidence we found of ordinary gym-based (non-sport) peptide/GH-secretagogue use comes from inside the anti-doping system itself — the Sports Tribunal's 2021 decision explicitly records that the substance (MK-677) was bought "to assist with his gym training" from people at the athlete's own gym, and the Tribunal observed there was "scant reference in gymnasia to the risks of an anti-doping violation" [Sports Tribunal of New Zealand, *Drug Free Sport New Zealand v Anon*, ST 07/21, 1 December 2021, paras 9, 30](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ST-0721-Decision.pdf). This is corroborated independently by RNZ's September 2024 reporting that young men are increasingly using steroids, SARMs and peptides for physique reasons, driven by social media/gym culture, with conversations "becoming more open in gyms" and even high-school-age gym-goers discussing use [RNZ, *Young men turning to illegal measures to enhance appearance*, 30 September 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/529473/young-men-turning-to-illegal-measures-to-enhance-appearance).
- **The anti-doping system only ever sees a narrow, accidental slice of gym-based peptide use** — someone who happens to also be registered with a sports body bound by SADR. All four NZ cases were only caught because Customs (not the sports system) intercepted a personal import and referred it onward. There is no NZ data source — from Sport Integrity Commission, Medsafe, or elsewhere — that estimates general-population or gym-population peptide/IPED use; the sport case record is a byproduct of border enforcement, not a surveillance system for gym use.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The WADA classification claims and the four case outcomes are High confidence (primary tribunal decisions and the WADA site itself, cross-checked across independent documents). The "pattern" claim — that this fully represents NZ's peptide anti-doping history — is Medium: our sweep covered the Sports Tribunal's full case-decision taxonomy (all ~65 substance/violation-type tag pages) plus New Zealand Rugby's separately-hosted judicial committee decisions, but other national sporting organisations may run their own anti-doping tribunals outside the Sports Tribunal (as rugby does) that we did not systematically search — see "What would change this conclusion."

### WADA's classification of the peptides named in the framing issue

We fetched the current WADA Prohibited List content directly (the live `wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list` page returned an AWS WAF JavaScript challenge to every fetch method available to us — plain HTTP, this harness's WebFetch tool, and the project's browser-escalation script `fetch.mjs`, which reported "agent-browser not installed" / "cloakbrowser not installed" for its browser rungs; we did not install new browser tooling in this session). We instead retrieved a Wayback Machine snapshot of the same page dated 20 May 2026 (48 days before this research), via `node scripts/archive-cite.mjs`, and fetched that snapshot directly — it renders the full page content (the AWS challenge appears to apply only to direct requests to the live domain, not to the Internet Archive's cached copy) [Wayback snapshot, 20 May 2026, of WADA *Prohibited List*](https://web.archive.org/web/20260520144041/https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list). Per AGENTS.md's fetch-ladder rule, we treat this as BLOCKED-then-resolved (tooling, not a dead source), not a dead citation, and record exactly how we got the content.

From that page:

- **S0 (Non-Approved Substances):** "Any pharmacological substance which is not addressed by any of the subsequent sections of the List and with no current approval by any governmental regulatory health authority for human therapeutic use (e.g. drugs under pre-clinical or clinical development or discontinued, designer drugs, substances approved only for veterinary use) is prohibited at all times. This class covers many different substances including but not limited to **BPC-157**, 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP), ryanodine receptor-1-calstabin complex stabilizers ... and troponin activators." The page states as a rule: **"All prohibited substances in this class are Specified Substances."** [WADA, *Prohibited List*, Wayback snapshot 20 May 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260520144041/https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list)
- BPC-157's addition to S0 dates specifically to the **2022 List** (in force 1 January 2022), where USADA's athlete advisory records: "BPC-157 was not prohibited prior to 2022 ... Additionally, for the first time, a substance has been included by name as an example in section S0 (Non-approved Substances) of the List" [USADA, *Athlete Advisory: Explanation of Key Changes on 2022 WADA Prohibited List*](https://www.usada.org/athlete-advisory/key-changes-2022-prohibited-list/). This matches the framing issue's claim exactly and is independently consistent with our sibling finding on peptide import enforcement, which cites Medsafe's own June 2025 MCC submission listing BPC-157 among peptides intercepted at the NZ border [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md).
- **S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics):** the page states as a rule: **"All prohibited substances in this class are non-Specified Substances."** Sub-categories relevant to the framing issue:
  - **S2.2.4 Growth hormone releasing factors** — includes GHRH and its analogues (e.g. **CJC-1293, CJC-1295, sermorelin and tesamorelin**); growth hormone secretagogues (GHS) and their mimetics (e.g. anamorelin, capromorelin, **ibutamoren (MK-677)**, **ipamorelin**, lenomorelin/ghrelin, macimorelin, tabimorelin); and GH-releasing peptides/GHRPs (alexamorelin, examorelin/hexarelin, GHRP-1 through GHRP-6).
  - **S2.3 Growth factors and growth factor modulators** — includes "**Thymosin-β4 and its derivatives e.g. TB-500**", alongside IGF-1, FGFs, HGF, MGFs, PDGF, VEGF.
  [WADA, *Prohibited List*, Wayback snapshot 20 May 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260520144041/https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list)
- Sport Integrity Commission's own NZ-facing prohibited-substances page confirms it adopts this list wholesale and links directly to the current WADA list, describing the 2026 List's S2 update ("Pegmolesatide was added as an example of a new EPO-mimetic agent") — evidence the NZ regulator tracks WADA's annual updates rather than maintaining a separate NZ-specific peptide list [Sport Integrity Commission, *Prohibited substances*, Wayback snapshot 27 January 2026 (live page returned HTTP 403 to curl and to this harness's WebFetch tool on direct request — treated as BLOCKED per the fetch ladder, not dead)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260127024041/https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/substances/prohibited-substances).

### The specified/non-specified classification, confirmed by NZ tribunal decisions applying it directly

Three separate NZ anti-doping decisions quote the operative Prohibited List clause verbatim as it applied to their case, and all three agree with WADA's own site:

> "BPC157 is prohibited at all times and is a specified substance under S0 (Non-Approved Substances) of the List." — New Zealand Rugby Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, applying the **2024** List [*NZR v Archie Schluter*, decision dated 19 August 2024, para 2](https://www.nzrugby.co.nz/assets/NZR-Anti-Doping-Tribunal-Decision-Schluter.pdf)

> "BPC-157 is a non-approved substance and prohibited at all times. This substance is specified." — New Zealand Rugby Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, applying the **2022** List [*NZR v A Player*, decision dated 9 July 2024, para 3](https://www.nzrugby.co.nz/assets/NZR-Anti-Doping-Tribunal-Decision-PLAYER-A.pdf)

> "Under the Prohibited List 2024, which is incorporated into the SADR, Ipamorelin, Tesamorelin and TB-500 are non-specified substances, while BPC-157 is a specified substance." — Sports Tribunal of New Zealand, applying the **2024** List [*Sport Integrity Commission v Foster*, ST 20/24, 21 May 2025, para 16](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/ST2024-Decision-FINAL.pdf)

These three quotations, spanning two consecutive annual List editions (2022 and 2024) and two different tribunals (Sports Tribunal and NZ Rugby's Judicial Committee), agree with each other and with WADA's own site description. We treat "BPC-157 is Specified; the GH-secretagogue/GHRH/growth-factor peptides (ipamorelin, tesamorelin, TB-500) are Non-Specified" as High confidence.

**Why this classification detail matters practically, not just academically:** Non-Specified Substance violations carry a four-year default ineligibility period unless the athlete proves the violation was unintentional; Specified Substance violations default to two years, and can go lower under the Recreational Athlete/no-significant-fault provisions. In both rugby cases below, the athlete's ability to argue non-intentional use of BPC-157 (a Specified Substance) was central to landing at a two-year rather than four-year sanction — see the Foster case reasoning at paras 16–17 of the decision cited above, which sets the sanction for the non-specified GH-secretagogues at four years each and for BPC-157 at two years, before combining them.

### The NZ peptide/GH-secretagogue anti-doping case sweep

We swept the Sports Tribunal of New Zealand's full case-decision index — its ~65 substance/violation-type category tag pages (covering every named prohibited substance and violation type it has ever decided on, from "19-norandrosterone" through "whereabouts") plus its site search — and separately checked New Zealand Rugby's own anti-doping judicial committee, whose decisions are hosted on nzrugby.co.nz rather than sportstribunal.org.nz because rugby ADRVs (unlike most other sports) are heard by NZR's own Judicial Committee, not the Sports Tribunal, even though both apply the same SADR. We cross-checked all findings against Sport Integrity Commission's official "Current sanctions" register, which lists every athlete currently serving an anti-doping ban with the deciding body named [Sport Integrity Commission, *Current sanctions*, Wayback snapshot 27 January 2026 (live page 403s — BLOCKED per fetch ladder, not dead)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260127093302/https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/rules-rights/current-sanctions).

This produced four peptide/GH-secretagogue cases — every one we could find in the full case record:

| 9 Jul 2024 (backdated to 9 Aug 2023) | "A Player" (anon; former international, now recreational) | Rugby | NZR Anti-Doping Judicial Committee | Chorionic Gonadotropin, CJC-1295 (ipamorelin), BPC-157 | Possession/use | 2 years | NZ Customs intercepted a parcel addressed to him, Oct 2022; he said the substances treated chronic medical conditions, unrelated to sport |

**The pattern across all four cases:**

1. **Every case began with a NZ Customs border interception of a personally-addressed parcel**, referred onward to Medsafe and/or the anti-doping body — none began with a positive drug test in competition. This is the same entry channel documented in our sibling finding on peptide border enforcement [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md).
2. **None of the four were elite or national-level athletes.** The football case was explicitly found to be a "Recreational Athlete" under the WADA Code's 2021-introduced flexible-sanctioning category; the two rugby cases involved a player who had stopped elite/international rugby years earlier for health reasons and a current recreational club player; Foster was registered with two lower-profile sports (surf lifesaving, athletics) rather than a high-performance programme.
3. **In three of four cases, the stated reason for use was not sport performance** — injury/medical treatment (both rugby cases) or general gym training (the football case) — and anti-doping bodies accepted this as non-intentional in all three, materially reducing the sanction from the four-year default. Foster is the outlier: he was sanctioned for trafficking (importing for someone else), which carries a minimum four-year term regardless of intent, reduced to three years for early admission.
4. **Sanctions still ranged widely (4 months to 3 years)** because the SADR's Recreational Athlete/no-significant-fault provisions give tribunals real discretion once intent is disproven — the ST 07/21 decision is a detailed, reasoned example of a tribunal deliberately undercutting the parties' own jointly-proposed 13-month sanction down to 4 months because it judged the athlete had essentially no realistic way to know SADR applied to him.
5. **Frequency has increased**: one case in 2021, then none we could find for roughly two years, then three cases in the 2023–2025 period (interceptions clustering Oct 2022 – Sept 2024, decisions clustering mid-2024 – mid-2025). This tracks the same steep rise in Medsafe/Customs peptide-parcel interceptions generally (15 parcels in 2022 rising to over 370 in 2025) documented in our sibling finding [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) — consistent with, though not proof of, the sport cases being a fixed small fraction of a growing base rate of personal peptide importation, most of which never touches the sport system at all because most importers aren't registered athletes.

### Signal on non-elite gym/IPED use

We found no NZ dataset that measures general-population or gym-population peptide or IPED (image and performance enhancing drug) use directly — no equivalent in NZ to the UK's National IPED Info Survey [IPEDinfo, *2015 Survey Results*](https://www.ipedinfo.co.uk/resources/downloads/2015%20National%20IPED%20Info%20Survey%20report.pdf) exists here as far as we could establish. The signal we do have is indirect but comes from two independent, credible angles:

1. **The Sports Tribunal's own words, in a legal decision, describing ordinary gym culture.** In ST 07/21, the respondent explained he obtained MK-677 (ibutamoren) because "some of those at the gym had MK-677 Ibutamoren capsules" and he had seen it advertised on Facebook; he took it purely "to assist with his gym training" and had no intention of using it for football, which he played "for fun." The Tribunal itself remarked: "it understands there may be scant reference in gymnasia to the risks of an anti-doping violation notwithstanding the use of substances which, as in this case, fall foul of SADR" [ST 07/21 decision, paras 9, 12, 30](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ST-0721-Decision.pdf). This is a rare instance of a formal NZ legal proceeding directly documenting gym-based GH-secretagogue use as unremarkable and normalised among the athlete's gym peers, entirely separate from any sporting context — the only reason it became visible at all was that Customs intercepted the parcel and the buyer happened to also be a registered (if very casual) football player.
2. **RNZ's September 2024 reporting**, independent of the anti-doping system, that young men in NZ are increasingly using "SARMs, anabolic steroids, anti-estrogens, and peptides" for physique reasons, driven by social media fitness content; it reports that "conversations about using steroids and other banned substances are becoming more open in gyms, and among younger people, with even high school students talking about using" [RNZ, *Young men turning to illegal measures to enhance appearance*, 30 September 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/529473/young-men-turning-to-illegal-measures-to-enhance-appearance).

Both point the same direction — non-competitive, image/recovery-motivated peptide and related-compound use inside ordinary NZ gym culture, disconnected from organised sport — but neither is a quantified prevalence estimate, and the two are not fully independent in the sense the method requires (the RNZ piece is journalism drawing on interviews and expert commentary, not a dataset; the Tribunal case is a single anecdote, however credibly documented). **We rate this specific claim (peptide/GH-secretagogue use is present and normalised in general NZ gym culture, well beyond the athletes ever sanctioned) as Medium confidence**: the direction is corroborated from two independent angles, but the scale is unknown and unmeasured by any NZ source we could find.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A systematic search of every National Sporting Organisation's own anti-doping tribunal** (as rugby runs its own Judicial Committee outside the Sports Tribunal) would firm up "four cases total" — we found rugby's separate process only because search results happened to surface it; other codes (e.g. rugby league, which has its own "NZRL Clean Sport & Athlete Integrity" page) may run similar internal processes we did not check. If other codes have unreported peptide ADRVs, our case count is a floor, not a ceiling.
- **The actual, current live WADA Prohibited List page and PDF** — both blocked by an AWS WAF JavaScript challenge to every fetch method we had (plain HTTP, this harness's WebFetch, and the project's `fetch.mjs` browser-escalation script, which reported neither `agent-browser` nor `cloakbrowser` installed in this environment). We resolved this via a ~48-day-old Wayback snapshot instead; a fresher direct fetch (e.g. after installing `agent-browser`/`cloakbrowser` per AGENTS.md's one-time setup, or from a different network) would let us confirm the snapshot hasn't drifted from the live page, and would let us pull the full official PDF rather than the HTML summary page.
- **A quantified NZ gym/IPED prevalence survey** (nothing like the UK's National IPED Info Survey exists here as far as we found) would replace our two-source, direction-only signal on non-elite use with an actual estimate. This is a plausible candidate for a project skill-request or a future research issue if a human steward wants it pursued (we did not open one, per the "don't fan out unnecessarily" guidance — this issue was answerable as scoped).
- **Confirmation of Sport Integrity Commission's "Safeguarding anti-doping protections for recreational athletes" news item** — we found this page via search but could not fetch it (live page 403s, and no Wayback snapshot existed; a fresh-capture request via `archive-cite.mjs` failed, likely due to Wayback rate-limiting in this session). It may contain directly relevant detail on how NZ's Recreational Athlete category has been adjusted, possibly partly in response to cases like ST 07/21 — we did not want to claim its contents without having read it, so we've left it out entirely rather than guess.
- **We could not verify whether other NZ athletes have been sanctioned for peptides but processed entirely within a National Sporting Organisation's internal disciplinary system with no public decision published** (rugby publishes its decisions on nzrugby.co.nz, but we can't confirm every code does the same, or that publication is complete/exhaustive).
