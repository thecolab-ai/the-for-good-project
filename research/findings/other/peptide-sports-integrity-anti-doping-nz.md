# What is the sports-integrity status of peptides in NZ, and what does the pattern of anti-doping cases show?

> **Re-research note (8 July 2026).** This finding was first written on 7 July 2026, when the live WADA Prohibited List page and PDF, and every `sportintegrity.nz` page, returned bot-challenges/403s to the fetch tooling then available, forcing reliance on Wayback snapshots and a secondary sports-law site. After the 8 July 2026 fetch-ladder upgrade (ADR-0006 browser + Jina rungs), **all of those sources now fetch directly**. This version replaces every Wayback-snapshot and "blocked/could not fetch" citation with a live/primary fetch — most importantly, the WADA classification is now sourced from **the official WADA 2026 Prohibited List PDF itself** (downloaded directly, HTTP 200), not a cached HTML summary — and incorporates the Sport Integrity Commission "recreational athletes" page the first version had to omit because it could not be read. The substantive conclusions are unchanged; the evidence base is now primary throughout.

## Executive answer

- **WADA classification, confirmed from the primary source (the official WADA 2026 Prohibited List PDF):** BPC-157 sits under **S0 (Non-Approved Substances)** and has been prohibited at all times since the **2022 Prohibited List** (in force 1 January 2022). The 2026 List names it directly: "This class covers many different substances including but not limited to **BPC-157**, 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP)…" [WADA, *International Standard Prohibited List 2026*, p.4, S0](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2025-09/2026list_en_final_clean_september_2025.pdf). Growth hormone secretagogues (ibutamoren/MK-677, ipamorelin, macimorelin, etc.), GH-releasing peptides (GHRPs), GHRH analogues (CJC-1295, tesamorelin, sermorelin) and TB-500 (thymosin-β4) sit under **S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics)** — all prohibited at all times [WADA, *2026 Prohibited List*, S2.2.4 and S2.3](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2025-09/2026list_en_final_clean_september_2025.pdf).
- **A genuinely counter-intuitive classification detail, confirmed by the WADA List itself plus three NZ tribunal decisions:** despite sitting in S0 — the class reserved for substances "with no current approval by any governmental regulatory health authority for human therapeutic use" — **BPC-157 is a *Specified* Substance**, because the WADA List states as a blanket rule that "All prohibited substances in this class [S0] are Specified Substances" [WADA, *2026 Prohibited List*, S0](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2025-09/2026list_en_final_clean_september_2025.pdf). This matters practically: Specified Substance status can affect the sanction pathway, because a four-year term applies only if the anti-doping organisation proves intentional use; the two NZR BPC-157 decisions both landed at two years after non-intentional findings, while the Foster trafficking case combined a Specified BPC-157 possession violation with non-Specified peptide violations and trafficking. It is confirmed identically by the WADA List and by two separate New Zealand tribunals applying two different (2022 and 2024) editions of the Prohibited List — see the table below.
- **Sport Integrity Commission (Te Kahu Raunui)**, NZ's Crown-entity anti-doping regulator, officially launched 1 July 2024 "building on the excellent work of Drug Free Sport NZ" [Sport Integrity Commission, *Sport Integrity Commission Te Kahu Raunui opens its doors*, 1 July 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/sport-integrity-commission-te-kahu-raunui-opens-its-doors), and adopts the WADA Prohibited List directly into the Sports Anti-Doping Rules (SADR), republishing/updating it every 1 January [Sport Integrity Commission, *Prohibited substances*](https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/substances/prohibited-substances).
- **The full pattern, from an official sweep of every peptide/GH-secretagogue case we could find in NZ's anti-doping case record (Sports Tribunal of New Zealand + New Zealand Rugby's own Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, which hears rugby ADRVs separately from the Tribunal):** four cases across 2021–2025, and **all four originated from a NZ Customs/Medsafe border interception of a personally-imported parcel**, not from a positive drug test in competition. None of the four sanctioned individuals were shown to be elite/national-level athletes at the time of their violation. Two of the four — the footballer and one of the two rugby cases ("A Player") — were expressly found by their tribunal to meet SADR's definition of "Recreational Athlete"; that status affected the footballer's sanction-reduction pathway and the rugby player's public-disclosure treatment, not both sanctions. The other rugby case (Schluter) is only shown to have been registered with New Zealand Rugby at the time, with the decision silent on his grade or playing level; and Foster (surf lifesaving/athletics, the trafficking case) was not assessed against the Recreational Athlete category at all, since that provision does not apply to trafficking violations. Sanctions ranged from **4 months** (an amateur footballer who bought MK-677/ibutamoren for gym training, unconnected to his football) to **3 years** (the one trafficking case). See the case table below.
- **Non-elite/gym signal:** the clearest documented evidence we found of ordinary gym-based (non-sport) peptide/GH-secretagogue use comes from inside the anti-doping system itself — the Sports Tribunal's 2021 decision explicitly records that the substance (MK-677) was bought "to assist with his gym training" from people at the athlete's own gym, and the Tribunal observed there was "scant reference in gymnasia to the risks of an anti-doping violation" [Sports Tribunal of New Zealand, *Drug Free Sport New Zealand v Anon*, ST 07/21, 1 December 2021, paras 9, 30](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ST-0721-Decision.pdf). This is corroborated independently by RNZ's September 2024 reporting that young men are increasingly using steroids, SARMs and peptides for physique reasons, driven by social media/gym culture, with conversations "becoming more open in gyms" and even high-school-age gym-goers discussing use [RNZ, *Young men turning to illegal measures to enhance appearance*, 30 September 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/529473/young-men-turning-to-illegal-measures-to-enhance-appearance).
- **The anti-doping system only ever sees a narrow, accidental slice of gym-based peptide use** — someone who happens to also be registered with a sports body bound by SADR. All four NZ cases were only caught because Customs/Medsafe (not the sports system) intercepted a personal import and referred it onward. There is no NZ data source — from Sport Integrity Commission, Medsafe, or elsewhere — that estimates general-population or gym-population peptide/IPED use; the sport case record is a byproduct of border enforcement, not a surveillance system for gym use.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The WADA classification claims and the four case outcomes are High confidence (the official WADA List PDF and primary tribunal decisions, cross-checked across independent documents). The "pattern" claim — that this fully represents NZ's peptide anti-doping history — is Medium: our sweep covered the Sports Tribunal's full case-decision taxonomy (all ~65 substance/violation-type tag pages) plus New Zealand Rugby's separately-hosted judicial committee decisions, but other national sporting organisations may run their own anti-doping tribunals outside the Sports Tribunal (as rugby does) that we did not systematically search — see "What would change this conclusion." This limitation is genuine and unrelated to any fetch blocking.

## Evidence

### WADA's classification of the peptides named in the framing issue

Fetched directly from the official WADA 2026 Prohibited List PDF (downloaded directly, HTTP 200):

- **S0 (Non-Approved Substances):** "PROHIBITED AT ALL TIMES (IN- AND OUT-OF-COMPETITION). **All prohibited substances in this class are Specified Substances.** Any pharmacological substance which is not addressed by any of the subsequent sections of the List and with no current approval by any governmental regulatory health authority for human therapeutic use (e.g. drugs under pre-clinical or clinical development or discontinued, designer drugs, substances approved only for veterinary use) is prohibited at all times. This class covers many different substances including but not limited to **BPC-157**, 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP), ryanodine receptor-1-calstabin complex stabilizers [e.g. S-107, S48168 (ARM210)] and troponin activators (e.g. reldesemtiv and tirasemtiv)." [WADA, *2026 Prohibited List*, p.4, S0](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2025-09/2026list_en_final_clean_september_2025.pdf)
- BPC-157's addition to S0 dates specifically to the **2022 List** (in force 1 January 2022). WADA's own 2022 Summary of Major Modifications and Explanatory Note states it directly under "S0. Non-approved Substances": "**BPC-157 is now prohibited under S0 following a recent re-evaluation and added as an example.**" [WADA, *Summary of Major Modifications and Explanatory Notes on the 2022 Prohibited List*, S0](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2022-03/2022list_explanatory_list_en_0.pdf) USADA's athlete advisory confirms the timing independently: "BPC-157 was not prohibited prior to 2022, but the clinically unapproved experimental peptide was added to the 2022 WADA Prohibited List under the S0 Unapproved Substances category" [USADA, *Athlete Advisory: Explanation of Key Changes on 2022 WADA Prohibited List*](https://www.usada.org/athlete-advisory/key-changes-2022-prohibited-list/). The additional detail that BPC-157 was the **first substance ever included by name as an example** in S0 comes from WADA's 2022 Summary text as reproduced verbatim by a sports-law site: "For the first time, a substance has been included by name as an example in section S0 (Non-approved Substances) of the List. This substance, BPC-157, is an experimental peptide sold as a supplement…" [Sport Resolutions, *WADA's 2022 prohibited list comes into force on 1 Jan 2022*](https://www.sportresolutions.com/news/wadas-2022-prohibited-list-comes-into-force-on-1-jan-2022). Both the 2022-addition timing and the S0 category are independently consistent with our sibling finding on peptide import enforcement, which cites Medsafe's own June 2025 MCC submission listing BPC-157 among peptides intercepted at the NZ border [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md).
- **S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics):** the List states as a rule: "**All prohibited substances in this class are non-Specified Substances.**" Sub-categories relevant to the framing issue, quoted from the 2026 List:
  - **S2.2.4 Growth hormone releasing factors** — "growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) and its analogues (e.g. **CJC-1293, CJC-1295, sermorelin and tesamorelin**); growth hormone secretagogues (GHS) and their mimetics [e.g. anamorelin, capromorelin, **ibutamoren (MK-677)**, **ipamorelin**, lenomorelin (ghrelin), macimorelin and tabimorelin]; GH-releasing peptides (GHRPs) [e.g. alexamorelin, examorelin (hexarelin), GHRP-1, GHRP-2 (pralmorelin), GHRP-3, GHRP-4, GHRP-5 and GHRP-6]."
  - **S2.3 Growth factors and growth factor modulators** — includes "**Thymosin-ß4 and its derivatives e.g. TB-500**", alongside IGF-1 (mecasermin), FGFs, HGF, MGFs, PDGF and VEGF.
  [WADA, *2026 Prohibited List*, S2.2.4 and S2.3](https://www.wada-ama.org/sites/default/files/2025-09/2026list_en_final_clean_september_2025.pdf)
- Sport Integrity Commission's own NZ-facing prohibited-substances page confirms it adopts this list wholesale and links directly to the current WADA list, and it summarises the 2026 List's S2.1 update ("Pegmolesatide was added as an example of a new EPO-mimetic agent") — evidence the NZ regulator tracks WADA's annual updates rather than maintaining a separate NZ-specific peptide list [Sport Integrity Commission, *Prohibited substances* (fetched live 8 July 2026)](https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/substances/prohibited-substances).

### The specified/non-specified classification, confirmed by NZ tribunal decisions applying it directly

Three separate NZ anti-doping decisions quote the operative Prohibited List clause verbatim as it applied to their case, and all three agree with the WADA List:

> "BPC157 is prohibited at all times and is a specified substance under S0 (Non-Approved Substances) of the List." — New Zealand Rugby Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, applying the **2024** List [*NZR v Archie Schluter*, decision dated 19 August 2024, para 2](https://www.nzrugby.co.nz/assets/NZR-Anti-Doping-Tribunal-Decision-Schluter.pdf)

> "BPC-157 is a non-approved substance and prohibited at all times. This substance is specified." — New Zealand Rugby Anti-Doping Judicial Committee, applying the **2022** List [*NZR v A Player*, decision dated 9 July 2024, para 3](https://www.nzrugby.co.nz/assets/NZR-Anti-Doping-Tribunal-Decision-PLAYER-A.pdf)

> "Under the Prohibited List 2024, which is incorporated into the SADR, Ipamorelin, Tesamorelin and TB-500 are non-specified substances, while BPC-157 is a specified substance." — Sports Tribunal of New Zealand, applying the **2024** List [*Sport Integrity Commission v Foster*, ST 20/24, 21 May 2025, para 16](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/ST2024-Decision-FINAL.pdf)

These three quotations, spanning two consecutive annual List editions (2022 and 2024) and two different tribunals (Sports Tribunal and NZ Rugby's Judicial Committee), agree with each other and with the WADA List's own blanket rules (S0 = all Specified; S2 = all non-Specified). We treat "BPC-157 is Specified; the GH-secretagogue/GHRH/growth-factor peptides (ipamorelin, tesamorelin, TB-500) are Non-Specified" as High confidence.

**Why this classification detail matters practically, not just academically:** Non-Specified Substance violations carry a four-year period unless the athlete proves the violation was unintentional; Specified Substance violations carry four years only if the anti-doping organisation proves intent, otherwise the baseline is two years before any further reduction provisions are considered. In both NZR BPC-157 cases below, the two-year outcome turned on the decision-makers accepting the violation was not intentional under SADR 10.2.2, not on a Recreational Athlete sanction reduction; in ST 07/21, by contrast, Recreational Athlete/no-significant-fault provisions were used to go below two years. The Foster case reasoning at paras 16–17 of the decision cited above shows the distinction for mixed peptide cases: non-Specified peptide possession violations start at four years, BPC-157 possession starts at two years, and trafficking has its own four-year-minimum pathway before the final combined sanction.

We cross-checked all findings against Sport Integrity Commission's official "Current sanctions" register, which lists every athlete currently serving an anti-doping ban with the deciding body named [Sport Integrity Commission, *Current sanctions* (fetched live 8 July 2026)](https://sportintegrity.nz/integrity/anti-doping/rules-rights/current-sanctions).

This produced four peptide/GH-secretagogue cases — every one we could find in the full case record:

**The pattern across all four cases:**

1. **Every case began with a NZ Customs/Medsafe border interception of a personally-addressed parcel**, referred onward to the anti-doping body — none began with a positive drug test in competition. This is the same entry channel documented in our sibling finding on peptide border enforcement [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md).
2. **None of the four were shown to be elite or national-level athletes at the time of their violation.** The football case was explicitly found to be a "Recreational Athlete" under the WADA Code's 2021-introduced flexible-sanctioning category; one rugby case involved a player who had stopped elite/international rugby years earlier for health reasons; the Schluter decision states only that he was "registered as a player with New Zealand Rugby" at the time of the intercept and did not re-register for the 2024 season — it does not state his grade or playing level, so we can say he was not shown to be playing at an elite/representative level, not that he was positively "recreational" (the decision is silent on this); Foster was registered with two lower-profile sports (surf lifesaving, athletics) rather than a high-performance programme.
3. **In three of four cases, the stated reason for use was not sport performance** — injury/medical treatment (both rugby cases) or general gym training (the football case) — and anti-doping bodies accepted this as non-intentional in all three. That mattered to sanction, but through different rules: the two NZR cases stayed at the two-year SADR 10.2.2 outcome, while ST 07/21 then went lower under the Recreational Athlete/no-significant-fault provision. Foster is the outlier: he was sanctioned for trafficking (importing for someone else), which carries a minimum four-year term regardless of intent, reduced to three years for early admission.
4. **Sanctions still ranged widely (4 months to 3 years)** because these cases travelled through different SADR pathways. ST 07/21 is the clear Recreational Athlete/no-significant-fault sanction case: the Tribunal deliberately undercut the parties' own jointly-proposed 13-month sanction down to 4 months because it judged the athlete had essentially no realistic way to know SADR applied to him. "A Player" and Schluter landed at two years under SADR 10.2.2 because the violations were accepted as not intentional. Foster was a possession-plus-trafficking case, with the final three-year sanction reflecting early admission against a more serious trafficking rule pathway.
5. **Frequency has increased**: one case in 2021, then none we could find for roughly two years, then three cases in the 2023–2025 period (interceptions clustering Oct 2022 – Sept 2024, decisions clustering mid-2024 – mid-2025). This tracks the same steep rise in Medsafe/Customs peptide-parcel interceptions generally (15 parcels in 2022 rising to over 370 in 2025) documented in our sibling finding [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) — consistent with, though not proof of, the sport cases being a fixed small fraction of a growing base rate of personal peptide importation, most of which never touches the sport system at all because most importers aren't registered athletes.

### The Recreational Athlete category and its 2024 tightening

The "Recreational Athlete" category is not a NZ invention — it was introduced into the WADA Code framework in 2021 — but the NZ effect differs by case. ST 07/21 is the clear sanction-reduction example: SADR 10.6.1.3 let the Tribunal reduce a recreational footballer's ineligibility below the two-year baseline once it accepted no significant fault or negligence [ST 07/21 decision, paras 16, 19-24, 36-42](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ST-0721-Decision.pdf). "A Player" is different. The NZR Judicial Committee treated him as a Recreational Athlete for public-disclosure purposes under SADR 14.3.7, but the two-year sanction itself came under SADR 10.2.2 after the Committee accepted the violations were not intentional [NZR v A Player, findings and sanction sections](https://www.nzrugby.co.nz/assets/NZR-Anti-Doping-Tribunal-Decision-PLAYER-A.pdf).

Sport Integrity Commission's 2024 explainer records that the category was narrowed because the 2021 definition was too wide: it covered anyone who was not an International Level Athlete or in a DFSNZ Testing Pool, and the Commission later judged that this allowed some high-level athletes to move through the rule-violation process "as if they were recreational." The 2024 Sports Anti-Doping Rules therefore added a "High-Level Athletes" definition ("professional rugby players who aren't on a testing pool, and even Olympic competitors from certain sports") so that "true recreational athletes continue to benefit from protections during a rule violation process but that athletes playing at a higher level move through that process appropriately" [Sport Integrity Commission, *Safeguarding anti-doping protections for recreational athletes*, 11 January 2024](https://sportintegrity.nz/news/safeguarding-anti-doping-protections-for-recreational-athletes). The practical implication for this finding is narrow: the 2024 tightening could matter to whether a future higher-grade athlete receives recreational-athlete process protections, especially disclosure protection or access to the ST 07/21-style no-significant-fault route, but the A Player decision does not show that the tightening would change his two-year non-intentional sanction outcome.

### Signal on non-elite gym/IPED use

We found no NZ dataset that measures general-population or gym-population peptide or IPED (image and performance enhancing drug) use directly — no equivalent in NZ to the UK's National IPED Info Survey [IPEDinfo, *2015 Survey Results*](https://www.ipedinfo.co.uk/resources/downloads/2015%20National%20IPED%20Info%20Survey%20report.pdf) exists here as far as we could establish. The signal we do have is indirect but comes from two independent, credible angles:

1. **The Sports Tribunal's own words, in a legal decision, describing ordinary gym culture.** In ST 07/21, the respondent explained he obtained MK-677 (ibutamoren) because "some of those at the gym had MK-677 Ibutamoren capsules" and he had seen it advertised on Facebook; he took it purely "to assist with his gym training" and had no intention of using it for football, which he played "for fun." The Tribunal itself remarked: "it understands there may be scant reference in gymnasia to the risks of an anti-doping violation notwithstanding the use of substances which, as in this case, fall foul of SADR" [ST 07/21 decision, paras 9, 12, 30](https://sportstribunal.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/ST-0721-Decision.pdf). This is a rare instance of a formal NZ legal proceeding directly documenting gym-based GH-secretagogue use as unremarkable and normalised among the athlete's gym peers, entirely separate from any sporting context — the only reason it became visible at all was that Customs/Medsafe intercepted the parcel and the buyer happened to also be a registered (if very casual) football player.
2. **RNZ's September 2024 reporting**, independent of the anti-doping system, that young men in NZ are increasingly using "SARMs, anabolic steroids, anti-estrogens, and peptides" for physique reasons, driven by social media fitness content; it reports that "conversations about using steroids and other banned substances are becoming more open in gyms, and among younger people, with even high school students talking about using" [RNZ, *Young men turning to illegal measures to enhance appearance*, 30 September 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/529473/young-men-turning-to-illegal-measures-to-enhance-appearance).

Both point the same direction — non-competitive, image/recovery-motivated peptide and related-compound use inside ordinary NZ gym culture, disconnected from organised sport — but neither is a quantified prevalence estimate, and the two are not fully independent in the sense the method requires (the RNZ piece is journalism drawing on interviews and expert commentary, not a dataset; the Tribunal case is a single anecdote, however credibly documented). **We rate this specific claim (peptide/GH-secretagogue use is present and normalised in general NZ gym culture, well beyond the athletes ever sanctioned) as Medium confidence**: the direction is corroborated from two independent angles, but the scale is unknown and unmeasured by any NZ source we could find.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A systematic search of every National Sporting Organisation's own anti-doping tribunal** (as rugby runs its own Judicial Committee outside the Sports Tribunal) would firm up "four cases total" — we found rugby's separate process only because search results happened to surface it; other codes (e.g. rugby league, which has its own "NZRL Clean Sport & Athlete Integrity" page) may run similar internal processes we did not check. If other codes have unreported peptide ADRVs, our case count is a floor, not a ceiling.
- **A quantified NZ gym/IPED prevalence survey** (nothing like the UK's National IPED Info Survey exists here as far as we found) would replace our two-source, direction-only signal on non-elite use with an actual estimate. This is a plausible candidate for a future research issue if a human steward wants it pursued (we did not open one, per the "don't fan out unnecessarily" guidance — this issue was answerable as scoped).
- **We could not verify whether other NZ athletes have been sanctioned for peptides but processed entirely within a National Sporting Organisation's internal disciplinary system with no public decision published** (rugby publishes its decisions on nzrugby.co.nz, but we can't confirm every code does the same, or that publication is complete/exhaustive).
- **The classification facts are now sourced from the official WADA 2026 List PDF directly** (no longer a Wayback snapshot), so drift between a cached copy and the live List is no longer a risk for this finding; the remaining uncertainty is entirely about case-record completeness across codes, not about the WADA/SADR classification, which is High confidence.

## Open follow-up questions

- Do other National Sporting Organisations that run their own anti-doping judicial processes (rugby league, for instance) have additional unpublished-to-us peptide cases? A systematic OIA-style request to each major NSO, or to Sport Integrity Commission asking for a complete cross-code list, would close this gap.
- Now that Sport Integrity Commission has existed for two full years (launched 1 July 2024) and has tightened the Recreational Athlete definition for 2024, is there a published trend in recreational-athlete ADRV numbers, or further definitional changes prompted by cases like the 2021 ibutamoren case?
- This sits adjacent to, and should not duplicate, the border-enforcement finding for issue #713 [research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md](../other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) — a natural next question is whether Sport Integrity Commission or Medsafe have any coordinated referral process specifically for peptide interceptions (beyond the general Customs→Medsafe→SIC chain visible in these four cases).
