# A small NZ grocery sample shows hidden unit-price rises when packs shrink

## Executive answer

- In a bounded Grocer.nz/Consumer NZ sample, the clearest hidden shrinkflation examples were Keri orange juice and Purex Mega Long toilet paper: Consumer NZ reported the pack reductions, and Grocer history showed same-store shelf-price ceilings that stayed flat while unit prices rose about 25.0% for Keri and 9.8% for Purex [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer Keri old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_5913.parquet); [Grocer Keri new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_80641.parquet); [Grocer Purex old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_31181.parquet); [Grocer Purex new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_178923.parquet).
- Cadbury Roses is a real old/new pack-size case in this sample, but not a hidden-flat-shelf-price case: Consumer NZ reported 420g to 380g, and Grocer same-store histories show the 380g product's observed price ceiling was higher than the 420g product's ceiling, so the unit-price rise came from both a smaller box and a higher shelf price [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer Cadbury old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_25595.parquet); [Grocer Cadbury new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_111202.parquet).
- Several Consumer NZ examples are valid shrink/skimp leads but could not be turned into clean Grocer shelf-price examples: Rebel gluten-free wraps and Pams jams lacked an old 80g/500g matching Grocer product ID in my search, Anchor blue milk powder kept the same 400g pack but changed mixing yield, and Nescafe cappuccino removed a shaker rather than shrinking the sachet count/weight [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer product search via public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).
- This finding publishes only a small set of product/store examples and method, not a bulk Grocer extract, because the accepted Grocer data-audit finding says the public assets support bounded analysis but do not provide a safe bulk republication licence [Grocer data audit finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md).
- Unit pricing is the practical consumer defence: the Commerce Commission says covered grocery retailers must display unit prices clearly and legibly, at no less than 25% of the marked-price size, in store, online, and in advertised prices, with physical-store compliance by 31 August 2024 and online compliance by 31 August 2025 [Commerce Commission unit-pricing guidance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/information-for-retailers-unit-pricing/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the arithmetic is reproducible from public Grocer product-history files and Consumer NZ's named examples, but Grocer does not retain historical size changes inside a single product record, old/new catalogue matching is partly inferential, and history rows do not prove what a shopper saw on a particular shelf ticket.

## Evidence

### Method and reproducibility

I fixed the product set before doing the final arithmetic: Consumer NZ's seven February 2026 examples, plus only old/new Grocer product IDs surfaced by direct product search for those named brands and product names; I did not bulk-publish Grocer rows or run an all-products shrinkflation leaderboard [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer data audit finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md).

I used the vendored `grocer-nz` skill with a local virtualenv (`.venv/bin/python .skills/skills/grocer-nz/scripts/cli.py`) on 2026-07-07; the skill documentation says product metadata includes `id`, `name`, `brand`, `unit`, `size`, and `redirected_to`, and per-product history rows include `updated_at`, `store_id`, and `price_cent` [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).

The unit-price calculations below use observed shelf price divided by pack quantity: dollars per litre for juice, dollars per 100g for chocolate, and dollars per 100 sheets for toilet paper; Grocer history prices are in New Zealand cents, so `699` means NZ$6.99 [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).

For old/new matching, I treated Consumer NZ's named examples as the external corroboration that a size change occurred, then used Grocer product IDs and histories only to test whether same-store shelf prices were flat, lower, or higher around the old/new product pair [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

### Shortlist of calculable examples

| Purex Mega Long White 6-pack at Woolworths Papakura (`store_id=118`), Grocer IDs `31181` and `178923` | 6 x 450 sheets -> 6 x 410 sheets | $9.00 old observed in September 2025; $9.00 new observed as the same store's post-change ceiling | $0.33/100 sheets -> $0.37/100 sheets, up 9.8% | Hidden case: same observed ceiling, 8.9% fewer sheets. |
| Cadbury Roses at PAK'nSAVE Riccarton (`store_id=239`), Grocer IDs `25595` and `111202` | 420g -> 380g | $17.95 old observed ceiling before August 2025; $19.95 new observed ceiling after August 2025 | $4.27/100g -> $5.25/100g, up 22.8% | Real shrink case, but not hidden by a flat shelf price because the observed ceiling also rose. |

Consumer NZ reported Keri juice shrinking from 3L to 2.4L and quoted Coca-Cola NZ saying the change was a response to orange-crop and juice-concentrate cost pressure; Grocer's current base catalogue has Keri Orange Juice With Apple Base product `5913` at `3l` and product `80641` at `2.4l` [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

At Fresh Choice Leeston, the old Keri 3L history (`product_id=5913`) ran from 2022-06-14 to 2024-09-23 and had a maximum observed price of 699 cents, while the new Keri 2.4L history (`product_id=80641`) ran from 2024-09-13 to 2026-07-06 and also had a maximum observed price of 699 cents; because $6.99 divided by 3L is $2.33/L and $6.99 divided by 2.4L is $2.91/L, the unit price rose 25.0% at the same shelf-price ceiling [Grocer Keri old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_5913.parquet); [Grocer Keri new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_80641.parquet).

Consumer NZ reported Purex Mega Long changing from 450 sheets per roll to 410 sheets per roll and quoted Essity saying the October 2025 sheet reduction was chosen to avoid passing on costs and increasing the recommended retail price; Grocer's base catalogue has product `31181` as "Purex Toilet Paper Mega Long White 6 Pack" with size `6 x 450sheets` and product `178923` as the same 6-pack name with size `410sheets`, which I interpret as 410 sheets per roll because the product name still says 6 Pack and Consumer NZ reports the per-roll change [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

At Woolworths Papakura, the old Purex 6 x 450 sheet history (`product_id=31181`) ran from 2022-06-14 to 2025-09-25 and had a maximum observed price of 900 cents, while the new Purex 6 x 410 sheet history (`product_id=178923`) ran from 2025-10-08 to 2026-07-06 and also had a maximum observed price of 900 cents; because $9.00 divided by 2,700 sheets is $0.33 per 100 sheets and $9.00 divided by 2,460 sheets is $0.37 per 100 sheets, the unit price rose 9.8% at the same observed shelf-price ceiling [Grocer Purex old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_31181.parquet); [Grocer Purex new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_178923.parquet).

Consumer NZ reported Cadbury Roses changing from 420g to 380g and quoted Mondelez NZ saying the pack-size change was made after significantly higher cocoa and input costs; Grocer's base catalogue has Cadbury Roses product `25595` at `420g` and product `111202` at `380g` [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

At PAK'nSAVE Riccarton, the old Cadbury Roses 420g history (`product_id=25595`) ran from 2022-06-14 to 2025-08-05 and had a maximum observed price of 1,795 cents, while the new Cadbury Roses 380g history (`product_id=111202`) ran from 2025-08-11 to 2026-07-06 and had a maximum observed price of 1,995 cents; because $17.95 divided by 420g is $4.27 per 100g and $19.95 divided by 380g is $5.25 per 100g, the unit price rose 22.8%, but this is not hidden by a flat shelf price because the observed shelf-price ceiling rose 11.1% [Grocer Cadbury old history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_25595.parquet); [Grocer Cadbury new history](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/price_history_v3/price_history_111202.parquet).

### Consumer NZ examples I did not turn into shelf-price claims

Rebel Bakehouse gluten-free wraps are a strong size-change lead, but not a clean Grocer price-history example in this pass: Consumer NZ reported an example decreasing from 80g to 46g, and the current Grocer catalogue has Rebel Bakehouse gluten-free wrap products `187164` and `187165` at `4 x 46g`, but my direct Grocer searches did not find a matching old gluten-free 80g product ID to compare same-store shelf prices against [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

The Rebel example also needs careful interpretation because Consumer NZ quoted Rebel Bakehouse saying it resized the gluten-free wraps instead of increasing price and aligned gluten-free-wrap prices with regular wheat wraps, resulting in a 13% to 21.7% price reduction depending on in-store promotional activity; even the maximum quoted price reduction would not necessarily offset a 4 x 80g to 4 x 46g quantity drop, but without the old Grocer product ID I did not publish a same-store unit-price row [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality).

Pams jam and marmalade are also a size-change lead rather than a Grocer-confirmed shelf-price case: Consumer NZ reported Pams jams shrinking from 500g to 450g and quoted Foodstuffs saying recommended retail prices had come down to reflect the change, while Grocer's current search surfaced Pams strawberry, raspberry, plum, apricot, blackcurrant, and mixed berries jams at `450g` but did not surface the old 500g product IDs in my direct searches [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).

Anchor Blue Milk Powder is a skimp/yield example rather than a pack-size example: Consumer NZ reported two 400g bags where the directions changed from making 3.2L to 2.75L, and quoted Anchor saying there was no formulation, powder-weight, or recommended-retail-price change; if a shopper follows the new directions at the same pack price, the effective price per made-up litre rises, but Grocer's product-size field cannot detect that because the pack remains 400g [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality).

Nescafe cappuccino sachets are a value-removal/skimpflation example rather than a size-change row for this method: Consumer NZ reported the chocolate shaker was removed from the 10-pack in November 2024 and from the 26-pack in April 2025, and quoted Nestle saying the recommended retail price for both packs increased in April 2025; Grocer pack-size metadata and price history can test sachet prices, but not the value of a removed shaker without separate product-content evidence [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).

### Why unit-price visibility matters

Consumer NZ's February 2026 shrinkflation article says its 2025 grocery survey found 78% of respondents were concerned supermarkets were not transparent about shrinkflation and only 12% said it was usually clear, which is a survey finding from a consumer organisation rather than an official regulator statistic [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality).

Consumer NZ's unit-pricing guide, produced in partnership with the Commerce Commission, says unit pricing helps shoppers compare changed packaging, different product sizes, and specials, and says the relevant unit can be per kilogram, litre, item, or 100 sheets depending on product type [Consumer NZ unit-pricing guide, 2025](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/unit-pricing-the-secret-to-saving-at-the-supermarket).

The Commerce Commission's retailer guidance says unit-price regulations require certain grocery retailers to display unit prices clearly and legibly, at no less than 25% of the marked price, in store, online, and in advertised prices, and says physical stores had until 31 August 2024 and online stores until 31 August 2025 to comply [Commerce Commission unit-pricing guidance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/grocery/information-for-retailers-unit-pricing/).

Australia's ACCC reached the same transparency direction in its 2025 supermarket inquiry recommendations: it recommended that supermarkets be required to publish notifications when package-size changes occur in a manner adverse to consumers, which supports treating the NZ examples above as a disclosure problem rather than only a shopper-arithmetic problem [ACCC media release, 2025](https://www.accc.gov.au/media-release/accc-recommends-supermarket-reforms-to-provide-better-outcomes-for-consumers-and-suppliers).

## What would change this conclusion

- A retailer, manufacturer, or Consumer NZ evidence pack with exact old and new shelf tickets, dates, store locations, and product barcodes would improve confidence, because Grocer history rows do not preserve shelf-ticket wording, barcode-level package images, or what a shopper actually saw in store [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).
- A Grocer or retailer data field that retained historical `size` values for the same product ID would change the method, because this pass had to match separate old/new product IDs by brand, name, and Consumer NZ corroboration [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md); [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality).
- A full, permissioned barcode/product-image archive would let a future researcher verify Rebel Bakehouse old 80g wraps, Pams old 500g jams, and other discontinued sizes without guessing from current catalogue search results [Grocer data audit finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md).
- The Purex calculation would need correction if product `178923`'s Grocer `410sheets` size means 410 sheets total rather than 410 sheets per roll; I treated it as per-roll because the product name says 6 Pack and Consumer NZ reports the change as 450 sheets per roll to 410 sheets per roll [Consumer NZ, 2026](https://www.consumer.org.nz/shopping/supermarkets/7-supermarket-products-that-have-shrunk-in-size-or-quality); [Grocer public base catalogue](https://assets-prod.grocer.nz/public/base_v3.duckdb.br).
- I could not verify Grocer's upstream collection method, exact update schedule, or whether a given `price_cent` row is shelf, online, club, or another price representation; this finding is therefore a price-history signal, not a legal or regulatory finding about any retailer's conduct [grocer-nz skill notes](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/.skills/blob/main/skills/grocer-nz/SKILL.md).
