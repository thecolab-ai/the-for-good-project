# Iwi settlement capital funds well-documented social-spending outputs across jobs, procurement, housing, education and marae support, but public NZ evidence rarely measures outcomes and almost never proves causal community impact

## Executive answer

- **Outputs are abundant and well-documented.** Iwi annual reports disclose concrete spending and participation numbers across all five categories asked about: employment commitments and workforce programmes, whānau-business procurement spend, home-ownership and housing-development programmes, education grants/scholarships, and marae development funding. [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Group Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui, *Te Pūrongo ā-tau 2025*](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa, *Annual Report 2024/25*](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf); [Raukawa, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://www.raukawa.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Raukawa-Annual-Report-2025_web.pdf)
- **Outcomes are measured almost exclusively at the aggregate "Māori economy" level, not attributed to individual iwi's settlement capital.** MBIE/BERL's *Te Ōhanga Māori 2023* shows Māori employment growing 16% against 6% population-wide (2018-2023) and the Māori asset base rising from $69b to $126b, but this spans all Māori individuals and businesses — with or without any iwi-entity link — not iwi-settlement-specific activity. [MBIE/BERL, *Te Ōhanga Māori 2023*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/te-ohanga-maori-2023-report.pdf); [RNZ, 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/te-manu-korihi/567090/thriving-maori-economies-revealed-in-new-report)
- **Genuine causal attribution — that a specific iwi's settlement capital *caused* a measured jobs, procurement, housing, education, or marae outcome, as distinct from general economic growth or Crown programmes — is essentially absent from the public record**, and two independent authoritative sources say so explicitly: NZ Treasury's November 2025 analytical note states there is "less empirical evidence" on links between Māori economic/cultural factors and employment outcomes, and a 2025 Ihi Research report for Te Ara Ahunga Ora recommends *future* work to track participants and calculate social return, which would not be a live recommendation if such evaluation already existed. [NZ Treasury, *Mana Āheinga*, AN 25/13, Nov 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/an25-13.pdf); [Ihi Research, *Iwi managed investment/savings schemes*, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Ihi_RC_Report_Iwi-managed-investment_2025_final.pdf)
- **The single closest thing to a causal claim found anywhere in this domain is a forecast, not a measured result, and rests on Crown capital rather than iwi settlement capital.** HUD's evaluation of the $730m Whai Kāinga Whai Oranga iwi-led housing programme forecasts a 7.06:1 social-return ratio, but the evaluators' own sensitivity test shows the ratio collapses to about 1.02–1.16:1 once Māori-specific cultural-value proxies are stripped out — meaning most of the claimed value is attributed to the *iwi-led delivery model*, not to the underlying (Crown-funded) housing investment itself. [Te Paetawhiti Ltd & Associates/HUD, *Evaluation of Whai Kāinga, Whai Oranga Iwi-led Prototypes*, June 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/WKWO/WKWO-report-30-06-25-with-SROI-appendix.pdf)
- **Where a genuine iwi-specific dollar figure exists (e.g. Ngāi Tahu's whānau-business procurement spend, Waikato-Tainui's ex-state-homes purchase), it typically rests on a single self-reported source** — the iwi's own annual report or a single piece of trade media — with no independent audit or second source found.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the *output*-level facts are strongly sourced from primary iwi annual reports and government evaluations; the *absence of causal evidence* is itself well-evidenced (two independent sources state the gap explicitly); but many individual dollar figures rest on a single self-reported source, and I could not access every iwi's most recent reporting.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This finding answers issue #495 by testing, for six named iwi with the most public reporting (Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui, Te Rarawa, Ngāti Awa, Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei, Raukawa, and Tūhoe where evidence existed), whether public sources support **outputs** (spending occurred, a programme exists, participants were counted), **outcomes** (a measured change in wellbeing, employment, housing, or education indicators), or **causal claims** (an evaluation attributing that outcome specifically to iwi settlement capital, as distinct from general economic growth, Crown funding, or unrelated trends) across five categories: jobs, procurement, housing, education grants, and marae support. Research was conducted by four parallel researchers, each covering one category, using primary iwi annual reports (fetched directly and text-extracted where PDFs blocked automated parsing), government evaluations, Stats NZ/MBIE/Treasury/TPK material, and secondary NZ media only where no primary source existed. This finding builds on and does not repeat the commercial-performance detail already in [`iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline.md`](iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline.md) and [`iwi-venture-outcomes.md`](iwi-venture-outcomes.md); it goes deeper on what the "distributions" totals in those findings actually fund and what evidence exists that they changed anything.

### Jobs and employment

**Outputs.** Ngāi Tahu Holdings reports "more than 500 kaimahi (staff)" group-wide. [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf) Waikato-Tainui states an explicit strategic commitment to grow "puutea, tuuranga mahi, and whenua" (profit, jobs, land) and runs Te Rau Mahi, a workforce-development programme under its "Growing our People" pillar, though no numeric placement figures were verifiable in the pages retrieved. [Waikato-Tainui, *Te Rau Mahi*](https://waikatotainui.com/te-rau-mahi/) Te Rarawa's 2021 acquisition of a construction company was explicitly framed around apprenticeships and workforce growth rather than a jobs target. [NZ Herald, 2021](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/far-north-iwi-te-rarawa-buys-construction-company-to-aid-housing-development-trade-training-and-education/2TZSQC5QEXEJ3QCHW2HPYZMVZQ/)

**Outcomes (aggregate, not iwi-specific).** MBIE/BERL's *Te Ōhanga Māori 2023* — the government's periodic Māori-economy statistical series — reports Māori employment grew 16% against 6% for the total population (2018-2023), high-skilled Māori employment rose from 119,100 to 171,100 (the first time the series recorded more high-skilled than low-skilled Māori employees), and Māori self-employment grew 49% against 12% nationally. [MBIE/BERL, *Te Ōhanga Māori 2023*](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/te-ohanga-maori-2023-report.pdf) The same report breaks out that, of a $126b Māori asset base, $41b sits with "Māori trusts, incorporations, and other Māori collectives including post settlement entities" — but this is an asset, not employment, figure, and is not disaggregated by individual iwi. [RNZ, 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/te-manu-korihi/567090/thriving-maori-economies-revealed-in-new-report) A magazine article citing (without a traceable primary source) roughly 21% Māori representation in the fisheries workforce is too thinly sourced to treat as a firm outcome figure. [Te Karaka / Ngāi Tahu, 2023](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/opportunities-and-resources/publications/te-karaka/a-take-without-end/) Te Whata, the iwi-data platform built by Te Kāhui Raraunga for the Data Iwi Leaders Group, publishes per-iwi employment statistics sourced from Stats NZ census affiliation data — but this measures employment among people who *whakapapa* to an iwi, not employment generated by that iwi's commercial entities, so it should not be read as an attribution measure. [Te Whata, Ngāi Tahu Whānui collective](https://tewhata.io/ngai-tahu-whanui-collective/economic/work/employment/)

**Causal claims.** None found linking a specific iwi's settlement capital to a measured employment outcome. NZ Treasury's *Mana Āheinga* analytical note states directly that the empirical evidence connecting Māori cultural/economic participation factors to employment, self-employment and income outcomes is thin, "though the link is plausible." [NZ Treasury, AN 25/13, Nov 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/an25-13.pdf) The same note cites a 2025 Massey University thesis finding Kaupapa Māori firms show no productivity advantage but recruit trades workers more easily — a firm-level, non-iwi-settlement-specific result, referenced only via the Treasury note's summary.

### Procurement

**Outputs.** Amotai — the Māori and Pasifika supplier-diversity intermediary co-founded by Auckland Council in 2018 — reports more than 2,200 registered Māori and Pasifika suppliers and 180+ buyer members by mid-2024, and offers iwi free "buyer" membership, but publishes no consolidated figure for total spend it has facilitated or how many iwi entities have taken up buyer membership. [Auckland Council/OurAuckland, 2024](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2024/08/supplier-diversity-celebrated-with-first-ever-awards-ceremony/); [Amotai, *Buyers*](https://amotai.nz/amotai-buyers) Central government's Progressive Procurement Policy (not iwi-specific, but the enabling environment) lifted Māori businesses' share of government contracts to about 6% by FY2021/22 (target 5%, later raised to 8%), representing roughly 3,200 contracts worth about $930m against $51.5b of annual government procurement. [Beehive, 2023](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-target-increased-keep-powering-m%C4%81ori-economy) The clearest iwi-specific procurement commitment found is Waikato-Tainui's stated policy that "every contract held between [Tainui Group Holdings] and major suppliers and contractors includes a requirement that suitably qualified tribal members are prioritised for jobs and that businesses owned by a tribal member are given consideration for sub-contracts." [Tainui Group Holdings, *Supporting our iwi*](https://www.tgh.co.nz/our-iwi/supporting-our-iwi/) — a policy statement (output), with no dollar figures or contract counts disclosed.

The most quantified iwi-specific procurement data found is Ngāi Tahu's: its whānau-owned business registry, Te Aka Haumi o Tahu, grew from 599 (FY2023) to 652 (FY2024) to 900 (FY2025) registrations, and the FY2025 annual report states whānau-business procurement spend rose $3.8m to $23.1m, including Ngāi Tahu Seafood contracting 50 whānau fishers who caught 100% of the kōura settlement quota. [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Annual Report 2023-2024*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2024-comp.pdf); [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf)

**Outcomes.** Auckland Council's own sustainable-procurement reporting is the clearest local outcome-adjacent figure found anywhere in this research, though it is council spend, not iwi spend: $37.2m directed to Māori- and Pasifika-owned businesses over three years, with 258 jobs created against a 100-job target in FY21 alone. [Auckland Council/OurAuckland, 2021](https://ourauckland.aucklandcouncil.govt.nz/news/2021/09/auckland-council-drives-social-procurement-initiative/) No comparable independently-measured outcome (e.g. verified revenue or employment growth at recipient Māori businesses) was found for any iwi's own procurement spend.

**Causal claims.** None found. The one peer-reviewed paper located on this topic — Hurt-Suwan's 2021 *Kōtuitui* study on social procurement and precarious employment in Auckland construction — is qualitative, stakeholder-perception-based, not iwi-specific, and makes no outcome attribution; it was blocked (HTTP 403) on both direct fetch and WebFetch, so its content here is reported only via its indexed abstract and should be treated as unverified pending a further fetch attempt. [Hurt-Suwan, *Kōtuitui*, 2021 — BLOCKED, unverified beyond abstract](https://rsnz.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1080/1177083X.2020.1767164) No study — BERL, NZIER, TPK, or peer-reviewed — was found attributing measured Māori-business growth or local economic effects specifically to an iwi's own procurement policy, as distinct from general government Progressive Procurement policy.

### Housing

**Outputs.** Ngāi Tahu's Whai Rawa member savings scheme reports 37,295 members, $186.43m funds under management, and $45.4m in total withdrawals since 2006 (spanning home-ownership, education and retirement combined — the annual report does not isolate a housing-only figure). [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf) Its separate Nōhaka Rau shared-equity home-ownership programme (Te Rūnanga takes up to 30% equity, interest-free, 15-year term) had supported 31 whānau into homeownership cumulatively by FY2025 (9 in FY2025 alone). [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf) Te Rarawa's Māhuru development in Kaitāia — 44 planned whānau homes, roughly 70-80% complete as of October 2025, first residents expected early 2026 — is funded by a $33m Ministry of Housing and Urban Development grant (Crown capital) on land Te Rarawa acquired in 2016, built by Te Rarawa's own construction and plumbing subsidiaries; more than 80 whānau registered interest. [Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa, *Annual Report 2024/25*](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf); [Business North, Oct 2025](https://digitalpublications.online/waterfordpress/business-north-october-25/18/) Waikato-Tainui purchased 18 ex-state-housing properties for over $10.385m (right of first refusal) and is reselling them to tribal members at cost to increase homeownership, though uptake numbers were not disclosed. [OneRoof, 2025](https://www.oneroof.co.nz/news/waikato-tainui-snaps-up-10m-of-ex-state-homes-for-iwi-buyers-48709) Separately, Crown-funded (not iwi-capital) housing delivered via iwi partnership is the largest quantified body of data: HUD's $730m Whai Kāinga Whai Oranga programme has committed $247.9m for 568 homes across four iwi-led prototypes, with 179 Code Compliance Certificates issued (31%) and 106 occupied (18%) as of March 2025. [Te Paetawhiti Ltd & Associates/HUD, 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/WKWO/WKWO-report-30-06-25-with-SROI-appendix.pdf) Waikato-Tainui separately received a $35m Crown grant (via the same fund) for 57 affordable rentals (enabling up to 100 total) on Waikato-Tainui land at Hopuhopu. [Beehive, 2024](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/significant-investment-affordable-housing-waikato-tainui)

**Outcomes.** Population-level Māori housing outcomes provide the denominator against which any impact should be judged: the share of Māori living in whānau-owned dwellings fell from 71% in the 1930s to 30.4% in 2023, and Māori make up 30.9% of those severely housing-deprived nationally — with the HUD evaluation itself stating "government policy, programmes and investment have not meaningfully reversed the trend." [Stats NZ, *Te Pā Harakeke: Māori housing and wellbeing 2021*, as cited in the HUD evaluation](https://www.stats.govt.nz/reports/te-pa-harakeke-maori-housing-and-wellbeing-2021/) No iwi-specific programme reviewed above has yet published data connecting its outputs to a measured shift in this population-level trend, and most (Māhuru, Hopuhopu, the WKWO prototypes) had not reached occupancy at the time of the source reports.

**Causal claims.** The HUD Whai Kāinga Whai Oranga evaluation is the single most substantial causal-type claim found in this entire research question. Its Social Return on Investment analysis forecasts $1.403b of social value against $231.4m invested (a 7.06:1 ratio), but a sensitivity test removing financial-value proxies for Māori-specific cultural outcomes (living on ancestral land, proximity to whānau, ahi kā, iwi registration) drops the ratio to as low as 1.02–1.16:1 — the evaluators state plainly that "without the partnership this programme may only create 1:1.02 of social value for whānau, possibly less if the Crown had purchased the land." [Te Paetawhiti Ltd & Associates/HUD, 2025, pp.4, 58-59](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/WKWO/WKWO-report-30-06-25-with-SROI-appendix.pdf) This must be read with three caveats: it is a *forecast* over a 5-6 year window from partial delivery data, not a measured retrospective outcome; it is a HUD-commissioned evaluation of HUD's own programme, using self-acknowledged-limited SROI methodology; and the capital being evaluated is Crown money, with the iwi contribution being land, governance and delivery leadership — not the iwi's own Treaty settlement commercial capital. No independent second source scrutinising this SROI methodology was found.

### Education grants

**Outputs.** Ngāi Tahu's FY2025 Financial Summary itemises its education spend precisely: 722 Learner Support Fund grants ($597,000), 141 special-assessment-fund grants ($113,000), 69 Ka Pūtea tertiary scholarships ($178,000), 613 Ka Pūtea tertiary grants ($281,000), 19 Yamada O'Regan secondary scholarships ($41,000), and 567 school-starter packs. [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Financial Summary 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-FinancialSummaryA4.pdf) None of these line items individually reconcile to the $69m total 2024 distribution figure reported in [`iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline.md`](iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline.md) — that total also includes Whai Rawa contributions, home-ownership support and health/marae funds, confirming that top-line "distribution" figures in iwi annual reports are not primarily education spend. Whai Rawa's own 2024-calendar-year education-specific withdrawal data (103 withdrawals, average $4,429, roughly $456,000 total) comes only from a third-party research report commissioned for a government retirement-policy review, not from Whai Rawa's own annual report, which discloses only combined cumulative withdrawal totals ($43.59m to March 2025). [Ihi Research, *Iwi managed investment/savings schemes*, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Ihi_RC_Report_Iwi-managed-investment_2025_final.pdf); [Whai Rawa, *Annual Report 2025*](https://whairawa.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Whai-Rawa-Annual-Report-2025.pdf) Waikato-Tainui paid out 9,262 grants in FY2024/25 (up 1,717 year-on-year), including 993 tertiary grants worth $1.5m, 45 doctoral scholarships, 183 postgraduate grants, and 7,421 kaumātua grants (mainly GP/pharmacy/dental support). [Waikato-Tainui, *Te Pūrongo ā-tau 2025*](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf) Raukawa's FY2024/25 report gives a precise $100,125 education-grant actual spend (uncapped/demand-driven, no budget ceiling), against $766,792 total distributions. [Raukawa, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://www.raukawa.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Raukawa-Annual-Report-2025_web.pdf) Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei's Ngā Oha Ako tutoring subsidy (up to $1,000/year/student) and its Toi Tupu (Whai Rawa-style) scheme notably lacks any education-withdrawal category at all — a structural difference from Ngāi Tahu's scheme. [Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei, *Ngā Oha grants*](https://ngatiwhatuaorakei.com/toi-tupuroa/nga-oha-grants/) Te Rarawa, Ngāti Awa and Tūhoe publish only combined, TDB-sourced aggregate figures (already in the commercial-baseline finding) with no public recipient counts or category breakdowns found.

**Outcomes.** None found beyond participation counts. Ngāi Tahu's annual report states an aspiration to "uplift education outcomes for whānau Kāi Tahu" but reports no measured attainment change (e.g. NCEA pass rates, qualification completion) tied to its grants.

**Causal claims.** None found. As with jobs, the Ihi Research report itself recommends (rather than reports) "long-term tracking" of participants and an SROI calculation of savings-scheme impact on employment, welfare reliance and homeownership — direct evidence that this evaluation does not yet exist. [Ihi Research, 2025, p.27](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Ihi_RC_Report_Iwi-managed-investment_2025_final.pdf)

### Marae support

**Outputs.** Te Puni Kōkiri's Māori Development Fund ($40.21m/year from July 2025) directly co-funds marae development with post-settlement governance entities: its FY2024/25 annual report describes a "Marae Ora" partnership with the Tūwharetoa Settlement Trust for marae infrastructure assessment and trustee governance/financial-literacy training, and states TPK supported 114 marae nationally on governance and emergency capability that year. [Te Puni Kōkiri, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/documents/download/documents-4929-A/TPKAnnualReport-2025.pdf) Waikato-Tainui made a one-off $20m "Special Marae Grant" in 2020 across all 68 Raupatu marae ($100,000 base plus a per-capita top-up) and runs six ongoing marae-support grant lines, though no consolidated ongoing annual total is published. [Waikato-Tainui, 2020](https://waikatotainui.com/news/20mill-kete-puutea-divied-up-between-raupatu-marae/) Ngāi Tahu's Marae Development Fund approved a single $461,000 grant in FY2025, up from $57,500 in FY2024 (both years had exactly one approved application) — an 8x jump on a single project that is unusual enough to flag as needing a second source before treating as a stable annual pattern. [Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu, *Financial Summary 2024-2025*](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-FinancialSummaryA4.pdf) Raukawa has allocated more than $6.48m to its 16 marae since 2013 ($282,124 in FY2024/25 against a $632,000 budget — with 10 of 16 marae receiving nothing that year, a real distributional gap rather than an even spread). [Raukawa, *Annual Report 2024-2025*](https://www.raukawa.org.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Raukawa-Annual-Report-2025_web.pdf) Nationally, TDB Advisory-sourced reporting puts total post-settlement iwi distributions (education, marae, housing, health, culture, sport and community grants combined, across 10 iwi) at $235m in 2023, up from $138m in 2022 — but with no category-level breakdown. [NZ Herald, 2024](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/companies/agribusiness/iwi-wealth-the-value-of-financial-assets-owned-by-10-big-post-settlement-iwi-unchanged/STHS5T2D4ZBHFLHGH52CPQKBAQ/)

**Outcomes and causal claims.** None found. No source measures marae capacity, usage, or vitality before and after a specific funding round, and no evaluation attributes any such change to iwi settlement capital specifically.

## What would change this conclusion

- A published, methodologically robust evaluation (peer-reviewed, or from BERL/NZIER/Treasury/TPK) that used a comparison group or counterfactual to isolate the effect of a specific iwi's settlement-derived commercial capital — as distinct from Crown grants routed through iwi delivery vehicles, general Māori-economy growth, or regional economic trends — on member employment, business procurement outcomes, housing outcomes, education attainment, or marae vitality would materially change this conclusion. None was found.
- Direct access to the Ihi Research report's recommended future "long-term tracking" and "SROI" work, once it exists, would fill exactly the gap this finding identifies.
- Independent verification of self-reported iwi figures (e.g. Ngāi Tahu's $23.1m procurement-spend claim, Waikato-Tainui's $10.385m ex-state-homes purchase) against audited financial statements or a second independent source would strengthen or revise those specific numbers.
- I could not access several likely-relevant sources due to fetch blocks (HTTP 403) rather than confirmed dead links, and did not exhaust the full fetch ladder in AGENTS.md for all of them within the time available: the Hurt-Suwan *Kōtuitui* peer-reviewed procurement paper, an Education Counts marae-literacy-programme executive summary, and TPK's progressive-procurement buyer case-studies page (404, possibly moved rather than removed). A follow-up researcher should retry these via `node scripts/fetch.mjs` before concluding they are unreachable.
- I could not reconcile Whai Rawa's combined withdrawal totals against the separately-reported education-specific withdrawal figures (from a third-party report, not Whai Rawa's own annual report), nor confirm why the Spinoff's ~$49m first-home-withdrawal figure differs from the FY2025 annual report's $45.4m combined total.
- This finding does not assess whether iwi members themselves consider these programmes successful against their own (often non-monetary, tikanga-based) measures of wellbeing — that requires an iwi-mandated review and Māori data-governance process, not public financial-document analysis.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would a rigorous, iwi-mandated evaluation methodology for measuring social/community outcomes from settlement capital look like, and could it be designed without external econometric imposition or compromising Māori data sovereignty? (Related to the still-open [#497](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/497) in this stream, which asks a related public-reporting-standard question.)
- Now that HUD's Whai Kāinga Whai Oranga homes are approaching occupancy, would a follow-up evaluation (post-occupancy, not forecast) confirm or revise the 7.06:1 / 1.02-1.16:1 SROI range?
- Why does Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei's Whai Rawa-style scheme (Toi Tupu) omit an education-withdrawal category that Ngāi Tahu's equivalent scheme includes, and does this reflect a deliberate governance choice worth understanding across other iwi schemes?
