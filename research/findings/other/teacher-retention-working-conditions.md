# Teacher retention pressure is showing up more as school mobility, workload stress, early-career support gaps and subject/place mismatch than as a high national exit rate

## Executive answer

- New Zealand's latest public workforce data does **not** show a high national exit-rate crisis: the teacher leaving rate was 7.3% in 2024, down from 7.5% in 2023 and the lowest since the series began in 2004 except for the 2020 COVID dip. [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement)
- The bigger school-level churn signal is **mobility**, not exits: regular-teacher turnover was 17.3% in 2024; 8.6% of regular teachers moved school, 3.1% moved to day relief, and 5.6% left teaching, so moving schools accounted for 49.6% of turnover. [Education Counts, teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)
- The strongest visible working-condition signals in public survey evidence are workload, stress and change fatigue: TALIS 2024 reports full-time New Zealand lower-secondary teachers working 47.5 hours a week, 33% experiencing work stress "a lot", and the top stressors being changing requirements, administrative work, and curriculum/programme changes. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html); [Education Counts, TALIS 2024 first report](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report)
- Pay dissatisfaction is real, but the public evidence I found does not rank it against workload/change pressure as a measured exit or mobility driver: 51% of New Zealand teachers were satisfied with salary in TALIS 2024, up 16 percentage points since 2018 and above the OECD average of 39%, while 65% named changing requirements and 56% named administrative work as stressors. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)
- Hard-to-staff vacancies are most consistent with **specialist and place mismatch**: the Ministry says national/regional projections can still miss school-level recruitment difficulty, especially in te reo Māori, science/technology/mathematics, Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium, rumaki/reo rua and priority staffing schools, while PPTA's 2026 principal survey reports suitability, LAT use, non-specialist teaching, and location/housing comments rather than a simple applicant-count problem. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc); [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the movement, turnover and TALIS patterns are current and official, and the secondary vacancy/suitability evidence is a current sector survey; confidence stays Medium because no public dataset I found links individual teachers' reasons for exit or school movement to workload, pay, leadership, housing or outside-job opportunities.

## Evidence

### The national exit rate is low by the Ministry's own series

Education Counts defines the leaving rate as teachers who taught at any point in one year and were not teaching at all in the next, measured as a share of the total teacher workforce. [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement)

On that definition, the leaving rate was 7.3% in 2024, down from 7.5% in 2023; Education Counts describes it as the lowest leaving rate since the series began in 2004 except for the 2020 COVID-19 dip. [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement)

The same indicator report says 7,532 teachers entered teaching between 2024 and 2025 while 5,514 left, increasing the teacher workforce by 2,018 teachers, or 2.7%, from 75,968 in 2024 to 77,986 in 2025. [Education Counts teacher entry and leaving rates indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0018/207063/Teacher-entry-and-leaving-rates-Indicator.pdf)

This does not mean retention is solved: it means the public question should not be framed as "teachers are leaving the profession at unusually high rates" without further evidence. [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement); [Education Counts, teacher turnover, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover)

### School-level turnover is mostly mobility, not exits

Education Counts separates teacher turnover into three movements from a school: leaving teaching, moving to another school, and moving to day relief. [Education Counts, teacher turnover, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover)

In 2024, regular-teacher turnover was 17.3%; 8.6% of regular teachers moved school, 3.1% moved to day relief, and 5.6% left teaching, so the largest component was movement to another school. [Education Counts teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)

Primary schools had a higher 2024 turnover rate than secondary schools, 18.2% compared with 16.0%, and Education Counts attributes the difference mainly to more primary teachers moving schools: 9.7% of regular primary teachers moved to another school, compared with 7.1% of regular secondary teachers. [Education Counts teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)

Younger and older teachers are different risks: teachers under 35 had a 22.6% turnover rate in 2024, teachers aged 35-64 had 14.5%, and teachers aged 65+ had 27.0%; Education Counts says older-teacher turnover is driven by retirement and day relief, while younger-teacher turnover includes higher leaving, day relief movement, fixed-term contracts and early-career school fit. [Education Counts teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)

Schools with students facing more socioeconomic barriers had only slightly higher aggregate turnover in 2024, 18.0% versus 17.3% for moderate-barrier schools and 16.9% for fewer-barrier schools; Education Counts cautions that this pattern may be confounded by school size and sector, and that school-to-school variation is large. [Education Counts teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)

Within Equity Index bands, the higher-turnover pattern is driven mainly by teachers moving schools, not leaving teaching: the "many" and "most" socioeconomic-barrier bands had moving-school rates of 9.6% and 9.4%, while their leaving-teaching rates were 5.7% and 5.8%. [Education Counts teacher turnover indicator, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/207064/Teacher-turnover-rates-Indicator.pdf)

### Workload, stress and change fatigue are the strongest visible working-condition signals

TALIS 2024 reports that New Zealand full-time lower-secondary teachers worked 47.5 hours per week, higher than the OECD average of 41 hours, and spent only 21.5 hours on teaching; it also reports 4.1 hours per week on administrative work, higher than the OECD average of 3.0. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

Education Counts reports the same pattern in its TALIS 2024 first report: full-time Year 7-10 teachers averaged 48 hours per week, full-time Year 1-6 teachers averaged 51 hours per week, and both were among the highest hours internationally. [Education Counts, TALIS 2024 first report](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report)

The most common TALIS stressors for New Zealand lower-secondary teachers were keeping up with changing requirements from authorities (65%), too much administrative work (56%), and keeping up with curriculum or programme changes at school (55%). [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

TALIS also reports that 43% of New Zealand lower-secondary teachers believed too many change initiatives were introduced at their school, 56% wanted a period of stability before new changes, and 40% said they were asked to implement changes without necessary resources. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

Those stress patterns line up with sector evidence: NZEI Te Riu Roa reported from a 2026 primary-teacher survey that 87.5% of respondents said curriculum changes increased workload, 83.2% felt overwhelmed by the pace and extent of change, and 77.6% believed the rollout was rushed and poorly managed. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, primary curriculum-change survey, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/survey-reveals-rushed-curriculum-changes-overwhelming-primary-teachers)

PPTA's 2026 secondary staffing survey adds qualitative principal comments that curriculum and assessment churn, administrative tasks, stress-related sickness and a lack of stability are affecting recruitment and retention, but those comments are not a causal or representative exit survey. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

### Pay matters, but the public evidence does not rank it as a retention driver

The 2025-2027 secondary collective agreement salary scale has trained secondary teachers on $62,862 to $105,686 from 28 January 2026, rising to $64,119 to $107,886 from 28 January 2027. [Ministry of Education circular 2026/01](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/education-circulars/education-circular-202601)

TALIS 2024 reports that 51% of New Zealand lower-secondary teachers were satisfied with salary, higher than the OECD average of 39% and up 16 percentage points since 2018; 73% were satisfied with employment terms excluding salary. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

That is still a weak majority, not a clean bill of health; however, the same TALIS source identifies workload and change demands at higher rates than salary dissatisfaction. Because those are different survey constructs, this supports only an inference that a retention strategy based only on base pay may miss visible stressors, not a measured ranking of exit causes. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

The evidence for subject-specialist alternatives is plausible but weakly quantified. PPTA principals explicitly identify secondary subject-specialist degrees as giving graduates "multiple positive options other than teaching", and the 2026 salary scale tops out below some sector salary benchmarks such as Engineering New Zealand's 2023 average engineering salary of $124,000 and Hays' public guide saying software developers typically earn about $105,000 and senior developers $120,000-$130,000 or more. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); [Engineering New Zealand, 2023 remuneration survey summary](https://www.engineeringnz.org/news-insights/engineer-salaries-increase-challenges-looming/); [Hays New Zealand, software developer jobs salary guide](https://www.hays.net.nz/it/software-developer-jobs)

That comparison does **not** prove teachers are exiting to engineering or software roles; it only supports a low-confidence inference that mathematics, science and technology graduates have outside options that may weaken recruitment and retention into secondary teaching. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655); [Ministry of Education circular 2026/01](https://www.education.govt.nz/our-work/publications/education-circulars/education-circular-202601)

### Leadership looks more like a moderating condition than a nationally measured exit driver

TALIS 2024 does not point to principal relationships as the obvious national weakness: 88% of New Zealand lower-secondary teachers agreed their principal had good professional relationships with staff, 76% said the principal provided useful feedback, and 91% agreed the principal had confidence in teachers' expertise. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

Leadership still matters locally because school fit, support, release time, mentoring and allocation of workload are school-level conditions, but the public data I found does not rank "leadership" against workload, pay, housing or subject alternatives as a measured cause of teacher exits. [Education Counts, teacher turnover, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover); [ERO, Ready, set, teach, 2024](https://www.evidence.ero.govt.nz/documents/ready-set-teach-how-prepared-and-supported-are-new-teachers)

### Early-career support is a real retention risk, especially because early churn is higher

Education Counts' 2019 new-teacher retention paper found that domestic ITE graduates have relatively high retention, with 78% of 2014 starters still employed in 2018 and more than 60% of 2004 starters still employed 15 years later; it also found teachers who leave teaching are more likely to do so in their first five years. [Education Counts, He Whakaaro: new teacher retention, 2019](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/series/he-whakaaro/he-whakaaro-new-teacher-retention-in-new-zealand-schools)

ERO's 2024 report on new teachers found only 29% of new teachers reported being capable in their first term, compared with 87% after two years, and 34% of new teachers were not certain they would stay in teaching for the next five years. [ERO, Ready, set, teach, 2024](https://www.evidence.ero.govt.nz/documents/ready-set-teach-how-prepared-and-supported-are-new-teachers)

ERO also reports that 40% of principals find it difficult to retain new teachers, with mentor teachers and school leaders linking retention difficulty to workload, insufficient support and weak connection in the first two years. [ERO, Ready, set, teach, 2024](https://www.evidence.ero.govt.nz/documents/ready-set-teach-how-prepared-and-supported-are-new-teachers)

TALIS gives a more positive but still incomplete cross-check: 95% of New Zealand teachers who recently joined their current school reported formal or informal induction, 48% of novice teachers had an assigned mentor, and 98% worked in schools offering some mentoring programmes. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html)

The practical read is that induction exists in many schools, but early-career support quality and subject-specific mentoring remain uneven enough to matter for retention, especially in small or hard-to-staff secondary settings. [ERO, Ready, set, teach, 2024](https://www.evidence.ero.govt.nz/documents/ready-set-teach-how-prepared-and-supported-are-new-teachers); [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

### Housing and location are visible school-level barriers but not yet causally quantified

The Ministry's 2025 Teacher Demand and Supply projection says national and regional projections can hide ongoing difficulty for individual schools, particularly in STM, te reo Māori, Kaupapa Māori/Māori-medium, rumaki/reo rua and priority staffing schools. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc)

The Ministry's own policy response recognises place-based recruitment and retention barriers: the Teacher Bonding Scheme offers up to $40,000 before tax over five years for teachers recruited to eligible Priority Staffing Schools, and the scheme aims to reduce recruitment challenges, improve distribution and increase retention in those schools. [Education Workforce, Teacher Bonding Scheme, 2025](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/principals-and-schools/initiatives-schools-and-kura/teacher-bonding-scheme)

Go Rural provides a $4,000 grant for student teachers taking eligible five-week-plus block placements in one of 454 rural or isolated schools, explicitly to reduce financial barriers to experiencing teaching in those settings. [Education Workforce, Go Rural: Isolated Placements Fund, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/go-rural-isolated-placements-fund)

PPTA's 2026 principal comments include teachers moving from Auckland or Wellington for affordable housing, small-town and isolated-area recruitment difficulty, schools supporting transport costs, and school housing with cheap rent helping retention; these comments are useful signals but not a representative housing-causality measure. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

MBIE/Tenancy Services rental-bond data gives context for why housing could matter: a local skill query of MBIE's public rental-bond series returned Auckland Region median rent of $645-$650 per week and Wellington Region median rent of $595-$600 per week over December 2025-February 2026, but that dataset is not linked to teacher movement and cannot show how many teachers leave or avoid those regions because of housing. [MBIE/Tenancy Services rental bond data by region](https://www.tenancy.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Tenancy/Rental-bond-data/detailed-monthly-region-tenancy-v2.csv?m=f5a009ae246bab76553a0965e4933f454b880a4d)

### Hard-to-staff vacancies are driven by suitable specialists and place fit

PPTA's 2026 survey is based on 155 principals, a 30% response rate, with data as at 9 March 2026, and PPTA says it has run similar staffing surveys annually since 1996. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

The survey reported that the average number of suitable applicants for classroom roles was 1.3 New Zealand applicants and 0.9 overseas applicants per job, and principal comments repeatedly distinguish raw applicant volume from suitability for New Zealand secondary, subject and school contexts. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

PPTA also reports that 24% of principals had to cancel or transfer classes because specialist teachers were unavailable, with affected subjects including Maths, Science, Physics, Technology, Samoan language, Te Ao Haka and Te Reo Māori. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)

This aligns with the Ministry's TDS warning that shortages in te reo Māori and science/technology/mathematics cannot be offset by a general surplus elsewhere, because secondary teachers are not interchangeable across subjects. [Teacher Demand and Supply Planning Projection, 2026](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-02/2025-Teacher-Demand-and-Supply-Planning-Projection.pdf?VersionId=9K6lGp0GSfngLdf1wHMslUq5nCKe0HUc)

## What would change this conclusion

- A current Ministry, Teaching Council or union exit survey linking individual teachers' departures and school moves to named reasons would materially change the ranking of drivers, especially pay, leadership and housing. [Education Counts, entering and leaving teaching, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-movement); [Education Counts, teacher turnover, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover)
- A linked teacher-movement dataset by subject, region, school type, Equity Index, tenure, contract type and salary step would test whether mobility is benign career progression or harmful churn for hard-to-staff schools. [Education Counts, teacher turnover, May 2026](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/teacher-turnover)
- A public evaluation of the Teacher Bonding Scheme, Go Rural, EBITE or other place/subject incentives showing strong retention effects would move housing/location and incentive design from plausible factors to stronger causal factors. [Education Workforce, Teacher Bonding Scheme, 2025](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/principals-and-schools/initiatives-schools-and-kura/teacher-bonding-scheme); [Education Workforce, Go Rural: Isolated Placements Fund, 2026](https://workforce.education.govt.nz/becoming-teacher-new-zealand/become-teacher-scholarships/go-rural-isolated-placements-fund)
- I could not verify a public, current, causal estimate of how many teachers leave the profession for non-teaching STEM/private-sector jobs, or how many move schools because of housing costs; those remain plausible mechanisms, not proven drivers. [PPTA secondary staffing survey, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/advice-and-issues/teacher-supply/document/2655)
- TALIS 2024 New Zealand estimates should be used with caution because the OECD country note flags a higher risk of non-response bias, even though Education Counts says its bias analysis indicated the final sample was representative across school categories. [OECD, TALIS 2024 New Zealand country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024-country-notes_e127f9e2-en/new-zealand_0906a779-en.html); [Education Counts, TALIS 2024 first report](https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/publications/schooling2/large-scale-international-assessments/talis-2024-first-report)
- A human education-workforce practitioner should review the weighting before the stream passes G1, because public statistics do not capture the lived difference between healthy mobility, poaching, burnout, leadership failure, and teachers seeking a school that is a better fit. ## Open follow-up questions

- Which schools combine high moving-school turnover, repeated readvertising, subject-specialist vacancies and high Equity Index or isolation status, and are those the same schools targeted by current bonding incentives?
- Do beginning teachers who start on fixed-term roles in hard-to-staff schools move into permanent roles locally, move to other schools, or leave teaching at different rates?
- Which subjects have the clearest wage/opportunity gap between teaching and non-teaching jobs for the same qualification base?
