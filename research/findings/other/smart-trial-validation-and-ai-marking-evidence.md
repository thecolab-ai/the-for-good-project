# The '85-school, 12,000-engagement' SMART trial is traceable to a real item-testing exercise, but no trial or validation report has been published, two OIAs are still pending under lawful extensions, and no public independent AI-marking accuracy evaluation was found

## Executive answer

- **The trial the Government cites is real and now traceable to a primary document, but it was an item-testing exercise, not a published validation/psychometric study.** A 28 November 2025 Ministry of Education report to the Minister (METIS 1354601, proactively released) describes a "SMART Item Trial" running 10–28 November 2025 involving "approximately 12,500 students and their teachers" across schools invited to test items — closely matching the Beehive announcement's "trialled in 85 schools involving around 12,000 student assessment engagements. Feedback from participating schools has been positive." [Ministry of Education, "Report: Assessment and Reporting Tool (SMART) Assessments", 28 Nov 2025](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI); [Beehive, 2 Feb 2026, verified via Wayback snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202053921/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning). This is a reasonable inference from matching dates, scale and "positive feedback" language, not a confirmed identity — the released report never uses the words "85 schools", and the list of participating schools is withheld.
- **No trial report, psychometric validation report, or independent review of the trial has been published.** The same document records that a "trial analysis" was scheduled to complete 5 December 2025 and that the Ministry would "arrange for an independent review of the results to help us ascertain next steps" because of the risk that trial items "perform significantly worse than anticipated" — but no such analysis or independent review is publicly available anywhere in the sources checked for this finding. [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)
- **The specific OIA that would surface this ("Assessment Content Review and Validation Processes", request 34749) is not actually unlawfully overdue — it was validly extended under s15A(1)(b) to 17 July 2026, a date that has not yet passed.** FYI.org.nz's automatic "delayed" badge is based on the original (pre-extension) 18 June 2026 estimate and has not been updated to reflect the extension letter dated 18 June 2026. A second related OIA ("SMART governance and oversight structures", request 34790) was similarly extended, to 3 August 2026. [FYI/MoE, request 34749, extension letter](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34749/response/146949/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048260%20Extension.pdf.html); [FYI/MoE, request 34790, extension letter](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34790/response/147278/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048425%20Extension.pdf.html)
- **A third, related OIA has now been answered (3 July 2026) and directly confirms a transparency gap: this OIA channel has not made Ministry-Janison AI-marking validation material public.** The requester asked for "communications with Janison" discussing "the use of New Zealand student data by Janison for any purpose[,] AI marking and the data used to support it"; the Ministry refused this under section 18(f) (substantial collation/research), saying the information "is not held in a way that allows for easy retrieval by subject" and that manually searching would be "a significant administrative exercise". This does not show whether validation was absent, inadequate, or performed internally; it shows that these records were not released through this request. [FYI/MoE, request 34630, response 3 July 2026](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34630/response/147617/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048013%20Response.pdf.html)
- **I found no public independent technical evaluation, academic study, or governmental audit of SMART's AI-marking accuracy in the Ministry, Beehive, Tāhūrangi, FYI, Janison, NZCER comparator, union, and targeted web-search sources checked for this finding.** The only accuracy claims I found in circulation are the Government's unsourced "positive feedback" line and union reports (NZEI, PPTA) that the AI marks by word count rather than quality; PPTA has publicly called for "an independent review into the purchase and rollout of this tool because... the accuracy of the AI is a concern," which is sector context for the concern but does not prove no review exists. By contrast, NZCER's competing tool PAT Tuhituhi (schools may choose it instead of SMART) has publicly disclosed a comparable validation exercise — a Term 1 2026 trial of "more than 14,000 assessments" and "a month with 32 human markers looking at all of the auto scoring from 17,000 scripts, comparing to separate human marking" — showing this kind of disclosure has precedent in the NZ assessment sector and is not being matched in the SMART sources I could find. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback); [NZCER, "Introducing PAT Tuhituhi"](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/introducing-pat-tuhituhi-pat-writing); [NZCER, "PAT Tuhituhi – your questions answered"](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/pat-tuhituhi-your-questions-answered)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the OIA status facts and the newly located 28 November 2025 Ministry report are primary documents I read directly and can cite precisely; the identification of that report's "Item Trial" with the Beehive-cited "85-school" trial is a well-supported but not certain inference; the absence of public independent AI-marking evidence is a scoped negative finding (I did not find X in the sources checked), which is inherently less certain than a positive one and could be overturned by an unpublished, paywalled, or public-but-missed evaluation.

## Evidence

### What the Government has publicly claimed about the trial

The 2 February 2026 Beehive announcement states the new approach "has been developed following consultation with principals' associations and teachers, and trialling in 85 schools involving around 12,000 student assessment engagements. Feedback from participating schools has been positive." No trial report, methodology note, or independent evaluation is linked from the release. I re-verified this exact quote directly against a Wayback Machine snapshot after the live page returned a bot-challenge to `curl` and `node scripts/fetch.mjs` classified it as tooling-related. [Beehive, 2 Feb 2026, verified via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202053921/https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/assessment-and-reporting-changes-parents-shine-light-learning)

### A primary document that plausibly is the underlying trial

A Ministry of Education report to the Minister of Education, "Report: Assessment and Reporting Tool (SMART) Assessments" (METIS 1354601, dated 28 November 2025, signed by the Minister 12 December 2025), was proactively released and is publicly hosted on education.govt.nz. It is an image-only (scanned/watermarked) PDF with no extractable text layer, so this finding is based on directly reading the rendered page images. [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)

The report describes a "SMART Item Trial" running 10–28 November 2025 to "test the assessment items for both NZC and TMoA to capture teacher and student feedback and to refine the experience before go-live," and states: "In-classroom trials of SMART are currently underway (10–28 November), involving approximately 12,500 students and their teachers. Feedback from these trials will be shared once analysis is complete." It also records: "Early outcomes of the Item Trial has been positive. Feedback from schools and kura have indicated that they have enjoyed participating." [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)

The report's Annex 5, "Schools and Kura invited to Item Trials," is withheld in full under section 9(2)(ba)(i) of the Official Information Act (protecting information given in confidence), so I could not independently count or verify a "85 schools" figure against a school list. [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report, Annex 5](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)

The match between this document and the Beehive claim is close but not exact: "approximately 12,500 students" (28 November 2025, mid-trial) versus "around 12,000 student assessment engagements" (2 February 2026, after the Ministry's own 5 December 2025 "trial analysis completed" milestone had passed) is consistent with a slightly-refined final count, and no other trial of comparable scale in the same period appears in any source reviewed for this or the earlier #640 finding. I treat the identification as a well-supported inference, not a confirmed fact, because the Item Trial report never states "85 schools" and no school-count figure survived redaction.

### What the trial was actually testing — and why the item bank needed it

The same report explains the trial's purpose was psychometric, not a pedagogical or AI-marking accuracy evaluation: it was testing whether newly-developed or repurposed assessment items performed well enough, statistically, to be used at scale. The report states that a "comprehensive psychometric review of existing e-asTTle items" found "fewer than 10% of items were used extensively, leaving the majority underutilised. This lack of robust data meant psychometricians could not apply standard methods to ensure reliability and validity," and that "almost all NZC items being trialled (over 98%) require modification." [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)

Under "Risks," the report states: "The item trial is currently underway. The results of the trial will inform item development and procurement activities that affect the Term 2 assessment in 2026. There is a risk that the trial items perform significantly worse than anticipated, requiring more items to be developed or sourced than currently planned. We will arrange for an independent review of the results to help us ascertain next steps." [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)

No independent review of the trial results, and no "trial analysis" of the kind the report's own timetable scheduled for completion on 5 December 2025, appears in any public source checked for this finding — including the Ministry's proactive-release page, Beehive, Tāhūrangi, and the FYI OIA requests below. This is the central evidence gap the issue asked about: the Ministry's own document shows it planned and promised this analysis, but there is no public trace of it having been released.

The report also records that the 2026 assessment windows include "one week for manual marking of writing/tuhituhi for Years 3–5," alongside AI-assisted marking, which the separate SMART overview page confirms applies across Years 3–8 writing (see below). I could not resolve from public sources whether this means AI marking does not apply to Years 3–5 writing, or whether the manual-marking week instead covers exemplar-based review/confirmation of AI scores for those year levels; this is flagged as unresolved. [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI); [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, verified via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p)

### Chasing the delayed OIA responses — current status as of 8 July 2026

Three FYI.org.nz OIA requests target exactly this gap. I fetched each directly (via `node scripts/fetch.mjs`, since raw `curl`/plain HTTP to fyi.org.nz timed out repeatedly in this environment — a tooling issue, not a dead site) and read the full correspondence and event history, not just the summary badge.

**Request 34749, "Assessment Content Review and Validation Processes."** This asks the Ministry to describe how SMART assessment content was developed, reviewed, validated and approved, including whether psychometricians, curriculum specialists and other experts were involved, and whether pilot testing, trialling, calibration or validation occurred before the 2025 trial phase or 2026 rollout — precisely the question this issue was opened to chase. The Ministry's original 20-working-day deadline was 19 June 2026 (later corrected to 18 June 2026 in its extension letter). On 18 June 2026, the Ministry extended the deadline under section 15A(1)(b) of the Official Information Act (consultations needed) to **17 July 2026**. FYI.org.nz's page still displays a "delayed" / "icon_waiting_response_overdue" badge because that badge is calculated against the original 18 June 2026 estimate and does not appear to account for the logged extension. As of the date of this finding (8 July 2026), the request is **not** unlawfully overdue — it remains within its lawfully extended window, which has 9 days left to run. [FYI/MoE, request 34749](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34749-assessment-content-review-and-validation-processes); [FYI/MoE, request 34749, extension letter text](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34749/response/146949/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048260%20Extension.pdf.html)

**Request 34790, "SMART governance and oversight structures."** Similarly extended, on 25 June 2026, under section 15A(1)(b), to **3 August 2026** (an extension of 25 working days). This is also not yet overdue. [FYI/MoE, request 34790](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34790-smart-governance-and-oversight-structures); [FYI/MoE, request 34790, extension letter text](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34790/response/147278/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048425%20Extension.pdf.html)

**Request 34630, "SMART tool data use."** This one has now been substantively answered, on 3 July 2026 — after the 7 July 2026 access date of the earlier #640 finding, so it had not yet surfaced in this project's record. The response is analysed in detail in the next two sections. [FYI/MoE, request 34630, response](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34630/response/147617/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048013%20Response.pdf.html)

### What the newly-answered OIA (34630) reveals about AI-marking transparency

The requester's part 3(a) asked for "all emails and written communication between Ministry staff and Janison Solutions Pty Ltd" discussing "the use of New Zealand student data by Janison for any purpose[,] AI marking and the data used to support it." The Ministry's 3 July 2026 response refuses this in full: "attempting to locate and collate all emails and written communication between Ministry staff and Janison Solutions Pty Ltd in relation to the use of SMART data over this timeframe would be a significant administrative exercise to undertake and would detract from the team's ability to carry out its core functions. The information requested in this part of your request is not held in a way that allows for easy retrieval by subject and identifying, assessing and collating all relevant material would require a substantial manual search across a very large volume of records identified. I am therefore refusing this part of your request under section 18(f)." [FYI/MoE, request 34630, response](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34630/response/147617/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048013%20Response.pdf.html)

The same response confirms there has been no proposal or expansion to use SMART for Years 9–10 school performance reporting to date (refused under section 18(e) as "the information does not exist"), and refuses (also under s18(f)) a separate request for internal Ministry senior-leadership emails about SMART data use, because an initial search "produced thousands of results." It does release, in part, three ministerial-level documents about SMART data use (one already public — the March 2026 Cabinet pack this project has already reviewed — one partially released with privacy and free-and-frank-advice redactions, and one withheld in full under section 9(2)(f)(iv)). None of the released material concerns AI-marking accuracy specifically; it concerns data governance and Cabinet process. [FYI/MoE, request 34630, response](https://fyi.org.nz/request/34630/response/147617/attach/html/4/GEMS%2048013%20Response.pdf.html); [Ministry Cabinet pack, 2026 (already cited in #640)](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/Cabinet%20Pack%20-%20Establishing%20a%20new%20standardised%20tool_Redacted_0.pdf?VersionId=h4gLPqajurvR8Cjn1c0RZxcJN3VJHYg6)

The practical effect for this issue: even where a requester has directly and specifically asked for the Ministry–Janison correspondence on AI marking, the Ministry has not released it, on the grounds that it cannot be efficiently retrieved — not on the grounds that no relevant records exist. This leaves open whether internal review of AI-marking accuracy exists at all, but confirms it has not been made public through this OIA channel.

### Independent evidence on the AI marking's accuracy

I found no independent (i.e., non-Ministry, non-vendor, non-union) technical evaluation, academic study, or governmental audit of SMART's AI-marking accuracy in the public sources checked for this finding: Ministry proactive releases and SMART pages, Beehive material, Tāhūrangi, the three FYI OIA threads reviewed here, Janison's public SMART/marking material surfaced by search, NZCER comparator material, union statements, and targeted web searches for SMART/Janison/AI-marking accuracy.

The only detailed accuracy claims I found come from unions, who are advocacy bodies, not technical evaluators, though their claims are specific enough to be checkable in principle. NZEI Te Riu Roa's Principals' Council chair said "some teachers have reported that the AI failed to evaluate the quality of writing, instead giving identical marks to completely different student scripts based merely on word count" — I re-verified this exact quote directly against the live NZEI page. [NZEI Te Riu Roa, 2026](https://www.nzeiteriuroa.org.nz/about-us/media-releases/parents-urged-to-check-school-use-of-inaccurate-government-ai-tool) PPTA reported that "the AI in the SMART writing tool seemed to mark students higher based on how much they wrote, as opposed to the quality of the writing," that "several students received the same mark for their writing assessment" despite "significant differences" in script quality, and called for "an independent review into the purchase and rollout of this tool because it currently isn't fit for use in secondary schools, the accuracy of the AI is a concern and the implementation and change management processes were woeful" — I re-verified this quote directly against the live PPTA page. [PPTA, 2026](https://www.ppta.org.nz/news-and-media/smart-tool-not-ready-creates-more-concerns-than-solutions-deputy-principals-feedback) I treat PPTA's call as sector context for why an independent review matters, not as proof that no independent review exists.

The Ministry's official position, stated on the Tāhūrangi SMART overview page (re-verified via Wayback Machine snapshot after the live page returned a bot-challenge), is: "Does SMART use Artificial Intelligence (AI)? Yes, SMART uses AI. Teachers and kaiako are provided with AI assistance for marking of open-ended questions. SMART is not intended to replace teachers' professional judgement or subjective feedback." The same page confirms year-level writing exemplars exist for Years 3–8, each showing "writing at each score band (1–6) with accompanying feedback," which "teachers can use... to mark pieces of writing that SMART cannot (for example, pieces shorter than 100 words), or to confirm or change the AI-assisted scoring for students' writing." [Tāhūrangi SMART overview, verified via Wayback](https://web.archive.org/web/20260706080321/https://newzealandcurriculum.tahurangi.education.govt.nz/smart-overview-and-access/5637300577.p) This describes a workflow with human review built in, but it is not itself evidence of the AI's accuracy — no error rate, inter-rater agreement statistic, or validation methodology is disclosed on this page or anywhere else I could find.

I searched specifically for a Janison technical whitepaper or published accuracy/reliability documentation for its AI marking product; none was found. Janison's public marketing material describes generic quality-control features ("standardisation, blind quality monitoring and double marking") without SMART- or New-Zealand-specific accuracy data. [Janison, "Online exam marking"](https://www.janison.com/online-assessment-software/marking/)

**A useful comparator exists in the same sector, which sharpens the concern rather than resolving it.** Schools choosing an assessment tool for 2026 can use SMART, e-asTTle, or NZCER's PATs — including PAT Tuhituhi, a competing AI-marked writing assessment. NZCER has publicly disclosed, on its own website, a validation exercise for PAT Tuhituhi's automated scoring: "We completed a trial in Term 1, collecting and marking more than 14,000 assessments, whilst developing new reporting formats," and, in a separate Q&A: "we also spent a month with 32 human markers looking at all of the auto scoring from 17,000 scripts, comparing to separate human marking as well. This is where we began our work for norms and where students sit." [NZCER, "Introducing PAT Tuhituhi"](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/introducing-pat-tuhituhi-pat-writing); [NZCER, "PAT Tuhituhi – your questions answered"](https://www.nzcer.org.nz/news-and-blogs/pat-tuhituhi-your-questions-answered) This is not a peer-reviewed or third-party-audited study either — it is self-disclosed by the tool's own developer, and NZCER did not publish precise agreement/accuracy statistics — but it demonstrates that a comparable NZ organisation disclosed its methodology and sample size at all, which SMART/Janison and the Ministry have not done publicly for SMART.

I also found the Ministry's general (non-SMART-specific) guidance on AI use in marking, published October 2025, which cautions: "Use of AI tools to mark complex content or summative assessment requires caution and alignment with school policy," and separately, "the use of AI tools to support marking of NCEA assessments is discouraged" for internal assessments, because "the responsibility for this claim lies with the assessor." [Ministry of Education, "Marking work with AI tools", October 2025](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-10/1373%20GenAI%20Marking%20Tools%20flyer_V2.pdf?VersionId=Oz_o7IGHBcO7x7DAX6SXlIT2JF60QBmv) This flyer does not mention SMART and is guidance for teachers' own use of general-purpose AI tools, not a statement about SMART's built-in scoring engine, so I do not treat it as describing the same thing — but it documents that the Ministry's own general policy position, in the same period SMART was rolling out, treats AI marking of "complex" and "summative" student work as requiring particular caution. NZ Herald reported this guidance caused confusion among educators; University of Canterbury AI-in-education academic Kathryn MacCallum — an independent, non-union voice — said the guidance "raises more questions than it answers," with unclear specifics about "which tools, models, and validation processes apply." That article does not mention SMART either. [NZ Herald, 16 Oct 2025](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/ministry-revises-ai-marking-rules-as-teachers-warn-of-confusion/E6AHUPGGGJHPTJ2S4GOT5RCGVA/)

I found no evidence of an Ombudsman inquiry, Privacy Commissioner review, or academic audit specifically addressing SMART's AI marking.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published trial analysis or independent review of the November 2025 SMART Item Trial** — the Ministry's own 28 November 2025 report commits to exactly this, on a 5 December 2025 internal timeline, but none is public. Its release (proactively, or via OIA 34749 once it responds by 17 July 2026) would directly resolve most of this issue's blocking unknown. [Ministry of Education, 28 Nov 2025 report](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2026-03/R%201354601%20-%20Assessment%20and%20Reporting%20Tool%20%28SMART%29%20Assessments_Redacted.pdf?VersionId=zSrg4fzU1fzpet7Z9jPh8MxSCpzzwdpI)
- **The Ministry's substantive response to OIA 34749** (due 17 July 2026) and **OIA 34790** (due 3 August 2026) — both are still within their lawfully extended windows as of this finding's access date and should be re-checked after those dates.
- **A published school count or list for the trial** would confirm or refute the "85 schools" identification against the Item Trial described here; Annex 5 of the source document is currently withheld in full.
- **An independent technical audit of SMART's AI-marking reliability** (inter-rater agreement against expert human markers, error rate by script length, bias analysis) — comparable to what NZCER has disclosed for PAT Tuhituhi — would directly answer the accuracy question this issue asks about. None is published for SMART.
- **Release, even in redacted form, of Ministry–Janison correspondence specifically on AI marking** — currently refused under section 18(f) in the 34630 response — would show whether the Ministry has itself reviewed AI-marking accuracy internally, even if that review has not been made public.
- I could not verify how large the "very large volume of records" refused under section 18(f) actually is, or whether a narrower, more specific OIA request (e.g., scoped to a single named workstream or a shorter date range) would succeed where this broader one did not.
- This finding needs review from someone with OIA/administrative-law expertise on whether FYI.org.nz's "delayed" badge accurately reflects section 15A extension practice more generally (this finding checked three requests only), and from an assessment/psychometrics specialist on whether the "Item Trial" described here is methodologically capable of supporting AI-marking accuracy claims at all, even if its results were released.
