# NZ Super reform evidence is strongest for age, indexation, income-testing, NZSF, and KiwiSaver shifts, but only some options have public fiscal estimates

## Executive answer

- **Strongest public fiscal evidence:** changing NZ Super indexation, raising the eligibility age, income-testing/abatement, NZ Super Fund pre-funding, and compulsory or stronger KiwiSaver-style private saving have credible public modelling from Treasury or the Retirement Commission, but the models answer different questions and should not be ranked as one simple savings table [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Treasury WP 13/28](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2013-11/twp13-28.pdf); [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- **Largest modelled fiscal lever in current Treasury work:** CPI-only indexation can broadly stabilise NZS as a share of GDP in Treasury's 2025 Long-term Fiscal Statement, but it has the harshest adequacy risk because mostly-NZS pensioners would fall behind wider living standards; Treasury's TAWA note in the LTFS says CPI-only indexation without a wage floor from 2010 would have put 26% of people aged over 65 below 50% of median incomes, versus 6% under current settings [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).
- **Age rises save money but do not fully solve the fiscal path:** Treasury's 2025 scenario raising the age to 68 by 2040 still leaves NZS rising from 5.6% of GDP in 2025 to 7.0% in 2065, and Treasury says holding NZS flat as a GDP share by age alone would require the age to reach 72 by 2065; older Treasury advice estimated a move to 67 from 2037 would save $4.4b gross, or $3.89b after Jobseeker/Supported Living Payment offsets, by 2042 [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf).
- **Targeting options are real but under-specified:** Treasury's 2025 LTFS models means-testing as a 40 cent abatement above either $60,000 or $10,000 of non-NZS income and says comparable savings require thresholds at relatively low non-NZS income levels; the Retirement Commission says an income test would reduce expenditure but could not be quantified without a chosen threshold and coverage rule [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).
- **Residence-rule tightening is already partly enacted and smaller than age/indexation:** Treasury's 2017 modelling estimated a 20-year residence requirement would save $354.3m gross, or less after benefit offsets, in 2050; Work and Income says the residence test is now already phasing from 10 to 20 years depending on date of birth [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf); [Work and Income NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The conclusion rests on current Treasury and Retirement Commission sources, but several options do not have public, current, threshold-specific fiscal modelling, and the distributional evidence is often qualitative or model-limited rather than a full poverty, ethnicity, disability, housing-tenure, and occupation analysis.

## Evidence

This finding answers which NZ Super reform options have credible public evidence on both fiscal savings and distributional effects, not which option should be adopted. It treats "credible public evidence" as official or peer-reviewed/public-policy work that gives at least one fiscal estimate, modelled pathway, or explicit reason why the estimate cannot be made without more policy design, plus some evidence on who gains or loses [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).

### Raising the age

Raising the age has credible fiscal evidence, but it is weaker as a standalone "solve NZS" answer than common debate implies. Treasury's 2025 LTFS models raising the eligibility age to 68 by 2040 and still projects NZS rising from 5.6% of GDP in 2025 to 7.0% in 2065 under that scenario; Treasury says age-only stabilisation would require the age to rise to 72 by 2065 and keep climbing thereafter [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). A released 2017 Treasury report estimated that raising the age to 67 from 2037 would save about $4.4b gross by 2042, reduce recipients by 108,000, and fall to about $3.89b before tax after $511m of additional Jobseeker Support and Supported Living Payment costs [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf). The same 2017 report says implementing the change from 2027 instead of 2037 would provide an additional $36b in fiscal savings, which is a load-bearing estimate but from older advice [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf).

The distributional evidence is consistent that an age rise is hardest on people with lower capacity to work longer. Treasury 2025 says people close to retirement lose lifetime consumption under an age rise because they receive NZS for fewer years and have limited time to benefit from lower future taxes [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). Treasury 2017 says an age rise would disproportionately affect people in physically demanding industries and people with poor prospects for older years, and it raises the possibility of a means-tested transitional benefit for vulnerable people aged 65 to 67 [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf). The Retirement Commission's NZ Super issues paper concludes that any age change would disproportionately disadvantage manual workers, carers and those they care for, and people with poor health, with women, Māori, and Pacific Peoples overrepresented in those groups [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).

### Indexation

Indexation has the strongest savings evidence and the clearest adequacy warning. Treasury's 2025 LTFS says CPI-only indexation would keep NZS broadly flat as a share of GDP, because expenditure would grow more slowly than the economy [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). Treasury WP 13/28, using an older mixed CPI/wage indexation design rather than pure CPI-only indexation, estimated cumulative national-savings impacts by 2061 of about 51% of GDP if government keeps its fiscal position unchanged and about 87% of GDP if government saves the fiscal gains [Treasury WP 13/28](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2013-11/twp13-28.pdf).

The same evidence makes indexation the highest-poverty-risk option. Treasury 2025 says CPI indexation would make older New Zealanders worse off, especially people near or in retirement, and would gradually lower the relative incomes of pensioners mostly reliant on NZS [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). Treasury's LTFS footnote reports TAWA microsimulation showing that if CPI indexation without a wage floor had been introduced in 2010, 26% of people aged over 65 would be living below 50% of median incomes, compared with 6% now [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). That is a decisive distributional warning because NZ Super is the main anti-poverty floor for many older people; the Retirement Commission's issues paper says NZ Super is the sole income for 40% of retirees and another 20% have only a little more, citing Perry's household-income work [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).

### Income test, surcharge, abatement, and asset-related targeting

Income-testing and surcharge-style clawbacks have credible directional evidence but not a current public dollar estimate without choosing the abatement threshold. Treasury 2025 models means-testing using a 40 cent abatement for each dollar of non-NZS income over either $60,000 or $10,000, and says the threshold would need to be relatively low to generate savings comparable with age or indexation changes [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). The Retirement Commission says income-testing would reduce expenditure, but it explicitly says the reduction cannot be quantified without deciding the income level and whether the test applies to all superannuitants or only a selected age group [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).

The distributional case for income-testing is stronger than the fiscal precision. Treasury says means-testing would preserve NZS's role in poverty prevention and support people unable to work or accrue assets, but could discourage saving or continued work if extra income abates NZS [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). The Retirement Commission says a tax surcharge or clawback existed from 1985 to 1998, may have merit in a broader retirement-policy discussion, and could be designed to have little or no effect on most superannuitants while clawing back from high extra-income recipients, but it does not provide a current fiscal estimate [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf). Asset-testing or estate recovery is even thinner: the Retirement Commission canvasses an estate-repayment loan model whose savings depend on the chosen wealth threshold, and Treasury WP 13/28 models abatement only against compulsory private-saving accumulations, not general housing or financial wealth [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf); [Treasury WP 13/28](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2013-11/twp13-28.pdf).

### Residence rules

Residence-rule tightening has public fiscal estimates, but they are small relative to the main age and indexation levers and are now partly baked into current law. Treasury 2017 estimated that a 15-year residence rule with five years after age 50 would save $67.13m gross in 2050, while a 20-year rule would save $354.30m gross in 2050 [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf). Treasury's 2017 modelling also estimated that by 2050 the 20-year rule would reduce NZS recipients by about 6,604 and increase Jobseeker/Supported Living Payment recipients by about 3,556, which means net savings are lower than gross NZS savings [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf). Work and Income's current eligibility page says the residence requirement is now already phasing by date of birth from 10 years to 20 years from age 20, with five years required from age 50 [Work and Income NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html).

The distributional issue is concentrated on migrants and people with mixed residence histories, not the broad retiree population. Treasury 2017 says the fiscal impact depends on reciprocal social-security agreements and on the share of people who would move to another social-security benefit if they failed the residence test [Treasury NZ Superannuation Policy Options, 2017](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2017-03/sup-3753736.pdf). Work and Income separately says overseas pensions can affect NZ Super payments, so residence-rule changes interact with the direct-deduction system rather than simply excluding all overseas-connected retirees [Work and Income NZ Super eligibility](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html).

### NZSF pre-funding

Increasing NZSF pre-funding is fiscally credible as a tax-smoothing tool, not as an entitlement cut. Treasury's BEFU 2026 model page says the workbook calculates the contribution rate for pre-funding NZS under section 43 of the New Zealand Superannuation and Retirement Income Act 2001, and contains NZS data for gross and net expenditure and recipient numbers [Treasury NZSF contribution-rate model BEFU 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026). Treasury's model guide explains that the formula uses net NZS expenditure because superannuitants pay tax on NZS, making net cost the taxpayer cost [Treasury NZSF Model Guide 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026). Treasury's 2025 LTFS says current-formula NZSF withdrawals after 2055 would only shave about half a percentage point of GDP off NZS expenditure by 2065, and that withdrawals would be around 6% of gross NZS payments, or 7.5% of net costs, by 2065 [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf).

The distributional tradeoff is intergenerational. Treasury says bigger NZSF contributions would require successive governments to run operating surpluses on average, which means current generations pay more tax or receive fewer public services and transfers so future taxpayers face lower NZS funding pressure [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf). This makes expanded pre-funding a financing choice rather than a direct adequacy change for current pensioners [Treasury NZSF Model Guide 2026](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/new-zealand-superannuation-fund-contribution-rate-model-befu-2026).

### KiwiSaver shift

Shifting more retirement adequacy onto KiwiSaver has credible long-run savings evidence only when paired with compulsion and NZS abatement. Treasury WP 13/28 models compulsory private saving where accumulations reduce NZS entitlements and estimates cumulative national-savings impacts of about 30% of GDP by 2061 if government keeps its fiscal position unchanged, or about 38% of GDP if government saves the fiscal gains [Treasury WP 13/28](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2013-11/twp13-28.pdf). The same paper says this option's total fiscal effect does not become positive until 2036 and does not overtake raising the age until 2057, so it is not a quick fiscal fix [Treasury WP 13/28](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2013-11/twp13-28.pdf).

The distributional evidence warns against treating KiwiSaver as an equal substitute for NZS. The Retirement Commission's 2025 review says KiwiSaver is voluntary and workplace-based, and that groups including the self-employed, women with caregiving gaps, Māori and Pacific Peoples can face more complex retirement needs [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). A MartinJenkins report for the 2025 review says men's KiwiSaver balances are on average 25% higher than women's, and a Retirement Commission/Hnry report says 44% of self-employed survey participants were contributing KiwiSaver members in 2024/25 compared with 78% of employees [MartinJenkins, Improving women's retirement income, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/MJ-Women-and-retirement-incomes-30062025.pdf); [Retirement Commission/Hnry, self-employed savings, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Improving-the-retirement-savings-of-the-self-employed_Final.pdf). The Retirement Commission's 2025 review therefore recommends a 10-year roadmap that addresses KiwiSaver, NZ Super, and private savings together, including contribution rates, compulsion, suspension rules, early access, income testing, and adequacy for renters, rather than treating KiwiSaver as a standalone replacement [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current Treasury or MSD costing of a specific income-test, surcharge, asset-test, estate-recovery, or partial-taper design would change the confidence ranking for targeting options, because the current public evidence often stops at "depends on threshold" [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).
- A full distributional model by ethnicity, gender, disability, occupation, housing tenure, wealth, and health status would change the fairness assessment, because Treasury's OLG model does not illuminate all population groups and the Retirement Commission often gives qualitative or illustrative distributional evidence [Treasury LTFS 2025](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-09/ltfs-2025.pdf); [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- New Budget, PREFU/HYEFU, or party-manifesto settings after 8 July 2026 could change the policy baseline for the age, indexation, residence, NZSF, and KiwiSaver options [Treasury forecasts page](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/budgets/forecasts).
- I could not verify a current public NZ estimate for a revived surcharge's revenue under a specified threshold, a general asset test, or an estate-recovery threshold; those should be treated as policy leads rather than costed options until a design is specified [Retirement Commission NZ Super Issues and Options](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Policy/TAAO-RRIP-NZ-Super-issues-paper.pdf).
- This finding used public sources only and did not include interviews with older people, Māori and Pacific organisations, disability advocates, unions, employers, MSD, Treasury, Inland Revenue, or the Retirement Commission; those perspectives are needed before judging political durability or lived-experience fairness [Retirement Commission 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## Open follow-up questions

- What would a concrete NZ Super income-test or surcharge design raise at thresholds such as $60,000, $80,000, $100,000, or the average wage, and what effective marginal tax rates would it create?
- How would age, indexation, and income-test options affect older renters after Accommodation Supplement, disability support, and health costs are included?
- What would a Treaty-consistent distributional assessment of NZ Super reform require, given Māori age structure, life expectancy, housing, wealth, and whānau support patterns?
