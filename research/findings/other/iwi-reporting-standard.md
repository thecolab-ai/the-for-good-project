# A comparable iwi economic-development reporting standard should publish minimum stewardship metrics while leaving outcome data under iwi governance

## Executive answer

- The best public standard is a **two-layer annual reporting protocol**: a common public stewardship table for financial scale, risk, distributions, assurance and data provenance, plus an iwi-governed outcome section where each iwi chooses purpose-aligned indicators and decides what is public, member-only, partner-only or confidential. This follows the existing split between financial reporting and service-performance reporting in charity/XRB practice, where registered charities file annual returns with either performance reports or financial statements, and Tier 1/2 charities report service performance information alongside financial statements. [Charities Services annual return guidance](https://www.charities.govt.nz/im-a-registered-charity/annual-returns/updated-annual-return); [Charities Services service performance reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-1-and-tier-2/service-performance-reporting)
- The **minimum comparable public metrics** should be entity-level and aggregated: reporting entity and consolidation basis; reporting period; assurance status; total assets, net assets/equity, liabilities and net debt/gearing; revenue/income, surplus/deficit and comprehensive income; asset allocation by broad class; distributions and reinvestment; distribution rate against net assets; major concentration risks; related-party/control disclosures; and a short statement of investment mandate, distribution policy and material changes. These are observable in current iwi annual reports and comparable investment summaries, while venture-level returns and member-level outcomes are not consistently public. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [TDB Advisory iwi investment report 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)
- The standard should **not require a universal ROI league table, venture register, whānau outcomes database, or fixed wellbeing KPI set**. Māori data-sovereignty sources treat Māori data as a taonga, say Māori data should be subject to Māori governance, and emphasise Māori authority over collection, access, use, analysis and sharing. [Te Mana Raraunga Charter](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/58e9b10f9de4bb8d1fb5ebbc/t/5913020d15cf7dde1df34482/1494417935052/Te%2BMana%2BRaraunga%2BCharter%2B%28Final%2B%26%2BApproved%29.pdf); [Te Kāhui Raraunga Māori Data Governance Model](https://www.kahuiraraunga.io/maoridatagovernance)
- Public iwi denominators need a data-quality note: Stats NZ rates 2023 Census iwi-affiliation data as **moderate quality**, says only 73.2 percent came directly from 2023 Census responses, and warns that iwi counts rose for several reasons including collection changes, alternative data sources, better Māori response rates, multiple affiliations and awareness of whakapapa. [Stats NZ DataInfo+ iwi affiliation quality statement](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/303501b1-6e9b-4cc2-b46b-338183672ff5)
- A reviewer can compare **stewardship and transparency**, not whether one iwi is "better" at rangatiratanga than another. The public comparison should ask whether an iwi has preserved and grown collective capital, managed risk, explained distributions and disclosed data limits; any judgement about whether outcomes are tika for that iwi needs iwi mandate and Māori data-governance review. [Te Whata about page](https://tewhata.io/about/); [CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance](https://www.rd-alliance.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/CARE20Principles20for20Indigenous20Data20Governance_OnePagers_FINAL20Sept2006202019.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium -- the proposed minimum metrics are well supported by current reporting practice, charity/company rules, Stats NZ quality guidance and Māori data-sovereignty sources, but it remains a desk-research proposal that needs iwi governors, iwi data specialists, Māori commercial lawyers and members to test before adoption.

## Evidence

### Scope and answer

This finding answers issue #497 by specifying a public reporting standard a reviewer could use to compare iwi economic-development performance without requiring iwi to surrender control over purpose, data definitions, member data or commercially sensitive investment records. It builds on sibling findings and public reports showing that annual reports already support comparisons of assets, debt, distributions, asset allocation and selected venture outcomes, but do not provide a complete venture-level or whānau-outcome dataset. [iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline finding](iwi-settlement-commercial-baseline.md); [iwi-venture-outcomes finding](iwi-venture-outcomes.md); [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

The standard should apply to public annual reports by post-settlement governance entities, rūnanga, trusts and commercial arms, but it should not override each iwi's constitution, trust deed, member-accountability process, commercial confidentiality or tikanga-based reporting obligations. Te Tari Whakatau's Red Book says settlement governance entities are expected to be representative, transparent and accountable before settlement assets transfer, while Te Mana Raraunga says Māori data sovereignty supports tribal sovereignty and the realisation of Māori and iwi aspirations. [Te Tari Whakatau Red Book 2025](https://www.whakatau.govt.nz/assets/Treaty-Settlements/The-Red-Book/MOJ0152_OTS_red-book_Aug25_FINAL_WEB.pdf); [Te Mana Raraunga Charter](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/58e9b10f9de4bb8d1fb5ebbc/t/5913020d15cf7dde1df34482/1494417935052/Te%2BMana%2BRaraunga%2BCharter%2B%28Final%2B%26%2BApproved%29.pdf)

### What existing reporting rules suggest

Charity reporting provides the closest local template for combining financial stewardship with purpose reporting: Charities Services says all registered charities must file an annual return within six months of balance date, and the return is submitted with either a Tier 3/4 Performance Report or Tier 1/2 financial statements. [Charities Services annual return guidance](https://www.charities.govt.nz/im-a-registered-charity/annual-returns/updated-annual-return)

Charities Services defines a Performance Report as a summary of activities and finances that includes purpose, key activities, income, expenses, assets, liabilities and explanatory notes, and Tier 4 guidance says small cash-basis charities must report what they did during the year and cash received and paid. [Charities Services annual return guidance](https://www.charities.govt.nz/im-a-registered-charity/annual-returns/updated-annual-return); [Charities Services Tier 4 reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-4)

For larger charities, Charities Services says all Tier 1 and Tier 2 charities must report service performance information with financial statements, and that the PBE FRS 48 standard is principles-based rather than a fixed output/outcome list. [Charities Services service performance reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-1-and-tier-2/service-performance-reporting)

That matters for iwi reporting because it supports a minimum common structure without forcing a single theory of success: the public standard can require the same headings and definitions for stewardship metrics, while the service-performance/outcome section remains purpose-led and iwi-defined. [Charities Services service performance reporting](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/tier-1-and-tier-2/service-performance-reporting); [Te Kāhui Raraunga Māori Data Governance Model](https://www.kahuiraraunga.io/maoridatagovernance)

Assurance rules also suggest a proportionate approach: Charities Services says charities with total operating expenditure over $550,000 in each of the previous two years must have a review or audit, and those over $1.1 million must have an audit. [Charities Services audit and review requirements](https://www.charities.govt.nz/reporting-standards/statutory-audit-and-review-requirements)

Company reporting is weaker as a public comparability base because Companies Office annual returns are not financial statements and do not ask for financial information, while only some large companies, large overseas companies and FMC reporting entities must file audited financial statements. [Companies Register annual-return guidance](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/company-annual-returns/why-annual-returns-matter/); [Companies Register financial-reporting guidance](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/financial-reporting/who-needs-to-submit-financial-statements/)

Companies Office guidance says a New Zealand company with at least 25 percent overseas voting ownership is large if, for each of the two preceding accounting periods, consolidated assets exceeded NZ$66 million or consolidated revenue exceeded NZ$33 million, while a New Zealand subsidiary of an overseas body corporate is large at lower NZ$22 million asset or NZ$11 million revenue thresholds. [Companies Register financial-reporting guidance](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/financial-reporting/who-needs-to-submit-financial-statements/)

### Annual-report practice: what is already comparable

Current iwi reporting already makes some common public metrics feasible. Ngāi Tahu reports total assets, net assets, distributions, portfolio entities, commercial activity across sectors, debt and distribution sustainability issues in its 2024/25 annual report. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf)

Waikato-Tainui reports total assets, net assets, annual distribution, net debt, net debt-to-equity, tribal funds, portfolio concentration, SIPO/Puna governance and material asset strategies such as Ruakura and the Brookfield partnership. [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

Te Rarawa reports commercial asset growth, total assets, consolidation and liquidity priorities, operating performance, sector exposures, investment beliefs and selected scholarship/housing/employment contributions, but its public report does not present every metric in exactly the same form as Ngāi Tahu or Waikato-Tainui. [Te Rarawa annual report 2024/25](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

TDB's iwi investment reports show a feasible independent comparison layer for assets, asset allocation, returns, gearing and distributions across selected iwi, but TDB also warns that comparisons are affected by settlement dates, risk, valuation methods and reporting differences. [TDB Advisory iwi investment report 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The current annual-report record therefore supports a stewardship standard, not a definitive impact ranking: public reports can show whether collective capital is being preserved, grown, distributed, reinvested and risk-managed, but they do not consistently prove causal effects on whānau wellbeing, member wealth, procurement, jobs or housing. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rarawa annual report 2024/25](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

### What should stay out of the public minimum

A public standard should not require a comprehensive venture register with acquisition price, counterparty, internal rate of return, rejected opportunities, bank covenants, board papers or post-investment reviews, because current public annual reports show selected ventures unevenly and those records can be commercially sensitive. [Ngāi Tahu annual report 2024/25](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

A public standard should not require member-level or whānau-level outcomes, because Te Mana Raraunga defines Māori data as data produced by Māori or about Māori and their relationships, says Māori data should be subject to Māori governance, and says Māori should be involved in decisions about collection, access, analysis and interpretation. [Te Mana Raraunga Charter](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/58e9b10f9de4bb8d1fb5ebbc/t/5913020d15cf7dde1df34482/1494417935052/Te%2BMana%2BRaraunga%2BCharter%2B%28Final%2B%26%2BApproved%29.pdf)

A public standard should not require uniform iwi wellbeing goals, because Te Whata says iwi control how indicators are configured and that Te Whata was designed so iwi can tell their own narratives according to their own priorities and world views. [Te Whata about page](https://tewhata.io/about/)

The CARE Principles independently support this boundary because they emphasise collective benefit, Indigenous authority to control Indigenous data, responsibility in data use, and ethics across the data lifecycle. [CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance](https://www.rd-alliance.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/CARE20Principles20for20Indigenous20Data20Governance_OnePagers_FINAL20Sept2006202019.pdf)

### How a reviewer should use the standard

A reviewer should compare whether the report gives enough public evidence to answer five stewardship questions: what entity is reporting, how much collective capital is stewarded, what risk the capital is exposed to, how benefits are distributed or reinvested, and what limits apply to the outcome data. [Charities Services annual return guidance](https://www.charities.govt.nz/im-a-registered-charity/annual-returns/updated-annual-return); [TDB Advisory iwi investment report 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

A reviewer should not convert per-member net assets or distributions into a welfare ranking without checking denominator quality, because Stats NZ says 2023 Census iwi-affiliation data is moderate quality, uses alternative sources for some missing responses, and may have changed partly because of collection changes, better response rates, multiple affiliations and whakapapa awareness. [Stats NZ DataInfo+ iwi affiliation quality statement](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/item/nz.govt.stats/303501b1-6e9b-4cc2-b46b-338183672ff5)

A reviewer should treat iwi-defined outcome indicators as accountable disclosure of purpose and progress, not as permission to impose one external definition of success. Te Kāhui Raraunga's Māori Data Governance Model says Māori data is a taonga needing culturally grounded protection and care, and Te Whata says iwi data should let iwi tell their own stories in ways relevant to their contexts. [Te Kāhui Raraunga Māori Data Governance Model](https://www.kahuiraraunga.io/maoridatagovernance); [Te Whata about page](https://tewhata.io/about/)

## What would change this conclusion

- Iwi governors, iwi data specialists, Māori commercial lawyers or Te Mana Raraunga/Te Kāhui Raraunga review could change the proposed boundary between public, member-only and confidential data.
- A public iwi-approved reporting framework may already exist outside the sources found here; I did not find one that standardises public iwi economic-development reporting across financial, risk, distribution, outcome and data-governance fields.
- A reliable public or iwi-approved dataset of venture-level acquisitions, capital injected, write-downs, exits, returns, employment and social outcomes would make a stronger performance standard possible.
- If future Stats NZ or Te Whata releases provide iwi-approved quality metadata and denominators designed for economic-development reporting, the data-provenance section should be updated.
- I could not verify how many iwi PSGEs currently follow charity tiers, company financial-statement filing rules, incorporated-society reporting rules or voluntary annual-report practices; the proposal is therefore designed as an overlay for public annual reports, not a legal compliance map.
- This finding does not decide what iwi should report to members, what must remain confidential, or what tikanga should govern specific datasets; those decisions require iwi mandate.
