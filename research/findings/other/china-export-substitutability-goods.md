# Logs and meat by-products are NZ's hardest China exposure to substitute; sheepmeat has EU headroom, dairy and beef are quota-constrained, and kiwifruit has unquantified EU capacity

## Scope

This finding answers the goods half of issue #502 — dairy, logs/wood, meat, and fruit — by testing product-market fit, pricing, standards, logistics, buyer relationships, and capacity constraints for each. It builds on the concentration baseline already established for issue #498 [china-export-exposure-baseline.md](china-export-exposure-baseline.md), which found logs/wood at 60.7% China-bound, milk powder/butter/cheese at 36.3%, fruit at 27.5%, and meat/offal at 18.7% of exports in the March 2026 quarter. Education and tourism substitutability are **split off** as separate sub-issues (see "What was split off" below) — services markets substitute on different mechanics (visa policy, provider capacity, airline routes) than commodity/agricultural goods, and covering all six sectors to this depth in one file would thin every section. This file is narrowed to the four goods sectors named in #502.

## Executive answer

- **Logs/wood are the hardest to substitute.** China takes roughly 90%+ of NZ log export volume and absorbs the majority of China's own softwood log imports, making this a mutually concentrated relationship. The next markets — South Korea (~2m m³/year) and Japan (declining, tens of thousands of m³/quarter) — are an order of magnitude smaller than China's ~19m m³/year, so even a 25% China shortfall would roughly equal or exceed South Korea's *entire* current NZ offtake. India, the one market with real unmet historical demand (~1.7m m³/$250m peak), collapsed to ~$9.5m in 2023 after a 2022 NZ biosecurity rule change broke compatibility with India's mandatory pre-export methyl-bromide fumigation; a 2023 on-arrival-fumigation trial has only partly restored it (~$77m in 2025), and freight to India costs roughly 55-70% more per m³ than to China, eroding India's higher headline price [interest.co.nz, Dec 2025](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/136706/despite-easing-overall-demand-new-zealand-logs-get-higher-china-market-share-also); [Wood Central, "Fumigation Could Reboot NZ's $250m Log Trade with India"](https://woodcentral.com.au/fumigation-could-reboot-nzs-250m-log-trade-with-india/).
- **Meat splits sharply by product: sheepmeat has real headroom in the EU, beef does not, and offal has almost none anywhere.** The EU's *existing* WTO-only sheepmeat quota is 125,769 tonnes, of which NZ used only 69,837 tonnes in 2025 — meaning roughly 56,000 tonnes of already-tariff-free EU sheepmeat access sits unused before the NZ-EU FTA's new 38,000-tonne top-up (rising over 7 years) is even counted [MFAT, NZ-EU FTA Key Outcomes PDF](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/assets/Trade-agreements/EU-NZ-FTA/NZ-EU-FTA-Key-Outcomes-.pdf). Beef has no comparable spare room: the EU's total prospective beef quota tops out at just 10,000 tonnes after 7 years, and the US's 213,402-tonne WTO beef quota — long underfilled — was exceeded by about 40,000 tonnes in 2023 and was materially over quota again in 2024, so that swing capacity is largely used up and now sits behind a higher ~42% out-of-quota tariff after the US's August 2025 tariff hike [Beef Magazine, "Global beef roundup: Focus on New Zealand"](https://www.beefmagazine.com/market-news/global-beef-roundup-focus-on-new-zealand); [beeflambnz.com, "US tariff hike: what it means"](https://beeflambnz.com/news/us-tariff-hike-what-it-means-nz-red-meat). China is also the largest buyer of NZ's fifth-quarter/offal basket (casings, tripe, hides, skins) — a product mix the US (tallow, blood, prepared meats) and EU (hides, skins, blood) don't overlap with — and no alternative bulk buyer pool for that specific mix was found in this research [MIA, 2025 red meat export release](https://mia.co.nz/new-zealand-red-meat-exports-reached-record-levels-during-2025/).
- **Dairy commodity powder is hard to substitute because the loss itself would move the world price, not just NZ's market access.** Milk powder, butter, and cheese trade largely off one global reference price (GlobalDairyTrade), and China is large enough to affect that index — so losing a third of the world's largest buyer likely depresses the price NZ receives on *all* remaining volume, not only the China-bound share. The EU's new FTA milk-powder quota tops out at just 15,000 tonnes by 2031 against a China dairy trade estimated at roughly 1.4 million tonnes/NZ$8 billion a year, about half of it milk powder; recovering the entire EU quota would offset under 10% of even a 25% China powder shortfall. India, often assumed to be the natural swing buyer given its population, is the least available of all: it is both near self-sufficient in bulk milk and has explicitly excluded bulk milk powder, butter, and cheese from its now-signed-but-not-yet-in-force FTA with New Zealand [MFAT, NZ-India FTA Key Outcomes](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement/key-outcomes); [Beehive, dairy duty-free release](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-zealand-dairy-products-can-now-enter-china-duty-free).
- **Kiwifruit's easiest-to-lose variety is also its highest-return variety, but the EU prevents a clean "no alternative scale" conclusion.** SunGold is 88% of Zespri's mainland China volume and commands a structural premium over Green (Zespri's own 2024/25 final orchard-gate returns: SunGold NZ$11.81/tray vs Green NZ$8.36/tray), so a China shock would disproportionately hit the higher-return variety; this pass did not verify a primary market-by-variety table for Europe, Japan, or South Korea, so any destination-specific SunGold discount on redirected volume remains an inference rather than a measured claim. The EU is already the largest NZ kiwifruit export market by volume (32% vs China's 27%) and tariffs were eliminated in 2024, so Europe is a real alternative channel, but this pass did not quantify how much extra SunGold Europe could absorb at current returns. Japan is mixed rather than simply declining: the cited market report shows a recent rebound in last-twelve-month value and volume, but also latest-six-month volume decline, record-low proxy pricing, and NZ's already-high 97-98% import-market share, limiting growth by competitive displacement [USDA FAS, *New Zealand Kiwifruit Sector*](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=New+Zealand+Kiwifruit+Sector_Wellington_New+Zealand_NZ2026-0002.pdf); [freshfruitportal.com, "Greater China was a standout market for Zespri in 2024"](https://www.freshfruitportal.com/news/2025/03/06/greater-china-was-a-standout-market-for-zespri-in-2024/); [gtaic.ai, Japan kiwifruit market 2025](https://gtaic.ai/market-reports/fresh-kiwifruit-market-japan-trends-in-2025).
- **Ranking, hardest to easiest to substitute:** logs (hardest — concentrated on both the buy and sell side, no comparable-scale alternative) ≈ meat offal/fifth-quarter (near-zero alternative buyer pool) > dairy commodity powder (world-price mechanics, tiny FTA quota headroom, India structurally closed) > beef (US swing capacity now exhausted) > SunGold kiwifruit (high-return variety exposed to unverified same-price alternative capacity, though Europe is already a large market) > sheepmeat (genuine, quantifiable unused EU quota headroom is the one clear bright spot in this research).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The trade-quota mechanics (EU/UK FTA numbers, US WTO quota fill rates, MIA 2025 volumes) rest on primary government and industry-body sources I fetched and read directly. Several supporting figures — freight-cost ratios, some market-share percentages, and most Zespri per-market pricing — come from trade press rather than primary tables; these are flagged inline as lower confidence.

## Evidence

### Method

Four parallel research passes (dairy, logs/wood, meat, fruit) gathered sources against a common six-part framework: product-market fit in alternative markets, premium pricing, standards/regulatory barriers (tariffs, FTA status, phytosanitary/halal), logistics, buyer-relationship maturity, and capacity constraints in alternative markets. I then directly fetched and read six of the most load-bearing documents myself — MFAT's NZ-India and NZ-EU FTA key-outcomes pages, the NZ Meat Board's UK FTA sheepmeat quota page, MIA's 2025 red meat export release, Fresh Fruit Portal's reporting of Zespri Greater China comments, and Zespri's own 2024/25 financial-results page — to confirm the numbers before using them as this finding's central evidence. The Zespri Greater China figures remain secondary trade-press reporting, not a primary Zespri market table; Zespri's own page is used only for global sales and orchard-gate return figures.

### Dairy

**Product-market fit.** China took roughly 30-35% of NZ dairy exports historically (35% cited in [interest.co.nz, "Allan Barber assesses MPI's latest SOPI update"](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/134060/allan-barber-assesses-mpis-latest-sopi-update-sector-now-delivers-more-half-our), citing MPI SOPI data); a 2025 USDA FAS Wellington report likewise puts China at about 35% of total NZ dairy export volume for January-August 2025 [USDA FAS, *Dairy and Products Annual — New Zealand*, 2025](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Dairy+and+Products+Annual_Wellington_New+Zealand_NZ2025-0013.pdf). DCANZ lists China, Australia, the US, Japan, and Malaysia as the traditional top-five markets, with exports reaching "more than 130 different countries" [DCANZ, "The New Zealand dairy industry"](https://dcanz.com/the-new-zealand-dairy-industry/). Southeast Asia is the clearest fast-growing alternative — NZ Treasury's FEU special topic says dairy exports there grew "at almost twice the rate of dairy exports to the rest of the world excluding China" over the prior decade and represented roughly 16% of NZ dairy exports in 2021; the same topic notes that weakening Chinese import demand is expected to weigh on global dairy prices, with demand growth from Southeast Asia, the Middle East and North Africa likely to offset slowing Chinese demand [NZ Treasury, FEU special topic](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/research-and-commentary/rangitaki-blog/feu-special-topic-medium-term-outlook-dairy-exports) (page re-verified through the fetch ladder 2026-07-08).

**Premium pricing.** Milk powder, butter, and cheese trade largely off the GlobalDairyTrade (GDT) auction — a global reference price, not a China-specific price; GDT itself describes the index as calculated from total quantity sold across products, contract periods, and sellers [GlobalDairyTrade, product results](https://www.globaldairytrade.info/en/product-results/). Trade press reported the 3 February 2026 GDT index rose 6.7%, but I could not verify the previously cited claim that Chinese buyers absorbed 35% of that event's offered volume, so that auction-level buyer-share claim has been removed [Cheese Reporter, 4 Feb 2026](https://cheesereporter.com/news/2026/02/04/global-dairy-trade-price-index-rises-6-7-mozzarella-price-jumps-10-6/). The broader mechanism rests instead on China's roughly 35% share of NZ dairy export volume: losing a buyer that large plausibly affects the reference price as well as destination mix. The clearest confirmed branded premium is China-specific infant formula: Fonterra's Anmum brand targeted China's "super-premium" formula segment [nutraingredients.com, 2019, Anmum Assura](https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2019/08/22/Upgraded-Anmum-Fonterra-China-targets-super-premium-market-after-Hong-Kong-launch/). India is not entirely closed for this category — MFAT says the NZ-India FTA phases out tariffs on "bulk infant formula and other dairy-based preparations" over seven years — but that carve-in does not extend to bulk milk powder, butter, cheese, or liquid milk, and this pass did not find evidence of an equivalent-scale premium infant-formula market outside China [MFAT, NZ-India FTA Key Outcomes](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement/key-outcomes).

**Standards and FTA status.** China removed its last safeguard duties on NZ dairy on 1 January 2024, giving fully tariff-free access on dairy averaging "1.4 million tonnes worth NZ$8 billion over the past three years", around half of which was milk powder [Beehive, dairy duty-free release](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/new-zealand-dairy-products-can-now-enter-china-duty-free), corroborated by [ChemLinked](https://food.chemlinked.com/news/food-news/all-new-zealand-dairy-products-exported-to-china-are-exempted-from-tariff-since-2024). By contrast, I confirmed directly from MFAT's own page that the NZ-India FTA — signed but **not yet in force** — carves core dairy out entirely: tariffs are phased out over seven years only for "bulk infant formula and other dairy-based preparations, and peptones," with albumin tariffs halved within a quota, while India commits only to *consider* extending "similar treatment" to other dairy products in future, and to review the FTA a year after entry into force [MFAT, NZ-India FTA Key Outcomes](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement/key-outcomes) (fetched and read directly, 2026-07-05). I also confirmed directly from MFAT's NZ-EU FTA outcomes PDF that New Zealand "currently has no preferential access to the EU milk powder market" at all, and that the new FTA quota starts at 5,000 tonnes, rising to just 15,000 tonnes seven years after entry into force, at a 20%-of-MFN in-quota tariff — a volume MFAT itself notes "equates to over 20% of the EU's annual milk powder imports," i.e. the EU milk powder import market as a whole is small [MFAT, NZ-EU FTA Key Outcomes PDF](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/assets/Trade-agreements/EU-NZ-FTA/NZ-EU-FTA-Key-Outcomes-.pdf) (fetched and read directly). The same document shows butter access rising to a combined 36,000 tonnes (≈60% of EU butter imports) and cheese to a combined 31,031 tonnes (≈15% of EU cheese imports) after seven years — both real but structurally small relative to NZ's total dairy trade. Halal certification for Middle East/Southeast Asia markets is a mature, solved barrier: MPI maintains an approved Halal Assurance System and a public list of halal-certified dairy premises [MPI, Halal Assurance System](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/lists-of-approved-premises-for-specified-products/new-zealand-approved-dairy-halal-premises/).

**Logistics.** Reefer/cold-chain shipping is required to all these markets regardless of destination [DHL, exporting food products from NZ](https://www.dhl.com/discover/en-nz/logistics-advice/import-export-advice/exporting-food-products-from-new-zealand). This finding no longer relies on route-specific dairy transit-time estimates, because I did not find inspectable route pages with enough detail on route, mode, and date to make the comparison reviewable.

**Buyer relationships.** Fonterra has restructured rather than exited China: it completed the Lactalis sale of its global consumer and associated businesses, but Fonterra's own release says the sale excluded the Greater China consumer business and that Fonterra will continue to own the Anchor brand there [Fonterra, Mainland Group sale completion](https://www.fonterra.com/americas/en/our-stories/media/fonterra-completes-sale-of-mainland-group-to-lactalis.html). Its FY25 results also show Greater China as "the engine" of a $6.4 billion global foodservice business built on UHT cream, butter, and mozzarella sales to chefs [b2bnews.co.nz](https://b2bnews.co.nz/news/fonterras-4-point-2b-china-foodservice-bet-explained/); [dairyreporter.com, Fonterra FY25](https://www.dairyreporter.com/Article/2025/09/25/fonterra-reports-strong-fy25-as-it-sets-sights-on-protein-cheese-push/). That means the restructuring shifts exposure toward China-focused consumer retention and B2B foodservice rather than eliminating China risk. Southeast Asian relationships are also established and growing — Fonterra reports "10.3% increase in imports year-on-year" in other Asian markets, driven by "successful brand promotion" in Southeast Asia specifically [foodnavigator-asia.com](https://www.foodnavigator-asia.com/Article/2025/03/04/dairy-dilemma-fonterra-sees-strong-asean-growth-and-china-drop-in-imports-amidst-overall-profit-decline/). India, by contrast, has no comparable bulk-dairy buyer relationship, because the market has been structurally closed to it.

**Capacity constraints.** USDA ERS describes India as the world's largest producer and consumer of milk, with production generally matching consumption and imports minimal [USDA ERS, India](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/international-markets-us-trade/countries-regions/india) — meaning India is closed by both policy (FTA exclusion) and structural self-sufficiency, not one or the other. India may export some dairy categories such as butter/ghee, but that does not make it a plausible bulk milk-powder sink for NZ. The EU is itself a major dairy exporter (24% of global dairy exports on one industry estimate) rather than a demand sink, and its FTA quotas — while currently under-utilised because historically restrictive WTO-only tariffs made them commercially unviable — are capped at volumes tiny relative to NZ's China dairy trade even before considering whether the EU's own production growth leaves room for more imports [dairyindustries.com, "Differences in EU and UK New Zealand trade quotas"](https://www.dairyindustries.com/news/42940/differences-in-eu-and-uk-new-zealand-trade-quotas/).

**Sector conclusion — Hard to substitute in the short-to-medium term.** The binding constraint isn't finding buyers for the physical product; it's that (a) the two most obvious swing markets, the EU and India, are either capped by treaty at a small fraction of the trade at risk or structurally closed altogether, and (b) losing a very large share of the world's largest dairy buyer plausibly moves the reference price that determines what NZ earns on every tonne it still sells, everywhere.

### Logs and wood products

**Product-market fit.** China's share of NZ log exports is reported at 90.5% of volume in one recent period [interest.co.nz, Dec 2025](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/136706/despite-easing-overall-demand-new-zealand-logs-get-higher-china-market-share-also), with NZ supplying roughly 57-75% of China's own softwood/total log imports depending on period and product definition (figures vary by source and denominator — flagged as a range, not a single fixed number, pending a direct Stats NZ/ITTO cross-check). South Korea and Japan are the next markets but are small and shrinking: Q1 2024 volumes were 268,000 m³ to South Korea and just 35,000 m³ to Japan, against 5.48 million m³ to China in the same quarter (+44% y/y) — a scale gap the NZ Forest Owners Association itself frames as roughly 19 million m³/year to China vs 2 million m³/year to South Korea [Wood Central, "Fumigation Could Reboot NZ's $250m Log Trade with India"](https://woodcentral.com.au/fumigation-could-reboot-nzs-250m-log-trade-with-india/), attributed to NZFOA. India was historically a top-3 market at roughly $250 million/1.7 million m³ a year, but collapsed after a 2022 NZ environmental rule (mandatory recapture/destruction of methyl bromide fumigant emissions) made compliance with India's pre-export fumigation requirement impractical, dropping the trade to $9.5 million in 2023; a September-October 2023 MPI trial permitting on-arrival (in-ship-hold) fumigation for underdeck breakbulk logs has partly restored it, to roughly $77 million year-to-date 2025 [same Wood Central source]; MPI confirms methyl bromide emission-control rules exist for export fumigation generally [MPI, fumigating with methyl bromide](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/export/timber-wood-products/timber-wood-products/fumigating-with-methyl-bromide) (MPI's India-specific phytosanitary requirements page exists but did not render readable content in this pass — flagged for direct follow-up).

**Premium pricing.** NZ pine landed in China at roughly NZ$198/m³ in 2023, cheaper than German/US spruce (NZ$326/m³) or hardwood (NZ$243/m³) — the source frames this cheapness as NZ's competitive edge in China's packaging/construction-formwork use case, not a premium [interest.co.nz](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). India shows a higher headline CFR price (roughly US$137/JASm³ vs China's US$112-119/JASm³ in a December 2025 report), but bulk-carrier freight to India runs "mid-40s to China and 70s to India" in USD/JASm³ — a 55-70% freight premium that erodes most or all of India's apparent price advantage at the forest-gate netback level [interest.co.nz, Dec 2025 and June 2026 reports](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/139128/recent-sharpish-devaluation-nz-dollar-expected-provide-some-relief-forest-owner). (One earlier November 2024 report cites a much larger 13x India-vs-China freight ratio, but this appears specific to a minor container-shipping mode rather than the bulk-carrier mode that moves the bulk of log volume — treat the 13x figure as non-representative.)

**Standards and regulatory barriers.** The binding barrier for India is phytosanitary, not tariff: India's PQ Order 2003 requires logs to be fumigated with methyl bromide before export, and NZ's 2022 domestic emissions rule made pre-export compliance commercially difficult, which is what collapsed the trade (see above). Tariff relief from the newly-signed NZ-India FTA is prospective only — the agreement was signed 27 April 2026 and is not yet in force, with NZ First reported as not supporting it, so any log-specific tariff reduction should not be treated as current [MFAT, NZ-India FTA](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-concluded-but-not-in-force/new-zealand-india-free-trade-agreement); [Al Jazeera, Dec 2025](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/22/india-and-new-zealand-conclude-free-trade-agreement). As a relevant precedent for how strictly China itself enforces phytosanitary rules, China banned Australian log imports state-by-state from October 2020 over bark beetle detections, extending to a roughly 2.5-year full ban lifted around 2023 [The New Daily](https://thenewdaily.com.au/news/world/2020/11/12/victorian-timber-log-exports-blocked-from-entering-china-due-to-bark-beetle/) — a ban NZ was not subject to, which directly benefited NZ's China market share at Australia's expense.

**Logistics.** The verified logistics constraint in this pass is freight cost rather than transit time: the interest.co.nz reports cited above put bulk-carrier freight to India materially above China-bound freight, enough to erode India's higher headline log price. A comparable, inspectable transit-time estimate by route, mode, and date was not found. Whether NZ's largest log-export ports (Tauranga, Northport, Eastland/Gisborne) could operationally handle a major shift away from China's large-parcel bulk trade was not verified in this research and is flagged as an open question.

**Buyer relationships.** NZ-China log trade rests on "long-standing relationships fostered between New Zealand exporters and Chinese importers" that reportedly let the industry respond quickly to COVID-era disruptions [interest.co.nz](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength). I did not find an inspectable primary source for the earlier claim that India's importer base is materially narrower or more regionally concentrated, so that claim is not used as evidence here.

**Capacity constraints.** South Korea and Japan's volumes are small and reported as declining, not growing, over recent years. India's own market is described as facing weak construction demand, expensive domestic financing (10-25% lending rates), and active competition from South American and Australian pine already selling into the same ports — with only around 10 vessels a month arriving at Kandla, a small trade relative to China's daily offtake [interest.co.nz, June 2026](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/139128/recent-sharpish-devaluation-nz-dollar-expected-provide-some-relief-forest-owner). China's own total log imports fell roughly 32% in 2022 on weak domestic construction, a sign of global oversupply rather than untapped alternative demand waiting elsewhere [interest.co.nz, property-downturn log analysis](https://www.interest.co.nz/rural-news/131245/impact-china%E2%80%99s-property-downturn-new-zealand-exports-pine-proves-its-strength).

**Sector conclusion — Hardest of the four goods sectors to substitute.** This is a mutually concentrated relationship (NZ supplies most of China's log imports; China takes nearly all of NZ's log exports), the credible alternative markets are an order of magnitude smaller and mostly flat-to-declining, and the one market with real historical scale (India) carries a live phytosanitary friction that has already collapsed the trade once and a meaningful freight-cost penalty even where compliant.

### Meat

**Product-market fit.** MIA's full-year 2025 release, which I fetched and read directly, shows the US already as NZ's largest red meat market by value ($3.2 billion; beef 173,056 tonnes, sheepmeat 32,581 tonnes), with China second at $2.5 billion (sheepmeat 156,345 tonnes — the largest sheepmeat market by volume; beef 112,246 tonnes — second-largest beef market) [MIA, "New Zealand red meat exports reached record levels during 2025"](https://mia.co.nz/new-zealand-red-meat-exports-reached-record-levels-during-2025/) (fetched and read directly, 2026-07-05). The EU ($1.8 billion, sheepmeat-heavy) and UK ($858 million) both grew sharply in 2025. Crucially, the same release states China's fifth-quarter trade "mainly consisted of casings and tripe, hides and skins, and offals," while "the US mostly took tallow, blood products and prepared meat products" and the EU "mainly hides and skins and blood products" — confirming these markets buy structurally different by-product baskets, not overlapping ones.

**Premium pricing.** MIA's 2025 release gives enough value-and-volume data to show the direction of realised-price differences: US beef exports were worth $2.1 billion on 173,056 tonnes (about $12.13/kg), while China beef was worth $876 million on 112,246 tonnes (about $7.80/kg); EU sheepmeat was worth $1.4 billion on 69,837 tonnes (about $20.05/kg), compared with China sheepmeat at $1.1 billion on 156,345 tonnes (about $7.04/kg) [MIA, 2025 red meat export release](https://mia.co.nz/new-zealand-red-meat-exports-reached-record-levels-during-2025/). This suggests a shift away from China could raise average realised prices for some prime cuts if the volume can be placed at all; price is not the main binding constraint in the quota-constrained products.

**Standards and FTA status.** The UK FTA's beef quota starts at 12,000 tonnes, rising by 2,980 tonnes/year toward full liberalisation by year 11; its sheepmeat FTA quota of 35,000-50,000 tonnes cannot be accessed at all until NZ uses 90% of its existing WTO UK sheepmeat quota — a condition I confirmed by fetching the NZ Meat Board's own page directly: "it is a condition of the UK-NZ FTA this FTA sheepmeat quota is unable to be accessed until 90% of the existing World Trade Organisation (WTO) UK Sheepmeat and Goatmeat quota is utilised" [nzmeatboard.org, UK FTA Sheepmeat](https://www.nzmeatboard.org/quotas/uk-fta-sheepmeat) (fetched and read directly, 2026-07-05). The EU picture is sharper by product: I confirmed directly from MFAT's NZ-EU FTA outcomes PDF that NZ's beef access is small — a pre-existing 1,102-tonne WTO quota plus a new FTA quota of 3,333 tonnes rising to only 10,000 tonnes after seven years — but that sheepmeat access is comparatively large: NZ "already has good access to the EU for sheep meat through a New Zealand only WTO duty-free quota of 125,769 tonnes," on top of which the FTA adds "an additional duty-free quota growing to 38,000 tonnes after seven years," for a combined 163,769 tonnes [MFAT, NZ-EU FTA Key Outcomes PDF](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/assets/Trade-agreements/EU-NZ-FTA/NZ-EU-FTA-Key-Outcomes-.pdf) (fetched and read directly). Against the MIA-confirmed 2025 EU sheepmeat volume of 69,837 tonnes, that means roughly 56,000 tonnes of the *existing* WTO quota alone was unused in 2025, before the new FTA top-up is even counted. On the US side, the WTO beef/veal quota is 213,402 tonnes at a low in-quota tariff [NZ Meat Board, US Beef and Veal](https://www.nzmeatboard.org/quotas/us-beef-and-veal); Beef Magazine reports the TRQ was exceeded by about 40,000 tonnes in 2023 and that 2024 NZ beef shipments to the US rose another 15% to nearly 275,000 tonnes, implying the quota was materially exceeded again [Beef Magazine, "Global beef roundup: Focus on New Zealand"](https://www.beefmagazine.com/market-news/global-beef-roundup-focus-on-new-zealand). Beef + Lamb NZ's tariff explainer appears to retain older boilerplate saying NZ had not filled the quota since 2015, so I no longer use it as support for the fill-rate claim; I use it only for the 2025 tariff rates. A further US "reciprocal" tariff increase from 7 August 2025 pushed in-quota beef tariffs to roughly 15.6% and out-of-quota to roughly 42%, adding an estimated $300 million/year in cost to NZ red meat exports if volumes hold [beeflambnz.com, "US tariff hike: what it means for NZ red meat"](https://beeflambnz.com/news/us-tariff-hike-what-it-means-nz-red-meat). Halal certification for Middle East markets is mature and non-novel: FIANZ is the sole certifier recognised for Saudi Arabia and Kuwait, and roughly 45% of NZ red meat (worth $3.7 billion/year) is already halal-certified [MPI, Halal Assurance System for exports](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/export/food/exporting-halal-animal-products/halal-assurance-system-for-exports-from-new-zealand). The NZ-UAE CEPA entered into force 28 August 2025, eliminating tariffs on essentially all goods including red meat immediately, though NZ currently holds only around 1% of UAE sheepmeat and 3% of UAE beef import share, indicating headroom from a very low base [middleeastbriefing.com, UAE-NZ CEPA](https://www.middleeastbriefing.com/news/uae-new-zealand-cepa-removes-99-percent-duties-on-traded-goods/) (Medium confidence — broader GCC FTA status with Saudi Arabia was reported as "finalised but not yet in force" by the research pass but should be re-checked against MFAT's live tracker before being relied on).

**Logistics.** Saudi Arabia is a closer form-factor match for frozen sheepmeat than the EU/UK chilled-premium trade: MIA says more than 95% of NZ sheepmeat exports to Saudi Arabia are frozen, and that market will benefit when the NZ-GCC FTA enters into force [MIA, 2025 red meat export release](https://mia.co.nz/new-zealand-red-meat-exports-reached-record-levels-during-2025/). NZ processors also demonstrated some responsiveness during 2025's China softness, with MIA reporting higher values to the US, UK, EU, Canada, Saudi Arabia, and several other markets in the same year.

**Buyer relationships.** NZ's red-meat export relationships run through a relatively small set of established processor-exporters rather than spot-market farmers; MIA's member list includes Alliance Group, Silver Fern Farms, AFFCO, ANZCO, and other processor-exporters, and Alliance describes The Lamb Company as a North American co-operative venture involving Alliance, Silver Fern Farms, ANZCO, and an Australian partner [MIA, all members](https://mia.co.nz/all-members/); [Alliance, North America](https://www.alliance.co.nz/north-america/). Silver Fern Farms has deepened its China relationship via a 50:50 joint venture with COFCO subsidiary Shanghai Maling Aquarius since 2017, indicating company-level as well as national concentration [chinadaily.com.cn, 2021](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202106/02/WS60b6e656a31024ad0bac3058.html) (Medium confidence, not independently re-verified in full). North American access therefore looks mature, but this establishes buyer maturity rather than spare demand.

**Capacity constraints.** The clearest quantified headroom in this entire finding is EU sheepmeat: roughly 56,000 tonnes of unused existing WTO quota plus a 38,000-tonne FTA top-up phasing in over seven years, against a 25-50% cut to China's 156,345-tonne 2025 sheepmeat volume (39,000-78,000 tonnes) — genuinely comparable in scale, though not a certainty, since unused quota doesn't guarantee the EU market can absorb that much additional NZ sheepmeat at current prices without some price effect. Beef has no comparable headroom: the EU's total prospective beef quota (10,000 tonnes) and the UK's slowly-phasing beef quota (12,000 tonnes, +2,980/year) are both small relative to a 25-50% cut to China's 112,246-tonne 2025 beef volume (28,000-56,000 tonnes), and the US's larger quota is already exceeded. Offal/fifth-quarter has the least alternative capacity of all in the evidence found here: MIA identifies China, the US, and the EU as the three major fifth-quarter markets, but also says they buy different baskets — China mainly casings/tripe, hides/skins, and offals; the US mainly tallow, blood products, and prepared meats; and the EU mainly hides/skins and blood products [MIA, 2025 red meat export release](https://mia.co.nz/new-zealand-red-meat-exports-reached-record-levels-during-2025/). A separate quantified alternative buyer pool for the China-specific fifth-quarter basket was not found.

**Sector conclusion — Splits by product: sheepmeat is moderate (genuine quantified EU headroom), beef and offal are hard (quota ceilings and product-mix mismatch respectively).**

### Fruit (kiwifruit and apples)

**Product-market fit.** USDA FAS's 2026 New Zealand kiwifruit sector report puts China at roughly 27% of NZ kiwifruit export volume, against the EU at 32% and Japan at 14% [USDA FAS, *New Zealand Kiwifruit Sector*](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=New+Zealand+Kiwifruit+Sector_Wellington_New+Zealand_NZ2026-0002.pdf). Fresh Fruit Portal's reporting of Zespri Greater China comments states Greater China took 54 million trays in 2024 — a record — worth an expected $1.4 billion in net sales, split 42.6 million trays to mainland China, 8.6 million to Taiwan, and 3 million to Hong Kong, with SunGold at 88% of the mainland China volume [freshfruitportal.com, "Greater China was a standout market for Zespri in 2024"](https://www.freshfruitportal.com/news/2025/03/06/greater-china-was-a-standout-market-for-zespri-in-2024/) (secondary trade press, fetched and read directly, 2026-07-05). Zespri's global 2024/25 season sold a record 220.9 million trays for NZ$5.03 billion in global fruit sales revenue; the company's public reporting did not disclose a full per-market breakdown beyond citing "strong performance in Europe and the US" and a "strong start in Asia" [Zespri, 2024/25 financial results](https://canopy.zespri.com/public/home/news/zespri-2024-25-financial-results). The NZ$5.9 billion figure belongs to the later 2025/26 season (248.1 million trays), not 2024/25, so it is not used as the denominator here [Zespri, 2025/26 financial results](https://www.zespri.com/en-NZ/publications/newsroomdetail/2025-26-financial-results). Apples are more concentrated on China specifically as an import destination: NZ holds a reported 78% share of China's *imported* apple market by value (2024), though this measures NZ's share of China's imports rather than China's share of NZ's total apple exports, and a consolidated NZ apple export table by destination was not found (NZAPI/Horticulture NZ PDFs did not render as extractable text in this pass) [producereport.com](https://www.producereport.com/article/new-zealand-apple-exports-china-soar-which-varieties-are-trending) — flagged Low-Medium confidence pending a direct Stats NZ or NZAPI table.

**Premium pricing.** The clearest confirmed premium is SunGold-over-Green, not China-over-elsewhere: Zespri's own 2024/25 final orchard-gate returns show conventional SunGold at NZ$11.81/tray against conventional Green at NZ$8.36/tray [Zespri, 2024/25 financial results](https://canopy.zespri.com/public/home/news/zespri-2024-25-financial-results). Since Fresh Fruit Portal reports SunGold was 88% of mainland China volume, a China-demand shock would hit the highest-return variety hardest; however, I did not find a primary market-by-variety table showing whether Europe, Japan, or South Korea could absorb redirected SunGold at the same price, so the redirected-price effect is treated as an unquantified risk rather than a measured discount [freshfruitportal.com, "Greater China was a standout market for Zespri in 2024"](https://www.freshfruitportal.com/news/2025/03/06/greater-china-was-a-standout-market-for-zespri-in-2024/). A direct SunGold price comparison across China vs Japan vs Europe was not found in this research pass and is flagged as an open gap requiring Zespri's investor-grade market-by-market pricing data.

**Standards and FTA status.** Kiwifruit has been tariff-free into China since 2016 under the original NZ-China FTA [NZ Horticulture Export Authority, kiwifruit trade](https://www.hea.co.nz/2012-05-11-03-05-28/kiwifruit-trade). The EU's May 2024 FTA immediately removed an 8.8% MFN tariff on NZ kiwifruit (and apples), reportedly worth $43-47 million/year to the kiwifruit sector [freshfruitportal.com, "New Zealand kiwifruit gets tariff relief under EU trade deal"](https://www.freshfruitportal.com/news/2024/03/27/new-zealand-kiwifruit-gets-tariff-relief-under-eu-trade-deal/); USDA FAS separately says the NZ-EU FTA eliminated EU kiwifruit tariffs immediately and that the change was supporting export growth into EU markets [USDA FAS, *New Zealand Kiwifruit Sector*](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=New+Zealand+Kiwifruit+Sector_Wellington_New+Zealand_NZ2026-0002.pdf). I did not find an inspectable South Korea kiwifruit tariff schedule in this pass, so the earlier Korea tariff-parity claim is no longer used. Apples face a materially worse position in the one market often assumed to be an easy alternative: a 15% US tariff (raised from 10% in August 2025) remains on NZ apples even though the equivalent tariff on kiwifruit was lifted in November 2025, with reporting that US apple sales are now loss-making at that tariff level and that at least one major exporter skipped the US market this season [freshplaza.com, "New Zealand kiwifruit and apple exports adapt to U.S. tariffs"](https://www.freshplaza.com/north-america/article/9794450/new-zealand-kiwifruit-and-apple-exports-adapt-to-u-s-tariffs/) (Medium confidence — a related freshplaza URL on the same topic returned a 403 in this pass and could not be independently re-verified beyond the search snippet).

**Logistics.** NZ's March-May harvest is counter-seasonal to essentially every major market — China, Japan, Korea, and Europe all harvest in the Northern Hemisphere autumn — so seasonality doesn't favour China over alternatives; it's a wash for substitutability. Zespri's charter shipping network is heavily Asia-weighted: its first 2024 charter release expected 63 charter vessels, including 42 to Asia, 14 to the Mediterranean, 4 to North America's West Coast, and 3 to Northern Europe, while its final-shipment release counted 62 charter vessels including 43 to Asia, 13 to the Mediterranean, 3 to Northern Europe, and 3 to North America's West Coast [Fruitnet, first 2024 charter](https://www.fruitnet.com/produce-plus/first-zespri-kiwifruit-charter-of-2024-heads-to-china/259164.article); [Zespri, final 2024 shipments](https://canopy.zespri.com/public/home/news/final-shipments-of-zespri-s-largest-ever-crop-depart). This could make redirecting volume within Asia (Japan, Korea, Southeast Asia, Taiwan) logistically faster than a hard pivot to Europe or North America, where charter capacity is comparatively thin.

**Buyer relationships.** Zespri's China distribution is concentrated but deep: two distributors (Joy Wing Mau and Goodfarmer, the latter a 13-year relationship handling roughly a quarter of China volume) plus two retail partners account for roughly 80% of Zespri's China business, and Zespri's stated strategy is to expand China coverage from 60-70 cities to 120 cities by 2033/34 — active deepening, not hedging [fruitnet.com, Zespri China distribution](https://www.fruitnet.com/asiafruit/zespri-inks-deal-to-increase-china-sales/254806.article). In Japan, NZ already holds a 97-98% import-market share, leaving little room to grow via competitive displacement — growth there is capped by category size, not competitor share [gtaic.ai, Japan kiwifruit market 2025](https://gtaic.ai/market-reports/fresh-kiwifruit-market-japan-trends-in-2025).

**Capacity constraints.** Japan is not a simple decline story: the cited market report says fresh-kiwifruit imports grew 11.64% in value and 9.33% in volume over the latest twelve months, but also reports a 6.73% volume decline in the latest six-month period and a record-low proxy price, while NZ already holds a 97-98% import share [gtaic.ai, Japan kiwifruit market 2025](https://gtaic.ai/market-reports/fresh-kiwifruit-market-japan-trends-in-2025). That leaves some demand-growth possibility, but little obvious competitor-displacement room. Europe is the larger unquantified alternative: USDA FAS says the EU was already NZ's largest kiwifruit export market by volume in MY 2024/25 (32%, versus China at 27%), exports grew in all markets by volume, and EU tariffs were eliminated immediately under the NZ-EU FTA [USDA FAS, *New Zealand Kiwifruit Sector*](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=New+Zealand+Kiwifruit+Sector_Wellington_New+Zealand_NZ2026-0002.pdf). On a rough tray-share translation, Greater China's 54 million trays imply an EU market around 64 million trays if the USDA volume shares map closely to Zespri trays, so a 13.5-27 million tray Greater China shock would equal roughly 21-42% of current EU scale; that is too large to assume painless absorption, but not enough evidence to call European headroom "far short." Zespri has spent over a decade building a 7,500-hectare Northern Hemisphere grower base specifically to grow European/North American supply, while growers have separately flagged oversupply risk in some votes on expanding that supply further, reinforcing that Europe has real but not unlimited absorption capacity [rnz.co.nz, "Zespri asks growers to back increasing Northern Hemisphere supply"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/country/519238/zespri-asks-growers-to-back-increasing-northern-hemisphere-supply).

**Sector conclusion — Moderate-to-hard.** The mitigating factor unique to this sector is that Zespri has already spent a decade building genuine (if smaller-scale) alternative capacity in Europe; the complicating factor is that the variety most exposed to China (SunGold) is also the highest-return variety on Zespri's disclosed orchard-gate measure, while same-price alternative SunGold capacity was not verified in this pass.

### Cross-sector capacity math

Lining up each sector's approximate 25-50% China-shortfall volume against its best-documented alternative-market headroom:

| Sector | Approx. China volume at risk (25-50% cut) | Best alternative headroom found | Headroom vs shock |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sheepmeat | 39,000-78,000t | EU: ~56,000t unused existing WTO quota + 38,000t new FTA quota (7-yr phase-in) | Comparable in scale — the one genuinely close match |
| Beef | 28,000-56,000t | EU FTA quota caps at 10,000t (7-yr); US quota already exceeded | Far short |
| Milk powder | Roughly 175,000-350,000t (25-50% of an estimated ~700,000t China-bound powder volume: 1.4m tonnes total China dairy exports x about half milk powder) | EU FTA quota caps at 15,000t (7-yr) | Far short |
| Logs | Roughly 4.75-9.5m m³ (25-50% of ~19m m³/year) | South Korea's entire current NZ offtake is ~2m m³/year | Far short |
| Kiwifruit (Greater China trays) | 13.5-27m trays (25-50% of 54m) | EU is already the largest NZ kiwifruit export market by volume (32% vs China at 27%); rough tray-share translation puts current EU scale around 64m trays, but no market-by-variety or same-price SunGold absorption table was found | Unquantified — real EU scale, but not proven same-price headroom |

This table is a rough order-of-magnitude comparison built from the figures cited above, not a formal trade model. The milk-powder row is now explicitly derived from the Beehive-reported 1.4m tonnes of annual NZ dairy exports to China, "around half" of which was milk powder, rather than from a separate Stats NZ pull. The table also doesn't account for price effects, phased quota timing, or whether "headroom" quota could actually be filled commercially. It is included because the pattern — one comparable match (EU sheepmeat), three clearly far-short quota/market alternatives (beef, milk powder, logs), and one large but unquantified alternative channel (EU kiwifruit) — is the single clearest finding of this research and is easy to lose inside the sector-by-sector detail above.

## What would change this conclusion

- A primary Stats NZ or DCANZ market-by-market dairy export table (not found in this pass) would let the "China share" and "alternative headroom" figures be checked against hard volumes rather than percentages and industry commentary.
- Direct confirmation of the NZ-India FTA's ratification timeline, and whether its "future consultation on dairy" clause is ever exercised, would change the dairy and logs conclusions if India's market opened further than currently committed.
- A primary MPI/Te Uru Rākau log export table by destination (the MPI SOPI and NZFOA Facts & Figures PDFs did not render as extractable text in this pass) would resolve the 55-92% range of "China share of NZ log exports" cited across sources into one confirmed figure.
- Confirmation of the NZ-GCC FTA's actual entry-into-force status (reported by the research pass as "finalised but not yet in force," not independently re-verified against MFAT's live tracker) would change how much Middle East headroom to credit for meat.
- Zespri's own investor-grade market-by-variety and market-by-market pricing tables would let the SunGold China-vs-Japan-vs-Europe substitution risk be stated as fact rather than as an inference from China's reported SunGold-heavy mix and Zespri's disclosed SunGold-vs-Green orchard-gate premium.
- A formal MPI, Treasury, or industry-body model that explicitly quantifies a 25-50% China demand-shock scenario sector by sector does not appear to exist publicly — none of the four research passes found one. If one exists but wasn't surfaced, it would supersede the rough order-of-magnitude comparison in the "cross-sector capacity math" table above.
- I could not verify: route-specific dairy transit times with inspectable route pages; exact current freight-cost ratios beyond the log-freight estimates cited above; NZ port capacity to physically handle a shift toward smaller, more frequent shipments if trade redirected to India/Korea rather than China's large-parcel model; and a direct primary-source Chinese-customs or GACC dataset for any sector (all China-side figures are sourced via NZ/US government or industry-body reporting on the trade, not Chinese primary sources).
