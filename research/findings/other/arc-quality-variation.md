# Public quality evidence shows NZ aged residential care is regulatorily audited and complaint-monitored, but the four quality signals — complaints, audit non-conformance, enforcement and resident outcomes — are not publicly joined into a single defensible variation picture

## Executive answer

- **NZ has a working quality-architecture, not a single quality score.** Around 32,000 people live in aged residential care (ARC), funded at nearly $2 billion in 2022/23 (8% of total health funding), and every certified provider is audited by designated auditing agencies against Ngā Paerewa (Health and Disability Services Standards NZS 8134:2021, in force from 28 February 2022), with certification periods of one to four years, unannounced spot audits, and a four-level attainment rating plus risk rating for every criterion [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024-online-version); [Ministry of Health, Rest home certification and audits](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits); [Ministry of Health, More about audit reports](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits/about-the-audit-reports).
- **Complaints are a high-volume but partial signal.** HDC received 154 complaints naming an aged residential care facility in 2024/25 (down from 184 in 2023/24, 185 in 2022/23, 183 in 2021/22), and around 18% of all HDC complaints are about the care of older people, but HDC itself cautions that complaint volume does not necessarily reflect the quality of care [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).
- **Audit non-conformance is common and structured, not a pass/fail.** Most rest home audits record some criteria as "partial attainment" (PA), and partial attainment does not indicate failure — it triggers a Corrective Action Request (CAR) with a timeframe; the public audit summary uses a traffic-light system from "commendable" to "major shortfalls" [Ministry of Health, More about audit reports](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits/about-the-audit-reports); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part2.htm).
- **Enforcement is real but layered and hard to aggregate.** HealthCERT (Ministry of Health) can require corrective action, add conditions to certification, demand more frequent audits, and impose fines for offering services without certification; DHBs (now Health NZ) hold funding-contract levers including appointing a temporary manager or cancelling funding; HDC can find a provider in breach of the Code and refer to the Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal or Human Rights Review Tribunal [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf); [Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2001/0093/latest/whole.html).
- **The Aged Care Commissioner's evidence is directional, not a quantified variation map.** The Commissioner's March 2024 monitoring report made 20 recommendations across topics including transitions of care, investing in primary and community care models, preventative interventions for dementia, and reliable home and community services; in 2024/25 she undertook 162 sector engagements and 53 engagements with older people to monitor implementation, and HDC will report progress through 2025/26 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Aged Care Commissioner, Amplifying the voices of older people, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/).
- **No public dataset joins these signals with resident outcomes.** The OAG found in 2012 that improvements to the audit process had uncertain effect on the quality of care delivered to residents, that the certification process was "too paper-based" and "more concerned with compliance than outcomes," and that the volume of complaints "did not necessarily reflect the quality of care"; the Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation (2024) found workforce shortage was a common barrier to meeting standards across all provider types, and that smaller standalone facilities lacked resources to implement changes [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm); [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the regulatory architecture, audit process, complaint volumes, and enforcement levers are well-documented in official sources, but the direct link from these signals to resident-level outcome variation is not publicly established, and the most recent comprehensive audit-quality review (OAG) dates to 2012.

## Evidence

### 1. The scale and regulatory baseline

Around 32,000 people lived in aged residential care facilities in 2022/23, and a further 80,000 older people received home and community support services; together these services supported 2.1% of the population and received nearly $2 billion in funding (8% of total health funding) [Health NZ, Health and Independence Report 2024](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/health-and-independence-report-2024-online-version).

ARC includes rest-home, dementia, psychogeriatric and continuing/hospital-level care; providers must be certified under the Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001, which promotes the safe provision of health and disability services and encourages providers to continuously improve quality [Health and Disability Services (Safety) Act 2001](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2001/0093/latest/whole.html); [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

From 1 June 2009 to 28 February 2022, rest homes were audited against Health and Disability Services Standards NZS 8134:2008; from 28 February 2022 they are audited against Ngā Paerewa Health and disability services standard NZS 8134:2021 [Ministry of Health, Rest homes](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/certified-providers/rest-homes).

HealthCERT, a part of the Ministry of Health, is responsible for ensuring rest homes and other providers "provide safe and reasonable levels of service for consumers" under the HDSS Act 2001 [Ministry of Health, Certification of health care services](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part2.htm).

### 2. The audit and certification process

Certification periods range from one to four years (historically up to five), determined by how well the provider performs at certification audit; the first certification period is always provisional for one year [Ministry of Health, Rest home certification and audits](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part2.htm).

The Treasury/Productivity Commission case study describes the certification-period matrix: the Ministry scores the provider's performance on a 0–5 scale against eight "high-risk areas," seven "general" factors, and three "other" matters; the cumulative score determines the certification period [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

There are five types of audit: certification audits (full, all criteria, every one to four years), unannounced spot/surveillance audits (midway through the certification period), provisional audits (change of ownership), partial provisional audits (new services or capacity), and verification audits (pre-2014 term for reconfigurations) [Ministry of Health, Rest home certification and audits](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part2.htm).

Audits are conducted by designated auditing agencies (DAAs) — private organisations designated by the Director-General of Health; providers choose which DAA audits them; each audit team includes at least one registered nurse; auditors observe the rest home, interview staff, residents and family, and review clinical records, policies and procedures [Ministry of Health, Rest home certification and audits](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits); [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

### 3. Audit attainment, risk ratings, and corrective actions

Each criterion is rated on a four-level attainment scale: CI (Continuous improvement — achievement beyond full attainment), FA (Fully attained — full attainment and meets requirements), PA (Partial attainment — partial attainment and improvement required), and UA (Unattained — not met); NA means the standard or criterion does not apply [Ministry of Health, More about audit reports](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits/about-the-audit-reports).

Where criteria are partially attained (PA) or unattained (UA), a risk rating is assigned — negligible, low, moderate, high, or critical — reflecting the assessed potential risk for consumers [Ministry of Health, More about audit reports](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits/about-the-audit-reports).

Partial attainment is common and does not indicate failure: "Most rest home audits have some standards and criteria assessed as partial attainment. Partial attainment indicates the areas that require improvement." Where improvement is required, the audit report includes a Corrective Action Request (CAR) stating the action required and a timeframe for completion [Ministry of Health, More about audit reports](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/for-residents-and-families/rest-home-certification-and-audits/about-the-audit-reports).

The Ministry publishes a summary of each rest home's audit report with a traffic-light system ranging from "commendable elements above the required levels of performance" to "major shortfalls, significant action is needed to achieve the required levels of performance"; a "red traffic light" for major shortfalls can be removed after corrective actions and a DHB site visit confirm completion [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part2.htm).

### 4. Complaints to HDC

HDC received 3,477 complaints in 2024/25 (a 4% decrease on the previous year but 48% above pre-pandemic levels), and closed a record 4,406 (a 40% increase), with a net clearance rate of 127% [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The number of complaints naming an "aged residential care facility" as the most common organisation complained about was 154 in 2024/25, down from 184 in 2023/24, 185 in 2022/23, and 183 in 2021/22 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

Around 18% of complaints to HDC are about the care provided to older people, and the most common primary issues in complaints about older people's care are inadequate/inappropriate treatment (13%), delay in treatment (9%), missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (9%), and general safety issue in facility (5%) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

A historical HDC analysis of complaints about residential aged care facilities from 2010 to 2014 found 502 complaints over five years (about 100 per year), with failure to communicate effectively with family the most common issue (over half of cases), falls at issue in 20% of cases, deteriorating condition in 22%, and fluid/nutrition and pain management each in 15–16% [HDC, Residential Aged Care Complaints 2010–2014](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf).

HDC cautions that complaint volume is not a direct quality measure: the OAG's 2012 analysis noted that "the volume of complaints did not necessarily reflect the quality of care and that different factors can influence the volume of complaints," including whether a complainant used a rest home's internal complaints procedure first [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm).

### 5. HDC breach findings and enforcement

In 2024/25 HDC closed 190 investigations and commenced 160 new investigations; 145 investigations resulted in a finding that the provider breached the Code, and 177 resulted in recommendations being made [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

A residential aged-care provider was found in breach of the Code in 2024/25 for "a concerning pattern of demeaning and disrespectful treatment by multiple staff members towards a woman in their care"; the Aged Care Commissioner found the "continued widespread and repeated actions by staff reflected a culture of disrespect and disregard for the dignity of people under the care of the provider" [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

Published HDC decision 24HDC02350 records that McKenzie Healthcare accepted proposed Right 4(1) and 4(2) breaches on 4 October 2024 for systems issues contributing to poor care for three residents [HDC decision 24HDC02350](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/24hdc02350/).

Published HDC decision 21HDC02752 records that the Aged Care Commissioner found Presbyterian Support Southland breached Right 4(1) of the Code in the care of a rest home resident [HDC decision 21HDC02752](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2024/21hdc02752/).

HealthCERT holds the primary enforcement responsibility: where audits identify weaknesses, the Ministry may require corrective action, add conditions to certification, require more frequent audits, and impose fines for offering health services without certification or for obstructing or misleading an audit [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

DHBs (now Health NZ) do not hold regulatory enforcement powers but hold funding-contract levers under the Aged Related Residential Care Services (ARRCS) contract, including the ability to appoint a temporary manager or cancel funding [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

Where an HDC investigation finds a breach of the Code, the Commissioner may lay a disciplinary charge with the Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal (for registered health professionals) or the Human Rights Review Tribunal (for other providers), and may publish the provider's name unless it would not be in the public interest or would unfairly compromise privacy [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

### 6. The Aged Care Commissioner's monitoring work

The Aged Care Commissioner is a statutory role within HDC that advocates for older people, monitors the responsiveness of the health and disability system to their needs, and drives quality improvement [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

In March 2024 the Aged Care Commissioner (Carolyn Cooper) published a monitoring report, "Amplifying the voices of older people in Aotearoa New Zealand," making 20 recommendations to improve care for older people; the report's insights emphasised transitions of care, investing in primary and community care models, preventative interventions for dementia, and reliable home and community services [Aged Care Commissioner, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

In 2024/25 the Aged Care Commissioner undertook 162 sector engagements to promote the monitoring report's recommendations and 53 engagements with older people to understand their experiences; HDC is actively monitoring implementation of the 20 recommendations and will report through 2025/26 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The Aged Care Commissioner resigned in August 2025; recruitment for a new Commissioner was under way at the time of the annual report [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The Commissioner works closely with HealthCERT and the Office for Seniors, and the Aged Care Commissioner is a statutory decision-maker on complaints, providing oversight of the issues about which older people are concerned and driving quality improvement through recommendations [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

### 7. Ngā Paerewa implementation findings

The Ministry commissioned an independent evaluation of the implementation of Ngā Paerewa (NZS 8134:2021), which found that implementation varied among providers: some required minimal changes, others larger adjustments; the level of support needed depended on location, experience, organisation size and availability of dedicated resources [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

Aged Residential Care (ARC) facilities specifically struggled with the new restraint criteria (Section 6 of Ngā Paerewa), and requests for support on Section 6 were mostly from ARC providers [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

A common struggle with health workforce shortage was identified across all stakeholder groups as a key factor in how well providers could action Ngā Paerewa; the evaluation concluded this "requires a systems-wide approach and cannot be solely remedied by the providers or the MOH" [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

Provider confidence was measured at 68% agreeing they could meet Ngā Paerewa standards (9% disagreeing); only 54% agreed the grace period duration was sufficient; and group care facilities were less likely to find the grace period sufficient (48%) than individual/standalone facilities (57%) [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

Many facilities, particularly smaller standalone facilities, did not have resources to support them in making changes; the evaluation recommended that the "educator" role cannot be filled by the regulator or auditor, so other resourcing is required such as resourcing national organisations, funding an educator, or setting expectations with contract holders [Ministry of Health, Ngā Paerewa implementation evaluation](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2024-05/final_nga_paerewa_report_accessible_pdf.pdf).

### 8. The audit-quality caveat: process improvement ≠ resident outcome improvement

The Office of the Auditor-General's 2012 follow-up report on rest home services found that while the quality of the auditing and certification process had improved (through third-party accreditation of DAAs, the integrated audit approach, faster response to identified issues, and ongoing monitoring), "the effect that these improvements have had on the quality of care delivered to rest home residents is less certain" [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm).

The OAG found that DHB planning and funding managers' satisfaction with certification as reliable assurance of quality of care was mixed: only 2 of 18 were "very satisfied" that certification provides reliable assurance of quality, 9 were "fairly satisfied," and 6 were "neither satisfied nor dissatisfied" [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm).

The OAG found that issues-based audits by DHBs were still identifying major safety and quality issues that "should have been picked up during the certification audits," and that certification audits were "more of a systems' and processes' audit rather than an audit of individual care" [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm).

Respondents to the OAG's public survey felt the certification process was "generally too paper-based," described as "an exercise more concerned with compliance than outcomes for residents," and suggested spending more time with residents and families and actively observing staff behaviour [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up, Part 4](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm).

### 9. The fragmented complaints-architecture problem

The aged care system has multiple complaints avenues — a provider's internal procedure, HDC, the DHB/Ministry of Health, the Disputes Tribunal, and investigations by police, ACC or the coroner — with no formal division of labour about treatment of complaints between the Ministry, DHBs and HDC [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

The Treasury/Productivity Commission case study found that "under current arrangements, there is not systemic collection and analysis of complaints from the four main aged care complaints avenues," and that multiple avenues may confuse the public, dissuade complaints, lead to duplicative complaints, and make it harder to use complaints data to identify system-wide issues [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

In 2012/13, 111 complaints were lodged with HDC about rest homes and 156 were logged with the Ministry of Health, but "not all complaints logged with the Ministry were managed by the regulator" and "it was not unusual for the same complaint to be made to several organisations" [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A current public dataset joining audit attainment, CARs, certification periods, HDC complaints and resident outcomes by provider or region would change the core conclusion** — this finding says no such joined dataset is publicly available, but if Health NZ or the Ministry has published one since the research date, the "not publicly joined" claim would be overturned. [Ministry of Health, Rest homes](https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/certification-of-health-care-services/certified-providers/rest-homes); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf)
- **A post-2012 OAG or Ministry evaluation showing that audit process improvements now demonstrably link to resident outcome measures** would upgrade the audit-quality confidence from Medium; the 2012 OAG finding is the most recent comprehensive audit-quality review located, and it found the link uncertain. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2012 follow-up](https://ao.parliament.nz/2012/rest-home-services-follow-up/part4.htm)
- **A current systematic complaints-data integration across HDC, HealthCERT, Health NZ and provider-internal complaints** would address the fragmentation finding; the Treasury/Productivity Commission case study identified this gap in 2014 and no source found confirms it has been closed. [Treasury / NZ Productivity Commission, Case study: aged care regulation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf)
- **The Aged Care Commissioner's 2025/26 implementation monitoring report** (forthcoming) may quantify sector progress against the 20 recommendations and would be the first current, official, system-level quality-variation assessment; this finding should be updated when that report publishes.
