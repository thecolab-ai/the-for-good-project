# Who supplies and administers peptides in NZ, what oversight applies, and is the unfunded GLP-1 gap driving grey-market substitution?

## Executive answer

- New Zealand has **no peptide-specific regulatory regime**. Peptides fall under the general Medicines Act 1981 framework: some (GLP-1 agonists used for diabetes/weight loss) are fully approved, brand-name prescription medicines; others are "unapproved medicines" that a doctor can access on a named-patient basis only under **section 29** of the Act; and, since a Medicines Classification Committee decision reported as taking effect around **December 2025**, a growing list of peptide groups has been reclassified as prescription medicines, making unauthorised import/possession a breach of section 43 of the Act [1] [2] [3]. Medsafe's own June 2025 submission to that committee separately recommended a mitochondria-derived-peptide group entry (encompassing MOTS-c) for the same prescription-medicine treatment [19], though we could not confirm this specific recommendation was among those adopted in the reported December 2025 decision (see below).
- **Legitimate clinical supply** runs through two channels: (a) doctors prescribing Medsafe-approved products (Ozempic/Wegovy/Mounjaro) dispensed by ordinary pharmacies, and (b) doctors using a section 29 authorisation for an unapproved peptide, dispensed by a **compounding pharmacy** under Pharmacy Council competence standard 6.3 and the Health and Disability Services Pharmacy Services Standard NZS 8134.7 [4] [5]. This pathway exists in principle and is described by at least one real NZ private/functional-medicine clinic, but we could not independently confirm how widely it is actually used for wellness peptides (see limits, below).
- **Oversight of doctors** who prescribe/administer peptides for cosmetic, anti-ageing or performance purposes comes from three general-purpose Medical Council of New Zealand (MCNZ) statements — none written for peptides specifically: *Cosmetic procedures* (Nov 2017), *Prescribing* (Feb 2024), and *Sport and performance enhancing medicines* (Jun 2022, explicitly covering physique-enhancement as well as competitive sport) — plus the Health and Disability Commissioner's Code of Rights, which any provider (doctor or otherwise) can be judged against [6] [7] [8] [9].
- **Compounding pharmacists** are separately bound by Pharmacy Council competence standard 6.3 ("compounds extemporaneous therapeutic products safely and effectively"), which requires a documented quality-assurance system and compliance with NZS 8134.7 [5].
- Outside the clinical channel, NZ Herald, RNZ and 1News reporting through 2025–2026 documents a fast-growing **grey/black market** supplied by gyms, bodybuilding networks, social-media sellers (TikTok/Instagram/WhatsApp/Reddit) and overseas websites labelling products "for research purposes only" — a labelling loophole Medsafe explicitly says has no legal effect [10] [11] [12] [13].
- On the GLP-1 question specifically: Wegovy and Mounjaro are Medsafe-approved but **not Pharmac-funded**, costing roughly NZ$370–800/month privately [14] [15]. RNZ reporting on Māori and Pasifika access directly quotes a source describing friends and whānau turning to "the grey market and offshore websites to obtain the illicit peptide Retatrutide" because of cost, and a second outlet independently reports the same retatrutide substitution pattern tied to price/access barriers — but neither outlet, nor any other NZ source found, supplies **quantified data** (numbers of users, seizure volumes attributable to weight-loss motive, poisoning-centre calls) linking the funding gap to grey-market demand [16] [17]. This is a real, evidenced pattern at the level of individual testimony, not yet a measured trend.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the regulatory framework (Medicines Act, section 29, MCNZ/Pharmacy Council standards) is High confidence, drawn from primary official sources. The supply-channel picture for wellness/anti-ageing peptides is Medium (good NZ journalism, but thin institutional data). The causal claim that unfunded GLP-1s are *driving* grey-market substitution is Low–Medium: directionally supported by named individuals in NZ reporting, but not by any dataset.

## Evidence

### The legal/regulatory skeleton

Peptides are medicines under the Medicines Act 1981 and, if imported for human use without approval, fall into "unapproved medicine" territory, meaning Customs NZ can seize them at the border [3]. Medsafe's classification status determines whether a given peptide can be lawfully supplied at all:

- **Approved and Pharmac-listed-for-consideration, but not funded**: semaglutide (Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) are Medsafe-approved for weight management; Pharmac has added Wegovy to its "Options for Investment" list following a December 2025 Obesity Treatment Advisory Group recommendation, but has not funded it, and has not even received a funding application for Mounjaro's weight-loss indication [14] [15].
- **Unapproved but accessible on a named-patient basis**: section 29 of the Medicines Act 1981 lets a medical practitioner, nurse practitioner or pharmacist prescriber obtain an unapproved medicine for a specific named patient; a separate provision inserted by the same 19 November 2025 amendment, **section 29B**, requires the importer/manufacturer to report trade name, dose form, strength, pack size and quantity supplied to the Director-General monthly, and to keep (and provide on request) the prescriber's name, the patient's name and the place of supply [4] [18].
- **Newly prescription-only**: multiple 2025–2026 news reports state that in **December 2025**, Medsafe's Medicines Classification Committee reclassified a number of previously unscheduled peptide groups as prescription medicines, following a submission process that had been under consideration since at least mid-2025 [1] [2]. We treat the *fact* of a classification action as Medium-High confidence (two independent NZ outlets report it consistently), but flag that the precise list of affected peptides and Gazette notice numbers were not confirmed against a primary Medsafe/Gazette document during this research — that level of legislative detail is the subject of the companion research issue on classification status (issue tracked separately in this stream), and duplicating it in full is out of scope here. Medsafe's June 2025 submission to the Committee [19] — a normal text-layer PDF, machine-readable via `pdftotext` — is a **proposal, not an adoption record**: it recommends group-entry, prescription-medicine classification for ten peptide categories (including a "mitochondria-derived peptides" group that names MOTS-c, humanin and SHLP peptides), but nothing in [19] itself confirms which of these ten were the ones the Committee actually adopted in the December decision reported by [1] [2] — neither of which names MOTS-c or any other specific peptide.
- **Genuinely unapproved anywhere**: retatrutide is not approved by Medsafe, the FDA, or any other trusted regulator and remains in Eli Lilly's TRIUMPH Phase 3 trial programme; anything sold under that name in NZ is, by definition, outside any approval pathway [20] [21].

Medsafe's own consumer advisory is unambiguous that labelling peptides "for research purposes only" is a marketing device with no legal effect, that supplying such products for human use is unlawful, and that products sold this way have not been assessed for quality, safety or efficacy — with named risks including contamination, non-sterility, and (for retatrutide specifically) reports of fatal overdose overseas [11].

### Who supplies/administers peptides in NZ

**Legitimate clinical channel.** The cleanest documented pathway is: a doctor (commonly a private GP or "functional medicine" practitioner) writes a section 29 authorisation for a named patient, and a compounding pharmacy prepares the peptide to order. This is described consistently across several secondary/commercial sources (peptide-focused blogs and telehealth marketing sites) [22] [23], but because these are vendor-adjacent sources — exactly the kind CONTRIBUTING.md flags as "a claim, not confirmation" — we sought corroboration from an actual operating NZ clinic. We found one: **Autonomy Health** (Remuera, Auckland), whose lead physician's public post states the clinic does **not** prescribe "experimental peptides without robust safety data, regulated supply pathways, and a defined monitoring framework," while distinguishing them clearly from approved GLP-1 medicines, and noting only that some peptides "may be accessed under specific unapproved medicine provisions" (i.e., section 29) without claiming the clinic itself does so [24]. This confirms the pathway is understood and referenced by at least one real practice, but is not proof of how common it is. NZ's largest compounding pharmacy by self-description, CompoundLabs, did not list any peptide products in its public online catalogue at the time of this research [25] — suggesting compounded-peptide supply, if it exists at scale, is arranged privately rather than advertised, or is simply uncommon.

**Grey/black market channel.** NZ Herald, RNZ and 1News reporting through 2025–2026 is consistent and detailed on this point:
- Peptides are "picked up in the gym" and through bodybuilding/personal networks, alongside heavy marketing by social-media influencers on TikTok and Instagram [10].
- A 1News investigation obtained a vial from an independent seller contacted via social media within 42 minutes, for NZ$200/10mg, with no dosing or injection guidance provided — buyers reportedly turn to AI chatbots for injection instructions instead [12].
- The NZ Drug Foundation and the Needle Exchange Programme both report a "massive" rise in enquiries about injecting steroids, peptides and weight-loss drugs over the past 18 months [10]. A separate, independent 1News investigation reports the same trend from its own angle: NZ Drug Foundation programme lead Emily Hughes says peptide queries "were rare at the start of 2025" but are now daily, a shift the article ties to Google Trends data showing a jump in New Zealanders searching terms like "peptides", "buy peptides" and "peptides nz" since January 2025 [1].
- Vendors evade the Medicines Act by labelling products "for research purposes only" or "not for human consumption" while marketing and dosing them for human use [11] [12] [13].
- Direct-to-consumer NZ-facing online retailers selling BPC-157, CJC-1295/Ipamorelin, GHK-Cu and other peptides (e.g. "NZ Peptides," various "direct-peptides"/"direct-sarms" storefronts) are easily found by an ordinary web search and operate with no evident prescription requirement [26].

**Wellness/anti-ageing clinics specifically.** NZ Herald's feature on the social-media-driven "peptide therapy boom" profiles individuals (not formal clinics) who obtained peptides via a self-described "functional medicine doctor... importing peptides from the US," and separately via unnamed online/social channels; it quotes Dr David Gerrard (University of Otago) calling unapproved peptide use "dangerous" [13]. We could not find a published case of a *named* NZ wellness or anti-ageing clinic being identified in news reporting, Medical Council disciplinary decisions, or Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal decisions specifically over peptide supply — this is a genuine evidence gap, not an indication that it isn't happening (see "what would change this conclusion").

### Professional oversight that applies

No New Zealand professional body has issued peptide-specific guidance. Instead, general standards apply by extension:

- **MCNZ – Cosmetic procedures (Nov 2017).** Classifies non-surgical procedures (including injections) as "Category 2," settable by doctors with accredited cosmetic-medicine training, or others in a "collegial relationship" with an accredited practitioner; requires informed consent, and states the statement itself "may be used by the Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal, the Council and the Health and Disability Commissioner as a standard by which your conduct is measured" [6]. It does not mention peptides by name.
- **MCNZ – Sport and performance enhancing medicines (Jun 2022).** The most directly relevant standard found: it explicitly covers medicines used "for aesthetic purposes related to sport, such as to enhance an individual's physique" (not just competitive doping), states doctors must not prescribe or advise on substances "for the deliberate purpose of enhancing performance," requires doctors to discuss patients' supplement/substance sourcing and contamination risk, and again notes it can be used as a Tribunal/HDC conduct standard [7].
- **MCNZ – Prescribing (Feb 2024) / Principles for Quality and Safe Prescribing (Sep 2024, joint across seven responsible authorities).** General good-prescribing-practice standard that would govern any doctor's section 29 authorisation for a peptide [27].
- **Pharmacy Council – Competence Standards for Aotearoa New Zealand Pharmacists, Domain 6, Competency 6.3** ("Compounds extemporaneous therapeutic products safely and effectively"): requires pharmacists to source/produce quality extemporaneous products only when no proprietary product exists, apply pharmaceutics knowledge, follow "professional practices, conventions, applicable legislation, and standards for product preparation," and comply with the Health and Disability Services Pharmacy Services Standard NZS 8134.7 on dispensing and compounding — with a documented, monitored quality-assurance system [5].
- **Health and Disability Commissioner (HDC).** The Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights applies to any health or disability service provider (not just doctors/pharmacists) and gives consumers a right to services of an appropriate standard and to make a complaint; the MCNZ statements above are explicitly designed to be used as a Tribunal/HDC yardstick [8] [6] [7]. We searched for HDC or Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal decisions specifically concerning peptide supply by a wellness/cosmetic clinic and found none publicly indexed — an evidence gap, not a finding that none exist (HDC case summaries are not exhaustively searchable by ingredient).
- **Gyms and non-clinical sellers** fall outside all of the above: none of these professional oversight regimes reach a gym owner, personal trainer, or online seller who is not a registered health practitioner. That gap is precisely why Medsafe/Customs enforcement (seizure, prosecution threat under the Medicines Act) is the only lever available against that channel [1] [11].

### Does the unfunded GLP-1 status drive grey-market substitution?

This is the least settled part of the question. What is solid:

- Wegovy and Mounjaro are approved but unfunded, at private prices of roughly NZ$370–800/month depending on drug and dose [14] [15].
- RNZ's reporting on Māori and Pasifika access to weight-loss drugs quotes a Pasifika patient, Sara, stating that "friends, whānau and those she follows on social media have turned to the grey market and offshore websites to obtain the illicit peptide Retatrutide — or what they hope is Retatrutide" — explicitly because of cost, in an article whose broader argument is that price is "the main barrier for Pasifika" accessing the approved drugs [16].
- The 1News investigation into how easily peptides could be bought did not frame its purchase around weight loss specifically, and Medsafe's own consumer advisory names retatrutide as a product of concern independent of any funding-gap framing [11] [12].

What is missing: no NZ source we found — official (Medsafe, Pharmac, Ministry of Health) or journalistic — offers a **quantified** estimate of how many people are substituting grey-market peptides specifically because Wegovy/Mounjaro are unfunded, versus other motives (curiosity, social-media hype, cost of *any* legitimate weight-loss pathway including bariatric surgery, or peptides sought for indications other than weight loss). The Australian precedent that likely motivated this question — where compounding-pharmacy versions of semaglutide/tirzepatide were used by an estimated ~20,000 patients before Australia's 1 October 2024 ban on compounding these specific molecules, in response to global brand-name shortages [28] [29] [30] — does not appear to have a direct NZ analogue: we found no evidence of NZ compounding pharmacies compounding semaglutide or tirzepatide at any scale (CompoundLabs' public catalogue lists none), which suggests the *compounding-pharmacy* route to a "replica" GLP-1 that broke the rules in Australia has not taken hold here in the same form. Instead, the NZ pattern documented in reporting is substitution toward an **entirely unapproved, non-GLP-1-identical** compound (retatrutide, still in trials) bought from **overseas websites and social media**, not from a NZ-licensed compounding pharmacy. That is a materially different — and arguably riskier — supply chain than Australia's, because it carries no NZ pharmacist quality-assurance obligation at all.

## What would change this conclusion

- A Medsafe, Pharmac or Ministry of Health dataset (poisoning-centre calls, CARM adverse-event reports, or a patient survey) that quantifies how many grey-market peptide users cite GLP-1 cost/non-funding as their reason would let us upgrade the causal claim from Low-Medium to High, or refute it if the actual driver turns out to be unrelated (e.g. general fitness/aesthetic culture, not weight-loss cost).
- Any Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal or HDC decision naming a specific NZ wellness/anti-ageing clinic or compounding pharmacy over peptide supply would materially sharpen the "who supplies" picture — we searched but did not find one; it may exist but not be indexed under peptide-related search terms, or genuinely may not yet have occurred.
- Direct confirmation from Pharmac or Medsafe of the precise post-classification legal status and Gazette references (rather than triangulated news reporting) would raise the classification claim to High confidence — this overlaps with the companion research question on classification status in this stream and should be reconciled with that finding once published.
- Evidence that a NZ compounding pharmacy has compounded semaglutide/tirzepatide (the Australian pattern) would change the "no direct Australian analogue" conclusion; we did not find this, but our check was limited to one compounding pharmacy's public catalogue plus general search — a targeted survey of the ~dozen or so identifiable NZ compounding pharmacies was out of scope here.

## Open follow-up questions

- What does CARM (Centre for Adverse Reactions Monitoring) or National Poisons Centre data show for peptide-related calls/reports in 2024–2026, and can any be attributed to weight-loss motive specifically? (Overlaps with this stream's harm-evidence research question.)
- Has any NZ compounding pharmacy been asked, formally or informally, to compound a GLP-1 analogue, and if so how did the Pharmacy Council or Medsafe respond? A direct approach to the Pharmaceutical Society of NZ or a compounding-pharmacy trade body could answer this but requires a human researcher with sector access.
- Would extending the MCNZ's "Sport and performance enhancing medicines" framing explicitly to non-sport aesthetic/wellness peptide use (it already technically covers physique enhancement) close part of the oversight gap, or does enforcement capacity (not policy wording) remain the binding constraint? This is a judgement question better suited to the ideation stage of this stream.
