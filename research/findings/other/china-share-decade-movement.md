# NZ's China export share rose to a 2021 goods peak, then fell back as commodity cycles and COVID-disrupted services moved in different directions

Part of #438. This answers issue #499: how NZ's China share changed over the past decade, distinguishing price cycles, volumes, sector mix, COVID-era travel and education disruption, and policy changes.

## Executive answer

- **Goods exposure rose sharply, then partially unwound.** Calendar-year goods exports to China rose from **17.6%** of NZ merchandise exports in 2015 to **31.6%** in 2021, then fell to **24.8%** in 2025; the latest year-ended May 2026 workbook gives a similar current goods share of **24.1%** (**NZ$19.885b** of **NZ$82.665b**) [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/); [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx). **Claim confidence: High** for direction and magnitude, because it is official Stats NZ customs data; **Medium** for exact current percentage because it is single-source arithmetic.
- **The rise was mainly a sector-mix story, not a broad China story.** Dairy, meat, logs/wood, and fruit accounted for **64.3%** of China-bound goods exports in 2015 and **76.4%** in 2021, and they explain **NZ$9.78b** of the **NZ$11.43b** increase in China-bound goods exports from 2015 to 2021 [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/). **Claim confidence: Medium-High**, because the sector aggregation is my reproducible HS-code grouping, not a separately published Stats NZ table.
- **Price and volume both mattered, but differently by sector.** In the kg-measured part of the dairy grouping, China-bound quantities roughly doubled from **591m kg** in 2015 to **1,323m kg** in 2021 while the customs unit-value proxy rose from **NZ$3.82/kg** to **NZ$5.27/kg**; by 2025 quantity had fallen to **1,004m kg** but the unit-value proxy had risen to **NZ$7.59/kg**, so dairy's nominal China value stayed high [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/). For logs, China-bound log quantity was similar in 2021 and 2025, but the unit-value proxy fell from **NZ$175/m3** to **NZ$158/m3**, which helps explain the nominal pullback [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+ international trade methodology](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/3c8beb56-3dd7-463f-8f7c-68cd3e5f1b2d/). **Claim confidence: Medium**, because these are customs value/quantity proxies, not pure price indexes.
- **Services made China exposure look different before and after COVID.** In the year ended March 2020, China exports were **NZ$19.888b**, including **NZ$16.779b** goods and **NZ$3.109b** services; China travel services were **NZ$2.788b**, including **NZ$1.244b** education travel [Stats NZ goods and services trade by country, year ended March 2020 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/goods-and-services-trade-by-country-year-ended-march-2020.xlsx). In the March 2021 quarter, China travel exports had dropped to **NZ$173m**; by the March 2026 quarter they had recovered to **NZ$1.034b**, but China's total goods-and-services export share was **20.3%**, below the goods-only share [Stats NZ international trade March 2021 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2021-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-goods-and-services-march-2021-quarter-corrected.xlsx); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx). **Claim confidence: High** for the measured services movement.
- **Policy explains less of the decade movement than demand, prices, sector mix, and border settings.** The China FTA upgrade entered force in April 2022, after the 2021 goods-share peak, and reduced further tariffs/compliance frictions; later policy settings are mostly about re-growing services and opening other markets, including the 2025 International Education Going for Growth Plan and the 2025 Chinese-passport NZeTA trial from Australia [MFAT China FTA upgrade](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/fta-upgrade); [ENZ Going for Growth Plan announcement](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/government-announces-international-education-going-for-growth-plan); [Immigration NZ, Chinese visitors from Australia](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/). **Claim confidence: Medium**, because attribution is interpretive even where the policy dates are clear.

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the goods and services measurements are mostly official Stats NZ data and high confidence; the explanation is medium confidence because it combines official time series with sector aggregations and causal interpretation; the education channel is supported by the Ministry of Education's Export Education Levy workbook and ENZ enrolment sources, but a China-specific education-revenue figure still cannot be isolated because the workbook's fee and levy sheets report dollar values by education sector rather than citizenship [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx); [ENZ 2015 international education snapshot PDF](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2045-ENZ-SnapShot-Full-Year-Report-VISUAL.pdf); [ENZ 2025 enrolment release](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

## Evidence

### Scope and calculation method

I measured "China share" in four channels: calendar-year merchandise exports from Stats NZ's annual HS10-by-country export CSV files for 2015-2025; latest merchandise exports from Stats NZ's May 2026 overseas merchandise trade workbook; annual pre-COVID goods and services from Stats NZ's year-ended March 2020 goods-and-services-by-country workbook; and March-quarter goods and services from Stats NZ's international trade workbooks for 2021-2026 [Stats NZ overseas merchandise trade datasets](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/); [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx); [Stats NZ goods and services trade by country, year ended March 2020 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/goods-and-services-trade-by-country-year-ended-march-2020.xlsx); [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

The goods-sector grouping used the same commodity definitions as the Stats NZ current trade workbook where possible: dairy is HS 0401-0406, not the full HS 04 chapter; meat is HS 02; fruit is HS 0803-0814; logs/wood is HS 44; and the log unit-value proxy uses HS 4403 records with cubic-metre quantity units [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx); [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/). The exact dairy filter in the annual HS10 CSVs is `country == "China, People's Republic of"` and `hs[:4] in {"0401","0402","0403","0404","0405","0406"}`; that intentionally excludes HS 0409 honey and HS 0410 edible products of animal origin, which are in chapter 04 but outside this dairy grouping. Dairy sector-value totals include every `uom` under that filter, while the dairy unit-value proxy below uses only the same filtered rows with `uom == "KGM"` [2015 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2015_Exports_HS10_by_Country.zip); [2021 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2021_Exports_HS10_by_country.zip); [2025 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2025_Exports_HS10_by_Country.zip).

The price/volume discussion uses customs unit-value proxies, calculated as export FOB value divided by export quantity after applying the same sector and unit-of-measure filter; these proxies are useful for decomposing nominal movement but can be affected by product mix, quality, and composition, whereas the official merchandise trade price and volume indexes are designed to measure price and volume changes more formally [Stats NZ DataInfo+ international trade methodology](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/3c8beb56-3dd7-463f-8f7c-68cd3e5f1b2d/); [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

### Goods: a 2015-2021 surge, then a share fall

Stats NZ annual HS10-by-country files show China-bound goods exports rising from **NZ$8.611b** in calendar 2015 to **NZ$20.040b** in 2021, while total NZ goods exports rose from **NZ$48.974b** to **NZ$63.494b**; China's goods share therefore rose from **17.6%** to **31.6%** [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

The same annual files show China-bound goods exports were **NZ$20.132b** in 2022, **NZ$18.419b** in 2023, **NZ$17.745b** in 2024, and **NZ$19.934b** in 2025; because total goods exports rose to **NZ$80.519b** in 2025, China's goods share fell from the 2021 peak to **24.8%** in 2025 [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

The latest Stats NZ May 2026 merchandise workbook gives a current cross-check: in the year ended May 2026, total merchandise exports were **NZ$82.665b**, China exports were **NZ$19.885b**, Australia exports were **NZ$10.943b**, and United States exports were **NZ$9.734b**, which puts China's current goods share at **24.1%** [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx).

The goods-share path is therefore not a steady linear "decade rise": it is a 2015-2021 concentration surge followed by a 2022-2026 partial unwind [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/); [Stats NZ OMT May 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Overseas-merchandise-trade/Overseas-merchandise-trade-May-2026/Download-data/overseas-merchandise-trade-may-2026.xlsx).

### Sector mix: four sectors explain most of the rise

Dairy, meat, logs/wood, and fruit accounted for **NZ$5.540b** of **NZ$8.611b** China-bound goods exports in 2015, or **64.3%** of the China total; by 2021, the same four sectors accounted for **NZ$15.319b** of **NZ$20.040b**, or **76.4%** of the China total [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

From 2015 to 2021, China-bound goods exports rose **NZ$11.428b**; dairy contributed **NZ$4.825b**, meat **NZ$2.498b**, logs/wood **NZ$2.008b**, fruit **NZ$0.446b**, and all other goods **NZ$1.651b** [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

China also became a larger destination for these sectors through the upswing: China took **21.0%** of NZ dairy exports in 2015 and **42.5%** in 2021; **17.7%** of meat in 2015 and **42.4%** in 2021; **44.1%** of logs/wood in 2015 and **64.7%** in 2021; and **15.6%** of fruit in 2015 and **20.7%** in 2021 [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

The post-2021 movement is not just "China down" across every sector: from 2021 to 2025, China-bound dairy rose **NZ$0.639b** and fruit rose **NZ$0.799b**, while meat fell **NZ$1.631b** and logs/wood fell **NZ$0.404b**; the net China-bound goods total was almost flat across those two endpoints, falling only **NZ$0.106b** [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

MPI's latest SOPI framing supports why these sectors dominate NZ export value: the December 2025 update forecast food and fibre export revenue of **NZ$62b** for the year to 30 June 2026, with dairy at **NZ$27.4b**, meat and wool at **NZ$13.2b**, forestry at **NZ$6.3b**, and horticulture at **NZ$9.2b** [MPI SOPI page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/resources-and-forms/economic-intelligence/situation-and-outlook-for-primary-industries); [MPI SOPI data page](https://www.mpi.govt.nz/science/open-data-and-forecasting/situation-and-outlook-for-primary-industries-data).

### Price and volume: nominal exports moved for different reasons by sector

Using the HS 0401-0406 China filter above, whole-sector dairy exports rose from **NZ$2.419b** in 2015 to **NZ$7.245b** in 2021 and **NZ$7.883b** in 2025. Inside the KGM-only subset used for the dairy unit-value proxy, value, quantity, and unit value moved from **NZ$2.256b**, **591m kg**, and **NZ$3.82/kg** in 2015 to **NZ$6.976b**, **1,323m kg**, and **NZ$5.27/kg** in 2021, then **NZ$7.619b**, **1,004m kg**, and **NZ$7.59/kg** in 2025 [2015 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2015_Exports_HS10_by_Country.zip); [2021 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2021_Exports_HS10_by_country.zip); [2025 Stats NZ exports HS10 by country ZIP](https://www3.stats.govt.nz/HS10_by_Country/2025_Exports_HS10_by_Country.zip).

China-bound meat exports rose from **NZ$1.211b** in 2015 to **NZ$3.709b** in 2021, then fell to **NZ$2.078b** in 2025; the quantity proxy rose from **219m kg** in 2015 to **461m kg** in 2021 and fell to **278m kg** in 2025, while the unit-value proxy rose from **NZ$5.53/kg** in 2015 to **NZ$8.05/kg** in 2021 and eased to **NZ$7.47/kg** in 2025 [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

China-bound logs/wood exports rose from **NZ$1.551b** in 2015 to **NZ$3.559b** in 2021, then fell to **NZ$3.156b** in 2025; for HS 4403 logs, the quantity proxy was **10.52m m3** in 2015, **19.77m m3** in 2021, and **19.17m m3** in 2025, while the unit-value proxy moved from **NZ$131/m3** to **NZ$175/m3** and then **NZ$158/m3** [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

China-bound fruit exports rose from **NZ$0.359b** in 2015 to **NZ$0.806b** in 2021 and **NZ$1.605b** in 2025; the quantity proxy rose from **88.9m kg** in 2015 to **156.5m kg** in 2021 and **278.5m kg** in 2025, while the unit-value proxy rose from **NZ$4.04/kg** to **NZ$5.15/kg** and then **NZ$5.76/kg** [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

This means the 2015-2021 concentration surge combined volume growth and better unit values, while the 2021-2025 pullback was sector-specific: the kg-measured dairy subset stayed high mainly on unit value, meat fell on lower China-bound quantity and lower unit value, logs/wood fell mainly on unit value, and fruit rose mostly on volume [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/); [Stats NZ DataInfo+ international trade methodology](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/3c8beb56-3dd7-463f-8f7c-68cd3e5f1b2d/).

### Services, travel, and education: COVID moved the denominator and the channel mix

In the year ended March 2016, Stats NZ reported exports to China of **NZ$11.731b**, made up of **NZ$8.736b** goods and **NZ$2.994b** services; by the year ended March 2020, exports to China were **NZ$19.888b**, made up of **NZ$16.779b** goods and **NZ$3.109b** services [Stats NZ goods and services trade by country, year ended March 2020 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/goods-and-services-trade-by-country-year-ended-march-2020.xlsx).

The same workbook shows China's pre-COVID service exposure was mostly travel: China travel services were **NZ$2.625b** in the year ended March 2016 and **NZ$2.788b** in the year ended March 2020, with education travel rising from **NZ$876m** to **NZ$1.244b** over that period [Stats NZ goods and services trade by country, year ended March 2020 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country/Goods-and-services-trade-by-country-Year-ended-March-2020/Download-data/goods-and-services-trade-by-country-year-ended-march-2020.xlsx).

COVID-era border disruption changed the services picture quickly: China travel exports in Stats NZ's March-quarter international trade workbooks were **NZ$667.7m** in the March 2020 quarter and **NZ$172.9m** in the March 2021 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2021 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2021-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-goods-and-services-march-2021-quarter-corrected.xlsx), then **NZ$307.9m** in the March 2022 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2022 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2022-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2022-quarter.xlsx), **NZ$490.8m** in the March 2023 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2023 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2023-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2023-quarter.xlsx), **NZ$595.4m** in the March 2024 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2024 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2024-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2024-quarter.xlsx), **NZ$770.0m** in the March 2025 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2025 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2025-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2025-quarter.xlsx), and **NZ$1.034b** in the March 2026 quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

By the March 2026 quarter, China exports were **NZ$6.381b** of total NZ goods-and-services exports of **NZ$31.363b**, or **20.3%**; the same quarter's China goods exports were **NZ$5.215b** and China services exports were **NZ$1.166b**, so China was much more important for goods than services in that quarter [Stats NZ international trade March 2026 workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/International-trade/International-trade-March-2026-quarter/Download-data/international-trade-march-2026-quarter.xlsx).

Education evidence here should be read as an enrolment-share indicator, not a current China-specific education-revenue share. ENZ's 2015 international education snapshot put China at **27%** of international student enrolments, India at **23%**, and the top two markets at **50%** of enrolments in 2015 [ENZ 2015 international education snapshot PDF](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2045-ENZ-SnapShot-Full-Year-Report-VISUAL.pdf).

ENZ's April 2023 China insight said China accounted for **45%** of international student enrolments in 2021, even though enrolments from China fell **27.3%** from 2019 to 2020 and another **15.6%** from 2020 to 2021; that pattern is consistent with a COVID-era share effect where other source markets and border flows were also disrupted [ENZ China insight PDF, April 2023](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ_Intell%2BInsight_Apr23_v1.pdf).

ENZ's latest full-year release reported **92,580** international student enrolments in 2025, up **11%** on 2024 and **80%** of the pre-COVID peak, with China still the largest source at **34%** and India second at **14%** [ENZ 2025 enrolment release](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

The Ministry of Education's Export Education Levy full-year workbook (data correct as at 1 April 2026) now supports this China-as-largest-source pattern directly from administrative data on international fee-paying students. Its "Citizenship" sheet, titled "Selected citizenship by education sector 2015-2025", lists China explicitly in the top-level rows used here: row 15 for schools total, row 42 for total government-funded tertiary providers, and row 57 for total unfunded providers. Those rows show Chinese fee-paying students totalling **7,495** in schools, **15,455** across government-funded tertiary providers, and **2,025** in unfunded providers in 2025, against the all-country total rows 20, 55, and 66 of **19,865**, **51,050**, and **11,995** respectively; summed across those three top-level sectors that is roughly **24,975** of **82,910**, or about **30%** of fee-paying students. On the same basis China's fee-paying-student share was about **27%** in 2015 (**31,635** of **117,965**) and peaked near **41%** in 2021 (**15,140** of **37,395**) [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook, "Citizenship" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx).

The EEL 2021 peak is a share effect as well as an education-channel signal: China's EEL count fell from **31,635** in 2015 to **15,140** in 2021, but the all-country EEL total fell faster, from **117,965** to **37,395** [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook, "Citizenship" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). These headcount shares are approximate: the workbook notes that citizenship categories are selected based on 2023 student counts, students in funded providers are counted in each sub-sector they enrol in, and counts are rounded to the nearest 5 [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook, "Citizenship" sheet notes](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). They are broadly consistent with, but not identical to, ENZ's enrolment-share series: EEL gives about **27% / 41% / 30%** at 2015 / 2021 / 2025, while ENZ gives **27% / 45% / 34%** for the same endpoint years [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook, "Citizenship" sheet](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx); [ENZ 2015 international education snapshot PDF](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2045-ENZ-SnapShot-Full-Year-Report-VISUAL.pdf); [ENZ China insight PDF, April 2023](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/ENZ_Intell%2BInsight_Apr23_v1.pdf); [ENZ 2025 enrolment release](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments).

The education read is therefore: China's fee-paying-student and enrolment share is higher in 2025 than in 2015, but lower than the COVID-era 2021 peak; the sector's current recovery still has China as the largest source country [ENZ 2015 international education snapshot PDF](https://www.enz.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2045-ENZ-SnapShot-Full-Year-Report-VISUAL.pdf); [ENZ 2025 enrolment release](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/latest-data-shows-continued-growth-in-international-enrolments); [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). The workbook reports tuition-fee income and levy by education sector but not by citizenship, so it still cannot isolate a China-specific education-revenue figure [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook, "Levy", "Tuition Fee", and "Citizenship" sheets](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx); [data.govt.nz Education Counts dataset metadata](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/international-students-studying-in-new-zealand).

### Tourism: arrivals are still below 2019, but spend share is meaningful

Stats NZ reported **407,100** visitor arrivals from China in calendar 2019, down **9%** from 2018, and **1.54m** visitor arrivals from Australia in 2019 [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/).

In the year ended March 2026, Stats NZ reported **3.63m** overseas visitor arrivals, including **297,000** from China and **1.56m** from Australia, so China represented about **8.2%** of annual visitor arrivals compared with about **10.5%** in calendar 2019 [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/); [Stats NZ, visitor arrivals from China down in 2019](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/visitor-arrivals-from-china-down-in-2019/).

Stats NZ also reported a March-month mix shift: in March 2026, **39%** of visitor arrivals were from Australia, **15%** from the United States, and **7%** from China, compared with **36%**, **13%**, and **11%** respectively in March 2019 [Stats NZ international travel March 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/international-travel-march-2026/).

MBIE's IVS annual summary data reports **NZ$13.735b** of total international visitor spend in the year ended March 2026, including **NZ$1.463b** from China, so China's spend share was **10.7%**; the same IVS data reports China mean spend per visitor of **NZ$5,543**, above the all-country mean of **NZ$4,386** [MBIE IVS release page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism-research-and-data/tourism-data-releases/international-visitor-survey-ivs); [MBIE/TEIC IVS annual summary JSON](https://teic.mbie.govt.nz/ste/data/views/theEconomy/demand/IVSAnnual/ivsAnnualSummary.json).

Tourism policy is now pushing some China recovery rather than reducing China exposure: Immigration NZ's 12-month trial from 3 November 2025 lets Chinese passport holders with an eligible Australian visa visit NZ from Australia with an NZeTA for up to three months [Immigration NZ, Chinese visitors from Australia](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/).

### Policy changes: mostly timing and channel effects, not the main decade explanation

The New Zealand-China FTA upgrade entered force on **7 April 2022**, amended the 2008 FTA, eliminated further tariffs, and reduced compliance measures for exporters; because this date comes after the 2021 calendar-year goods-share peak, it cannot explain the main 2015-2021 goods-share rise [MFAT China FTA upgrade](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/fta-upgrade).

The China FTA upgrade may still matter at the margin for maintaining access and reducing frictions after 2022, but the observed post-2021 goods-share fall is better explained by the measured sector shifts in meat, logs/wood, dairy, and fruit, plus faster total-export growth outside China in 2025, than by a single China-policy switch [MFAT China FTA upgrade](https://www.mfat.govt.nz/en/trade/free-trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements-in-force/nz-china-free-trade-agreement/fta-upgrade); [Stats NZ annual HS10 by country export datasets, 2015-2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/large-datasets/csv-files-for-download/overseas-merchandise-trade-datasets/).

For education, the July 2025 International Education Going for Growth Plan targets doubling the sector's economic contribution to **NZ$7.2b** by 2034 and growing enrolments from **83,400** in 2024 to **105,000** in 2027 and **119,000** by 2034; those targets are current growth policy, not an explanation for the 2015-2025 China share already observed [ENZ Going for Growth Plan announcement](https://www.enz.govt.nz/news-and-research/ed-news/government-announces-international-education-going-for-growth-plan).

For tourism, the late-2025 NZeTA trial for Chinese-passport visitors from Australia is a current re-growth setting; it is too recent to explain the decade movement, but it could lift China's future visitor and spend shares if arrivals continue to respond [Immigration NZ, Chinese visitors from Australia](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/easier-travel-from-australia-to-new-zealand-for-chinese-visitors/).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Stats NZ Infoshare or Trade Dashboard extract with annual, destination-specific services exports by detailed service category through 2025 would improve the services time series and could change the exact goods-plus-services China share.
- Official sector price and volume indexes by destination, rather than HS value/quantity unit-value proxies, could change the price-versus-volume interpretation for dairy, meat, logs, and fruit.
- The archived Education Counts EEL workbook supplies historical China fee-paying-student counts by sector, moving the education channel partway to a volume narrative; a fee-income breakdown by citizenship, which the workbook's fee and levy sheets do not publish, would still be needed to complete the revenue narrative [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx).
- Firm-level or product-level evidence showing deliberate redirection from China to other markets at comparable prices would strengthen the role of diversification policy; evidence that the same sectors simply lost Chinese demand would weaken it.
- Revised MBIE IVS China estimates could change the tourism spend comparison because MBIE says the March 2026 release corrected a prior China/Macau/Hong Kong grouping issue in the IVS market breakdown [MBIE IVS release page](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/immigration-and-tourism/tourism-research-and-data/tourism-data-releases/international-visitor-survey-ivs).

I could not verify a China-specific education export *revenue* value: the EEL workbook gives China fee-paying-student counts in the "Citizenship" sheet but reports fee income only by sector in the "Levy" and "Tuition Fee" sheets [Education Counts, archived 2025 Export Education Levy workbook](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705114516id_/https://www.educationcounts.govt.nz/statistics/international-students-in-new-zealand/Export-Education-Levy-full-year-statistics-2025.xlsx). I also could not verify an official destination-specific price/volume decomposition or a second independent source for the exact 2015-2025 annual China goods-share calculations. I did not use confidential firm-level data, so this finding cannot say whether individual exporters intentionally diversified or merely experienced demand and price changes.
