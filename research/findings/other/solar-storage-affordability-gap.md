# Solar-plus-storage interventions help some NZ households, but do not by themselves close the renter and energy-hardship gap

## Executive answer

- Rooftop solar subsidies and low-cost finance can improve payback for owner-occupiers with suitable roofs, daytime load, and enough credit or rates-bill capacity, but they mainly reduce the upfront-cost barrier rather than the tenure, split-incentive, and fixed-charge barriers that keep many renters and low-income households out [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/); [Stats NZ, 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/home-ownership-increases-and-housing-quality-improves/); [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).
- The SolarSaver plug-in-solar saving claim is plausible only at the favourable end: a two-panel, 0.8 kW kit at MBIE's 14% solar PV capacity-factor assumption would generate about 981 kWh/year, so it saves about $336/year only if nearly all output offsets a roughly 34 c/kWh household bill; lower self-consumption, shading, zero/low export value, or a smaller kit pushes savings below $300 [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver); [MBIE, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/energy-in-new-zealand-2023.pdf); [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/); [Powerswitch, 2026](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates).
- Community batteries are promising for resilience, local solar sharing, and some renter access, but the NZ evidence says the commercial case is not yet proven under current tariffs: Australian trial evidence reviewed for NZ found community batteries generally need subsidies and specialised local-use/network tariffs to be financially viable [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/news/report-unlocking-potential-community-batteries-new-zealand); [ANU/ARENA, 2020](https://arena.gov.au/assets/2020/08/community-batteries-cost-benefit-analysis.pdf).
- These interventions do not directly fix the main affordability drivers already identified in stream #256: rising fixed/network charges, dry-year wholesale risk, and gentailer concentration or margins; solar can lower variable imports for participating households, but fixed daily charges and system-wide dry-year costs still flow through bills [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/electricity-lines/understanding-why-changes-to-lines-charges-may-impact-your-electricity-bill/); [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/energy_in_new_zealand_2025.pdf); [Electricity Authority, 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/gentailer-energy-margins-in-2024/).
- The non-partisan read is that SolarSaver and comparable ratepayer-finance proposals are mechanisms worth testing, not endorsements: Labour's proposal combines subsidies, plug-in solar, loans, and community batteries, while National has also proposed a property/rates-linked Home Energy Fund for solar, batteries, insulation, and resilience upgrades [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver); [National Party, 2026](https://www.national.org.nz/news/20260625-brownbishopwatts).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - solar generation arithmetic, tenure barriers, and community-battery viability caveats are well supported, but the actual renter outcomes depend on future NZ regulatory changes, product standards, tariff design, subsidy eligibility, landlord behaviour, and measured pilot data that do not yet exist.

### SolarSaver as a live case, not an endorsement

Labour's SolarSaver page proposes $160 million over four years, including $77 million for household subsidies, $30 million for community batteries, $22 million for EECA delivery, a $20 million Crown underwrite for lines-company loans, $7 million to establish a Ratepayer Assistance Scheme, and $4 million for workforce coordination [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver). The same page says low- and middle-income households, including renters, could receive a kickstart subsidy of up to $3,000; renters could use plug-in solar once regulations are changed; and community battery applicants could include communities, councils, charities, iwi, and lines companies [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver).

Comparable mechanisms are not unique to that party proposal. National announced a Home Energy Fund on 25 June 2026, describing low-interest, long-term loans repaid through rates for solar, batteries, insulation, and other resilience upgrades, secured against property and supported by central government and participating councils [National Party, 2026](https://www.national.org.nz/news/20260625-brownbishopwatts). LGNZ's Ratepayer Assistance Scheme FAQ similarly describes a local-government-led low-cost loan scheme for ratepayers, including property improvement loans such as energy-affordability loans for solar, with repayment through a levy or on sale of the property [LGNZ, 2025](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/policy-advocacy/ratepayer-assistance-scheme/).

### Plug-in solar savings: the $300-$400/year claim is possible, but not a typical guaranteed result

SolarSaver claims a two-panel plug-in kit costs around $1,500, saves $300-$400/year, and pays for itself in four to five years [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver). Treating "two panels" as two modern 400 W panels is consistent with EECA's example that a typical home solar system could include 13 x 400 W panels for a 5.2 kW system [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/).

Using MBIE's published 14% annual capacity-factor assumption for NZ grid-connected solar PV, a 0.8 kW two-panel kit would generate about 981 kWh/year, calculated as `0.8 kW x 8,760 hours x 14%` [MBIE, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/energy-in-new-zealand-2023.pdf). EECA says the average NZ household uses about 7,000 kWh/year and pays about $2,400/year, implying an all-in average bill value near 34.3 c/kWh, though fixed daily charges mean not every bill dollar is avoidable by a small solar kit [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/); [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/electricity-lines/understanding-why-changes-to-lines-charges-may-impact-your-electricity-bill/).

Under favourable assumptions, the claim can work: if nearly all 981 kWh offsets household imports at about 34.3 c/kWh, the annual saving is about $336 [MBIE, 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/energy-in-new-zealand-2023.pdf); [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/). Under less favourable but plausible renter assumptions, the number falls: at 70% self-consumption and a 13 c/kWh export value for the rest, the saving is about $274/year; at 50% self-consumption and no paid export, it is about $168/year [Powerswitch, 2026](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates); [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/). The $300-$400/year range therefore needs a larger-than-0.8 kW kit, high daytime use, high avoidable variable tariffs, good north-ish orientation, little shading, or paid exports; it should not be presented as a typical renter outcome without measured NZ plug-in-solar data [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/); [Powerswitch, 2026](https://www.powerswitch.org.nz/solar-rates).

The current regulatory barrier is real. WorkSafe says PV systems involving more than 50 V AC or 120 V ripple-free DC need a registered electrician, 230 V terminations also require an electrician, and homeowner wiring rules do not cover PV systems [WorkSafe, 2024](https://www.worksafe.govt.nz/about-us/news-and-media/diy-solar-installation/). WorkSafe also says a mains inverter in a low-voltage, mains-parallel PV system must comply with AS/NZS 3000 and AS 4777.1, and that work is high-risk prescribed electrical work requiring certification and a record of inspection [WorkSafe, 2017](https://www.worksafe.govt.nz/about-us/news-and-media/certification-requirements-for-photovoltaic-pv-systems/).

### Subsidies and finance improve ownership economics, but do not reach renters automatically

EECA's 2026 household guidance puts typical installed costs at $8,500 for a 3 kW system, $11,500 for a 5 kW system, and $20,000 for a 10 kW system, with another $5,000-$15,000 for a battery [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/). EECA says 3-5 kW systems can work well financially and produce around 50%-80% of the electricity used by an average three-person all-electric household without an EV, while a 7-10 year payback needs the household to use most of the solar generated and have average-to-high daytime use [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/solar-costs-and-savings/). EECA's detailed modelling found no-battery 5 kW north-facing systems producing IRRs around 7%-14% in Queenstown, 6%-12% in Auckland, and 6%-11% in Christchurch, with returns sensitive to location, consumption, pricing structures, storage costs, and usage profile [EECA, 2025](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/insights/eeca-insights/understanding-the-value-of-residential-solar-pv-and-storage-in-new-zealand/).

That evidence supports targeted subsidies and cheap finance for the right owner-occupied homes, but the equity barrier is structural. Stats NZ reported that 66.0% of households owned their home or held it in a family trust in the 2023 Census, leaving about one-third without ownership control over the dwelling [Stats NZ, 2024](https://www.stats.govt.nz/news/home-ownership-increases-and-housing-quality-improves/). MBIE's 2024 energy-hardship report says renters were three to four times more likely than owner-occupiers to experience energy hardship and notes that homeowners have more incentive and autonomy to improve housing quality while renters have less control over housing quality and appliance efficiency [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024).

Ratepayer-linked finance helps a subset of households that cash rebates miss, but it is still centred on rateable property. LGNZ says RAS would be voluntary, available to eligible ratepayers or developers once in place, and subject to approval criteria [LGNZ, 2025](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/policy-advocacy/ratepayer-assistance-scheme/). Rewiring Aotearoa says the RAS loan would be tied to the rateable property rather than an individual homeowner and could be available to all ratepayers of participating councils, which is broader than bank green loans that tend to require mortgage headroom [Rewiring Aotearoa, 2026](https://www.rewiring.nz/ras). Those features make RAS a credible affordability lever for owner-occupiers and landlords, but not a direct renter entitlement unless paired with plug-in solar, enforceable rent/pass-through rules, public housing installs, or community bill-credit models [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31651-report-on-energy-hardship-measures-year-ended-june-2024); [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/franklin-energy-sharing-pilot).

### Community batteries: useful access model, weak standalone economics

Climate Connect Aotearoa describes community batteries as neighbourhood-scale storage, typically 100 kWh to 1 MWh, storing surplus local solar from multiple homes or businesses for later use [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/news/report-unlocking-potential-community-batteries-new-zealand). Its NZ report says community batteries can provide network, economic, and social benefits, including local network stability, reduced network losses, energy shifting, resilience, and access for households that cannot afford rooftop solar [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/news/report-unlocking-potential-community-batteries-new-zealand).

The same report is cautious on economics: it says many resilience and cohesion benefits are hard to monetise, Australian trials are unlikely to deliver positive net benefits under existing tariff structures and high capital costs without subsidies and/or specialised tariffs, and local-use-of-system charges may be needed to avoid double-charging battery flows [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/news/report-unlocking-potential-community-batteries-new-zealand). ANU's ARENA-funded cost-benefit analysis similarly found third-party community-battery models can be financially viable under some market conditions, but network-service payments are needed for future economic viability, DNSP-owned models are unlikely to be viable without regulated-asset-base treatment or leasing, and reduced local-use network costs are required to motivate charging from local solar [ANU/ARENA, 2020](https://arena.gov.au/assets/2020/08/community-batteries-cost-benefit-analysis.pdf).

NZ evidence remains pilot-stage. The Franklin Energy Sharing Pilot plans to test solar and battery sharing not located on recipients' properties, with bill credits reflecting solar generation and battery discharge during each 30-minute trading period, and it is supported through the Electricity Authority's Power Innovation Pathway while regulatory exemptions are considered [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/franklin-energy-sharing-pilot). Climate Connect Aotearoa says New Zealand has only one community-scale battery deployed, with another coming online as part of the Franklin pilot, so a $30 million fund with 50% capital support could accelerate trials but cannot be assumed to deliver broad household bill relief without tariff and benefit-sharing design [Climate Connect Aotearoa, 2025](https://climateconnectnz.com/news/report-unlocking-potential-community-batteries-new-zealand); [Labour, 2026](https://www.labour.org.nz/solarsaver).

### Fit against the wider affordability drivers

Solar-plus-storage interventions are best understood as bill-reduction tools for participating households, not as full electricity-affordability reform. The Commerce Commission says distribution and transmission costs make up just over 30% of the average power bill, started rising in 2025, and are likely to keep rising until at least 2030, with impacts varying by region and retailer [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/electricity-lines/understanding-why-changes-to-lines-charges-may-impact-your-electricity-bill/). A small solar kit does not avoid fixed daily charges, and even rooftop solar mainly offsets variable imports unless paired with tariffs and batteries that reward peak export or demand reduction [EECA, 2026](https://www.eeca.govt.nz/for-homes/solar-for-homes/plan-your-solar-system/); [Electricity Authority, 2026](https://www.ea.govt.nz/your-power/solar-power/).

Dry-year and market-structure pressures are also system-level. MBIE says 2024 dry conditions led to low hydro lake levels, hydro generation fell 11% from 2023 to its lowest level since 2013, and coal and diesel generation increased to meet demand, contributing to higher-cost supply [MBIE, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/energy_in_new_zealand_2025.pdf). The Electricity Authority says July-December 2024 gentailer energy margins averaged $76 million per week across six reporting gentailers, while noting energy margin is not the same as net profit because it excludes operating costs [Electricity Authority, 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/gentailer-energy-margins-in-2024/). Household solar and batteries can reduce exposure for those who get them, but they do not by themselves change hydro-firming settings, wholesale competition, hedge access, or the pass-through of network investment across non-participating households [Electricity Authority, 2026](https://www.ea.govt.nz/projects/all/energy-competition-task-force/); [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/electricity-lines/understanding-why-changes-to-lines-charges-may-impact-your-electricity-bill/).

## What would change this conclusion

- Measured NZ plug-in-solar trials for renting households, including actual kit capacity, orientation, self-consumption, export treatment, safety incidents, landlord consent, and annual bill savings, would replace the current arithmetic estimate.
- A final NZ electrical-safety pathway for compliant plug-in solar, with product standards, installation rules, retailer/distributor handling, and export treatment, could materially improve renter access.
- Public RAS or Home Energy Fund eligibility rules showing renters, low-income homeowners, landlords, and public/community housing can access benefits without rent increases would strengthen the equity case.
- Community-battery pilots publishing capital cost, operating cost, tariff treatment, bill-credit allocation, network-value payments, and participant outcomes would show whether a 50% capital grant is enough.
- A household-level distributional model combining tenure, income, roof suitability, daytime load, tariff, fixed charges, export rate, finance cost, and subsidy eligibility would change the current mechanism-by-mechanism assessment.
- Human review is needed from renters, budgeting services, tenancy advocates, lines companies, installers, and community organisations to test whether the proposed products are understandable, consentable, and low-risk in practice.
