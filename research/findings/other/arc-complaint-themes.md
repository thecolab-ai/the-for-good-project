# Current public evidence does not prove family communication is still the top NZ aged residential care complaint theme, but it remains a central recurring pain point alongside treatment, escalation, safety, and workforce-linked capacity

## Executive answer

- **The 2010-2014 baseline was unusually clear and ARC-specific:** HDC analysed 502 complaints about residential aged care facilities (514 facility cases) and found communication issues in 87% of cases, with "failure to communicate effectively with family" in 58%, making it the most common specific issue; other common themes were provider-to-provider communication (33%), inadequate response to complaint (25%), hygiene needs not met (22%), delayed/inadequate referral (19%), disrespectful manner/attitude (17%), deteriorating condition (22%), falls (22%), fluid/nutrition (16%), pain management (15%), and wound care (15%) [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf).
- **The best current ARC-specific public snapshot is less quantified but still puts communication first:** HDC's 2021/22 aged-care complaints page says HDC received 183 ARC-facility complaints that year and lists common themes as ineffective communication with consumers and whānau, inadequate care and treatment, management of behaviours from stress and distress, and inadequate coordination/escalation of care [HDC, 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/202122-aged-care-complaints/).
- **The latest official quantified data has shifted to broader categories, not the old ARC issue table:** HDC's 2024/25 annual report says ARC facilities were named in 154 complaints, down from 184 in 2023/24 and 185 in 2022/23; for complaints about older people's care, the most common primary issues were inadequate/inappropriate treatment (13%), delay in treatment (9%), missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (9%), and general safety issue in facility (5%) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).
- **So the defensible answer is:** communication with family/whānau is still a major and recurring ARC complaint theme, but the public record does **not** currently support saying it is still the quantified #1 theme in ARC complaints on the same basis as 2010-2014. The current top public themes are treatment/care quality, communication with consumers/whānau, escalation/coordination of deteriorating care, safety events such as falls or pressure injuries, dementia/distress behaviour management, and workforce/capacity constraints [HDC, 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/202122-aged-care-complaints/); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [Aged Care Commissioner, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/o3tb5dfa/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-across-aotearoa-new-zealand-march_2024.pdf).
- **The apparent shift is partly real and partly a reporting artefact:** current HDC reporting emphasises care/treatment and older-people system capacity, while communication often appears as a cross-cutting failure inside care, escalation, outbreak, end-of-life, and complaint-handling cases rather than as the single coded primary issue [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf); [HDC decision 24HDC02350](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/24hdc02350/); [HDC decision 21HDC03028](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2026/21hdc03028/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the baseline and latest HDC counts are official and strong, but no current public source reproduces the 2010-2014 ARC-specific thematic coding table, so the ranking comparison cannot be made at High confidence.

## Evidence

### The old baseline: communication dominated the 2010-2014 ARC complaint picture

HDC's 2016 report analysed complaints made between 2010 and 2014 about residential aged care facilities, covering 502 complaints and 514 facility cases because some complaints involved multiple facilities [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf). The report found communication was the largest category at 87% of cases, and the most common specific issue was failure to communicate effectively with family at 296 cases, or 58% [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf).

The same table put inadequate communication between providers at 169 cases (33%), inadequate response to complaint at 131 (25%), hygiene needs not met at 22%, delayed/inadequate referral at 19%, disrespectful manner/attitude at 17%, inadequate staffing levels at 14%, inadequate supervision/skills mix at 16%, and quality of aids/equipment at 16% [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf). HDC's clinical-theme sections also recorded falls in 22% of cases, deteriorating condition in 22%, fluid/nutrition in 16%, pain management in 15%, and wound care in 15% [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf).

That baseline was not just "people dislike poor communication": many clinical complaints also had communication sub-failures. The 2010-2014 report says pain-management complaints often involved communication between providers and with family, falls complaints often involved communication between providers and missed/delayed diagnoses, wound-care complaints often involved family communication, provider communication, and care planning, and deteriorating-condition complaints often involved care planning, provider communication, and delayed/inadequate referrals [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf).

### Current quantified HDC data: ARC complaint volume is public, but ARC-specific theme ranking is not

HDC's annual reports show ARC facilities remain a common organisation type in complaints: 169 complaints in 2019/20, 151 in 2020/21, 183 in 2021/22, 185 in 2022/23, 184 in 2023/24, and 154 in 2024/25 [HDC Annual Report 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/gu5l4iid/hdc-annual-report-2022.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/wzpdch0g/hdc-annual-report-2023.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/pobnpdgw/hdc-annual-report-2024.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The annual-report issue tables are not ARC-specific. Across all HDC complaints, the most common primary issue in 2024/25 was inadequate/inappropriate treatment (391 complaints), followed by missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (261), delay in treatment (252), inadequate/inappropriate examination/assessment (198), failure to communicate effectively with consumer (165), unexpected treatment outcome (149), lack of access to services (148), disrespectful manner/attitude (138), and waiting list/prioritisation (104) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). When HDC counts all issues in complaints, not just the primary issue, the 2024/25 leading categories were care/treatment (78%), communication (68%), access/funding (16%), consent/information (15%), medication (15%), and facility issues (12%) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

The same pattern is visible in recent annual reports: communication was the second largest all-issue category in 2019/20 (54%), 2020/21 (66%), 2021/22 (56%), 2022/23 (58%), 2023/24 (68%), and 2024/25 (68%), but this is across all HDC complaints, not just ARC [HDC Annual Report 2020](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/sxikmzfi/hdc-annual-report-2020.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2021](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/rcdn3yjz/hdc-annual-report-2021.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/gu5l4iid/hdc-annual-report-2022.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2023](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/wzpdch0g/hdc-annual-report-2023.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/pobnpdgw/hdc-annual-report-2024.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

For older people's care specifically, HDC's 2024/25 annual report says around 18% of HDC complaints were about care provided to older people, and lists the most common primary issues as inadequate/inappropriate treatment (13%), delay in treatment (9%), missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (9%), and general safety issue in facility (5%) [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). In 2023/24, the older-people primary issues were inadequate/inappropriate treatment (11%), unexpected treatment outcome (8%), delay in treatment (8%), missed/incorrect/delayed diagnosis (6%), and inadequate/inappropriate care (5%) [HDC Annual Report 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/pobnpdgw/hdc-annual-report-2024.pdf).

### Current ARC-specific thematic evidence: communication remains first in the latest public snapshot, but it is not quantified

HDC's 2021/22 aged-care complaints page is the most directly relevant recent ARC-specific thematic snapshot located. It says HDC received 183 complaints relating to ARC facilities in 2021/22 and lists the common complaint themes for that period as ineffective communication with consumers and their whānau, inadequate care and treatment, management of behaviours resulting from stress and distress, and inadequate coordination and escalation of care [HDC, 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/202122-aged-care-complaints/).

That page also says COVID-19 ARC complaints raised inconsistent visitor restrictions and lack of communication with whānau about loved ones' condition during restrictions, infection-prevention and control policy adequacy or non-compliance, care standards during restrictions including staffing levels, and staffing issues related to vaccine mandates and active COVID-19 cases [HDC, 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/202122-aged-care-complaints/). The page is strong evidence that communication remained prominent after the 2010-2014 period, but it does not provide counts or percentages for the listed themes [HDC, 2022](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/202122-aged-care-complaints/).

The Aged Care Commissioner's 2024 monitoring report is broader than ARC complaints, but it was informed by older people, whānau/family, carers, providers, and agencies, and it focuses on transitions from hospital to home, community support and ARC; primary/community care models and navigation; dementia prevention; and reliable home and community support [Aged Care Commissioner, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/o3tb5dfa/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-across-aotearoa-new-zealand-march_2024.pdf). Its recommendations include person-centred, culturally safe communication that involves whānau/family and carers, sustainable aged-care workforce actions, addressing psychogeriatric bed shortages, addressing gaps in kaupapa Māori aged-care services, and improving access to residential and community support options [Aged Care Commissioner, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/o3tb5dfa/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-across-aotearoa-new-zealand-march_2024.pdf).

The 2024 report page summarises the same focus areas and states that the report drew on the voices of older people, whānau/family including carers, service providers, and communities; it also says the report contains 20 recommendations to improve quality health and disability services for older people [HDC Aged Care Commissioner page, 2024](https://www.hdc.org.nz/our-work/aged-care-commissioner/amplifying-the-voices-of-older-people-in-aotearoa-new-zealand/).

### Recent public decisions: the same themes recur inside concrete ARC cases

Recent public HDC decisions are not a representative sample, but they show the current complaint themes in high-detail cases. In a 2026 decision about inadequate escalation in an aged residential care home, the Commissioner found failures in escalation of acute illness, adherence to a medical protocol, care-planning documentation, support for an EPOA to attend GP appointments and be involved in care, and emergency protocols; recommended actions included education on communication with and about older people and whānau [HDC decision 21HDC03028](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2026/21hdc03028/).

In a 2025 decision about poor care for three residents at a care home, HDC criticised nursing assessment, monitoring, contemporaneous reporting, communication, and care escalation, and separately found communication with family was minimal in quality and frequency for one resident's COVID-19 illness and end-of-life needs [HDC decision 24HDC02350](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/24hdc02350/). In another 2025 aged-care facility decision, the provider accepted systemic issues after serious harm, including poor adherence to policies and procedures, inadequate systems, unsatisfactory communication with family, delayed escalation to a GP, substandard communication with a nurse practitioner, missing falls risk assessment, and pressure-injury prevention gaps [HDC decision 22HDC02310](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/22hdc02310/).

These examples fit the current public theme cluster: not "communication only", but communication intertwined with care coordination, escalation, documentation, family/EPOA involvement, falls, wounds/pressure injuries, medication/protocol adherence, dementia/distress needs, and end-of-life care [HDC decision 21HDC03028](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2026/21hdc03028/); [HDC decision 24HDC02350](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/24hdc02350/); [HDC decision 22HDC02310](https://www.hdc.org.nz/decisions/search-decisions/2025/22hdc02310/).

### Why a clean "top five current ARC themes" cannot be stated from public data alone

The Treasury/Productivity Commission aged-care regulation case study warned that residents and families can complain through several avenues - provider internal processes, HDC, Ministry/DHB channels, the Disputes Tribunal for cost issues, and sometimes Police, ACC, or coronial processes - and that there was not systemic collection and analysis of complaints from the four main aged-care complaints avenues [Treasury/Productivity Commission, 2014](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf). That gap matters here because HDC is only one complaints channel, and annual HDC reporting no longer publishes an ARC-specific thematic table comparable to the 2010-2014 analysis [Treasury/Productivity Commission, 2014](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf); [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

HDC itself is moving toward more service-area thematic reporting, but the 2024/25 annual report says the reports underway or recently published cover public hospital services, residential disability support services, and maternity services, not a new ARC-specific thematic update [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf). HDC also planned a modern digital complaints management system in early 2026, which may improve future analysis, but it does not supply a retrospective current ARC theme table in the public record as of 9 July 2026 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current HDC, HealthCERT, Health NZ, or Ombudsman dataset that codes ARC complaints by specific issue in the same way as the 2010-2014 HDC report would change the ranking conclusion; if it showed failure to communicate with family/whānau still highest, confidence would move to High [HDC, 2016](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/agtdmfkm/residential-aged-care-report.pdf); [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).
- A new HDC report specifically updating ARC complaint themes for 2022/23-2025/26 would supersede this finding; HDC's 2024/25 annual report says service-area thematic work is underway for other areas, so a comparable ARC update is plausible but was not found in public sources as of 9 July 2026 [HDC Annual Report 2025](https://www.hdc.org.nz/media/ha5ntqul/hdc-annual-report-2025.pdf).
- Internal complaints logs from ARC providers, HealthCERT complaints, Health NZ contract-management complaints, and Advocacy Service complaints could materially change the picture because residents and families do not complain only to HDC [Treasury/Productivity Commission, 2014](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-rip-case-study-aged-care-regulation.pdf).
- I could not verify a current public, systematic thematic breakdown of complaints made directly to ARC providers, HealthCERT, Health NZ, the Advocacy Service, the Ombudsman, or coronial channels. A human with OIA capacity, sector access, or lived/family experience should test whether the HDC-only picture undercounts dignity, food, hygiene, staffing, cultural safety, or retaliation concerns that residents and families may raise locally but not escalate.
- I did not open child issues because the scoped question can be answered within one finding; the unresolved data-integration gap is a good future research question, but it is not necessary to split this issue.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would an OIA-backed, 2022/23-2025/26 ARC complaint taxonomy show if HDC, HealthCERT, Health NZ contract teams, provider-internal complaint logs, and Advocacy Service data were mapped to the 2010-2014 HDC categories?
- Are residents' own complaints different from family/whānau complaints, especially for dignity, autonomy, food, hygiene, loneliness, cultural safety, fear of retaliation, and day-to-day staff responsiveness?
- Do ARC complaint themes differ by facility type, ownership model, region, dementia/psychogeriatric service level, or cultural model of care?
