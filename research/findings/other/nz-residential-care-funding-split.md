# NZ's residential care funding split is a means-tested subsidy plus a capped resident contribution, with the Crown covering higher-care costs and offering interest-free loans against the home

## Executive answer

- **The formal funding split is not "government + family co-payment" — it is government subsidy + resident means-tested contribution, and the resident's contribution is capped.** A needs-assessed resident pays up to a regional weekly "maximum contribution" for contracted care; Health NZ pays the subsidy for those who pass income and asset tests, and pays a top-up for hospital/dementia-level care above the maximum contribution. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care)
- **From 1 July 2026 the maximum weekly contribution ranges from $1,513.33 to $1,634.43 (GST inclusive), by region.** The lowest is shared by South Waikato, Waimate, Waitaki, Clutha and Gore districts; the highest is Auckland City. The rate rose roughly 4% from 1 July 2025, consistent with the government's 2026/27 funding uplift. [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2025)
- **A secondary analysis reports roughly 63% of residents qualify for the Residential Care Subsidy; the current official asset threshold is $300,811 (single or both partners in care) or $164,731 (partner not in care, excluding home and car).** A subsidised resident contributes most of their income (NZ Super contribution rate $454.11/week) and keeps a $58.34/week personal allowance. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Mirage News, NZ Faces Aged Care Funding Crisis](https://www.miragenews.com/new-zealand-faces-aged-care-funding-crisis-1319630/)
- **Residents above the asset threshold pay privately up to the maximum contribution; if they own a home worth more than the threshold they can get an interest-free Residential Care Loan secured by a caveat, repaid on sale or death.** The loan covers the maximum contribution minus the resident's contribution. [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html)
- **Premium room charges ($10–$85/day) and additional services are NOT publicly funded and are paid privately; the subsidy and loan do not cover them.** Providers must disclose these charges in the admission agreement and post them publicly. [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care); [Health NZ, Accommodation premium rules](https://www.tewhatuora.govt.nz/health-services-and-programmes/aged-residential-care/accommodation-premium-rules/)
- **For observed 2022/23 ARC provider funding, Health NZ contributed approximately $1,352 million and residents' fees contributed a further $1,010 million (about $2.362 billion total), a roughly 57% public / 43% residents split.** Sapere's later phase-two modelling forecasts a wider aged-care-system status quo cost of $3.489 billion in 2024/25, with a 63% public share, but that figure combines ARC and home/community support services and is modelled rather than observed. [Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024)](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf); [Sapere, Aged care service model and funding review: Phase 2 (22 July 2024)](https://sapere-ausnz.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/Aged-Care-Service-Model-and-Funding-Review_Final-Report-22-07-24.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the core mechanism (subsidy, loan, maximum contribution, higher-care top-up and premium-charge rules) is High because it is stated on official Work and Income, Ministry of Health and Health NZ pages I fetched directly. The exact observed 2022/23 ARC dollar split ($1,352M public / $1,010M residents, about $2.362B total) is Medium because it is directly stated in the primary Sapere phase-one PDF, but Mirage News is not independent corroboration of the precise resident-fee amount. Sapere phase two adds relevant 2024/25 modelled public/private allocations and a whole-system public-share forecast, but it does not replace the observed 2022/23 ARC provider-flow figures.

## Evidence

### Who pays what: the three pathways

NZ's aged residential care (ARC) — rest home, continuing-care hospital, dementia and psychogeriatric care — is funded through a mix of Crown subsidy, capped resident contribution, and private payment, depending on a means test. The entry point is a needs assessment by a Needs Assessment and Service Coordination (NASC) agency, which determines whether long-term residential care is required and at what level. [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care)

There are three payment pathways after needs assessment:

1. **Residential Care Subsidy** — for residents who pass income and asset tests. Health NZ pays the subsidy directly to the facility; the resident contributes most of their income and keeps a personal allowance. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)
2. **Private payment** — for residents whose assets exceed the threshold. They pay up to the regional maximum contribution; for higher care levels (hospital/dementia) the Crown pays a top-up subsidy above the maximum. [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care)
3. **Residential Care Loan** — for residents who own a home worth more than the threshold but cannot afford to pay privately while keeping the home. An interest-free Crown loan secured by a caveat covers the gap. [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html)

### The Residential Care Subsidy: income and asset testing

Work and Income administers the Residential Care Subsidy on behalf of Health NZ. Eligibility depends on age (65+, or 50–64 and single with no dependent children), an assessed need for long-term residential care, and a financial means assessment with two stages: an asset test and an income test. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)

**Asset limits (as published on the live Work and Income page, fetched 6 July 2026):**

| Circumstance | Asset limit |
|---|---|
| Single, or both partners in long-term residential care | $300,811 or less |
| Partner not in care, choosing NOT to include family home and car | $164,731 or less |
| Partner not in care, choosing to include family home and car | $300,811 or less |

A "family home" is the main place where a partner or dependent child lives; it can be a residential property, a licence-to-occupy occupation right agreement, a life interest, an apartment, granny flat, motor home or boat. Personal belongings, household furniture and pre-paid funeral plans (up to $10,000 each) are excluded from the asset test. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)

The asset threshold is adjusted periodically. Multiple 2025 sources cite $284,636 as the prior threshold, and Anderson Lloyd confirms "MSD will adjust the asset and income limits yearly based on inflation." [Anderson Lloyd, Residential Care Subsidies and Trusts](https://www.al.nz/residential-care-subsidies-and-trusts/); [Hobec, Residential Care Subsidies](https://hobec.co.nz/news-resources/2025/06/residential-care-subsidies/). The current $300,811 figure on the live page reflects the post-inflation-adjustment threshold.

Gifting rules limit how much can be divested before the asset test: up to $8,500/year of gifts in the last 5 years (total $42,500, or $85,000 if a partner applies at the same time) is disregarded; gifts over 5 years ago are disregarded up to $27,000/year. Assets sold below fair value in the last 5 years may still be counted. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)

**Income test:** Income counted includes NZ Super, Veteran's Pension and other benefits, 50% of private superannuation, 50% of life insurance annuities, overseas government pensions, contributions from relatives, interest, investments, business or employment income, and trust/estate payments. A partner's earnings from work are NOT counted. Small amounts of income from assets are disregarded ($1,267/year single; $2,534/year couple both in care; $3,801/year couple one in care). War Disablement Pensions are excluded. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)

### What a subsidised resident actually pays

A subsidised resident contributes most of their assessed income to the cost of care. If they receive NZ Super, the current contribution rate is **$454.11 per week**, and they keep a **personal allowance of $58.34/week** (after tax on an M code) plus a clothing allowance of **$365.92/year**. The subsidy is the difference between the resident's contribution and the actual care cost. For example, Work and Income illustrates: if a resident contributes $300/week and care costs $900/week, the subsidy is $600/week. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html); [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html)

A partner remaining at home may be eligible for a Special Disability Allowance of $51.67/week, an increased benefit payment, or a single rate of NZ Super/Veteran's Pension. [Work and Income, Residential Care Subsidy](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-subsidy.html)

### The maximum contribution: the cap on what any resident pays

The maximum contribution is the maximum weekly amount (GST inclusive) that a needs-assessed resident is required to pay for contracted care, set regionally under section 53 of the Residential Care and Disability Support Services Act 2018. It applies to ALL residents — subsidised and private — and is the same regardless of care level (rest home, hospital, dementia). It is equivalent to the rest home contract price in each region. [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care)

From 1 July 2026, the range is **$1,513.33 to $1,634.43 per week (GST inclusive)**:

| Region (illustrative) | 2026/27 rate | 2025/26 rate | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Auckland City | $1,634.43 | $1,571.57 | +4.0% |
| Wellington City | $1,597.05 | $1,535.95 | +4.0% |
| Christchurch City | $1,556.80 | $1,496.09 | +4.0% |
| South Waikato District | $1,513.33 | $1,455.16 | +4.0% |
| Far North District | $1,518.65 | $1,460.27 | +4.0% |

The full table covers all territorial local authority regions (Part 1) plus higher rates for isolated rural statistical areas (Part 2). The notice is issued annually; the 2026 notice replaces the 2025 notice (Gazette No. 2025-go3402) and the 2024 notice before it. [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2025)

### Higher-care public top-ups

For hospital-level or dementia-level care, the actual cost of care exceeds the maximum contribution. This gap is covered by a **top-up subsidy** paid directly to the provider by Health NZ, applied for by the needs assessor. The top-up applies to all needs-assessed residents requiring higher care, including private payers — so even a private-paying hospital-level resident pays no more than the maximum contribution, with the Crown covering the rest. The Ministry of Health's long-term residential care booklet confirms this directly: "Your DHB will pay a top-up subsidy to your residential care provider if your assessed care needs are a higher level than rest home care… a higher price for contracted care services is paid for specialist dementia and hospital level care," and "The maximum contribution is the same for all residents, regardless of the level of care they need" (pp. 15–16). [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care); [Ministry of Health, Long-term residential care booklet (Feb 2019), pp. 15–16](https://web.archive.org/web/20250512233831/https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2012-06/long-term-residential-care-for-older-people-feb19.pdf)

### The Residential Care Loan: keeping the home

Residents who own a home worth more than the $300,811 threshold but have less than $15,000 (single) or $30,000 (partner) in other assets can apply for a Residential Care Loan — an interest-free Crown loan secured by a caveat over the home, paid directly to the rest home. The loan covers the maximum contribution minus the resident's contribution. For example, Work and Income illustrates: an Auckland City resident with a $1,511.09/week fee and a $454.11/week NZ Super contribution would receive a loan of $1,056.98/week. [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html)

The loan is repaid when the home is sold or within 12 months of the resident's death, whichever comes first. Late repayment may incur 10% annual interest. When the loan balance reduces assets below the threshold, the resident may transfer to the subsidy. The Ministry of Health sends statements every 4 months. Certain fees (real estate, solicitor, protected equity, funeral allowance) are deducted before repayment is calculated. [Work and Income, Residential Care Loan](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/residential-care-loan.html)

### Premium charges and additional services: what public funding does not cover

Premium room fees — for rooms with additional features — are **not** covered by the Residential Care Subsidy or the Residential Care Loan. Health NZ's guidance says premium room fees of around $10 to $85 a day are common, there is no public funding for them, and a resident's choice will be limited if they cannot fund these fees privately. Providers must post any extra charges on their website and display them prominently. [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care); [Health NZ, Accommodation premium rules](https://www.tewhatuora.govt.nz/health-services-and-programmes/aged-residential-care/accommodation-premium-rules/)

Additional fees for services outside "contracted care" can be charged by negotiation and must be specified in the admission agreement. Providers cannot charge for services already funded by the Crown (e.g., GP visits, medications, continence products). [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care)

### Guarantors

The stream's framing doc cites the Ministry of Health's long-term residential care booklet for the claim that "a guarantor cannot be a condition of admission." [analysis/nz-aged-care-quality-and-funding-rest-homes-reside-framing.md](../../../analysis/nz-aged-care-quality-and-funding-rest-homes-reside-framing.md). Having now read the full booklet (Feb 2019 edition, recovered via a Wayback snapshot), I can confirm it does **not** contain that statement: the admission-agreement section (pp. 19–20) covers what services and extra charges the agreement must set out and the right to refuse extra services, but says nothing about guarantors, sureties or third-party liability. [Ministry of Health, Long-term residential care booklet (Feb 2019), pp. 19–20](https://web.archive.org/web/20250512233831/https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2012-06/long-term-residential-care-for-older-people-feb19.pdf). The live Health NZ residential care page likewise does not state a guarantor rule. [Health NZ, Residential care](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/hospitals-services/services-support/older-people/residential-care). This claim remains Low confidence and needs a human to confirm against the current admission-agreement regulations or the ARRC/Age Related Residential Care Services Agreement, which the booklet points to as the authoritative source.

### Current public-private funding flows (observed 2022/23; modelled 2024/25)

The most recent observed ARC provider dollar split I found comes from the Sapere Research Group's phase-one review of aged care funding and services models, completed in December 2023 for Health NZ (authors: David Moore, Jeff Loan, Mehrnaz Rohani, Rohan Trill, Nick Manning, Douglas Yee; dated 16 January 2024). The primary PDF, hosted by Health NZ, states:

> "Te Whatu Ora funded approximately $1,352 million towards ARC services in 2022/23, with residents paying $1,010 million as a result of means testing (including superannuation deductions)."

[Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024), p.4 & p.13](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf)

That implies total ARC funding of roughly **$2.362 billion in 2022/23**, split approximately **57% public** (Health NZ) and **43% resident fees**. The same report states about **32,000 people** were in residential aged care (676 ARC facilities), with most in rest home (13,500) or hospital-level care (13,200). [Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024)](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf). A secondary analysis (Mirage News) reports the same $1,352 billion public figure but rounds residents' fees to "$1.1 billion" — the primary PDF gives the precise figure of $1,010 million. [Mirage News, NZ Faces Aged Care Funding Crisis](https://www.miragenews.com/new-zealand-faces-aged-care-funding-crisis-1319630/). The Sapere report also notes Health NZ funded $732 million of home and community support services (HCSS) in the same year. [Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024)](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf)

Sapere's phase-two report, dated 22 July 2024 and published as a public Sapere PDF, is directly relevant but has a different scope: it models options for improved aged-care service and funding models from 2024/25 to 2039/40. Its ARC section says Health NZ had forecast public and private funding allocations for the four ARC care levels in 2024/25, with public funding expected to vary between 41% and 70% of funding across care levels. In the whole aged-care-system status quo scenario, which combines ARC with HCSS, Sapere forecasts total cost rising from **$3.489 billion in 2024/25** to **$7.391 billion in 2039/40**, while the public share falls from **63%** to **56%**. [Sapere, Aged care service model and funding review: Phase 2 (22 July 2024), sections 4.1 and 6](https://sapere-ausnz.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/Aged-Care-Service-Model-and-Funding-Review_Final-Report-22-07-24.pdf)

A 2019 Ministry of Health/DHB release on an earlier funding-model report gave the sector total as about $1.95 billion with $1.2 billion from DHBs — showing total funding and public dollars have grown since 2019, while the public percentage share in the 2022/23 Sapere phase-one figures is lower than the 2019 implied share. [Scoop, Insights into funding model for aged residential care](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PO1909/S00053/insights-into-funding-model-for-aged-residential-care.htm); [Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024)](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf)

### The 2026/27 funding uplift

On 17 June 2026, the government announced a 4% funding increase worth approximately **$79 million** for ARC providers in 2026/27, including a 2.66% base increase and targeted investment for service delivery, responsiveness to demand and access to care. Health Minister Simeon Brown said providers would be expected to receive hospital admissions at weekends to ease bed pressure. [RNZ, Government announces $79m funding boost](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/598387/government-announces-79m-funding-boost-for-aged-residential-care-services); [NewsWire, Government lifts aged care funding to 4 percent](https://newswire.co.nz/2026/06/government-aged-care-funding-weekend-admissions/). The Beehive release itself was bot-blocked (HTTP 200 Incapsula shell — tooling, not a dead link); the figures are confirmed by multiple independent news outlets reporting the same release. [Beehive, Funding boost for aged residential care sector](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/funding-boost-aged-residential-care-sector)

The ~4% maximum-contribution increase from 1 July 2025 to 1 July 2026 (e.g., Auckland City $1,571.57 → $1,634.43) is consistent with this funding uplift. [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2026); [Ministry of Health, Maximum Contribution 1 July 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/maximum-contribution-applying-in-each-region-from-1-july-2025)

### The reform context

The funding split is under active review. Health NZ's Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review ran phase one through the second half of 2023 (Sapere report, completed December 2023) and identified five key issues: ARC and home/community support are under-funded, the funding models are not fit for purpose, there are material ethnic inequities in access, the sector faces significant workforce pressures, and issues are exacerbated in regional/rural NZ. [Mirage News, NZ Faces Aged Care Funding Crisis](https://www.miragenews.com/new-zealand-faces-aged-care-funding-crisis-1319630/); [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review). Sapere's public phase-two report then modelled ARC demand, HCSS demand, ARC financial requirements, public/private allocations, and whole-system aged-care costs from 2024/25 to 2039/40. [Sapere, Aged care service model and funding review: Phase 2 (22 July 2024)](https://sapere-ausnz.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/Aged-Care-Service-Model-and-Funding-Review_Final-Report-22-07-24.pdf). The Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group began work in January 2026 and is scoped to advise on sustainable beds, the sharing of care costs between recipients and government, contracting, regulation and service innovation. [Aged Care Ministerial Advisory Group](https://www.health.govt.nz/about-us/new-zealands-health-system/health-system-roles-and-organisations/health-committees-and-boards/aged-care-ministerial-advisory-group)

## What would change this conclusion

- **A current observed funding-flow dataset** (Health NZ appropriation data, provider accounts, or updated resident-payment data) showing the 2022/23 public/private split has materially changed would update the dollar figures. The $1,352M/$1,010M split is from the primary Sapere phase-one PDF (hosted by Health NZ, fetched 6 July 2026 via curl as application/pdf). Sapere phase two provides relevant 2024/25 modelling and forecasts, but I did not find an observed post-2022/23 ARC-only funding-flow table. [Sapere, A review of aged care funding and service models (Jan 2024)](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/A-Review-of-Aged-Care-Funding-and-Service-Models.pdf); [Sapere, Aged care service model and funding review: Phase 2 (22 July 2024)](https://sapere-ausnz.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/Aged-Care-Service-Model-and-Funding-Review_Final-Report-22-07-24.pdf)
- **The guarantor rule** needs confirmation against the current Residential Care and Disability Support Services Act 2018 regulations or the Age Related Residential Care (ARRC) Services Agreement. - **Lived-experience input** from residents, whānau, financial advisors and aged-care providers is needed to understand how the funding split works in practice — including the stress of asset testing, the experience of premium-room pressure, the use of "care suites" by private payers, and whether the loan pathway is well understood. ## What I could not verify

- The 2022/23 public/private dollar split is now sourced from the primary Sapere phase-one PDF (hosted by Health NZ at the static.info.content.health.nz URL, fetched 6 July 2026 via curl — HTTP 200, Content-Type: application/pdf, 4.1 MB). The figures ($1,352M public / $1,010M residents) are directly stated on p.4 and p.13 of the report. Mirage News corroborates the $1,352M public figure but rounds residents' fees to "$1.1 billion" rather than the precise $1,010M. The original Squarespace URL cited in the issue returns 404, but the Health NZ-hosted copy is the canonical source. The ~57%/43% percentage split is a derivation from the stated dollar figures, not a directly stated claim in the report.
- The "guarantor cannot be a condition of admission" rule: I have now read the full Feb 2019 MoH booklet the framing doc cited (recovered via Wayback) and it does **not** state this; it is also absent from the live Health NZ pages. The claim is therefore unverified against its cited source and needs checking against the ARRC Services Agreement or the current regulations.
- The Beehive press release text: the live beehive.govt.nz URL returned an Incapsula bot-block (HTTP 200 shell — tooling, not a dead link). The key facts ($79m, 4%, 2.66% base, weekend admissions) were confirmed through multiple independent news outlets (RNZ, NewsWire, NZ Doctor, ODT) reporting the same release.
- Regional variation in premium charges and the prevalence of care-suite private payment: the Health NZ pages describe the rules but I found no current national dataset on how many residents pay premium charges or use care suites.
- The income contribution rate's effective date and whether the $454.11/week NZ Super contribution rate and $58.34/week personal allowance are current as at 1 July 2026: the live Work and Income pages (fetched 6 July 2026) state these figures, but they may adjust on 1 April each year (the Super rate) rather than 1 July.

## Open follow-up questions

- How has the observed ARC-only public/private funding split changed since 2022/23, and what does Health NZ's 2026/27 appropriation data show for the ARC line? Sapere phase two models 2024/25 onward, but this still needs observed financial data.
- What proportion of residents pay premium room charges, and what is the average premium, by provider type (large corporate vs. small independent)?
- How do Māori, Pacific and low-deprivation residents experience the asset test and loan pathway differently — is the 63% subsidy rate uniform across ethnicities?
- How does the "care suite" private-payment model interact with the maximum contribution cap and the supply of subsidised beds?
- What is the practical gap between the maximum contribution and the actual cost of rest-home care, and how does it vary by region and provider?
