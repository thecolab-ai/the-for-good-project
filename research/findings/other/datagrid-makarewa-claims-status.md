# DataGrid Makarewa claims catalogue: the dispute is a mix of confirmed consent facts, contested impact interpretations, and a few unsupported assertions — and DataGrid's core rebuttal is that critics (CAFCA) use a disputed 1GW figure

## Executive answer

- The strongest confirmed facts are that Environment Southland granted Datagrid NZ Partnership Limited consents for a datacentre near Makarewa — including groundwater take, wetland removal, and coastal marine cable activities — and that the wider approval package spans Southland District Council, Invercargill City Council and the Overseas Investment Office. [Environment Southland, 2026](https://www.es.govt.nz/about-us/news?item=id%3A2xhpahbzw17q9s3f3x4z) [DataGrid, 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- **The single most load-bearing point in the whole dispute is now fully recovered and confirmed as a dispute, not resolved as a consent ceiling:** DataGrid's response to critics is that they are describing "quite different" projects, and the concrete disagreement is a **power-scale factor of ~3.5x**. The Campaign Against Foreign Control of Aotearoa (CAFCA) claims the centre needs **1 gigawatt** (≈25% of national output, "nearly twice Tiwai"); DataGrid CEO Rémi Galasso says the public-record figure, which he describes as consistent with resource consent conditions, is **about 280MW**, roughly one third of CAFCA's number. [CAFCA, 2026](https://www.cafca.org.nz/views-analyses-and-research/press-statements/2026/06/time-to-pull-plug-on-power-hungry-data-centres/) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- The public opposition petition (a separate actor from CAFCA) is real and closed with 26,027 supporters, but several of its claims are overbroad or unsupported by the checked sources — projected water-price rises, power-bill rises, and "government spent $5.1b" building it. On price, DataGrid has now given a direct on-record rebuttal (dedicated long-term renewable contracts, "doesn't place upward pressure on consumer power prices"), though no independent NZ price model exists either way. [Change.org, 2026](https://www.change.org/p/stop-the-building-of-an-ai-centre-in-invercargill) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- The "$5b/$5.1b" number traces to the project's **claimed private investment cost** (CAFCA calls it "$5 billion foreign-owned"; The Press reports "$3.7 billion") — **not** to government spending. No checked source supports the petition's claim that the Crown spent it. [CAFCA, 2026](https://www.cafca.org.nz/views-analyses-and-research/press-statements/2026/06/time-to-pull-plug-on-power-hungry-data-centres/) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- DataGrid's power and consent claims are better evidenced than its broader climate/economic claims: the 15-year, 140MW Mercury PPOA is confirmed by DataGrid and DCD, while broader low-carbon, emissions-savings, and jobs-benefit statements still need independent modelling or the underlying technical/economic assessments. [DataGrid Mercury release, 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/mercury) [DCD, 2026](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/datagrid-signs-power-supply-deal-with-mercury-for-planned-280mw-data-center-campus-in-invercargill-new-zealand/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the catalogue now rests on primary consent sources plus the full text of both the critics' (CAFCA) and DataGrid's positions, so the *framing* of the dispute is High-confidence; it stays Medium overall because several impact/benefit claims (household prices, marginal-grid emissions, the "half a million cars" comparison, jobs numbers) still depend on modelling and technical attachments outside this issue.

## Evidence

### The recovered core: what DataGrid actually said, and to whom

On 2 July 2026 The Press (Michael Fallow) reported DataGrid CEO Rémi Galasso responding to the **Campaign Against Foreign Control of Aotearoa (CAFCA)**. Galasso said "these are important questions and people are right to be asking them," but that several CAFCA claims described "a project quite different from the one we have consented and are building." [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre) This line — previously unretrievable because thepress.co.nz serves a JavaScript app shell to plain HTTP fetchers — was recovered in full via the stealth-browser and Jina reader rungs of the fetch ladder on 8 July 2026.

The concrete substance of "quite different" is a specific, checkable numeric gap. CAFCA's June 2026 press release ("Time to Pull Plug on Power-Hungry Data Centres") states: "The proposed $5 billion foreign-owned Datagrid AI centre near Invercargill would require 1 gigawatt of electricity to operate. That is nearly twice as much as the 570 megawatts that Rio Tinto's Tiwai Point aluminium smelter consumes," and would force NZ to "sacrifice more than one third of the power we produce." [CAFCA, 2026](https://www.cafca.org.nz/views-analyses-and-research/press-statements/2026/06/time-to-pull-plug-on-power-hungry-data-centres/) Galasso's rebuttal: "it was public record, and consistent with resource consent conditions, that the facility was designed around one third of that, about 280 megawatts." [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre) DataGrid's own consent release and DCD trade reporting both cite 280MW, and the Electricity Authority puts Tiwai at 572MW — so if 280MW is the comparable load measure, it is roughly *half* of Tiwai, not the "nearly twice Tiwai" CAFCA asserts for its 1GW figure. [DataGrid, 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent) [DCD, 2026](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/new-zealands-datagrid-gets-approval-for-280mw-campus-near-invercargill/) [Electricity Authority, 2025](https://www.ea.govt.nz/news/eye-on-electricity/the-tiwai-point-smelter-demand-response-in-winter-2024/)

The article also recovered DataGrid's on-record rebuttals to the other two flashpoints:
- **Consumer power prices:** Galasso said DataGrid was "working with energy stakeholders nationally and in Southland 'to ensure the facility doesn't place upward pressure on consumer power prices'," that it "would not be competing for the supply that served homes and businesses," and would be "contracted under dedicated long-term renewable arrangements." [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- **Water:** DataGrid contended Southland's cooler temperatures mean mechanical cooling (the main water driver in hot climates) "would rarely be needed," and rainwater from the site's roofs was expected to supply "most or all" of the facility's water requirements. [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)
- **Approvals:** Galasso said the project "carried the written support of every affected party, local rūnaka and councils," and that it had OIO approval. CAFCA's Murray Horton countered that OIO approval "wasn't surprising as it approved nearly all projects" from foreign companies. [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)

### Synthesis of the table

The debate is not "critics wrong" or "DataGrid wrong"; it is a mix of confirmed base facts, contested interpretations, and a few unsupported assertions. The confirmed base facts: Environment Southland granted regional consents for a Makarewa datacentre and related cable works (groundwater take, wetland removal, coastal cable), and — per DataGrid and now The Press — the full package includes district/city approvals, OIO approval, and iwi/affected-party written support, for a 78,000 sqm, 280MW facility on a 49ha site. [Environment Southland, 2026](https://www.es.govt.nz/about-us/news?item=id%3A2xhpahbzw17q9s3f3x4z) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)

The recovered Press/CAFCA exchange clarifies the *shape* of the disagreement that was previously invisible. The headline "quite different projects" is not spin — it points at a real, checkable 3.5x power-scale gap (CAFCA's 1GW vs DataGrid's public-record 280MW figure), but the consent load schedule is still needed to settle the ceiling. Where CAFCA and the petition are on firmest ground is that the facility is a very large, foreign-owned, near-baseload electricity user whose value capture is debatable; where they overreach is in specific causal forecasts the checked evidence does not support — a 1GW load stated as settled fact, household water-price and power-bill rises stated as fact, "government spent $5.1b," and "destroy wildlife" at broad scale. [CAFCA, 2026](https://www.cafca.org.nz/views-analyses-and-research/press-statements/2026/06/time-to-pull-plug-on-power-hungry-data-centres/) [Change.org, 2026](https://www.change.org/p/stop-the-building-of-an-ai-centre-in-invercargill)

DataGrid's strongest claims are those tied to a public release and corroborated by trade reporting: the resource-consent announcement, the 280MW/78,000 sqm project description, and the Mercury 140MW/15-year PPOA. [DataGrid, 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/resourceconsent) [DCD, 2026](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/datagrid-signs-power-supply-deal-with-mercury-for-planned-280mw-data-center-campus-in-invercargill-new-zealand/) Its weakest are broader benefit claims needing independent assessment: low-carbon operation under marginal-grid conditions, jobs/economic benefit, latency reduction, and emissions savings versus overseas compute — the same claims its own rebuttal leans on rhetorically without published modelling. [DataGrid Mercury release, 2026](https://www.datagrid.nz/pr1-rc/mercury) [The Press, 2026](https://www.thepress.co.nz/nz-news/361035235/datagrid-says-critics-describing-quite-different-projects-southlands-data-centre)

- **The consent load ceiling would resolve the 280MW-vs-1GW dispute.** If the consent conditions cap the facility at ~280MW, DataGrid's "quite different projects" rebuttal is vindicated; if the consent (or a phased/ultimate campus ambition) allows toward 1GW, CAFCA's figure gains footing. - The Southland District Council and Invercargill City Council decision records could sharpen "full resource consent" from "confirmed per company/press" to independently confirmed across all authorities, or reveal scope/condition differences not visible in the ES summary (#214).
