# Early large settlements, relativity clauses, asset location, and capital access structurally widen post-settlement iwi outcomes

## Executive answer

- Settlement outcomes are unequal before investment skill is considered: TDB's 2025 comparison lists original redress of $170m for Ngāi Tahu and $170m for Waikato-Tainui, but records total settlement received to date, including relativity payments, of $573m and $566m respectively; the same table lists smaller or later redress amounts such as $43m for Ngāti Awa, $20m plus interest for Ngāti Pāhauwera, $50m for Raukawa, and $18m for Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)
- Relativity clauses create a structural top-up available only to the two early comprehensive settlements in this sample: official Crown material says Waikato-Tainui can request five-yearly payments to keep its settlement at 17% of total Treaty settlement expenditure, while Ngāi Tahu can request payments to keep its settlement at 16.1%. [Waikato-Tainui relativity payments table, 2021](https://www.govt.nz/assets/Documents/OTS/Waikato-Tainui-Raupatu/2021-02-05-RM-payments-table-Waikato-Tainui.pdf); [Ngāi Tahu relativity payments table, 2021](https://www.govt.nz/assets/Documents/OTS/Ngai-Tahu/2021-02-05-RM-payments-table-Ngai-Tahu.pdf)
- Asset mix and geography then amplify differences: TDB says Waikato-Tainui and Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei benefited over 2016-2025 from long-duration property appreciation in Hamilton and Auckland, while Ngāi Tahu and Raukawa are more balanced and Ngāti Awa and Ngāti Pāhauwera are more primary-industry weighted. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)
- Capital access is another divider: TDB says iwi cannot issue external equity because assets are collectively held for all members, so growth relies mainly on settlement assets, retained earnings and debt; Waikato-Tainui's 2025 report says its Ruakura/Brookfield model was chosen partly because its development opportunities exceeded what bank debt alone could finance. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)
- The public evidence supports a structural-advantage finding, not a precise attribution model: TDB explicitly removes relativity payments from return-on-invested-capital because they are capital redress rather than returns from deployed capital, and it warns that RoIC is not risk-adjusted and does not capture differences in diversification, leverage, accounting treatment or culturally significant asset choices. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium -- the direction of structural inequality is well supported by official relativity material, iwi annual reports and TDB's cross-iwi comparison, but public sources do not isolate the causal contribution of each factor from investment execution, valuation policy, member growth, or iwi-defined non-financial objectives.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This finding answers issue #496 by asking whether starting settlement size, relativity clauses, asset mix, geography, debt capacity and partnership access shape unequal iwi outcomes, rather than re-answering the sibling baseline question about current assets and distributions. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The main comparison source is TDB's 2025 report on ten iwi, which says those iwi collectively manage about $8.5b and represent about 70% of settlement-derived iwi assets nationally; TDB also says the sample was selected by settlement size, iwi population and availability of financial reports, so it is a high-value but non-random public comparison. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The Crown settlement context matters because the Auditor-General says settlements are not just cash transfers: about 150 public organisations held about 12,000 individual commitments under about 80 settlements with about 70 groups, and $2.738b of financial and commercial redress had been transferred by late 2024. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf)

### Settlement size, timing and relativity

Waikato-Tainui and Ngāi Tahu began with large early settlements and a protection mechanism that later settlements generally do not have in the same form: TDB lists both original settlements at $170m and notes that total settlement received to date, inclusive of relativity payments, is $573m for Ngāi Tahu and $566m for Waikato-Tainui. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The official Crown relativity tables describe the same mechanism in policy terms: Waikato-Tainui's 1995 settlement can be topped up to maintain 17% of total Treaty settlement expenditure, and Ngāi Tahu's 1997 settlement can be topped up to maintain 16.1%. [Waikato-Tainui relativity payments table, 2021](https://www.govt.nz/assets/Documents/OTS/Waikato-Tainui-Raupatu/2021-02-05-RM-payments-table-Waikato-Tainui.pdf); [Ngāi Tahu relativity payments table, 2021](https://www.govt.nz/assets/Documents/OTS/Ngai-Tahu/2021-02-05-RM-payments-table-Ngai-Tahu.pdf)

A 2022 ministerial release confirms the mechanism was still live after the first payment cycles: Waikato-Tainui and Ngāi Tahu each received $1.625m as a negotiated resolution of relativity disputes, and the release says both iwi could request their next five-year payment later in 2022. [Beehive, 2022](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/waikato-tainui-and-ng%C4%81i-tahu-receive-treaty-settlement-adjustment)

Waikato-Tainui's own 2025 annual report says the relativity clause was triggered in FY2013 when Treaty settlements since 1994 exceeded $1.0b, that $396.0m had been received through the process, that claims are made every five years, and that no settlement will be receivable after 2045. [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

Ngāi Tahu's 2025 annual report shows why this is not just an accounting footnote: its portfolio timeline highlights a $98m Crown relativity payment and a $200m Crown relativity payment as major moments in the long-run growth story from $170m of transferred cash and purchase rights. [Ngāi Tahu annual report, 2025](https://ngaitahu.iwi.nz/assets/Documents/TRONT-AR2025-new.pdf)

TDB's RoIC method treats those top-ups as capital redress, not investment return: it adds distributions and interest back to pre-tax comprehensive income but subtracts relativity payments because they are additional settlement income rather than returns generated from deployed capital. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

### Asset mix and geography

Asset mix appears to turn starting conditions into different risk-return paths: TDB says property is the largest asset class for Ngāi Tahu, Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei, Ngāti Toa, Raukawa and Waikato-Tainui; financial assets are predominant for Ngāti Porou and Tūhoe; and primary industries remain the core holding for Ngāpuhi, Ngāti Pāhauwera and Ngāti Awa. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The geography signal is strongest in urban and corridor property: TDB says Waikato-Tainui's 7.2% average RoIC over 2016-2025 and Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei's 7.6% average were helped by long-duration property appreciation in Hamilton and Auckland, especially from 2016 to 2022. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

That same property exposure also creates cyclicality: TDB says Waikato-Tainui's FY2025 RoIC fell to 1.7% after investment-property and interest-rate-swap fair-value movements swung from gains to losses, and it says Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei recorded negative RoIC after $74m of investment-property valuation losses in a weak Auckland market. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

Waikato-Tainui's 2025 annual report states the same mechanism internally: its WRLT fund held 80% of value in local real-estate-related assets, and management says the property-heavy portfolio materially affects sustainable financial growth and balance-sheet stability during economic duress. [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

Primary-sector or rohe-based assets create a different exposure profile: TDB says Ngāi Tahu's FY2025 rebound was helped by farming yields, farmgate prices, commercial/Crown property gains and international shares, while seafood was weaker because of lower crayfish prices into China and fishery-health issues. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

Te Rarawa's smaller-portfolio annual report shows the same structural constraint at another scale: Te Waka Pupuri Pūtea says the commercial asset portfolio grew from $32.6m in 2016 to $115.2m in 2025, but the group slowed new investment, consolidated existing assets, strengthened liquidity, and managed debt exposure because market headwinds and debt-servicing pressure limited acceleration. [Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

### Debt, partnership access and scale

Debt capacity is partly an asset-mix outcome: TDB says iwi primarily invested in financial assets typically carry minimal or no gearing, while iwi with substantial property portfolios use more debt to fund development; TDB also says leverage can leave RoIC unchanged while increasing equity risk because fixed financing obligations still have to be met in downturns. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

Waikato-Tainui illustrates the opportunity and constraint together: TDB says property accounts for about $1.8b, or 73%, of Waikato-Tainui assets in 2025, while the annual report says Ruakura's value grew 100-fold over 12 years after rezoning from dairy farm to inland port/logistics/industrial hub and that debt was used to create that value. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

The same annual report says TGH's options to finance the Hamilton-to-Auckland corridor pipeline were limited, and that the Brookfield joint venture lets Brookfield provide cash for each new building while Waikato-Tainui contributes whenua on long-term ground leases, keeps whenua ownership, avoids increasing bank debt, and reduces bank interest by about $10m each year. [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf)

This makes partnership access a structural advantage rather than just a governance preference: a large, zoned, infrastructure-linked land platform can attract institutional capital on terms that retain whenua, while a smaller or less urban portfolio may need to prioritise liquidity and debt control before it can pursue similar scale. [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

The Crown delivery system can also shape opportunity unevenly after settlement: the Auditor-General says public organisations often see settlements as transactions rather than broader strategic relationships, and warns that failures to provide redress as intended can cause post-settlement governance entities, iwi and hapū to miss significant economic and social opportunities. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/treaty-settlements/docs/treaty-settlements.pdf/%40%40download/file/treaty-settlements.pdf)

### Inequality is clearer than attribution

Net assets per member makes the inequality visible but not definitive: TDB says the ten-iwi aggregate declined 1.1% in FY2025, with outcomes ranging from +6.4% for Ngāti Toa to -10.0% for Ngāti Pāhauwera, -9.8% for Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei and -6.3% for Waikato-Tainui; over 2016-2025, Waikato-Tainui's net assets per member grew 5.0% per year while Ngāi Tahu's fell 0.5% per year because membership growth slightly outpaced net-asset growth. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

That metric is still imperfect because TDB says net assets per member is affected by accounting treatment, valuation policies, capitalised versus expensed community investment, and membership-count differences; TDB also says some iwi retain or pursue assets for cultural or strategic significance rather than expected financial yield. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf)

The most defensible conclusion is therefore structural and probabilistic: large early settlements and relativity clauses give Waikato-Tainui and Ngāi Tahu a larger capital base; urban or strategically located property can convert that base into debt capacity and partnerable projects; but concentrated property or primary-industry exposure can also increase volatility, and smaller iwi can still grow strongly when they preserve liquidity, manage debt and match investments to local assets. [TDB Advisory, 2025](https://www.tdb.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/TDB-Iwi-Investment-Report-2025.pdf); [Waikato-Tainui annual report, 2025](https://www.tgh.co.nz/media/ptud0oru/wt_annual-report_final-pop-ups.pdf); [Te Rūnanga o Te Rarawa annual report, 2025](https://www.terarawa.iwi.nz/files/annual-reports/annual-report-2024-25.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A full iwi-approved panel dataset with settlement receipts by date, relativity receipts, asset allocation, debt, returns, distributions, member counts, valuation policies, and non-financial outcomes would allow a much stronger attribution model than this public-source synthesis.
- Public or iwi-approved deal-level evidence on partnership terms, bank covenants, debt pricing and rejected opportunities would test whether capital access is as structurally unequal as the Ruakura and Te Rarawa contrast suggests.
- Māori commercial lawyers, iwi governors, PSGE practitioners and members of affected iwi should review any policy use of this finding, because public financial reports cannot decide what trade-offs are tika for a particular iwi.
- I could not verify a complete list of which settlements do or do not contain relativity-like mechanisms; the finding only supports the narrower claim that Waikato-Tainui and Ngāi Tahu have prominent five-yearly relativity mechanisms and that TDB's sample treats them as unusual enough to note separately.
- I could not separate the effects of settlement starting conditions from governance quality, market timing, Crown delivery delays, member registration growth, and iwi-defined cultural or social objectives.

## Open follow-up questions

- How would rankings change if net assets per member were adjusted for unpaid Crown commitments, cultural asset retention, expensed housing/community investment, and iwi-specific valuation policies?
- Which later settlements include commercial redress options, deferred selection properties or rights of first refusal that have created property-like uplift comparable to relativity payments?
- What partnership models let smaller or less urban iwi access long-term capital without losing control of whenua or increasing debt beyond mandate?
