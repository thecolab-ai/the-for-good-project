# What support can a struggling NZ small business operator realistically access before formal insolvency, and where are the gaps?

This is the **support-gap child of Stream #257** (Part of #257). The stream's discover baseline established that public help points exist — IRD instalment arrangements, the Insolvency and Trustee Service (ITS), Companies Register guidance, MBIE/Regional Business Partner (RBP) advice, and Business Mentors NZ — but explicitly left open *"whether a struggling small operator can find early, independent, affordable restructuring help before liquidation"* ([framing finding, #257](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/nz-business-insolvencies-framing.md)). This finding tests that hypothesis against the primary guidance each channel publishes. It names no individual business, director, or adviser.

## Executive answer

- **Help exists, but there is no single "front door" for a business in distress.** A struggling operator must self-assemble a path across at least five unconnected silos — IRD (tax debt), ITS (insolvency information), the Companies Register (formal-process mechanics), business.govt.nz/RBP (advice), and paid mentoring/insolvency practitioners. Each channel is real and current, but none owns the whole journey, so navigation depends on the owner already knowing what to ask for. [IRD debt & insolvency](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency); [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business)
- **The main free advisory channels are built for growth, not crisis.** Business Mentors NZ requires owners to be *"motivated and determined to grow your business"* and charges a **$295 + GST** registration fee; the RBP Network markets *"unbiased business support"* for growth and capability. Neither foregrounds acute financial distress or insolvency risk. [Business Mentors NZ](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/); [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)
- **The tax system's strongest relief — a serious-hardship write-off — is only available to natural persons.** IRD's own standard practice statement grounds hardship relief in section 176 of the Tax Administration Act, which lets the Commissioner not recover amounts *"to the extent that it would place a **natural person** taxpayer in serious hardship"* ([SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)). A sole trader can seek that write-off; an incorporated micro-company cannot — it gets only an instalment arrangement or the Commissioner's discretionary write-off of *irrecoverable* debt, on which the business relief page promises nothing specific. [IRD company/partnership/trust relief](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/financial-relief-companies-partnerships-trusts)
- **There is no affordable early-restructuring mechanism.** Every formal pre-liquidation tool NZ offers — voluntary administration, receivership, a Part 14/15 compromise — must be run by a **licensed insolvency practitioner** (mandatory for all new appointments since 1 September 2020), i.e. a paid CA-level specialist. The temporary COVID-era Business Debt Hibernation scheme, the one low-cost DIY option NZ ever had, was **revoked on 31 May 2022** with no permanent successor. NZ has not adopted a simplified small-business restructuring regime like Australia's (in force since 1 January 2021 for companies with liabilities under A$1m). [Companies Office licensing](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/); [Business Debt Hibernation regulations (revoked)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0294/latest/LMS427861.html); [ASIC small business restructuring](https://www.asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/insolvency/insolvency-for-directors/small-business-restructuring-and-the-restructuring-plan/)
- **The clearest gaps are: timing (help is late and reactive), affordability (formal help needs a paid specialist), visibility (no distress-specific front door), and coverage of the incorporated micro-operator (who falls between personal-debt services and expensive corporate insolvency).** Independence is the least-bad dimension — IRD, ITS, and RBP advice are genuinely public and unbiased. [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [MoneyTalks](https://www.moneytalks.co.nz/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the existence, scope, and cost of each channel are verified against official/primary sources (High on those facts). The *gap* judgements are inferences from what the guidance does and does not cover; they are well-supported but would be strengthened by practitioner and lived-experience evidence, which an agent cannot ethically gather here. Proving a *negative* ("no affordable early-restructuring path exists") is inherently harder than proving a channel exists.

## Evidence

### User-journey map: what a struggling operator actually encounters

The table below walks a small operator from first distress to formal insolvency, showing what is available at each stage and the friction a real owner hits. It is a synthesis of the official guidance cited throughout this finding, not a claim that every owner follows these steps in order.

| Stage | What the owner is trying to do | What's realistically available | Friction / gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Recognise distress** | "Am I actually in trouble?" | No official self-assessment or early-warning trigger aimed at owners; distress is usually recognised via a missed tax/rent/supplier payment. | **Timing.** Nothing prompts the owner early; recognition is event-driven, often after arrears have compounded. |
| **2. Look for help** | Find *the* place to go | No single front door. Search leads variously to IRD (tax), business.govt.nz (general advice), or an accountant. | **Visibility.** The owner must already know which silo owns their problem. |
| **3. Deal with tax debt** | Stop IRD action | Instalment arrangement in myIR; financial-relief application; tax-pooling pilot for 2023–24 income tax. IRD urges contact *"before it gets worse."* | **Interest/penalties keep accruing** on an instalment arrangement; hardship write-off is natural-persons-only (see below). |
| **4. Get advice** | Understand options | RBP Network (free/subsidised, growth-oriented); Business Mentors NZ ($295+GST, growth-oriented); an accountant/lawyer (paid). | **Affordability + fit.** Free advice channels are built for growth, not turnaround; specialist turnaround advice is paid. |
| **5. Restructure to survive** | Rearrange debts, keep trading | Voluntary administration or a creditor compromise — both require a **licensed insolvency practitioner**. No low-cost DIY scheme since Business Debt Hibernation ended. | **Affordability + expertise.** Formal restructuring is uneconomic for a micro-firm; the cheap DIY option was withdrawn in 2022. |
| **6. Wind up** | Close down orderly | Liquidation (creditor/shareholder resolution or court); ITS involved only if court-appointed. | By this stage restructuring options are largely foreclosed. |

Sources for the stages: [IRD unable to pay](https://www.ird.govt.nz/situations/unable-to-pay-my-tax-debt); [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir); [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/); [Business Mentors NZ](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/); [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [Companies Office licensing](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/).

### IRD debt relief: real and reachable, but interest keeps running and hardship relief is for people, not companies

IRD's instalment-arrangement page says an arrangement *"is for debt you cannot pay in full by the due date, or overdue debt,"* is requested through myIR, and — importantly — that *"any overdue debt balances may cause use of money interest (UOMI) and penalties,"* with that interest *"included as part of your instalment amounts."* [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir) So an instalment arrangement stops further *collection* action if kept, but it does not stop the debt growing.

IRD's "I'm struggling to file and pay my tax" page is the closest thing to an early-action front door, and it does urge owners to act early: IRD *"can work with you to come up with a solution before it gets worse."* It lists three routes — instalment arrangements, financial relief (where IRD *"might be able to write off penalties or certain amounts"*), and a tax-pooling pilot for 2023–24 income-tax debt. [IRD unable to pay](https://www.ird.govt.nz/situations/unable-to-pay-my-tax-debt)

The load-bearing distinction is *who* can get a hardship write-off. IRD's Standard Practice Statement SPS 18/04, "Options for relief from tax debt," states that *"Section 176 of the Act allows the Commissioner to not recover outstanding amounts to the extent that it would place a **natural person** taxpayer in serious hardship,"* and that write-off can be combined with an instalment arrangement where *"the test for serious hardship is met."* [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en) The same statement notes the Commissioner's overarching duty under s176 to *"maximise the recovery of outstanding tax,"* with write-off otherwise reserved for amounts that are irrecoverable. [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)

That statutory line matters for the incorporated operator. IRD's separate relief page for **companies, partnerships and trusts** asks the applicant to explain *"what events or circumstances are stopping you from repaying the debt"* and to supply current assets, liabilities, shareholder current-account position, and optionally a twelve-month cash-flow forecast (IR591); it does **not** offer a serious-hardship write-off, because serious hardship is a natural-person test. [IRD company/partnership/trust relief](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/financial-relief-companies-partnerships-trusts); [IRD IR591 cash-flow forecast](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/send-a-twelve-month-cash-flow-forecast---ir591) The result: a **sole trader** (a natural person, personally liable for business tax) can seek hardship relief, while an owner who incorporated — often on professional advice, for limited liability — cannot get the company's core tax written off on hardship grounds and is left with instalment terms plus the Commissioner's discretion over genuinely irrecoverable debt. [ITS determining your type of debt](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/determining-your-type-of-debt)

**Confidence: High** on the mechanics and the natural-person restriction (IRD primary sources); **Medium** on the *practical* size of the sole-trader-vs-company gap, which depends on how IRD exercises discretion case by case — not something I can quantify from public data.

### The Insolvency and Trustee Service informs, but does not help you restructure early

ITS is the government's insolvency body, and its business-debt pages are clear and public. "Determining your type of debt" explains that a sole trader is *"personally responsible for all the debts of the business,"* partners are *"all equally responsible,"* and in a company *"if you have personally guaranteed the company debts you will be responsible if the company cannot pay."* It is explicitly *informational guidance rather than legal advice* and refers readers on to professional help. [ITS determining your type of debt](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/determining-your-type-of-debt)

Its "company insolvency options" page describes the three formal routes — receivership (*"a receiver is appointed by a secured creditor to deal with the secured assets"*), voluntary administration (*"an administrator is appointed to review and rearrange a business to avoid liquidation"*), and liquidation — and notes a liquidator is *"usually a chartered accountant, or an insolvency expert."* Crucially, ITS itself *"only deals with liquidations, and generally only if appointed by the court."* [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options) So the state insolvency service is a court-liquidation operator and an information publisher — it is not a place a distressed owner goes for *early* turnaround help. The Companies Register echoes the mechanics: a voluntary administrator is appointed to *"review and rearrange the company's business and financial affairs"* and, if the company can't be saved, to try to get creditors a better return than immediate liquidation. [Companies Register voluntary administration](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/when-your-company-fails/what-happens-during-voluntary-administration/)

**Confidence: High** — this is drawn directly from ITS and Companies Register guidance.

### Free/subsidised advice channels are oriented to growth and capability, not turnaround

The Regional Business Partner Network markets *"free or subsidised access to unbiased business support in your region,"* and MBIE promotes it (and Business Mentors NZ) to owners *"looking to grow, adapt, or get advice."* [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/); [MBIE RBP news, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/record-high-number-of-small-businesses-taking-advantage-of-regional-support-and-advice) Its public registration page makes no mention of insolvency, acute financial distress, or turnaround.

Business Mentors NZ — the long-running national mentoring charity (operating since 1991, *"over 85,000"* owners mentored) — charges a **$295 + GST** registration fee for a 12-month mentoring relationship and screens applicants for growth readiness: they must be *"motivated and determined to grow your business"* and *"ready to listen to and receive constructive feedback."* Its framing is growth and capability, not crisis management. [Business Mentors NZ](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/) A fee is modest in absolute terms, but for an owner already unable to pay tax or suppliers it is another barrier, and the growth framing signals the service is not designed for someone weighing whether to keep trading.

The one large *free* one-to-one money-help service, MoneyTalks (MSD-funded financial mentors, 0800 345 123), is aimed at *"anyone in financial hardship"* and works on *personal/household* money problems and debt — not company insolvency or trading decisions. [MoneyTalks](https://www.moneytalks.co.nz/); [MSD MoneyTalks](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html) It can help a sole trader as an individual in hardship, but it is not a business-turnaround service and does not cover an incorporated company's affairs.

**Confidence: High** on the fee and growth-orientation of these channels (their own sites); **Medium** on the inference that no free channel covers *turnaround* specifically — I searched for a business-debt equivalent of MoneyTalks and found none, but absence of evidence is weaker than evidence of absence.

### Formal early restructuring exists but requires a paid licensed specialist — and the cheap DIY option was withdrawn

Since 1 September 2020, *"anyone acting as an insolvency practitioner must hold a licence issued by an accredited body"* — and "insolvency practitioner" expressly includes *"the administrator or deed administrator of a company in voluntary administration,"* a liquidator, and a receiver. Practitioners cannot take *"any new insolvency engagements from 1 September 2020 without obtaining a licence."* [Companies Office licensing](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/) Licensing under the Insolvency Practitioners Regulation Act 2019 requires senior insolvency experience (broadly 1,000–2,000 hours depending on qualifications), confirming these are specialist, fee-charging professionals. [Insolvency Practitioners Regulation Act 2019](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2019/0029/latest/whole.html) The practical consequence: the only formal way to *restructure and keep trading* — voluntary administration, or a Part 14/15 compromise administered by such a practitioner — carries specialist professional fees that are frequently uneconomic for a company whose whole liabilities are small.

NZ once had a low-cost, DIY-style alternative: the COVID-era **Business Debt Hibernation** scheme let a company put existing debts on hold for up to seven months with the agreement of 50% of creditors, without appointing a practitioner. But the Companies (COVID-19—Business Debt Hibernation) Regulations 2020 were **revoked on 31 May 2022**, and I found no permanent successor. [business.govt.nz Business Debt Hibernation](https://www.business.govt.nz/covid-19/business-debt-hibernation/); [Business Debt Hibernation regulations (revoked)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0294/latest/LMS427861.html)

This is where the comparison with Australia is instructive. Australia introduced a **Small Business Restructuring** process on **1 January 2021** for companies with total liabilities under **A$1 million**, letting directors *retain control* while a registered practitioner helps build a creditor repayment plan — a deliberately cheaper, faster, debtor-in-possession route for small firms. [ASIC small business restructuring](https://www.asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/insolvency/insolvency-for-directors/small-business-restructuring-and-the-restructuring-plan/); [Australian Treasury simplified debt restructuring factsheet](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2020-12/simplified-debt-restructuring-fact-sheet_0.pdf) New Zealand's Insolvency Working Group (2015–2017) reviewed voluntary liquidations, voidable transactions, and practitioner regulation, and its recommendations fed later reforms — but it did not create a simplified small-business restructuring track, and NZ's formal options remain VA, receivership, compromise, and liquidation. [MBIE Insolvency Working Group](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/regulating-entities/insolvency-review-working-group) So NZ currently has **no low-cost, small-business-specific early-restructuring regime** — a gap Australia has explicitly filled.

**Confidence: High** on the licensing requirement and the Hibernation revocation (primary legislation and Companies Office); **Medium** on "NZ has no equivalent to Australia's regime," because that is a negative claim — I verified NZ's current formal options and the absence of a simplified track in the reform record, but cannot exhaustively prove no scheme exists under another name.

### Independence is the least-bad dimension

On independence, the public channels score comparatively well: IRD, ITS, and the Companies Register are government bodies with no sales motive, and RBP support is marketed as *"unbiased."* [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/) The independence concern is subtler and sits at the paid tier: turnaround and insolvency advice often comes from the same firms that earn fees from appointments (a liquidator is *"usually a chartered accountant, or an insolvency expert"* [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options)), so an owner seeking early, disinterested "should I even keep going?" advice may struggle to find it free of a downstream commercial interest. I did not find evidence quantifying how often this conflict bites, so I flag it as a hypothesis rather than a proven gap.

**Confidence: Medium** — the public channels' independence is clear; the paid-tier conflict is a reasoned concern, not a measured finding.

## What would change this conclusion

- **Practitioner or lived-experience evidence on how the channels perform in practice.** I verified what each channel *publishes*, not how a distressed owner *experiences* it. If turnaround accountants, financial mentors, or owners report that RBP advisers routinely triage distress well, or that IRD's discretionary write-off for irrecoverable company debt is granted readily, the "gap" would narrow. This needs a human with lived experience or frontline-adviser access — an ethical limit for an agent. [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)
- **Data on how often incorporated micro-firms are denied relief a sole trader would get.** The sole-trader-vs-company hardship asymmetry is clear in statute, but its real-world magnitude depends on IRD's exercise of discretion, which I could not quantify from public data. IRD case-mix or tax-technical data could confirm or shrink it. [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)
- **A successor to Business Debt Hibernation, or a live MBIE proposal for small-business restructuring.** If MBIE has consulted on or legislated a simplified regime since the 2015–2017 Working Group, the "no affordable early-restructuring path" claim would need updating. I found the Working Group scope and the current formal options, but did not exhaustively search every post-2022 MBIE consultation. [MBIE Insolvency Working Group](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/regulating-entities/insolvency-review-working-group)
- **Evidence of a distress-specific front door I missed.** I could not find a single government "your business is in financial trouble — start here" page equivalent to IRD's personal debt front door; if one exists (e.g. a business.govt.nz financial-difficulty hub), the visibility gap would soften. The specific business.govt.nz cash-flow/debt URL I tried returned 404, so I relied on the channel-level pages instead. [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business)
- **Could not verify:** the typical *cost* of a voluntary administration or compromise for a micro-company (fees aren't published); how many distressed owners reach an adviser before vs after a liquidation trigger; whether RBP capability funding is ever used for turnaround in practice; the take-up of IRD's tax-pooling pilot among distressed businesses.
