# What support can a struggling NZ small business operator realistically access before formal insolvency, and where are the gaps?

This is the **support-gap child of Stream #257** (Part of #257). The stream's discover baseline established that public help points exist — IRD instalment arrangements, the Insolvency and Trustee Service (ITS), Companies Register guidance, MBIE/Regional Business Partner (RBP) advice, and Business Mentors NZ — but explicitly left open *"whether a struggling small operator can find early, independent, affordable restructuring help before liquidation"* ([framing finding, #257](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/nz-business-insolvencies-framing.md)). This finding tests that hypothesis against the primary guidance each channel publishes. It names no individual business, director, or adviser.

## Executive answer

- **Help exists, but there is no single "front door" for a business in distress.** A struggling operator must self-assemble a path across at least five unconnected silos — IRD (tax debt), ITS (insolvency information), the Companies Register (formal-process mechanics), business.govt.nz/RBP (advice), and paid mentoring/insolvency practitioners. Each channel is real and current, but none owns the whole journey, so navigation depends on the owner already knowing what to ask for. [IRD debt & insolvency](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency); [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business)
- **The main free advisory channels are built for growth, not crisis.** Business Mentors NZ requires owners to be *"motivated and determined to grow your business"* and charges a **$295 + GST** registration fee; the RBP Network markets *"unbiased business support"* for growth and capability. Neither foregrounds acute financial distress or insolvency risk. [Business Mentors NZ](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/); [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)
- **The tax system's strongest relief — a serious-hardship write-off — is only available to natural persons.** IRD's own standard practice statement grounds hardship relief in section 176 of the Tax Administration Act, which lets the Commissioner not recover amounts *"to the extent that it would place a **natural person** taxpayer in serious hardship"* ([SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)). A sole trader can seek that write-off; an incorporated micro-company cannot — it gets only an instalment arrangement or the Commissioner's discretionary write-off of *irrecoverable* debt, on which the business relief page promises nothing specific. [IRD company/partnership/trust relief](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/financial-relief-companies-partnerships-trusts)
- **NZ's lowest-cost formal restructuring route can be run by the company's own board — but it isn't the signposted, protected early-restructuring path a distressed micro-operator needs.** *Correcting an earlier draft of this finding:* a **Part 14 compromise with creditors does not require a licensed insolvency practitioner.** Companies Act 1993 **section 228** lets the **board of directors** (or a receiver, a liquidator, or — with the court's leave — a creditor or shareholder) propose one, and MBIE deliberately **excluded** Part 14 compromises from the insolvency-practitioner licensing regime to preserve them as *"a simple and flexible alternative to other statutory insolvency processes."* [Companies Act 1993 s228](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0105/latest/DLM321171.html); [MBIE/IWG "Insolvency practitioners and voluntary liquidations" Cabinet paper](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/9b498bc179/insolvency-practitioners-and-voluntary-liquidations.pdf) The gaps are subtler than "you must pay a specialist": a compromise still practically needs paid professional help to design and to clear its approval threshold (*"a majority in number representing 75% in value of the creditors or class of creditors voting"*), it gives no automatic moratorium on enforcement while it is being proposed, and **nothing signposts it to a distressed owner as an early self-help tool.** The two routes owners most often hear about — **voluntary administration and receivership** — *do* require a paid licensed practitioner (mandatory for all new appointments since 1 September 2020) and are uneconomic for a micro-firm; the truly DIY option, COVID-era Business Debt Hibernation, was **revoked on 31 May 2022** with no successor; and NZ has **not** adopted a purpose-built simplified small-business restructuring regime like Australia's (in force since 1 January 2021 for companies with liabilities under A$1m). [Companies Office licensing](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/); [Business Debt Hibernation regulations (revoked)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0294/latest/LMS427861.html); [ASIC small business restructuring](https://www.asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/insolvency/insolvency-for-directors/small-business-restructuring-and-the-restructuring-plan/)
- **The clearest gaps are: timing (help is late and reactive), affordability and navigation (the restructuring routes owners are steered to need a paid specialist, while the one board-runnable route is unsignposted and still needs paid design help), visibility (no distress-specific front door), and coverage of the incorporated micro-operator (who falls between personal-debt services and expensive corporate insolvency).** Independence is the least-bad dimension — IRD, ITS, and RBP advice are genuinely public and unbiased. [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options); [MoneyTalks](https://www.moneytalks.co.nz/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the existence, scope, and cost of each channel are verified against official/primary sources (High on those facts). The *gap* judgements are inferences from what the guidance does and does not cover; they are well-supported but would be strengthened by practitioner and lived-experience evidence, which an agent cannot ethically gather here. The narrowed restructuring claim — that NZ lacks a *purpose-built, protected, small-business-specific* early-restructuring regime, not that it lacks any affordable route — is a bounded negative: I verified NZ's current formal options (Part 14 compromise, VA, receivership, liquidation) and the absence of an Australian-style debtor-in-possession small-business track in the reform record, but cannot exhaustively prove no such scheme exists under another name.

### IRD debt relief: real and reachable, but interest keeps running and hardship relief is for people, not companies

IRD's instalment-arrangement page says an arrangement *"is for debt you cannot pay in full by the due date, or overdue debt,"* is requested through myIR, and — importantly — that *"any overdue debt balances may cause use of money interest (UOMI) and penalties,"* with that interest *"included as part of your instalment amounts."* [IRD instalment arrangements](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/apply-for-an-instalment-arrangement-in-myir) So an instalment arrangement stops further *collection* action if kept, but it does not stop the debt growing.

IRD's "I'm struggling to file and pay my tax" page is the closest thing to an early-action front door, and it does urge owners to act early: IRD *"can work with you to come up with a solution before it gets worse."* It lists three routes — instalment arrangements, financial relief (where IRD *"might be able to write off penalties or certain amounts"*), and a tax-pooling pilot for 2023–24 income-tax debt. [IRD unable to pay](https://www.ird.govt.nz/situations/unable-to-pay-my-tax-debt)

The load-bearing distinction is *who* can get a hardship write-off. IRD's Standard Practice Statement SPS 18/04, "Options for relief from tax debt," states that *"Section 176 of the Act allows the Commissioner to not recover outstanding amounts to the extent that it would place a **natural person** taxpayer in serious hardship,"* and that write-off can be combined with an instalment arrangement where *"the test for serious hardship is met."* [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en) The same statement notes the Commissioner's overarching duty under s176 to *"maximise the recovery of outstanding tax,"* with write-off otherwise reserved for amounts that are irrecoverable. [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)

That statutory line matters for the incorporated operator. IRD's separate relief page for **companies, partnerships and trusts** asks the applicant to explain *"what events or circumstances are stopping you from repaying the debt"* and to supply current assets, liabilities, shareholder current-account position, and optionally a twelve-month cash-flow forecast (IR591); it does **not** offer a serious-hardship write-off, because serious hardship is a natural-person test. [IRD company/partnership/trust relief](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/financial-relief-companies-partnerships-trusts); [IRD IR591 cash-flow forecast](https://www.ird.govt.nz/managing-my-tax/debt-and-insolvency/send-a-twelve-month-cash-flow-forecast---ir591) The result: a **sole trader** (a natural person, personally liable for business tax) can seek hardship relief, while an owner who incorporated — often on professional advice, for limited liability — cannot get the company's core tax written off on hardship grounds and is left with instalment terms plus the Commissioner's discretion over genuinely irrecoverable debt. [ITS determining your type of debt](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/determining-your-type-of-debt)

**Confidence: High** on the mechanics and the natural-person restriction (IRD primary sources); **Medium** on the *practical* size of the sole-trader-vs-company gap, which depends on how IRD exercises discretion case by case — not something I can quantify from public data.

### The Insolvency and Trustee Service informs, but does not help you restructure early

ITS is the government's insolvency body, and its business-debt pages are clear and public. "Determining your type of debt" explains that a sole trader is *"personally responsible for all the debts of the business,"* partners are *"all equally responsible,"* and in a company *"if you have personally guaranteed the company debts you will be responsible if the company cannot pay."* It is explicitly *informational guidance rather than legal advice* and refers readers on to professional help. [ITS determining your type of debt](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/determining-your-type-of-debt)

Its "company insolvency options" page describes the three formal routes — receivership (*"a receiver is appointed by a secured creditor to deal with the secured assets"*), voluntary administration (*"an administrator is appointed to review and rearrange a business to avoid liquidation"*), and liquidation — and notes a liquidator is *"usually a chartered accountant, or an insolvency expert."* Crucially, ITS itself *"only deals with liquidations, and generally only if appointed by the court."* [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options) So the state insolvency service is a court-liquidation operator and an information publisher — it is not a place a distressed owner goes for *early* turnaround help. The Companies Register echoes the mechanics: a voluntary administrator is appointed to *"review and rearrange the company's business and financial affairs"* and, if the company can't be saved, to try to get creditors a better return than immediate liquidation. [Companies Register voluntary administration](https://companies-register.companiesoffice.govt.nz/help-centre/when-your-company-fails/what-happens-during-voluntary-administration/)

**Confidence: High** — this is drawn directly from ITS and Companies Register guidance.

### Free/subsidised advice channels are oriented to growth and capability, not turnaround

The Regional Business Partner Network markets *"free or subsidised access to unbiased business support in your region,"* and MBIE promotes it (and Business Mentors NZ) to owners *"looking to grow, adapt, or get advice."* [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/); [MBIE RBP news, 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/record-high-number-of-small-businesses-taking-advantage-of-regional-support-and-advice) Its public registration page makes no mention of insolvency, acute financial distress, or turnaround.

Business Mentors NZ — the long-running national mentoring charity (operating since 1991, *"over 85,000"* owners mentored) — charges a **$295 + GST** registration fee for a 12-month mentoring relationship and screens applicants for growth readiness: they must be *"motivated and determined to grow your business"* and *"ready to listen to and receive constructive feedback."* Its framing is growth and capability, not crisis management. [Business Mentors NZ](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/) A fee is modest in absolute terms, but for an owner already unable to pay tax or suppliers it is another barrier, and the growth framing signals the service is not designed for someone weighing whether to keep trading.

The one large *free* one-to-one money-help service, MoneyTalks (MSD-funded financial mentors, 0800 345 123), is aimed at *"anyone in financial hardship"* and works on *personal/household* money problems and debt — not company insolvency or trading decisions. [MoneyTalks](https://www.moneytalks.co.nz/); [MSD MoneyTalks](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/moneytalks/index.html) It can help a sole trader as an individual in hardship, but it is not a business-turnaround service and does not cover an incorporated company's affairs.

**Confidence: High** on the fee and growth-orientation of these channels (their own sites); **Medium** on the inference that no free channel covers *turnaround* specifically — I searched for a business-debt equivalent of MoneyTalks and found none, but absence of evidence is weaker than evidence of absence.

### A board can run a Part 14 compromise without a licensed practitioner — but NZ still lacks a protected, small-business-specific early-restructuring regime

**An earlier draft of this finding was wrong on a load-bearing point, and this section corrects it.** It is *not* true that every formal pre-liquidation restructuring route requires a licensed insolvency practitioner. A **Part 14 compromise with creditors** — the statutory route by which an insolvent company can vary or cancel its debts and keep trading — may be proposed by the company's **own board of directors**. Companies Act 1993 **section 228** provides that a compromise may be proposed by *"(a) the board of directors of the company; (b) a receiver … ; (c) a liquidator of the company; (d) with the leave of the court, any creditor or shareholder of the company."* [Companies Act 1993 s228](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0105/latest/DLM321171.html) MBIE confirmed the deliberate design: its Cabinet paper on insolvency-practitioner regulation records that *"the compromise system provides a simple and flexible alternative to other statutory insolvency processes. The simplicity arises in large part because the Companies Act does not prescribe an office or role of 'compromise trustee' or set out any specific duties, rights or powers of the person who designs or manages the compromise,"* and it decided to *"Exclude compromises under Part 14 of the Companies Act 1993"* from the licensing regime — expressly rejecting submissions that compromise managers should be licensed. [MBIE/IWG "Insolvency practitioners and voluntary liquidations" Cabinet paper](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/9b498bc179/insolvency-practitioners-and-voluntary-liquidations.pdf)

So the affordability problem is more specific than "you must hire a CA-level specialist." A Part 14 compromise is approved when *"a majority in number representing 75% in value of the creditors or class of creditors voting"* agrees, and it then binds dissenting creditors in that class — a high bar that in practice needs paid legal/accounting help to design and negotiate. [Companies Act 1993 Part 14 — Compromises with creditors](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1993/0105/latest/DLM321160.html) Unlike voluntary administration, a proposed compromise carries **no automatic statutory moratorium** halting creditor enforcement while it is put together (a creditor can seek that the court restrain proceedings, but there is no automatic stay). And crucially, nothing in the distressed-owner-facing guidance signposts a compromise as an early self-help option — ITS and the Companies Register describe VA, receivership, and liquidation, and route owners to professional help. [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options)

The two routes owners *are* commonly steered to **do** require a paid licensed practitioner. Since 1 September 2020, *"anyone acting as an insolvency practitioner must hold a licence issued by an accredited body"* — and "insolvency practitioner" expressly includes *"the administrator or deed administrator of a company in voluntary administration,"* a liquidator, and a receiver (but, per the Cabinet decision above, **not** a Part 14 compromise proponent). [Companies Office licensing](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/) Licensing under the Insolvency Practitioners Regulation Act 2019 requires senior insolvency experience (broadly 1,000–2,000 hours depending on qualifications), confirming these are specialist, fee-charging professionals — so voluntary administration and receivership are frequently uneconomic for a company whose whole liabilities are small. [Insolvency Practitioners Regulation Act 2019](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2019/0029/latest/whole.html)

NZ once had a genuinely DIY-style standstill: the COVID-era **Business Debt Hibernation** scheme let a company put existing debts on hold for up to seven months with the agreement of 50% of creditors, without appointing a practitioner. But the Companies (COVID-19—Business Debt Hibernation) Regulations 2020 were **revoked on 31 May 2022**, and I found no permanent successor. [business.govt.nz Business Debt Hibernation](https://www.business.govt.nz/covid-19/business-debt-hibernation/); [Business Debt Hibernation regulations (revoked)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0294/latest/LMS427861.html)

This is where the comparison with Australia is instructive — but the narrowed claim matters. Australia introduced a **Small Business Restructuring** process on **1 January 2021** for companies with total liabilities under **A$1 million**, letting directors *retain control* while a *registered* restructuring practitioner helps build a creditor repayment plan under a statutory framework with its own moratorium — a purpose-built, threshold-gated, debtor-in-possession route for small firms. [ASIC small business restructuring](https://www.asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/insolvency/insolvency-for-directors/small-business-restructuring-and-the-restructuring-plan/); [Australian Treasury simplified debt restructuring factsheet](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2020-12/simplified-debt-restructuring-fact-sheet_0.pdf) New Zealand's Part 14 compromise overlaps in spirit (debtor-run, out-of-court) but is a general-purpose tool, not a small-business-specific regime: it has no liability threshold, no bespoke moratorium, and no lighter-touch registered practitioner or fee cap. New Zealand's Insolvency Working Group (2015–2017) reviewed voluntary liquidations, voidable transactions, and practitioner regulation, and its recommendations feed an ongoing insolvency-law reform package (Companies Act, Insolvency Act, Limited Partnerships Act), but I found no adopted or consulted Australian-style simplified small-business restructuring track; NZ's formal options remain Part 14 compromise, VA, receivership, and liquidation. [MBIE Insolvency Working Group](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/regulating-entities/insolvency-review-working-group) So the defensible claim is not "no affordable route exists" but that **NZ has no purpose-built, protected, small-business-specific early-restructuring regime** comparable to Australia's — and its board-runnable Part 14 route is neither signposted to distressed micro-operators nor free of the need for paid design help.

**Confidence: High** on section 228 (who may propose a compromise), the Part 14 licensing exclusion, the VA/receivership licensing requirement, and the Hibernation revocation (primary legislation, the MBIE Cabinet paper, and Companies Office); **Medium** on "NZ has no purpose-built small-business restructuring regime like Australia's," because that is a bounded negative — I verified NZ's current formal options and the absence of a simplified track in the reform record, but cannot exhaustively prove no scheme exists under another name.

### Independence is the least-bad dimension

On independence, the public channels score comparatively well: IRD, ITS, and the Companies Register are government bodies with no sales motive, and RBP support is marketed as *"unbiased."* [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/) The independence concern is subtler and sits at the paid tier: turnaround and insolvency advice often comes from the same firms that earn fees from appointments (a liquidator is *"usually a chartered accountant, or an insolvency expert"* [ITS company insolvency options](https://www.insolvency.govt.nz/business-debt/company-insolvency-options)), so an owner seeking early, disinterested "should I even keep going?" advice may struggle to find it free of a downstream commercial interest. I did not find evidence quantifying how often this conflict bites, so I flag it as a hypothesis rather than a proven gap.

**Confidence: Medium** — the public channels' independence is clear; the paid-tier conflict is a reasoned concern, not a measured finding.

| A Part 14 compromise does **not** require a licensed insolvency practitioner — the board may propose it and it is excluded from the licensing regime — whereas voluntary administration and receivership **do** require one (since 1 Sep 2020). | [Companies Act 1993 s228 (board may propose) + MBIE Cabinet paper excluding Part 14 from licensing](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/9b498bc179/insolvency-practitioners-and-voluntary-liquidations.pdf) | [Companies Office licensing (VA/receiver/liquidator)](https://www.companiesoffice.govt.nz/all-registers/insolvency-practitioners/licensing/) + [Insolvency Practitioners Regulation Act 2019](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2019/0029/latest/whole.html) | High |
| NZ's DIY debt-standstill (Business Debt Hibernation) was revoked in 2022 with no successor, and NZ has no purpose-built, small-business-specific early-restructuring regime like Australia's SBR (its Part 14 compromise is a general-purpose, unsignposted tool, not a threshold-gated debtor-in-possession regime). | [BDH regulations revoked 31 May 2022](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2020/0294/latest/LMS427861.html) | [ASIC small business restructuring (AU regime, from 1 Jan 2021)](https://www.asic.gov.au/regulatory-resources/insolvency/insolvency-for-directors/small-business-restructuring-and-the-restructuring-plan/) | Medium (bounded negative claim for NZ) |
| The main free/near-free advisory channels are growth-oriented, not turnaround-oriented. | [Business Mentors NZ — "determined to grow"](https://www.businessmentors.org.nz/) | [RBP Network — growth/capability framing](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/) | Medium |

## What would change this conclusion

- **Practitioner or lived-experience evidence on how the channels perform in practice.** I verified what each channel *publishes*, not how a distressed owner *experiences* it. If turnaround accountants, financial mentors, or owners report that RBP advisers routinely triage distress well, or that IRD's discretionary write-off for irrecoverable company debt is granted readily, the "gap" would narrow. This needs a human with lived experience or frontline-adviser access — an ethical limit for an agent. [RBP Network](https://web.regionalbusinesspartners.co.nz/business/)
- **Data on how often incorporated micro-firms are denied relief a sole trader would get.** The sole-trader-vs-company hardship asymmetry is clear in statute, but its real-world magnitude depends on IRD's exercise of discretion, which I could not quantify from public data. IRD case-mix or tax-technical data could confirm or shrink it. [SPS 18/04](https://www.taxtechnical.ird.govt.nz/-/media/project/ir/tt/pdfs/standard-practice-statements/returns-and-debt-collection/sps18-04.pdf?la=en)
- **A successor to Business Debt Hibernation, or a live MBIE proposal for a small-business-specific restructuring track.** An insolvency-law reform package (Companies Act, Insolvency Act, Limited Partnerships Act) is underway on the back of the 2015–2017 Working Group; if it introduces an Australian-style threshold-gated, debtor-in-possession small-business regime — or reworks the Part 14 compromise into one — the "no purpose-built small-business restructuring regime" claim would need updating. I found the Working Group scope, the current formal options, and the general reform lineage, but did not exhaustively search every post-2022 MBIE consultation for a simplified small-business track, and found none adopted. [MBIE Insolvency Working Group](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/regulating-entities/insolvency-review-working-group)
- **Evidence of a distress-specific front door I missed.** I could not find a single government "your business is in financial trouble — start here" page equivalent to IRD's personal debt front door; if one exists (e.g. a business.govt.nz financial-difficulty hub), the visibility gap would soften. The specific business.govt.nz cash-flow/debt URL I tried returned 404, so I relied on the channel-level pages instead. [MBIE support for business](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business)
- **Could not verify:** the typical *cost* of a voluntary administration or compromise for a micro-company (fees aren't published); how many distressed owners reach an adviser before vs after a liquidation trigger; whether RBP capability funding is ever used for turnaround in practice; the take-up of IRD's tax-pooling pilot among distressed businesses.

## Open follow-up questions

Candidate research issues (would be `Part of #257. Stream: #257.` if opened by a human steward — this finding does not open them):

- What does a voluntary administration or creditor compromise actually cost a NZ micro-company, and at what liability size does it become uneconomic? (Practitioner fee evidence.)
- Would an Australian-style simplified small-business restructuring regime fit NZ, and what did MBIE's post-2022 insolvency/Companies Act reform work say about it?
- How do frontline advisers (RBP, accountants, financial mentors) actually triage a distressed small operator — is there an informal referral path that the published guidance doesn't show?
