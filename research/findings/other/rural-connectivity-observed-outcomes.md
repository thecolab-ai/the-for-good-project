# Observed rural connectivity harms are clearest for service access, farm operations, and learning continuity, not national outcome totals

## Executive answer

- The strongest observed NZ links between poor rural connectivity quality and outcomes are **access constraints**, not national harm totals: rural health communities told the Ministry of Health in 2025 that poor broadband limits telehealth expansion in places such as Hanmer Springs and Thames, and Health NZ is considering community hubs for people who lack devices or home internet access [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf).
- Education evidence shows home connectivity was treated as learning infrastructure during COVID-19 disruptions: the Ministry of Education connected around 40,000 learner households, evaluated educational and wellbeing impacts, and concluded the rollout likely supported learner engagement, achievement and progress, while also warning that some learners still lacked digital access [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx).
- Farm evidence links connection quality to reported operational and safety harms, but this impact channel is weaker because I found only one current public NZ source for the reported harms themselves: Federated Farmers' 2025 survey, reported by Federated Farmers through Farmers Weekly, found mobile coverage averaged 57% of farmland, more than one-third of farmers said mobile coverage had worsened, more than half had experienced weather-related internet outages, and respondents connected dropouts to everyday operations, NAIT compliance, and emergency-call risk [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/).
- The Commerce Commission independently confirms the rural service-quality and price substrate, but not the operational, compliance, outage, or safety harms reported by farmers: in its 2025 telecommunications monitoring report, rural households paid an average $85.77 per month for broadband versus $72.65 for urban households, and rural consumers faced technology choices that differ in coverage, performance, reliability, device compatibility, upfront costs and monthly price [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- I did **not** find a public NZ dataset that causally links rural connection quality to national counts of missed telehealth consults, learning loss, farm incidents, compliance failures, lost revenue, or business closures; this means the observed-outcomes answer is Medium confidence and should not be used as a dollar-cost estimate [data.govt.nz catalogue search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=rural+broadband+connectivity+outcomes+telehealth+education+farm&rows=10), [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - multiple NZ sources show observed access, learning, price, performance, and reported farm-operation/safety problems linked to connectivity quality, but the farm-harm channel is single-sourced and public sources still stop short of causal national outcome totals.

## Evidence

### Scope and search result

This finding answers a narrower question than "what is rural digital exclusion worth": it asks which **observed rural outcomes** can be linked to poor connection quality, meaning reported or measured access, operational, learning, safety, or service-use effects, rather than modelled economic benefits [issue #587](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/587), [prior cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/other/rural-digital-exclusion-cost.md). I checked the vendored NZ data skills first; the `data-govt-nz` catalogue search for rural broadband/connectivity/outcomes/telehealth/education/farm returned no combined outcome dataset, so the evidence below triangulates from official health and education reports, the Commerce Commission's telecommunications monitoring, and rural-sector survey reporting [data.govt.nz catalogue search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=rural+broadband+connectivity+outcomes+telehealth+education+farm&rows=10).

The key limitation is attribution. The sources below can link poor connection quality to **constrained service access or reported operational harm**, but they do not isolate broadband quality from other rural drivers such as distance, workforce shortages, affordability, devices, digital skills, business capability, terrain, weather, and service design [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf), [NZ Telehealth Leadership Group, 2020](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/assets/Uploads/Telehealth-Full-Report-2020-V7-Pages.pdf), [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf).

### Health: poor broadband is an observed constraint on telehealth access

The Ministry of Health's 2025 Rural Health Roadshow involved visits to 13 rural locations and was designed to hear directly from rural communities while reviewing progress against the Rural Health Strategy [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf). In the roadshow themes, the Ministry recorded that telehealth expansion is limited by poor broadband in rural areas, naming Hanmer Springs and Thames as examples, and also recorded that not all patients are comfortable with or able to use digital services [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf).

That is an observed access outcome: telehealth is a channel intended to reduce distance and waiting barriers, but rural community feedback says broadband quality limits whether the channel can expand [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf). The same report says telehealth and digital services can reduce travel and provide clinical advice without waiting for local clinics, then says Health NZ is exploring community hubs in pharmacies, urgent-care clinics, or other local settings for people who lack devices or internet access at home [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf).

The independent telehealth stocktake corroborates the mechanism. In the 2019 national DHB telehealth survey, all 20 DHBs responded to the organisational survey and 18 responded about clinical services; 15 DHBs cited patient access to devices as a barrier, and 12 cited patients' access to fast internet as a barrier [NZ Telehealth Leadership Group, 2020](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/assets/Uploads/Telehealth-Full-Report-2020-V7-Pages.pdf). This supports a Medium-confidence conclusion that poor connectivity quality can constrain telehealth access, but it still does not provide a public count of missed, failed, or downgraded rural telehealth consultations [NZ Telehealth Leadership Group, 2020](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/assets/Uploads/Telehealth-Full-Report-2020-V7-Pages.pdf), [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf).

**Per-claim confidence:** Medium - the access constraint is directly observed in community and DHB reports; the size of the resulting health outcome is not publicly quantified.

### Education: connectivity provision likely protected engagement, but rural attribution is partial

The Ministry of Education's COVID-era evaluation briefing says the Government contracted internet service providers to connect around 40,000 learner households and supplied more than 48,500 fit-for-education devices to schools and kura for learners in need [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx). The evaluation focused on learner attendance and engagement, progress and achievement, teaching practice, learner and whānau wellbeing, and sustained access to connectivity and devices [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx).

The observed education outcome is positive but not rural-specific: the Ministry concluded the rollout likely supported learner engagement, achievement and progress so recipients were not more adversely affected by COVID-19 than peers with digital access, and that connectivity and devices supported learner wellbeing and had positive social, cultural and economic impacts for learners and whānau [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx). The same briefing cautioned that strong causal conclusions about the size of the impact were not warranted because the evidence was not complete or detailed enough, including because rollout data did not show how long different recipient learners had digital access [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx).

A separate Ministry briefing on keeping households connected into 2022 makes the rural connection clearer but less outcome-specific: it said learners in rural communities were less likely to have reliable and affordable internet because of limited digital infrastructure, and that taking no action risked affected households losing access to online learning opportunities during further COVID-19 learning disruptions [Ministry of Education, 2021 connected-households briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-08/06.-ER-Keeping-households-connected-into-the-2022-school-year-127209.._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=lZsMyfJRRhorLL3A0JQspk4gTzX3Vh0w).

**Per-claim confidence:** Medium - the Ministry observed positive educational and wellbeing benefits from connectivity/device provision, and separately identified rural learners as less likely to have reliable and affordable internet, but the public evaluation does not isolate rural connection quality as a standalone causal variable.

### Farms: farmers report operational, compliance, outage, and safety effects

Federated Farmers' 2025 Rural Connectivity Survey, reported through Farmers Weekly by Federated Farmers, was completed by more than 600 farmers in August and September 2025 and found mobile coverage averaged 57% of farmland [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/). The same report says more than one-third of farmers reported worsened mobile coverage over the previous year, one-quarter said internet service had declined, and more than half had experienced weather-related outages [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/).

Those are observed service-quality outcomes from rural users, and the reported harms are operational rather than merely attitudinal: Federated Farmers linked dropouts to everyday operations, health and safety, NAIT digital livestock-recording compliance, and the risk of being unable to call for help after a farm accident [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/). The same report gives resilience evidence: only 40% of farmers used a landline in 2025, down from 64% in 2022, and more than one-third of copper users had received disconnection notices, while respondents described landlines as their only reliable link to emergency services during storms, blackouts, or medical incidents [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/).

The Commerce Commission provides independent support for the underlying quality and price gap. Its 2025 telecommunications monitoring report says rural consumers increasingly compare services that differ in coverage, performance, reliability, device compatibility, upfront costs and monthly price, and its rural pricing table shows rural households paying $85.77 per month on average compared with $72.65 for urban households, an $13.12 or 18% rural premium [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Per-claim confidence:** Low-Medium for the operational, compliance, outage, and safety impacts - the farm harms are current and directly reported by farmers, but I found only one current public NZ source for those harms and it is an advocacy survey report rather than an official statistically weighted national outcome dataset. **Medium** for the narrower rural quality/price substrate, because ComCom independently confirms rural quality and price differences.

### Farm-tech and small-business adoption: connectivity is a barrier, but not the only one

AgriTechNZ's 2022 digital-adoption baseline engaged more than 1,000 farmers and growers through a random telephone survey and qualitative follow-up, and described itself as a repeatable baseline for digital adoption across primary industries [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf). It found that current digital-technology use was highest in business management, lower in areas such as water/irrigation, plant/crop, and effluent management, and that 59% of the surveyed population leaned toward digital adoption while 41% leaned toward not seeing much value in using digital technology to run their business [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf).

The connectivity link is real but conditional. AgriTechNZ says perceived local connectivity can influence digital-adoption decisions, that the focus should shift from the percentage of population connected to the percentage of productive land connected, and that connectivity remains an issue, but it also states that on-farm internet-connectivity ratings alone were not a significant differentiator of adoption segments [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf). For sheep, beef, and sheep-and-beef farmers, the report says poor internet connectivity was slightly more significant as an adoption barrier, and that conservative segments were more likely to be restricted by poor internet service and cellphone coverage [AgriTechNZ, 2022](https://trustalliance.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/AgriTechNZ-Digital-Adoption-in-Primary-Industries-Report-2022.pdf).

For small businesses outside farming, the public evidence remains indirect. MBIE's 2024 business digital monitor says some businesses are falling behind on digital opportunities and identifies barriers such as lack of time, knowledge, affordable tools, and cybersecurity concerns, but the public page does not give a rural connection-quality-linked outcome count [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses). MBIE's Digital Boost evaluation reports observed participant outcomes such as increased revenue, resilience, and reduced time on existing tasks, but those are programme outcomes rather than rural-connectivity effects [MBIE, 2024 Digital Boost evaluation](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-boost-evaluation-research).

**Per-claim confidence:** Medium for farms, Low for rural SMEs - farm-tech adoption has a surveyed rural-sector evidence base, while non-farm SME outcome evidence is digital-capability evidence rather than connection-quality evidence.

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Health NZ dataset that reports telehealth attempts, failures, phone-only substitution, no-shows, or completed consults by rurality and connection quality would change the health conclusion from "access constraint observed" to a quantified outcome estimate [Ministry of Health, 2025](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-12/rural-health-roadshow-insights-report.pdf), [NZ Telehealth Leadership Group, 2020](https://www.telehealth.org.nz/assets/Uploads/Telehealth-Full-Report-2020-V7-Pages.pdf).
- A public Ministry of Education analysis linking learner outcomes to rural household connection quality, not just provision of devices/connectivity during COVID-19, would change the education conclusion [Ministry of Education, 2021 evaluation briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-03/12.-BN-1278617-Evaluation-Report-Provision-of-Internet-Connectivity-and-Dev..._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=C0OS1Kl_.9kVLTtO67kveAKgOmS2M4jx), [Ministry of Education, 2021 connected-households briefing](https://web-assets.education.govt.nz/s3fs-public/2025-08/06.-ER-Keeping-households-connected-into-the-2022-school-year-127209.._Redacted.pdf?VersionId=lZsMyfJRRhorLL3A0JQspk4gTzX3Vh0w).
- A second independent public NZ source confirming the same operational, compliance, outage, or safety impacts would raise the farm-harm confidence; provider, NAIT, emergency-service, ACC, or insurer data linking rural mobile/fixed outages to compliance failures, delayed emergency calls, injuries, or lost production would change the farm conclusion from single-sourced reported harm to observed administrative outcome evidence [Farmers Weekly / Federated Farmers, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/), [Commerce Commission, 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- A linked firm-level dataset combining rural broadband quality, digital-tool adoption, revenue, export activity, productivity, or survival would change the SME conclusion, because current MBIE evidence is about digital capability and programme participants rather than rural connection quality [MBIE, 2024](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-capability-of-new-zealand-businesses), [MBIE, 2024 Digital Boost evaluation](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/support-for-business/better-for-business/research-and-reports/digital-boost-evaluation-research).
- What I could not verify: national counts of rural telehealth failures caused by broadband quality; rural learning-outcome effects caused by home connection quality; farm accidents, compliance failures, or production losses caused by outages; and rural small-business revenue or closure outcomes caused by poor connection quality. These may exist in provider, agency, or research datasets, but I did not find them in public sources or the data.govt.nz catalogue search used for this finding [data.govt.nz catalogue search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=rural+broadband+connectivity+outcomes+telehealth+education+farm&rows=10).

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues opened; this issue was broad but answerable as a boundary-setting finding.
- Could Health NZ publish an anonymised rural telehealth quality dataset that distinguishes failed video, phone-only substitution, completed video, and in-person fallback?
- Could the Ministry of Education safely link rural household connectivity support to attendance, engagement, NCEA participation, or learning-progress indicators without exposing learner identity?
- Could NAIT, emergency-service, or rural insurer data quantify the operational and safety consequences of rural mobile coverage gaps?
