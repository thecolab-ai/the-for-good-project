# Youth mental-health access has widened in primary care, but specialist bottlenecks and weak outcomes data still limit proof of youth impact

## Executive answer

- Youth Access and Choice is a primary, mild-to-moderate support stream for 12-24-year-olds; by 2023/24 Youth services were available in all 20 districts, there were 24 Youth services, and Access and Choice overall saw more than 207,000 people, still below the 325,000-a-year aim. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)
- Specialist youth care is a different tier: ICAMHS serves 0-17-year-olds with moderate-to-severe concerns, while adult specialist services usually cover 18+; in 2024/25 only 69.9% of 0-18-year-olds were seen by specialist services within three weeks, compared with 80.9% overall, and 0-18-year-olds had the highest declined-referral rate at 14.9%. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)
- Expanded primary access has not yet translated into publicly proven youth outcomes: Te Hiringa Mahara says national Access and Choice outcomes data is not consistent, and Health NZ told the Auditor-General in 2026 that Youth Access and Choice outcome tools are used inconsistently and national-level collection is unreliable. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/System-performance-monitoring-2025/MHWC-System-performance-report-June-2026.pdf); [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)
- Crisis and school/community supports matter because they catch youth outside specialist services, but public evidence shows patchy coverage and weak outcomes measurement: crisis responses are fragmented and regionally variable, school-based health services cover about 35% of Year 9-13 students, and Youth One Stop Shops are not available in all regions. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf); [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - High for national Access and Choice coverage, specialist wait-time and declined-referral statistics from official monitoring; Medium for equity patterns; Low-to-Medium for outcomes because the main conclusion is that public outcome data is incomplete or unreliable, not that outcomes are poor.

## Evidence

### Scope: primary support and specialist care are not interchangeable

Access and Choice was rolled out from 2019/20 over five years for mild-to-moderate mental health, addiction and wellbeing needs in general practice, Kaupapa Maori, Pacific, youth and community settings. [Te Hiringa Mahara, Access and Choice page, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/our-work/mental-health-and-addiction-system/access-and-chp/)

Youth Access and Choice aims to offer immediate, barrier-free and youth-tailored support to 12-24-year-olds with mild-to-moderate mental-health concerns; the Auditor-General reported that services include schools, accessible community locations, mobile services, online support and face-to-face support. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

ICAMHS is the specialist tier for 0-17-year-olds with moderate-to-severe mental-health concerns, while specialist services for young people aged 18 and over are usually adult specialist services; that age boundary means 18-24-year-olds can lose youth-specific models just as many youth-distress measures remain high. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

The Government's current mental-health targets distinguish the same tiers: 80% of people accessing specialist mental health and addiction services should be seen within three weeks, while 80% of people accessing primary mental health and addiction services through Access and Choice should be seen within one week. [Health NZ, mental health and addiction targets, 2026](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/mental-health-and-addiction-targets); [Ministry of Health, mental health and addiction targets, 2026](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/mental-health-and-addiction-targets)

### Coverage: primary youth access expanded nationally, but coverage depth varies

Te Hiringa Mahara's five-year Access and Choice monitoring report says Access and Choice overall saw more than 207,000 people in 2023/24, about 64% of the 325,000 annual-reach aim, and delivered 676,261 sessions. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

The same report says IPMHA services were available to 68.0% of people enrolled with a general practice at 30 June 2024, near the 70% aim, but district coverage varied widely, with six districts at 91-100% coverage, ten at 66-86%, and three at 45-56%; Tairawhiti had IPMHA services but incomplete coverage data. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

Youth-specific Access and Choice coverage is broader in geographic presence than IPMHA enrolment coverage: by 2023/24, Youth services were implemented in all 20 districts and totalled 24 services, while Kaupapa Maori services totalled 32 services across all 20 districts and Pacific services totalled 13 services in nine targeted districts. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

Health NZ's 2026 response to the Auditor-General says IPMHA and Youth Access and Choice have been in place in every district since 2024, IPMHA is available to more than 3.55 million enrolled general-practice patients, and National Health Index-level encounter reporting for Youth Access and Choice began in January 2025, with 58,640 sessions for 7,803 distinct young people from January to October 2025. [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)

For integrated youth primary care outside Access and Choice, the public coverage picture is weaker: the Auditor-General found Youth One Stop Shops were not available in all regions and had no consistent national funding, objectives, outcomes data or performance measures, and Health NZ's 2026 response says coverage information for Integrated Youth Services remains hard to obtain because regional funding agreements vary. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)

School-based health coverage is also partial: the Auditor-General reported Health NZ-funded nurse-led school-based health services in about 300 decile 1-5 secondary schools, covering about 35% of Year 9-13 students, while about 65% were in decile 6-10 schools where that Health NZ-funded service was unavailable. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

### Wait times and declined referrals: specialist youth access remains the bottleneck

Te Hiringa Mahara's 2026 access summary says 0-24-year-olds accounted for 36.1% of people using specialist services in 2024/25, including 46,759 aged 0-18 and 19,363 aged 19-24. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

The 19-24 age group was the only age group whose specialist-service access did not increase in 2024/25, and it had 4,919 fewer users than in 2020/21, falling from 24,282 to 19,363. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

Children and rangatahi aged 0-18 had the longest specialist waits in 2024/25: 69.9% were seen within three weeks, below the 80% target and below the 80.9% overall result, and 40% waited longer than eight weeks for a third appointment compared with 23% overall. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

The Auditor-General's earlier specialist-service analysis points in the same direction: ICAMHS did not meet the non-urgent wait-time measures in 2023, when 63% of people aged 19 and under were seen within three weeks and 85% within eight weeks; in 2022/23, a 12-19-year-old waited on average over 60 days from referral to starting treatment, measured to the third appointment. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

Declined referrals are a clear access barrier for children: in 2024/25, 0-18-year-olds had the highest declined specialist-referral rate at 14.9%, and GP referrals for 0-18-year-olds were declined at 32.3% compared with 17.7% for all ages. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

The Auditor-General reported that ICAMHS declined referrals increased from 9% in 2012/13 to 14% in 2022/23, and that primary services, including Youth One Stop Shops and some Youth Access and Choice services, were seeing young people who were on specialist waiting lists or had had a specialist referral declined. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

### Crisis responses: youth need is visible, but coverage is not nationally cohesive

Te Hiringa Mahara's 2025 crisis-response monitoring report covers January 2020 to December 2024 and says crisis responses include crisis teams, crisis cafes, crisis response call centres, crisis respite, Police, ambulance and emergency departments, with wide variation across districts. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf)

The crisis report says over 30% of people with a crisis activity were under 25, young people were accessing emergency departments at a higher rate than other age groups, and young people made up double the rate of ambulance mental-health incidents, although that ambulance rate had reduced over five years. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf)

The same report says crisis phone-line waits rose from an average of about two minutes in 2020 to over five minutes by the end of 2024, and the Access Summary separately says national telehealth service use fell from 88,427 people in 2020/21 to 51,816 in 2024/25 while wait times rose, including over 20 minutes for 1737 Need to Talk in 2024/25. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)

Te Hiringa Mahara concludes that national crisis responses remain fragmented, regionally variable and lacking robust evaluation, and recommends a nationally cohesive network by 30 June 2027 that includes 24/7 phone-based support in every district, crisis teams, crisis cafes, crisis respite, acute alternatives, youth-specific, peer-led and Kaupapa Maori options. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf)

### Outcomes and equity: contacts improved, outcome proof has not caught up

Access and Choice is reaching priority groups better than simple population share would predict: in 2023/24, Maori were 26.8% of Access and Choice users, Pacific peoples were 10.9%, and 12-24-year-olds were 20.2%, compared with national population shares of 17.5% Maori, 8.9% Pacific and 16.5% aged 12-24 cited in the report. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

Youth Access and Choice also reached Maori and Pacific youth within the youth stream: the Auditor-General reported rangatahi Maori were 33% and Pacific young people were 11% of Youth Access and Choice clients for 1 November 2022 to 31 October 2023, and Te Hiringa Mahara's 2023/24 table shows Maori were 3,802 of 10,932 Youth service users, or about 35%. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Access-and-Choice-programme/2025/Access-and-Choice-Report-April-2025.pdf)

That equity signal is about reach, not outcomes. Te Hiringa Mahara's June 2026 system-performance report says it does not have consistent data to show whether increased Access and Choice access is translating into improved outcomes, even though many services capture outcome data. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/System-performance-monitoring-2025/MHWC-System-performance-report-June-2026.pdf)

Health NZ's 2026 response confirms the data-quality gap: within Access and Choice, Youth providers use tools including the Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire, Hua Oranga, Outcome Rating Scale, Session Rating Scale, Child Outcome Rating Scale and The Outcomes Measurement Model, but these are used inconsistently and national-level data collection is unreliable. [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)

Specialist outcomes data is also incomplete: the Auditor-General reported that only about 30% of 4-17-year-olds had HoNOSCA outcomes data collected on both admission to and discharge from community specialist mental-health services in the first quarter of 2023, compared with an 80% target. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm)

Health NZ's 2026 response says HoNOSCA remains mandated for ICAMHS and that 11 districts were near or above the 80% completion target for inpatient services in April-June 2025, but Health NZ also acknowledges more work is needed in community services, which are especially relevant to ICAMHS service users. [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Health NZ dataset linking youth Access and Choice, IPMHA, Kaupapa Maori, Pacific, Youth One Stop Shop, school-based, crisis and ICAMHS activity to consistent youth outcome measures would change the outcomes conclusion; the current public record mostly supports reach, waits and declined-referral findings. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/System-performance-monitoring-2025/MHWC-System-performance-report-June-2026.pdf); [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)
- A district-by-district public coverage export for Youth Access and Choice, Integrated Youth Services, Youth One Stop Shops and school-based health services would change the regional coverage conclusion; Health NZ currently says IYS coverage is hard to obtain because regional funding agreements vary. [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)
- A 2025/26 specialist youth access release could change the wait-time and declined-referral baseline; the latest youth-specific public summary I found was Te Hiringa Mahara's February 2026 summary for 2024/25. [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2026](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/HAA-monitoring/MHWC-Access-data-summary-Feb-2026.pdf)
- A human with rangatahi, whanau, Maori, Pacific, disabled, rainbow, rural or clinical experience should test whether "coverage in every district" matches practical access, because public coverage statistics do not capture travel, culture, disability access, trust, privacy in small communities, or suitability of adult-service pathways for 18-24-year-olds. [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Te Hiringa Mahara, 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/assets/Reports/Crisis-responses/MHWC-Crisis-responses-report-November-2025.pdf)

- Which Youth Access and Choice, Youth One Stop Shop, school-based and ICAMHS shared-care models reduce declined referrals or long waits without shifting high-acuity young people into brief primary care? [Auditor-General, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/youth-mental-health/part3.htm); [Health NZ response to Auditor-General, 2026](https://ao.parliament.nz/2026/youth-mental-health-follow-up/docs/health-nz-response.pdf/@@download/file/health-nz-response.pdf)
