# High-cost credit, repeat borrowing, BNPL defaults, and arrears fees appear to hit vulnerable NZ borrowers hardest

## Executive answer

- The clearest harm to prioritise is **high-cost consumer credit and repeat borrowing**, because NZ law singles out loans at 50%+ annual interest or equivalent default-rate exposure, caps total high-cost-loan interest and fees, and MBIE says those rules were introduced to reduce problem debt from excessive cost and repeat borrowing by vulnerable consumers [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions), [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [Commerce Commission, credit-law changes](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/changes-to-credit-laws/).
- **Default, late-payment, and debt-collection fees** matter most when a borrower is already in arrears, because they increase the amount owed at the point of least cash-flow resilience; this is strongly supported for high-cost credit and ordinary consumer-credit enforcement, and moderately supported for BNPL where MBIE explicitly says causation is hard to prove [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/), [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/), [MBIE, BNPL default-fee RIS addendum](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29100-regulatory-impact-statement-addendum-buy-now-pay-later-regulations).
- **BNPL product structure** is a priority risk even when no interest is charged: MBIE says BNPL can cause or worsen hardship for some consumers, missed instalments can trigger late fees, and the evidence base is still limited because causal attribution is difficult [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).
- **Eligibility and affordability criteria cut both ways**: weak screening can let unaffordable debt accumulate, but strict affordability or low-cost-loan criteria can exclude the same people from safer credit and push them toward BNPL, high-cost lenders, friends/family debt, or going without essentials [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html).
- Vulnerability is not theoretical: Stats NZ estimated that 9.1% of people were in material hardship in the year ended June 2025, and its 2025 child-poverty data estimated child material-hardship rates of 14.3% overall, 25.1% for Māori children, 31.0% for Pacific children, and 26.9% for disabled children [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/), [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of harm is well supported by official law/policy material, regulator enforcement, and financial-mentor evidence, but public NZ data does not cleanly quantify the marginal impact of each fee type by borrower vulnerability group.

## Evidence

### Vulnerability baseline: many households have no buffer

Stats NZ reported that 9.1% of people in New Zealand were living in households experiencing material hardship in the 12 months ended June 2025, which means a credit-cost transparency tool should assume a material share of borrowers have little room for repayment shocks [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). Stats NZ's 2025 child-poverty CSV estimated 169,300 children in material hardship, or 14.3% of all children; the same dataset estimated materially higher hardship rates for Māori children at 25.1%, Pacific children at 31.0%, disabled children at 26.9%, and children in disabled households at 27.5% [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

These hardship figures do not prove that credit fees cause poverty, but they are directly relevant to impact: a missed-payment fee, default interest charge, collection cost, or unaffordable instalment has a larger practical effect when the household already lacks essentials or cannot absorb an unexpected expense [Stats NZ, child poverty release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).

### 1. High-cost credit and repeat borrowing have the strongest evidence of direct harm

MBIE says the CCCFA defines a high-cost consumer credit contract as one with an average annual interest rate of 50% or more, or where the combined annual interest rate and default rate are likely to be 50% or more [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions). MBIE's 2024 consumer-credit RIS says the 2020 high-cost-credit rules were introduced to reduce problem debt by addressing excessive cost and repeat borrowing by vulnerable consumers [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

The Commerce Commission summarises the high-cost-credit rules as including a total cost cap limiting fees and interest to 100% of the first advance, a presumption that default fees above $30 are unreasonable unless proved otherwise, a 0.8% per-day cap on interest and fees added to a high-cost loan, and restrictions on entering into high-cost loans when the borrower already has or recently had other high-cost loans [Commerce Commission, credit-law changes](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/changes-to-credit-laws/). These rules are strong evidence that the policy system treats high-cost pricing, default fees, and repeat borrowing as a combined harm rather than as isolated charges [Commerce Commission, credit-law changes](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/changes-to-credit-laws/).

Regulator enforcement supports the same prioritisation. In 2024, the Commerce Commission said Eagle M.A.N Group had been ordered to pay a $200,000 penalty for high-cost loans breaching multiple CCCFA requirements; the Commission said Eagle MAN charged up to 182.5% annual interest on top of credit and default fees, issued loans whose interest and fees exceeded the first advance, and gave repeat high-cost loans to borrowers [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/). This is a specific enforcement case, not a market-wide prevalence estimate, but it demonstrates the harm pattern the rules are designed to prevent [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/).

**Confidence: High** that high-cost credit and repeat borrowing should be the first harm category in the stream. Public evidence supports the mechanism, the statutory response, and current enforcement examples, but not a complete count of affected borrowers.

### 2. Default, late-payment, and debt-collection fees compound arrears

Consumer Protection summarises responsible-lending duties as requiring lenders to limit interest and fees for high-cost loans, charge reasonable credit and default fees, assess affordability and suitability, and treat borrowers fairly when they cannot pay [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). The same guidance lists unfair or oppressive examples that include charging high fees for profit, giving too little time to pay missed payments, and letting fees and interest stack up by taking too long to act on overdue payments [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

Commerce Commission enforcement shows that unreasonable credit/default fees can affect large borrower groups even outside the high-cost-loan niche. Real Finance agreed in 2022 to compensate borrowers $1.36 million after acknowledging unreasonable fees on loans [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/). Co-operative Bank admitted CCCFA breaches in 2025 and remediated 48,249 customers $7.225 million for charging higher fees than it should have [Commerce Commission, Co-operative Bank](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/co-operative-bank-admits-charging-unreasonable-fees/).

FinCap's 2025 Voices report says missed repayments are concerning because further debt-collection fees can be added, prolonging and increasing debt harm for whānau supported by financial mentors [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994). FinCap's client base is not representative of all borrowers, but it is directly relevant to vulnerable borrowers because financial mentors work with people already facing financial stress [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994).

**Confidence: Medium-High** that arrears-linked fees should be a high-priority feature in any transparency or education artifact. The mechanism is direct and enforcement is strong, but public evidence does not consistently separate default fees, default interest, debt-collection fees, and repossession costs by borrower vulnerability.

### 3. BNPL is lower-cost when paid on time, but missed instalments and multiple accounts are a priority hardship risk

MBIE describes BNPL as short-term, unsecured, interest-free credit where consumers pay merchants through instalments, and says a BNPL lender may charge a fee if a consumer is late or misses an instalment [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf). MBIE's BNPL RIS states the policy problem plainly as BNPL causing or worsening financial hardship for some consumers, while also saying no substantial quantitative data clearly demonstrates causation because the sector is newer and hardship can arise from other debts, existing poverty, or overspending [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).

MBIE identifies several BNPL harm pathways: hardship may worsen when a consumer defaults and pays late fees, when BNPL payments cause default on another obligation, when consumers use BNPL for essentials because they are already struggling, or when consumers make BNPL payments but go without essentials or miss other debt repayments [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf). MBIE's 2024 addendum says the Government applied CCCFA fee provisions to BNPL because financial mentors raised concerns about accumulated BNPL default fees, and says default fees can worsen hardship by increasing the amount owed even though attributing hardship to those fees is difficult [MBIE, BNPL default-fee RIS addendum](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29100-regulatory-impact-statement-addendum-buy-now-pay-later-regulations).

Financial-mentor evidence is consistent with MBIE's concern. FinCap told MBIE in 2024 that unaffordable BNPL loans were among the most common consumer-credit issues financial mentors had raised since December 2021, and that mentors saw debt spirals where people took multiple loans and kept paying them while going without essentials to keep facilities open [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf). Christians Against Poverty submitted to MBIE in 2021 that the share of its clients with BNPL debt rose from 2% in 2019 to 17% in 2021, and that those clients had two BNPL debts on average [Christians Against Poverty, BNPL submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/18981-christians-against-poverty-buy-now-pay-later-submission-pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that BNPL missed-payment fees, multiple concurrent BNPL debts, and instalment timing should be prioritised. The evidence is strong enough to flag risk for vulnerable borrowers, but MBIE is explicit that public quantitative evidence is limited and causation is hard to isolate.

### 4. Add-on products and suitability checks matter, but the public evidence found here is thinner

Consumer Protection says lender suitability checks can include whether a borrower needs extra products such as insurance, and it gives credit-related insurance examples such as payment protection or mechanical-breakdown insurance added to car finance [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). This supports including add-ons in a total-cost-of-credit artifact, because an add-on can change the amount financed and the repayment burden even when the headline interest rate is unchanged [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

I did not find enough current public NZ evidence in this pass to rank add-on insurance above high-cost credit, BNPL default structures, or arrears/default fees for vulnerable borrowers. Add-ons should still be captured as a disclosure and suitability issue, especially for vehicle finance and consumer leases, but this finding treats them as **Medium/Low confidence** on scale and impact until a product-specific evidence pass is done [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

### 5. Eligibility criteria can protect borrowers or exclude them from safer credit

Affordability criteria have a protective purpose: Consumer Protection says lenders must be satisfied borrowers are likely to make required repayments without substantial hardship, and must make reasonable inquiries such as income and expenses checks [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). That matters for vulnerable borrowers because unaffordable lending can turn a short-term cash-flow problem into repeat borrowing, arrears, and collection costs [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

The exclusion side is also real. MSD says Good Shepherd's Good Loans are no-interest, fee-free loans for people on low incomes to pay for essential expenses and avoid or manage high-cost debt, but the same MSD page says those loans cannot be used for overdue bills, rent arrears, day-to-day living expenses, cash, or urgent needs because applications take time [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html). Good Shepherd's current eligibility page similarly says Good Loans cannot be used for day-to-day living expenses or urgent needs, and says the service is temporarily unable to take new debt-consolidation enquiries because of unprecedented demand [Good Shepherd NZ, Good Loans](https://goodshepherd.org.nz/get-support/our-services/loans/).

FinCap's 2024 submission makes the tradeoff concrete: it says people supported by financial mentors who can no longer access loans from CCCFA-bound responsible lenders can end up in substantial hardship to make repayments on further BNPL, and it reports whānau borrowing from BNPL lenders, Work and Income, friends, family, and other CCCFA lenders to relieve financial pressure [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [FinCap, affordability-code submission](https://fincap.org.nz/images/240610-SUB-FinCap-RLC-exposure-draft.pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that eligibility design should be treated as a harm vector, not just an access rule. It is harder to rank than direct fees because the same criterion can prevent unaffordable lending in one case and exclude someone from lower-cost credit in another.

## What would change this conclusion

- A current, representative NZ dataset linking borrower income, ethnicity, disability, product type, arrears status, default fees, collection fees, and repeat borrowing would materially change the ranking and could move the overall confidence to High.
- The conclusion on BNPL would change if post-September-2024 CCCFA/credit-reporting data showed that BNPL arrears, repeat use, or default-fee accumulation had materially fallen, or if it showed stronger causal harm than MBIE could establish in 2023-2024.
- The conclusion on add-on insurance would change if a product-specific review of vehicle finance, mobile trading, consumer leases, and personal loans found large public evidence of unsuitable add-ons or add-on-driven arrears.
- I could not verify a current public, regulator-grade count of NZ borrowers affected by each fee type; the evidence is strongest on mechanisms, rules, and selected enforcement/remediation cases.
- I did not contact borrowers, lenders, financial mentors, MoneyTalks, FinCap, Good Shepherd, Commerce Commission, FMA, or MBIE; lived-experience and frontline validation should happen before this stream designs a borrower-facing tool.
