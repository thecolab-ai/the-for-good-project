# For vulnerable NZ borrowers, live credit-cost harm now sits in BNPL, arrears/default fees, and access-exclusion tradeoffs — 50%+ high-cost lending is largely a solved historical harm

## Executive answer

This answers issue #71 as a **ranked scan across the fee, product-structure, and eligibility categories named in the issue**, not as five separate product findings. It is a deliberately broad interpretation of the issue's one question, meant to decide what a transparency artifact must cover; product-specific follow-up work should handle deeper questions about BNPL, car finance, add-ons, and debt collection [issue #71](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/71). This version restores figures and primary-source conclusions that an earlier pass could not fetch (the MBIE Regulatory Impact Statements), so several ratings are now anchored in the primary policy documents rather than secondary summaries.

- **High-cost 50%+ consumer credit is now largely a solved historical harm, not the largest live risk.** MBIE's 2024 consumer-credit RIS concludes the high-cost provisions "have led to the elimination of high-cost lending (at interest rates of 50% or more)": 12 high-cost providers left the market and the 9 that remained restructured products below 50%, and MBIE recommends **no change** to the threshold because it found "no evidence" that the same harms are caused by lower-rate loans [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, Aug 2024, paras D, 37, 55](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). Keep the pattern in scope for history and enforcement, but do not rank it as the top current harm.
- **The flip side of that elimination is an access/exclusion harm:** MBIE estimates the removal of high-cost loans left "an estimated 150,000 potential borrowers" without access to that source of credit — some of whom shift to BNPL, other lenders, government hardship assistance, or unmet need [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 38](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf).
- **BNPL product structure is a top live risk** even with no interest: MBIE's problem definition is bluntly that "buy now pay later is causing or worsening financial hardship for some consumers", ~10.4% of active BNPL accounts were in arrears (June 2023 Centrix) vs 4.5% of credit cards, and financial mentors document whānau juggling repayments across many BNPL loans that exceed weekly income [MBIE, BNPL RIS, Jul 2023](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf), [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf).
- **Default, dishonour, late-payment, and debt-collection fees matter most once a borrower is already in arrears**, because they add cost at the point of least cash-flow resilience; strongly supported for default and debt-collection fees, with regulator remediation running into the millions of dollars and tens of thousands of customers [Consumer Protection, unfair fees](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/unfair-fees), [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/).
- **Eligibility/affordability criteria cut both ways**: weak screening lets unaffordable debt accumulate, but strict criteria exclude the same people from safer credit and push them toward BNPL, other lenders, friends/family debt, or going without essentials [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html).
- Vulnerability is not theoretical: Stats NZ estimated 9.1% of people were in material hardship in the year ended June 2025, and its 2025 child-poverty data estimated child material-hardship rates of 14.3% overall, 25.1% for Māori children, 31.0% for Pacific children, and 26.9% for disabled children [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/), [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the direction of harm and its ranking are now well supported by the primary MBIE RIS documents, regulator enforcement, and financial-mentor evidence, but public NZ data still does not cleanly quantify the marginal impact of each fee type by borrower vulnerability group, and BNPL causation remains hard to isolate.

## Evidence

### Vulnerability baseline: many households have no buffer

Stats NZ reported that 9.1% of people in New Zealand were living in households experiencing material hardship in the 12 months ended June 2025, which means a credit-cost transparency tool should assume a material share of borrowers have little room for repayment shocks [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). Stats NZ's 2025 child-poverty CSV estimated 169,300 children in material hardship, or 14.3% of all children; the same dataset estimated materially higher hardship rates for Māori children at 25.1%, Pacific children at 31.0%, disabled children at 26.9%, and children in disabled households at 27.5% [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

These hardship figures do not prove that credit fees cause poverty, but they are directly relevant to impact: a missed-payment fee, default interest charge, collection cost, or unaffordable instalment has a larger practical effect when the household already lacks essentials or cannot absorb an unexpected expense [Stats NZ, child poverty release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). MBIE frames the same vulnerability structurally: consumer credit markets "are characterised by information asymmetries between lenders and borrowers", and consumers "can be made vulnerable to poor decision-making by their circumstances (such as strong pressure to make a purchase) or shortcomings (such as poor literacy)" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, paras 7-8](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

### 1. High-cost 50%+ credit: historically severe, statutorily targeted — and, per MBIE, now effectively eliminated

MBIE says the CCCFA defines a high-cost consumer credit contract as one with an annual interest rate of 50% or more, or where the rate combined with default interest is likely to reach 50% or more; the high-cost provisions cap the total cost of borrowing at the loan advance (a borrower "can never be required to repay more than twice what they borrowed"), cap the rate of charge at 0.8% per day, ban compound interest, create a rebuttable presumption that default fees over $30 are unreasonable, and prohibit high-cost loans to repeat borrowers (an unpaid balance on any high-cost contract in the prior 15 days, or two or more such contracts in the past 90 days) [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 35](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [NZ Legislation, CCCFA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/). Their stated purpose "was to reduce problem debt by addressing the excessive cost of credit for these loans and repeat borrowing by vulnerable consumers" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 34](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

Now that the RIS is readable in full, its conclusion is decisive and previously understated: MBIE's mandatory statutory review (s45L) "found that the high-cost credit provisions have led to the elimination of high-cost credit. Twelve high-cost credit providers left the market. Although nine remain, they have restructured their products to fall below the 50% interest rate threshold" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 37](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). MBIE's chosen option for high-cost credit was "No change to the high-cost credit provisions … we do not consider that the risks of harm are such as to require lowering the interest rate threshold", because "we have no evidence that the same kind of issues are caused by loans with a lower interest rate" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, Executive Summary (Issue D) and para 55](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). MBIE did, however, identify "13 lenders offering loans with interest rates of between 47.5% and 49.95%", i.e. bunched just under the threshold, leaving open a downward-pressure question rather than a fresh systemic-harm finding [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 56](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

The evidential basis for MBIE's "no systemic harm below 50%" call also matters for how much weight to give it. Because national lending data does not capture interest rates, MBIE relied on **financial-mentor client datasets** — data that "only relate to loans that have caused consumers to seek assistance from those mentors, so is not representative of all loans." MBIE explicitly says this limitation "has contributed to our assessment that there is a lack of evidence of systemic harm from lending at certain interest rates" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, "Quantitative information"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). So "no evidence of harm" is partly an absence-of-data statement, not a positive finding of safety.

Regulator enforcement still supports keeping the high-cost pattern in scope historically. In 2024, the Commerce Commission secured a $200,000 penalty against Eagle M.A.N Group, which charged up to 182.5% annual interest on top of credit and default fees, issued loans whose interest and fees exceeded the first advance, and gave repeat high-cost loans to borrowers [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/), [Buddle Findlay via Lexology, Eagle MAN](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=ba2074fb-1b73-4f22-859a-215ea2ca90bd). That is a specific case, not a market-wide prevalence estimate.

**Confidence: Medium-High** that 50%+ high-cost lending is now a largely eliminated, historical harm rather than the top current risk for vulnerable borrowers. The primary RIS states the elimination, the 12-left/9-restructured split, and MBIE's decision not to lower the threshold. It stays short of High only because MBIE's "no harm below 50%" conclusion rests on non-representative financial-mentor data, and 13 lenders now cluster at 47.5–49.95%.

### 2. The elimination created an access/exclusion harm that is now a live design question

The clearest new number recovered from the RIS is the scale of exclusion. MBIE states: "The elimination of high-cost loans has meant an estimated 150,000 potential borrowers no longer have access to this source of credit", and that a former high-cost lender said the combined effect of the provisions "rendered this type of credit economically unviable", with banks now reluctant to serve lenders categorised as high-cost [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, paras 38-39](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). The coalition's own framing captures the tension: a commitment to "Rewrite the CCCFA to protect vulnerable consumers without unnecessarily limiting access to credit" [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 15](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

MBIE also documents the parallel exclusion from *responsible* lending. Its 2022 investigation into the December 2021 changes found "more borrowers across all lending types who should pass the affordability test are subject to declines or reductions in credit amount", and it revoked the prescriptive affordability requirements from CCCFA regulations effective 31 July 2024 in response [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, paras 12, 17(d)](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). Financial mentors report the downstream effect: whānau who "can no longer access loans from CCCFA bound responsible lenders end up in substantial hardship to make repayments on further Buy Now Pay Later" [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that credit-access exclusion (from both high-cost withdrawal and conservative affordability responses) is itself a harm vector for vulnerable borrowers and belongs in any transparency/education artifact. The 150,000 figure is MBIE's own estimate; what those borrowers substitute into, and whether they are better or worse off, is not cleanly quantified.

### 3. BNPL is low-cost when paid on time, but missed instalments and loan-stacking are a priority live harm

MBIE's BNPL RIS opens with an unambiguous problem definition: "Buy now pay later is causing or worsening financial hardship for some consumers" [MBIE, BNPL RIS, Problem Definition](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf). It also cautions that "no substantial quantitative data exists due to the relative newness of the sector and difficulty in assigning causation" — BNPL use "may also be a symptom of hardship" as much as a cause, and hardship can arise from other debts, existing poverty, or overspending [MBIE, BNPL RIS, paras 23-25](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf). The harm pathways it names are specific: defaulting and paying late fees, BNPL payments causing default on another obligation, using BNPL for essentials because already struggling, or making BNPL payments while going without essentials [MBIE, BNPL RIS, para 23](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).

The recovered document supplies the quantitative texture the earlier pass could not cite:

- **Arrears:** the June 2023 Centrix monthly report showed "10.4 per cent of active BNPL accounts in arrears" (peaking at 10.5% in April 2023), compared with 4.5% of credit cards and 10% of personal loans — so BNPL arrears track personal loans, not credit cards [MBIE, BNPL RIS, para 27](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).
- **Consumer survey:** a 2021 MBIE survey of 1,781 respondents found "thirteen per cent of the current BNPL users surveyed said that BNPL had caused them to delay payment of bills or cut back on other essential expenses, or that they have missed three or more repayments", and "forty-four per cent of respondents who no longer used BNPL said it was because they found it hard to stop purchasing more than they could afford." MBIE flags the social-media sampling frame as non-representative [MBIE, BNPL RIS, para 28](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).
- **Instalment mechanics:** a missed instalment can trigger a fee "from flat fees (e.g. $10 for a missed instalment) to a percentage of the value of the transaction", and if paid by credit card the consumer risks additional credit-card fees or interest on top [MBIE, BNPL RIS, paras 3-4](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).

Financial-mentor evidence gives concrete loan-stacking cases. FinCap told MBIE that "the most common issues causing financial hardship for borrowers reported by financial mentors to FinCap since December 2021 are those relating to unaffordable Buy Now Pay Later loans", with debt spirals where people "take out multiple loans and continue to pay them, while going without essentials, to keep the facilities open." Its worked budgets include a whānau facing a "$191.70 per week" deficit with repayments across seven BNPL loans, and a person in work with "$139.12 per week from 10 separate loans from a single Buy Now Pay Later lender" [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf). Christians Against Poverty submitted that the share of its clients with BNPL debt rose from 2% in 2019 to 17% in 2021, with those clients holding two BNPL debts on average — one debt-help organisation's own client data, supporting evidence for vulnerable-client risk rather than market-wide prevalence [Christians Against Poverty, BNPL submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/18981-christians-against-poverty-buy-now-pay-later-submission-pdf).

On the fee-structure question specifically, MBIE's 2024 addendum records that "financial mentors also raised concerns about the accumulation of default fees (as they can stack up)", noting BNPL providers already voluntarily cap default fees at different levels — for example Klarna "range from $0 to a maximum of $24" and Zip "capped at $40 per order" — and MBIE's preferred option was a conditional exemption from CCCFA fee provisions rather than full application [MBIE, BNPL default-fee RIS addendum, Aug 2024, paras 27-28 and Executive Summary](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29100-regulatory-impact-statement-addendum-buy-now-pay-later-regulations).

**Confidence: Medium-High** that BNPL missed-payment/default fees, multiple concurrent BNPL debts, and instalment timing should be prioritised as a live harm for vulnerable borrowers. The MBIE problem definition, the Centrix arrears figures, the survey, and detailed mentor budgets all point the same way. It stays short of High because MBIE is explicit that causation is hard to isolate and no representative post-2-September-2024 outcome data is yet public.

### 4. Add-on products and suitability checks matter, but the public evidence found here is thinner

Consumer Protection says lender suitability checks can include whether a borrower needs extra products such as insurance, and gives credit-related insurance examples like payment protection or mechanical-breakdown insurance added to car finance [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). This supports including add-ons in a total-cost-of-credit artifact, because an add-on can change the amount financed and the repayment burden even when the headline interest rate is unchanged [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). MBIE's RIS confirms vehicle finance, consumer leases, and mobile-trader credit sales are all in-scope CCCFA credit products where such add-ons arise [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 1](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

I did not find enough current public NZ evidence in this pass to rank add-on insurance above high-cost credit, BNPL default structures, or arrears/default fees for vulnerable borrowers. Add-ons should still be captured as a disclosure and suitability issue, especially for vehicle finance and consumer leases, but this finding treats them as **Low confidence** on scale and impact until a product-specific evidence pass is done [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

### 5. Default, dishonour, late-payment, and debt-collection fees compound arrears

Consumer Protection summarises responsible-lending duties as requiring lenders to limit interest and fees for high-cost loans, charge reasonable credit and default fees, assess affordability and suitability, and treat borrowers fairly when they cannot pay [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). Its unfair-fees guidance lists late-payment default fees, demand-notice fees, repossession-warrant fees, debt-collection fees, and repossession fees as arrears-linked costs, plus default interest when repayments are missed or an account falls below an agreed limit [Consumer Protection, unfair fees](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/unfair-fees). MBIE's 2018 background paper named "payment dishonour fees" alongside repayment, default, and other credit fees that borrowers may not fully account for, but I did not find current public NZ data isolating dishonour-fee frequency, amounts, or impacts on vulnerable borrowers [MBIE, 2018 credit-regulation discussion paper background](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/3f19e04173/background-and-technical-detail-for-discussion-paper.pdf). The CCCFA's own guardrails confirm the mechanism matters: s41 requires default fees not be unreasonable, and the high-cost regime adds the $30 rebuttable-presumption cap [MBIE, BNPL RIS, para 7](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf), [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, para 35](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

Commerce Commission enforcement shows unreasonable credit/default fees can affect large borrower groups even outside the high-cost niche. Real Finance agreed in 2022 to compensate borrowers $1.36 million after acknowledging unreasonable fees [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/). Co-operative Bank admitted CCCFA breaches in 2025 and remediated 48,249 customers $7.225 million for charging higher fees than it should have [Commerce Commission, Co-operative Bank](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/co-operative-bank-admits-charging-unreasonable-fees/), [Simpson Grierson, Co-operative Bank](https://www.simpsongrierson.com/insights-news/legal-updates/co-operative-bank-admits-breaches-of-cccfa-for-unreasonable-fees/). The 2026 High Court judgment later imposed a $2.482 million penalty and records that the conduct during the pecuniary-penalty period affected about 28,261 customers overcharged $1.3–2 million, so the earlier $7.225 million/48,249-customer figure is the broader remediation programme rather than the narrower penalty-period harm [Commerce Commission v The Co-operative Bank judgment, 31 March 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/timeline/Commerce-Commission-v-The-Co-operative-Bank-Ltd-Judgement-31-March-2026.pdf). That case spanned home loans, personal loans, overdrafts, and credit-card cash advances, so it evidences scale risk, not that all remediated borrowers were vulnerable [Commerce Commission v The Co-operative Bank judgment, 31 March 2026](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/timeline/Commerce-Commission-v-The-Co-operative-Bank-Ltd-Judgement-31-March-2026.pdf).

FinCap's 2025 Voices report says missed repayments are concerning because further debt-collection fees can be added, prolonging and increasing debt harm for whānau supported by financial mentors [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994). FinCap's client base is not representative of all borrowers, but it is directly relevant to vulnerable borrowers already facing financial stress [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994).

**Confidence: Medium** that arrears-linked fees should be a high-priority feature in any transparency or education artifact. The mechanism is direct and enforcement is strong, but public evidence does not consistently separate default fees, dishonour fees, default interest, debt-collection fees, and repossession costs by borrower vulnerability.

### 6. Eligibility criteria can protect borrowers or exclude them from safer credit

Affordability criteria have a protective purpose: Consumer Protection says lenders must be satisfied borrowers can make repayments without substantial hardship, making reasonable income and expenses inquiries [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). That matters because unaffordable lending can turn a short-term cash-flow problem into repeat borrowing, arrears, and collection costs [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

The exclusion side is also real. MSD says Good Shepherd's Good Loans are no-interest, fee-free loans for people on low incomes to pay for essential expenses and avoid or manage high-cost debt, but the same page says those loans cannot be used for overdue bills, rent arrears, day-to-day living expenses, cash, or urgent needs because applications take time [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html). Good Shepherd's eligibility page similarly says no-interest loans cannot be used for day-to-day living expenses or urgent needs; any operational-capacity messages there are live service details and should be rechecked before use in design decisions [Good Shepherd NZ, Good Loans](https://goodshepherd.org.nz/get-support/our-services/loans/). FinCap makes the tradeoff concrete: people who lose access to responsible CCCFA lenders "end up in substantial hardship to make repayments on further Buy Now Pay Later", borrowing instead from BNPL, Work and Income, friends, family, and other lenders [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [FinCap, affordability-code submission](https://fincap.org.nz/images/240610-SUB-FinCap-RLC-exposure-draft.pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that eligibility design should be treated as a harm vector, not just an access rule. It is harder to rank than direct fees because the same criterion can prevent unaffordable lending in one case and exclude someone from lower-cost credit in another.

### Where the current rules have and haven't reduced harm

The strongest evidence of harm reduction is high-cost credit: MBIE concludes the provisions eliminated 50%+ lending and it recommends no change to the threshold, having found "no evidence" of the same harms below 50% (subject to the non-representative-data caveat above) [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, Issue D and paras 37, 55](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law). For BNPL and affordability, the evidence is less settled: BNPL was brought under the CCCFA from 2 September 2024 (with an exemption from full affordability assessments in favour of comprehensive credit reporting), so a post-reform borrower-outcome dataset is needed before saying whether arrears, default-fee accumulation, or payment juggling have fallen [MBIE, BNPL RIS, Executive Summary](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf), [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later). Regulatory responsibility for consumer credit also transferred from the Commerce Commission to the FMA on 1 July 2026, so future enforcement and monitoring sit with the FMA [FMA, consumer credit providers](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/services/consumer-credit/), [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, Problem Definition](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).

**Confidence: Medium-High** that current high-cost-credit rules have reduced the original high-cost-loan harm. **Confidence: Low** that public evidence can yet show whether BNPL regulation and revised affordability settings are working as intended for vulnerable borrowers.

## What would change this conclusion

- A current, representative NZ dataset linking borrower income, ethnicity, disability, product type, arrears status, default fees, collection fees, and repeat borrowing would materially change the ranking and could move overall confidence to High. MBIE itself notes national lending data does not capture interest rates, which is why it fell back on non-representative financial-mentor data [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, "Quantitative information"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).
- The high-cost-credit conclusion would change if post-reform evidence showed the ~150,000 excluded borrowers are materially worse off through illegal lending, lower-rate high-fee substitutes, or unmet urgent needs — or if the 13 lenders clustered at 47.5–49.95% were shown to reproduce high-cost harm just below the threshold [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS, paras 38, 56](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law).
- The BNPL conclusion would change if post-2-September-2024 CCCFA/credit-reporting data showed BNPL arrears, repeat use, or default-fee accumulation had materially fallen, or showed stronger causal harm than MBIE could establish in 2023–2024 [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later).
- The add-on-insurance conclusion would change if a product-specific review of vehicle finance, mobile trading, consumer leases, and personal loans found large public evidence of unsuitable add-ons or add-on-driven arrears.
- The dishonour-fee conclusion would change if current public lender, regulator, Banking Ombudsman, or financial-mentor data separated dishonour fees from other default, overdraft, debt-collection, and repossession costs for vulnerable borrowers.
- I did not contact borrowers, lenders, financial mentors, MoneyTalks, FinCap, Good Shepherd, the Commerce Commission, FMA, or MBIE; lived-experience and frontline validation should happen before this stream designs a borrower-facing tool.
