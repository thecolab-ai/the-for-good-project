# High-cost credit, repeat borrowing, BNPL defaults, and arrears fees appear to hit vulnerable NZ borrowers hardest

## Executive answer

This answers issue #71 as a **ranked scan across the fee, product-structure, and eligibility categories named in the issue**, not as five separate product findings. The ranking is clearest at the top and weaker for add-ons and eligibility because public NZ evidence does not quantify every harm category on the same basis [issue #71](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/71).

- The clearest harm to prioritise is **high-cost consumer credit and repeat borrowing**, because NZ law singles out loans at 50%+ annual interest or equivalent default-rate exposure, caps total high-cost-loan interest and fees, and MBIE says those rules were introduced to reduce problem debt from excessive cost and repeat borrowing by vulnerable consumers [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions), [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [NZ Legislation, CCCFA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/).
- **Default, late-payment, and debt-collection fees** matter most when a borrower is already in arrears, because they increase the amount owed at the point of least cash-flow resilience; this is strongly supported for high-cost credit and ordinary consumer-credit enforcement, and moderately supported for BNPL where MBIE explicitly says causation is hard to prove [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/), [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/), [MBIE, BNPL default-fee RIS addendum](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29100-regulatory-impact-statement-addendum-buy-now-pay-later-regulations).
- **BNPL product structure** is a priority risk even when no interest is charged: MBIE says BNPL can cause or worsen hardship for some consumers, missed instalments can trigger default fees, and the evidence base is still limited because causal attribution is difficult [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later), [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).
- **Eligibility and affordability criteria cut both ways**: weak screening can let unaffordable debt accumulate, but strict affordability or low-cost-loan criteria can exclude the same people from safer credit and push them toward BNPL, high-cost lenders, friends/family debt, or going without essentials [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do), [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html).
- Vulnerability is not theoretical: Stats NZ estimated that 9.1% of people were in material hardship in the year ended June 2025, and its 2025 child-poverty data estimated child material-hardship rates of 14.3% overall, 25.1% for Maori children, 31.0% for Pacific children, and 26.9% for disabled children [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/), [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of harm is well supported by official law/policy material, regulator enforcement, and financial-mentor evidence, but public NZ data does not cleanly quantify the marginal impact of each fee type by borrower vulnerability group.

## Evidence

### Vulnerability baseline: many households have no buffer

Stats NZ reported that 9.1% of people in New Zealand were living in households experiencing material hardship in the 12 months ended June 2025, which means a credit-cost transparency tool should assume a material share of borrowers have little room for repayment shocks [Stats NZ, household income and housing costs 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). Stats NZ's 2025 child-poverty CSV estimated 169,300 children in material hardship, or 14.3% of all children; the same dataset estimated materially higher hardship rates for Maori children at 25.1%, Pacific children at 31.0%, disabled children at 26.9%, and children in disabled households at 27.5% [Stats NZ child-poverty 2025 CSV](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Child-poverty-statistics/Child-poverty-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip).

These hardship figures do not prove that credit fees cause poverty, but they are directly relevant to impact: a missed-payment fee, default interest charge, collection cost, or unaffordable instalment has a larger practical effect when the household already lacks essentials or cannot absorb an unexpected expense [Stats NZ, child poverty release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).

### 1. High-cost credit and repeat borrowing have the strongest evidence of direct harm

MBIE says the CCCFA defines a high-cost consumer credit contract as one with an average annual interest rate of 50% or more, or where the combined annual interest rate and default rate are likely to be 50% or more [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions). The same MBIE page summarises the high-cost provisions as covering the first-advance total cost cap, a 0.8% maximum daily rate of charge, a rebuttable presumption that default fees over $30 are unreasonable, a compound-interest ban, and restrictions on high-cost loans to some repeat borrowers [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions). The CCCFA text also sets out the first-advance cost cap, repeat-borrower restrictions, and 0.8% rate cap [NZ Legislation, CCCFA](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/).

MBIE's 2024 consumer-credit RIS says the 2020 high-cost-credit rules were introduced to reduce problem debt by addressing excessive cost and repeat borrowing by vulnerable consumers, and MBIE's high-cost review page says the impacts included elimination of the high-cost lending market, a doubling of lenders offering loans in the 30-50% interest range, and no longer having debt spirals and repeated borrowing from high-cost loans [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions). This means the rules may already have reduced the specific high-cost-loan harm they targeted, while leaving a live question about borrowers shifted into lower-rate high-fee products or other forms of debt [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions).

Regulator enforcement still supports prioritising the high-cost pattern. In 2024, the Commerce Commission said Eagle M.A.N Group had been ordered to pay a $200,000 penalty for high-cost loans breaching multiple CCCFA requirements; the Commission said Eagle MAN charged up to 182.5% annual interest on top of credit and default fees, issued loans whose interest and fees exceeded the first advance, and gave repeat high-cost loans to borrowers [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/). This is a specific enforcement case, not a market-wide prevalence estimate, but it demonstrates the harm pattern the rules are designed to prevent [Commerce Commission, Eagle MAN](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2024/commerce-commission-secures-200k-penalty-against-lender-charging-up-to-182-5-interest/).

**Confidence: High** that high-cost credit and repeat borrowing should be the first harm category in the stream. Public evidence supports the mechanism, the statutory response, and current enforcement examples, but not a complete count of affected borrowers.

### 2. Default, late-payment, and debt-collection fees compound arrears

Consumer Protection summarises responsible-lending duties as requiring lenders to limit interest and fees for high-cost loans, charge reasonable credit and default fees, assess affordability and suitability, and treat borrowers fairly when they cannot pay [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). The same guidance lists unfair or oppressive examples that include charging high fees for profit, giving too little time to pay missed payments, and letting fees and interest stack up by taking too long to act on overdue payments [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

Commerce Commission enforcement shows that unreasonable credit/default fees can affect large borrower groups even outside the high-cost-loan niche. Real Finance agreed in 2022 to compensate borrowers $1.36 million after acknowledging unreasonable fees on loans [Commerce Commission, Real Finance](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2022/real-finance-to-pay-1-36m-to-borrowers-for-unreasonable-fees/). Co-operative Bank admitted CCCFA breaches in 2025 and remediated 48,249 customers $7.225 million for charging higher fees than it should have [Commerce Commission, Co-operative Bank](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/co-operative-bank-admits-charging-unreasonable-fees/). The Co-operative Bank case involved home loans, personal loans, overdrafts, and credit-card cash advances, so it supports the scale risk of unreasonable fees but should not be read as evidence that all remediated borrowers were vulnerable borrowers [Commerce Commission, Co-operative Bank](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/co-operative-bank-admits-charging-unreasonable-fees/).

FinCap's 2025 Voices report says missed repayments are concerning because further debt-collection fees can be added, prolonging and increasing debt harm for whanau supported by financial mentors [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994). FinCap's client base is not representative of all borrowers, but it is directly relevant to vulnerable borrowers because financial mentors work with people already facing financial stress [FinCap, Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994).

**Confidence: Medium-High** that arrears-linked fees should be a high-priority feature in any transparency or education artifact. The mechanism is direct and enforcement is strong, but public evidence does not consistently separate default fees, default interest, debt-collection fees, and repossession costs by borrower vulnerability.

### 3. BNPL is lower-cost when paid on time, but missed instalments and multiple accounts are a priority hardship risk

MBIE describes BNPL as a credit alternative that lets consumers access goods or services now and pay later through interest-free instalments, and says new BNPL consumer protections took effect on 2 September 2024 to protect consumers from potential financial harms while preserving access to low-cost credit [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later). MBIE's BNPL RIS states the policy problem as BNPL causing or worsening financial hardship for some consumers, while also saying no substantial quantitative data clearly demonstrates causation because the sector is newer and hardship can arise from other debts, existing poverty, or overspending [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf).

MBIE identifies several BNPL harm pathways: hardship may worsen when a consumer defaults and pays late fees, when BNPL payments cause default on another obligation, when consumers use BNPL for essentials because they are already struggling, or when consumers make BNPL payments but go without essentials or miss other debt repayments [MBIE, BNPL RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/27270-regulatory-impact-statement-applying-the-credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act-in-a-proportionate-way-to-buy-now-pay-later-lenders-proactiverelease-pdf). MBIE's 2024 addendum says financial mentors raised concerns about accumulated BNPL default fees, and says default fees can worsen hardship by increasing the amount owed even though attributing hardship to those fees is difficult [MBIE, BNPL default-fee RIS addendum](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29100-regulatory-impact-statement-addendum-buy-now-pay-later-regulations).

Financial-mentor evidence is consistent with MBIE's concern. FinCap told MBIE in 2024 that unaffordable BNPL loans were among the most common consumer-credit issues financial mentors had raised since December 2021, and that mentors saw debt spirals where people took multiple loans and kept paying them while going without essentials to keep facilities open [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf). Christians Against Poverty submitted to MBIE in 2021 that the share of its clients with BNPL debt rose from 2% in 2019 to 17% in 2021, and that those clients had two BNPL debts on average; this is one debt-help organisation's own client data and may not represent the broader borrowing population, so it is supporting evidence for vulnerable-client risk rather than market-wide prevalence evidence [Christians Against Poverty, BNPL submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/18981-christians-against-poverty-buy-now-pay-later-submission-pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that BNPL missed-payment fees, multiple concurrent BNPL debts, and instalment timing should be prioritised. The evidence is strong enough to flag risk for vulnerable borrowers, but MBIE is explicit that public quantitative evidence is limited, causation is hard to isolate, and post-September-2024 effects are not yet clear in the public sources reviewed here.

### 4. Add-on products and suitability checks matter, but the public evidence found here is thinner

Consumer Protection says lender suitability checks can include whether a borrower needs extra products such as insurance, and it gives credit-related insurance examples such as payment protection or mechanical-breakdown insurance added to car finance [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). This supports including add-ons in a total-cost-of-credit artifact, because an add-on can change the amount financed and the repayment burden even when the headline interest rate is unchanged [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

I did not find enough current public NZ evidence in this pass to rank add-on insurance above high-cost credit, BNPL default structures, or arrears/default fees for vulnerable borrowers. Add-ons should still be captured as a disclosure and suitability issue, especially for vehicle finance and consumer leases, but this finding treats them as **Medium/Low confidence** on scale and impact until a product-specific evidence pass is done [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

### 5. Eligibility criteria can protect borrowers or exclude them from safer credit

Affordability criteria have a protective purpose: Consumer Protection says lenders must be satisfied borrowers are likely to make required repayments without substantial hardship, and must make reasonable inquiries such as income and expenses checks [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do). That matters for vulnerable borrowers because unaffordable lending can turn a short-term cash-flow problem into repeat borrowing, arrears, and collection costs [Consumer Protection, lender duties](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do).

The exclusion side is also real. MSD says Good Shepherd's Good Loans are no-interest, fee-free loans for people on low incomes to pay for essential expenses and avoid or manage high-cost debt, but the same MSD page says those loans cannot be used for overdue bills, rent arrears, day-to-day living expenses, cash, or urgent needs because applications take time [MSD, microfinance](https://www.msd.govt.nz/what-we-can-do/providers/building-financial-capability/microfinance-debt-solutions.html). Good Shepherd's eligibility page similarly says no-interest loans cannot be used for day-to-day living expenses or urgent needs; any operational capacity messages on that page are live service details and should be rechecked before being used for design decisions [Good Shepherd NZ, Good Loans](https://goodshepherd.org.nz/get-support/our-services/loans/).

FinCap's 2024 submission makes the tradeoff concrete: it says people supported by financial mentors who can no longer access loans from CCCFA-bound responsible lenders can end up in substantial hardship to make repayments on further BNPL, and it reports whanau borrowing from BNPL lenders, Work and Income, friends, family, and other CCCFA lenders to relieve financial pressure [FinCap, 2024 consumer-credit submission](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29553-fincap-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-consultation-submission-pdf), [FinCap, affordability-code submission](https://fincap.org.nz/images/240610-SUB-FinCap-RLC-exposure-draft.pdf).

**Confidence: Medium** that eligibility design should be treated as a harm vector, not just an access rule. It is harder to rank than direct fees because the same criterion can prevent unaffordable lending in one case and exclude someone from lower-cost credit in another.

### Current rules may already be reducing some harm

The strongest counter-evidence is for high-cost credit: MBIE's high-cost review page says the provisions eliminated the high-cost lending market and ended debt spirals and repeated borrowing from high-cost loans, while also identifying indirect shifts into BNPL, other credit types, and more government hardship assistance [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions). MBIE's consumer-credit RIS also says the high-cost provisions led to elimination of lending at 50%+ interest rates, but the review requirement specifically asked whether the threshold should be reduced to 30-50%, which keeps open the question of whether harm has shifted just below the threshold [MBIE, consumer-credit RIS](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/29099-regulatory-impact-statement-fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-law), [MBIE, high-cost credit review](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms/fit-for-purpose-consumer-credit-legislation-high-cost-credit-contracts-provisions).

For BNPL and affordability checks, the public evidence is less settled. MBIE says BNPL protections took effect on 2 September 2024, so a post-reform borrower-outcome dataset would be needed before saying whether those protections materially reduced arrears, default-fee accumulation, or payment juggling [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later). MBIE's 2024-25 financial-services reform page says prescriptive affordability requirements in the Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Regulations 2004 were revoked from 31 July 2024, which is evidence that some earlier affordability settings were considered too prescriptive or burdensome, not proof that affordability checks should be weak [MBIE, 2024-25 financial services reforms](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/business/financial-markets-conduct-regulation/2024-financial-services-reforms).

**Confidence: Medium** that current high-cost-credit rules have reduced the original high-cost-loan harm; **Low-Medium** that public evidence can yet show whether BNPL regulation and revised affordability settings are working as intended for vulnerable borrowers.

## What would change this conclusion

- A current, representative NZ dataset linking borrower income, ethnicity, disability, product type, arrears status, default fees, collection fees, and repeat borrowing would materially change the ranking and could move the overall confidence to High.
- The conclusion on high-cost credit would change if post-reform evidence showed borrowers formerly using high-cost credit are now materially worse off because of exclusion, lower-rate high-fee substitutes, illegal lending, or unmet urgent needs.
- The conclusion on BNPL would change if post-September-2024 CCCFA/credit-reporting data showed that BNPL arrears, repeat use, or default-fee accumulation had materially fallen, or if it showed stronger causal harm than MBIE could establish in 2023-2024.
- The conclusion on add-on insurance would change if a product-specific review of vehicle finance, mobile trading, consumer leases, and personal loans found large public evidence of unsuitable add-ons or add-on-driven arrears.
- I could not verify a current public, regulator-grade count of NZ borrowers affected by each fee type; the evidence is strongest on mechanisms, rules, and selected enforcement/remediation cases.
- I did not contact borrowers, lenders, financial mentors, MoneyTalks, FinCap, Good Shepherd, Commerce Commission, FMA, or MBIE; lived-experience and frontline validation should happen before this stream designs a borrower-facing tool.
