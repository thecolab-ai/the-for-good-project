# NZ small-credit cost and eligibility disclosures are public but fragmented and only partly comparable

## Executive answer

- New Zealand consumer-credit disclosure duties still give a transparency tool a public-source base: the current consolidated CCCFA requires standard form terms and costs of borrowing to be publicly available, and Consumer Protection says lenders must make standard terms and costs available on websites or public-premises notices. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, ss 9J-9K](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)
- The regulator changed immediately before this finding was written: from 1 July 2026 the FMA took over regulatory responsibility for the CCCFA from the Commerce Commission, so this finding uses current FMA/MBIE/legislation sources for present-tense regulatory claims and treats older Commerce Commission material as historical context only. [FMA, "Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act (CCCFA)"](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/legislation/cccfa/); [Commerce Commission, "Transfer of responsibility for regulation of consumer credit"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/transfer-of-responsibility-for-regulation-of-consumer-credit/)
- Personal loans, overdrafts, and credit cards can usually be mapped from lender-owned pages, but the fields are not consistently named, rendered, or dated: ANZ publishes rates/fees/agreement pages and effective-dated credit-card documents; ASB pages include "current as at" wording that rendered without a date in the agent fetch; BNZ puts some overdraft costs in a dated PDF fee guide; Westpac publishes personal-loan rates and examples in product/calculator pages. [ANZ personal-loan rates](https://www.anz.co.nz/rates-fees-agreements/personal-loans/); [ASB personal-loan rates and fees](https://www.asb.co.nz/personal-loans/interest-rates-and-fees.html); [BNZ Personal account, service, and facility fees PDF](https://www.bnz.co.nz/assets/personal-banking-help-support/rates-and-fees/Personal-Account-Service-and-Facility-Fees.pdf?57f4fc38fc289941168e381b9bd5152ab3aed697=); [Westpac personal-loan calculator](https://www.westpac.co.nz/personal-loans/personal-loan-calculator/)
- BNPL disclosures are a separate category: MBIE says CCCFA protections apply to BNPL from 2 September 2024 and that a fee-provisions exemption started on 1 November 2024, while provider terms disclose repayment schedules and late-fee policies rather than annual interest rates. [MBIE, "Buy Now Pay Later"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later); [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance (Buy Now, Pay Later Exemptions) Amendment Regulations 2024, SL 2024/225](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/regulation/public/2024/0225/latest/096be8ed81eb09cb.pdf); [Afterpay NZ terms](https://www.afterpay.com/en-nz/terms-of-service); [Zip NZ Pay in 4 disclosure and terms](https://zip.co/nz/pay-in-4-disclosure-terms-conditions/)
- Existing consumer-facing comparison infrastructure matters but does not remove the fragmentation problem: Sorted gives borrowing guidance and points people to comparison sources, while MoneyHub and other aggregators publish comparison guides, but they are not a regulator-published, machine-readable disclosure dataset with uniform provenance and effective dates. [Sorted, "Before you use credit"](https://sorted.org.nz/guides/tackling-debt/before-you-use-credit/); [MoneyHub, "The Best Personal Loan Offers - July 2026"](https://www.moneyhub.co.nz/personal-loans.html)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - current primary legislation and FMA/MBIE transfer sources support the disclosure map, but the regulatory map is Medium rather than High because the 1 July 2026 transfer is fresh, FMA credit guidance is still partly transitional, this is not a full market census, and several lender pages are dynamic or only partly dated.

## Evidence

### Baseline disclosure duties

The public baseline is statutory, not voluntary: current CCCFA section 9J says lenders using standard form contract terms must ensure those terms are publicly available, and section 9K says creditors must ensure costs of borrowing for each class of consumer credit contract are publicly available. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, ss 9J-9K](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/)

For website publication, the current CCCFA says creditors with an Internet site must display credit fees, default fees, annual interest rates, and default interest charge rates prominently and clearly for each class of consumer credit contract. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, s 9K](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/)

The consumer-facing MBIE site summarises the same practical effect: lenders must make standard terms and costs of borrowing publicly available through their website or clearly displayed notices, and must provide important information before the agreement is signed. [Consumer Protection, "Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

Initial disclosure is broader than a headline interest rate: CCCFA schedule 1 includes annual interest rates, how multiple or base rates apply, interest calculation method and frequency, total interest where ascertainable on prescribed assumptions, credit fees and charges, payment amount/timing/number/frequency, default interest/default fees, cancellation rights, hardship rights, and dispute-resolution details. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, Schedule 1](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/)

The 2026 transfer changes who regulates the regime, not the need to track current statutory disclosure text: FMA says it takes on CCCFA regulatory responsibility from 1 July 2026, and ComCom says the FMA became the single conduct regulator for financial markets including consumer credit on that date. [FMA, "CCCFA transfer frequently asked questions"](https://www.fma.govt.nz/business/legislation/cccfa/cccfa-transfer-faq/); [Commerce Commission, "Transfer of responsibility for regulation of consumer credit"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/transfer-of-responsibility-for-regulation-of-consumer-credit/)

I did not identify a current-law change in the consolidated CCCFA that removes the section 9J/9K public-publication duties or the Schedule 1 initial-disclosure fields mapped here; the 2026 Act and FMA pages do, however, make any present-tense "Commerce Commission says" framing stale after 1 July 2026. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/); [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Amendment Act 2026](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2026/29/en/latest/)

### What can and cannot be compared fairly

A public tool can fairly compare disclosed fields where the field has the same meaning, such as establishment fee, monthly account fee, late-payment/default fee, cash-advance fee, purchase interest rate, minimum loan amount, maximum loan term, and whether the lender says rates or fees are subject to change. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, ss 9K and Schedule 1](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/general-help/consumer-laws/credit-contracts-and-consumer-finance-act)

A public tool can fairly calculate scenario totals only when it records assumptions: amount borrowed or purchase price, term, repayment frequency, drawdown pattern, repayment timing, default/missed-payment timing, whether the borrower qualifies for the advertised product, whether security is required, and whether the rate is fixed, variable, or personalised. [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, Schedule 1](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/); [Consumer Protection, "What lenders must do"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do)

A public tool should not present a single "best/cheapest" ranking across personal loans, overdrafts, credit cards, BNPL, and short-term credit without product-type caveats, because overdrafts price time in debit, credit cards price revolving balances and cash advances differently from purchases, BNPL prices on-time use mainly through instalment/default-fee rules, and short-term loans can be dominated by fixed fees over very short terms. [ANZ overdraft](https://www.anz.co.nz/personal/accounts/everyday-banking/overdraft/); [ASB credit-card rates and fees](https://www.asb.co.nz/credit-cards/interest-rates-fees.html); [MBIE, "Buy Now Pay Later"](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later); [SMARTCASH bad-credit loans](https://www.smartcash.co.nz/bad-credit-loans)

Existing aggregators should be treated as counter-evidence and possible partners, not ignored: Sorted gives public guidance before using credit, MoneyHub publishes current personal-loan comparison information, Canstar compares personal loans and credit cards, and Interest.co.nz publishes personal-loan rate tables with caveats that fees can change the effective cost. [Sorted, "Before you use credit"](https://sorted.org.nz/guides/tackling-debt/before-you-use-credit/); [MoneyHub, "The Best Personal Loan Offers - July 2026"](https://www.moneyhub.co.nz/personal-loans.html); [Canstar, "Compare the Best Low Interest Personal Loans in NZ"](https://www.canstar.co.nz/personal-loans/); [Interest.co.nz, "Personal loan"](https://www.interest.co.nz/borrowing/personal-loan)

Source freshness should be an explicit field, not an implicit promise: the safest minimum record is source URL, product type, provider name, field extracted, exact value, access date, effective date if shown, whether the source says rates/fees are subject to change, and whether a dynamic page/manual browser check was needed. [FMA, "Disclosure for lenders: guidelines"](https://www.fma.govt.nz/assets/Credit-Providers/Disclosure-for-lenders-Guidelines.pdf); [Credit Contracts and Consumer Finance Act 2003, s 9K and Schedule 1](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/52/en/latest/)

## What would change this conclusion

- A regulator-published, machine-readable open dataset of lender product costs, fees, eligibility criteria, and effective dates would change the finding from "public but fragmented" to "centrally available"; I found official law/guidance and lender pages, not a complete regulator-maintained product feed.
- A full market crawl with browser rendering and screenshots would change the confidence on provider coverage; this finding checked representative public examples, not every NZ bank, finance company, broker, pawnbroker, mobile trader, BNPL provider, community lender, or no-interest lender.
- A systematic evaluation of Sorted, MoneyHub, Canstar, Interest.co.nz, Money Compare, and other aggregators could change the product need from "new transparency dataset" to "improve, license, or partner with existing comparison infrastructure"; I only checked enough to avoid treating the field as empty.
- Direct confirmation from lenders or the FMA would change confidence on dynamic pages whose dates or rates rendered blank in the agent fetch; I did not contact any organisation.
- Legal review would be needed before deciding exactly what can be republished, especially for derived "total cost" calculations that might be perceived as a recommendation or a live quote; this finding maps public disclosures and does not give legal advice.
- I could not verify historical change logs for most provider pages; ANZ and BNZ had some effective/dated documents, but several web pages gave no explicit visible effective date in the fetched text.
- I could not verify application-stage personalised rates without entering borrower information, and no personal or identifying data was used.

## Open follow-up questions

- What exact total-cost scenarios should a stream steward approve for side-by-side comparison without implying unlike products are interchangeable?
- Which providers and product categories should be in the minimum viable market census: registered banks only, all certified/licensed lenders, BNPL providers, pawnbrokers, mobile traders, brokers, community lenders, or no-interest lenders?
- Do existing comparison/guidance sites already cover enough lender-rate, fee, eligibility, and freshness information for the intended users, or is the unmet need specifically provenance, open data, or vulnerable-borrower scenarios?
- What legal and consumer-testing guardrails are needed so a transparency tool remains education/information rather than regulated personalised financial advice?
- Can a lightweight monitoring process detect lender-page changes reliably enough for public use, given dynamic fields, PDFs, and application-flow disclosures?
