# NZ small-credit cost and eligibility disclosures are public but fragmented and only partly comparable

## Executive answer

- New Zealand consumer-credit law requires lenders to give borrowers disclosure before entry and to publish standard form contract terms and costs of borrowing where they use standard form contracts, but the public evidence is distributed across product pages, rates-and-fees hubs, PDF fee schedules, terms, sample disclosure documents, calculators, and application screens rather than one standard public dataset. [Commerce Commission, "Publication of standard form contract terms and cost of borrowing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/publication-of-standard-form-contract-terms-and-cost-of-borrowing/); [Commerce Commission, "Charging interest"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/charging-interest/)
- Personal loans, overdrafts, and credit cards can usually be mapped from lender-owned pages, but the fields are not consistently named, rendered, or dated: ANZ publishes rates/fees/agreement pages and effective-dated credit-card documents; ASB pages include "current as at" wording that rendered without a date in the agent fetch; BNZ puts some overdraft costs in a dated PDF fee guide. [ANZ personal-loan rates](https://www.anz.co.nz/rates-fees-agreements/personal-loans/); [ANZ credit-card rates](https://www.anz.co.nz/rates-fees-agreements/credit-cards/); [ASB personal-loan rates and fees](https://www.asb.co.nz/personal-loans/interest-rates-and-fees.html); [BNZ Personal account, service, and facility fees PDF](https://www.bnz.co.nz/assets/personal-banking-help-support/rates-and-fees/Personal-Account-Service-and-Facility-Fees.pdf?57f4fc38fc289941168e381b9bd5152ab3aed697=)
- BNPL disclosures are a separate category: MBIE says CCCFA protections apply to BNPL from 2 September 2024, with a later exemption from unreasonable-fee provisions from 1 November 2024, while provider terms disclose repayment schedules and late-fee policies rather than annual interest rates. [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later); [Afterpay NZ terms](https://www.afterpay.com/en-nz/terms-of-service); [Zip NZ Pay in 4 disclosure and terms](https://zip.co/nz/pay-in-4-disclosure-terms-conditions/)
- Short-term/high-cost lending is publicly disclosed, but a headline annual rate is especially easy to misread because fees and very short terms dominate the cost: SMARTCASH discloses 0.134% daily interest, 49% AIR, a $55 establishment fee, and $5 per payment processed on loans of $100-$300 for up to 31 days. [SMARTCASH bad-credit loans](https://www.smartcash.co.nz/bad-credit-loans); [SMARTCASH questions](https://www.smartcash.co.nz/your-questions)
- A defensible transparency tool can republish source-linked, access-dated fields and total-cost scenarios, but it should avoid ranking unlike products on one headline rate because eligibility, drawdown pattern, repayment behaviour, default timing, security, product purpose, and credit checks change the meaning of the displayed price. [Consumer Protection, what lenders must do](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do); [Commerce Commission, consumer credit fees](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/consumer-credit-fees/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the regulatory disclosure map is high confidence, and representative lender examples were checked against current public pages on 2 July 2026, but this is not a full market census and several lender pages are dynamic or only partly dated.

## Evidence

### Baseline disclosure duties

The public baseline is not voluntary: the Commerce Commission says lenders with websites must prominently and clearly display standard form contract terms and costs of borrowing on their sites, and must supply copies free of charge on request. [Commerce Commission, "Publication of standard form contract terms and cost of borrowing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/publication-of-standard-form-contract-terms-and-cost-of-borrowing/)

The "costs of borrowing" requirement is broad enough for a transparency tool to look for credit fees, default fees, annual interest rates, and default interest rates, because Commerce Commission website-review guidance describes section 9K as requiring those costs to be displayed clearly and prominently. [Commerce Commission, Lender website review 2017/2018](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0013/91111/Lender-website-review-201718-June-2018.pdf)

Initial disclosure must include interest-rate mechanics, including the annual interest rate, when each rate applies, the interest method and frequency, benchmark mechanics if used, total interest where known for contracts repaid within seven years, interest-free periods, and default interest. [Commerce Commission, "Charging interest"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/charging-interest/)

Fees must be disclosed in the initial disclosure statement with the fee name, when it is payable, and the amount or method of calculation; if a contract allows a lender to change or add fees, the Commerce Commission says the borrower must be told within five working days of the change. [Commerce Commission, "Consumer credit fees"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/consumer-credit-fees/)

For unilateral changes, the Commerce Commission says lenders must disclose changes to interest, fee/charge calculation or payment mechanics, and credit limits within five working days, but for non-high-cost contracts the lender may also use general publication methods such as website posting for some unilateral changes. [Commerce Commission, "Disclosure obligations"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/your-disclosure-obligations/)

Consumer Protection summarises the consumer-facing effect: borrowers should know total repayment, repayment amount and frequency, interest rate and fees, cancellation rights, and hardship options before signing, and standard form terms and costs should be published clearly and prominently where a lender uses them. [Consumer Protection, "When have I entered into a consumer credit contract?"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/consumers/dealing-with-typical-situations/borrowing-money-and-buying-on-credit/when-have-i-entered-into-a-consumer-credit-contract/)

### What can and cannot be compared fairly

A public tool can fairly compare disclosed fields where the field has the same meaning, such as establishment fee, monthly account fee, late-payment/default fee, cash-advance fee, purchase interest rate, minimum loan amount, maximum loan term, and whether the lender says rates or fees are subject to change. [Commerce Commission, "Consumer credit fees"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/consumer-credit-fees/); [Commerce Commission, "Charging interest"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/charging-interest/)

A public tool can fairly calculate scenario totals only when it records assumptions: amount borrowed or purchase price, term, repayment frequency, drawdown pattern, repayment timing, default/missed-payment timing, whether the borrower qualifies for the advertised product, whether security is required, and whether the rate is fixed, variable, or personalised. [Commerce Commission, "Charging interest"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/charging-interest/); [Consumer Protection, "What lenders must do"](https://www.consumerprotection.govt.nz/help-product-service/borrowing-money/what-lenders-must-do)

A public tool should not present a single "best/cheapest" ranking across personal loans, overdrafts, credit cards, BNPL, and short-term credit without product-type caveats, because overdrafts price time in debit, credit cards price revolving balances and cash advances differently from purchases, BNPL prices on-time use mainly through instalment/default-fee rules, and short-term loans can be dominated by fixed fees over very short terms. [ANZ overdraft](https://www.anz.co.nz/personal/accounts/everyday-banking/overdraft/); [ASB credit-card rates and fees](https://www.asb.co.nz/credit-cards/interest-rates-fees.html); [MBIE, Buy Now Pay Later](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/consumer-protection/buy-now-pay-later); [SMARTCASH bad-credit loans](https://www.smartcash.co.nz/bad-credit-loans)

Source freshness should be an explicit field, not an implicit promise: the safest minimum record is source URL, product type, provider name, field extracted, exact value, access date, effective date if shown, whether the source says rates/fees are subject to change, and whether a dynamic page/manual browser check was needed. [Commerce Commission, "Disclosure obligations"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/your-disclosure-obligations/); [Commerce Commission, "Publication of standard form contract terms and cost of borrowing"](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/business/credit-providers/publication-of-standard-form-contract-terms-and-cost-of-borrowing/)

## What would change this conclusion

- A regulator-published, machine-readable open dataset of lender product costs, fees, eligibility criteria, and effective dates would change the finding from "public but fragmented" to "centrally available"; I found official regulatory guidance and lender pages, not a complete regulator-maintained product feed.
- A full market crawl with browser rendering and screenshots would change the confidence on provider coverage; this finding checked representative public examples, not every NZ bank, finance company, broker, pawnbroker, mobile trader, or BNPL provider.
- Direct confirmation from lenders or a regulator would change confidence on dynamic pages whose dates or rates rendered blank in the agent fetch; I did not contact any organisation.
- Legal review would be needed before deciding exactly what can be republished, especially for derived "total cost" calculations that might be perceived as a recommendation or a live quote; this finding maps public disclosures and does not give legal advice.
- I could not verify historical change logs for most provider pages; ANZ and BNZ had some effective/dated documents, but several web pages gave no explicit visible effective date in the fetched text.
- I could not verify application-stage personalised rates without entering borrower information, and no personal or identifying data was used.

## Open follow-up questions

- What exact total-cost scenarios should a stream steward approve for side-by-side comparison without implying unlike products are interchangeable?
- Which providers and product categories should be in the minimum viable market census: registered banks only, all certified lenders, BNPL providers, pawnbrokers, mobile traders, brokers, or community/no-interest lenders?
- What legal and consumer-testing guardrails are needed so a transparency tool remains education/information rather than regulated personalised financial advice?
- Can a lightweight monitoring process detect lender-page changes reliably enough for public use, given dynamic fields, PDFs, and application-flow disclosures?
