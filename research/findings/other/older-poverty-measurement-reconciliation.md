# New Zealand's high OECD over-65 income-poverty rate is mostly a relative-income measurement warning, but hardship is real for older renters, single-person households, and Maori and Pacific older people

## Executive answer

- **Do not read the OECD 33.7% as saying one-third of older New Zealanders are in material hardship.** OECD's figure is a relative income measure: income below 50% of national median equivalised disposable household income; OECD itself warns that income data may miss older people's housing and other wealth [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html).
- **The best NZ public-data reconciliation is: high relative-income poverty, low average material hardship, high subgroup hardship.** MSD's 2025 older-person wellbeing paper says about 3-4% of older New Zealanders, around 25,000-30,000 people, are in material hardship or near hardship, while Stats NZ's 2025 all-population release puts material hardship at 9.1% for all people [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf); [Stats NZ household income and housing-cost statistics, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/).
- **Housing tenure is the decisive stress test.** MSD reports average 2019-2024 material-hardship rates for older people of 1% for mortgage-free owners, 5% for mortgaged owners, 16% for private renters receiving Accommodation Supplement, and 18% for public or council renters; it also reports that low-income older private renters spending more than 40% of income on housing rose from 51% in 2009 to 68% in 2024 [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- **Household type and ethnicity matter.** MSD reports average 2019-2024 material-hardship rates for older people of 1.3% for older couples, 4.1% for older one-person households, 11.0% for older people in family households with dependent children, 2% for European older people, 3% for Asian older people, 8% for Maori older people, and 14% for Pacific older people [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- **Policy implication:** adequacy and cost containment should not be framed as a simple choice. Broadly cutting NZ Super would hit a small but real hardship group, while a pure "raise the universal rate for everyone" response would be poorly targeted unless the goal is explicitly to move many NZS-only households over a relative income line [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf); [Retirement Commission review, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction is strongly supported by OECD, Stats NZ, MSD, and the Retirement Commission, but the older-person subgroup rates mostly come from pooled HES 2019-2024 estimates rather than one current 2025 cross-tab, and the public data cannot fully separate income adequacy from wealth, disability costs, family support, and housing quality.

## Evidence

### What the OECD indicator is measuring

OECD's 2025 Pensions at a Glance reports New Zealand's over-65 relative income-poverty rate as 33.7%, with 26.9% for ages 66-75 and 44.7% for ages over 75; the OECD average for all people aged over 65 is 14.8% [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html). The same OECD section defines the threshold as 50% of national median equivalised household disposable income and says the country differences in older people's housing and other wealth may not be reflected in income-poverty rates [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html).

That warning matters unusually much for New Zealand because MSD's older-person wellbeing paper says the OECD-style square-root BHC 50 measure is sensitive to a "pensioner spike" around NZ Super and rose as NZ Super fell below the moving median threshold, while the overall material-hardship rate for older people remained much lower [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf). The Retirement Commission makes the same interpretation in its 2025 review: some older people with little income beyond NZ Super can fall just below the relative poverty line when median wages rise, even if their living standards have not materially declined [Retirement Commission review, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

### NZ income, after-housing-cost, and hardship anchors

Stats NZ's year-ended June 2025 household income and housing-cost release gives the all-population anchor: 10.2% of people were below BHC 50, 16.5% were below current-year AHC 50, 23.9% were below current-year AHC 60, 9.1% were in material hardship, and 4.6% were both below AHC 60 and in material hardship [Stats NZ household income and housing-cost statistics, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/). The same workbook gives people aged 65+ a median equivalised disposable household income of $40,812 and mean of $55,170 in 2025, placing many older people in lower income quintiles without proving material deprivation [Stats NZ 2025 download workbook](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics/Household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.xlsx).

MSD's older-person paper is the better source for the 65+ reconciliation because it combines income, housing costs, liquid assets, material deprivation, and self-rated income adequacy, and explicitly breaks the older population down by tenure, living arrangement, employment status, ethnicity, and health status [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf). MSD's headline conclusion is that most older New Zealanders have enough income and assets to avoid material hardship, but a small group is in hardship or near hardship: around 3-4%, or roughly 25,000-30,000 older people [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).

The housing-cost trend points toward worsening future exposure even if the current average older cohort still benefits from high mortgage-free ownership. MSD reports that mortgage-free ownership among older New Zealanders fell from 83% in the mid-1990s to 71% on average from 2022 to 2024, and that among people aged 45-64 renting doubled from around 10% in the 1990s to 23% in 2024 while mortgage-free ownership fell from 60% to 32% [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf). The Retirement Commission independently uses the same tenure trend as a retirement-income warning, saying NZ Super offers a stable base but can be insufficient for renters and people without savings [Retirement Commission review, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

### Reconciliation for reform choices

The OECD 33.7% is real as an OECD relative-income statistic, but it is not a direct material-hardship rate. It is inflated by the position of NZ Super around a moving national median, by the OECD square-root equivalence scale, and by the fact that the indicator ignores housing wealth and housing costs [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html); [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).

The hardship signal is also real, but it is concentrated. Older renters receiving Accommodation Supplement, social-housing renters, older people still paying housing costs, one-person households, older people living with dependent children, Maori older people, and Pacific older people are the groups most visible in the public data [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf). That means cost-containment reforms should be tested against these groups, and adequacy reforms should be designed around housing costs and supplementary support rather than assuming the OECD headline alone identifies who is materially deprived [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf); [Retirement Commission review, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A Stats NZ or MSD 2025 microdata table that cross-tabs older people's AHC poverty, material hardship, tenure, household type, ethnicity, disability, and savings in one dataset could raise confidence from Medium to High or change which subgroup is prioritised [Stats NZ household income and housing-cost statistics, 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/household-income-and-housing-cost-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- A harmonised OECD or LIS-style cross-country material-hardship comparison for people aged 65+ could change the international conclusion; this finding found OECD income poverty and NZ material-hardship data, not a single harmonised international hardship series [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html); [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- New evidence showing that older private renters' hardship has fallen sharply after 2024 would weaken the "target housing costs" interpretation; MSD's 2025 paper instead warns that 2024-25 costs may increase hardship for some older groups [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- I could not verify, from public aggregate data, how much of the OECD 33.7% is mechanically attributable to the equivalence scale, how much to the BHC/AHC distinction, how much to tenure, and how much to private savings; that requires unit-record analysis under the right access controls [OECD old-age income poverty, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/pensions-at-a-glance-2025_76510fe4/full-report/old-age-income-poverty_3cd946d7.html); [MSD older wellbeing paper, 2025](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/working-papers/wp-01-25-the-financial-and-material-wellbeing-of-older-new-zealanders.pdf).
- This finding uses public sources only and does not include lived-experience evidence from older renters, Maori or Pacific older people, disability advocates, budget services, MSD frontline staff, or housing providers; those perspectives are needed before translating this measurement reconciliation into service or policy design [Retirement Commission review, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
