# Public and affordable housing programmes are targeted to high-need places, but are too small to close the supply gap on their own

## Executive answer

- The clearest answer is "material for the specific households reached, not material enough to close the high-need supply gap by itself": Budget 2024, Budget 2025, Budget 2026, the Housing Investment Plan 2025, and Kāinga Ora renewals together add thousands of public, social, supported, Māori-led, and affordable homes, but their named programme volumes are small beside the 19,704 applicants on the Housing Register at 31 March 2026 and the 39,737 new homes consented nationally in the year ended May 2026 [MSD, Housing Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/housing-register.html); [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).
- The targeting logic is much stronger than a simple national consent count: HUD's Housing Investment Plan 2025 selected Far North, South Auckland, Eastern Bay of Plenty, Gisborne-Tairāwhiti, Hastings, Hamilton, Tauranga, Wellington, and Christchurch after comparing Housing Register prevalence, severe housing deprivation, planned-but-not-yet-delivered social supply, population change, provider readiness, and local insight [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).
- Current delivery still lags programme ambition: by 30 April 2026, the Budget 2024 and Budget 2025 social-housing dashboard showed 211 Budget 2024 social homes delivered and 235 Budget 2025 Auckland social homes delivered; by 31 March 2026 it showed 97 Māori homes delivered from the post-December-2023 Māori housing funding stream [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, April 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026).
- Public and affordable programmes are not just extra units in the same market: they reach households that private construction often will not house, but some programme settings can also count purchases or leases from the existing market, so not every funded home should be read as net new supply [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the programme totals, national register counts, emergency-housing counts, and national/TLA consent counts are from official sources, but I could not verify a single current public dataset that cleanly joins high-need households, funded-housing completions, and private consents at the same geography and date.

## Evidence

### National scale: programme volumes are meaningful but not gap-closing

MSD reported 19,704 applicants on the Housing Register as at 31 March 2026, and the Transfer Register added 4,959 applicants already in public housing who needed to be rehoused, so the combined visible social-housing pressure was 24,663 applications before counting hidden severe deprivation or people not eligible for the register [MSD, Housing Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/housing-register.html); [MSD, Transfer Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/transfer-register.html). Stats NZ reported 39,737 new homes consented in the year ended May 2026, which is the relevant national private-and-public construction scale for comparing supply additions [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/).

Against those baselines, Budget 2024's 1,500 new social homes equal about 7.6 percent of the March 2026 Housing Register, about 6.1 percent of the combined Housing and Transfer registers, and about 3.8 percent of the year-ended-May-2026 national consent volume; those ratios are my calculations from HUD's 1,500-home Budget 2024 programme, MSD's March 2026 register counts, and Stats NZ's May 2026 consent count [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, April 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026); [MSD, Housing Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/housing-register.html); [MSD, Transfer Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/transfer-register.html); [Stats NZ, Building consents issued: May 2026](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/building-consents-issued-may-2026/). Budget 2025's 550 to 600 Auckland social homes equal about 2.8 to 3.0 percent of the Housing Register and 1.4 to 1.5 percent of national annual consents, and the Housing Investment Plan 2025's 675 to 770 social homes and affordable rentals equal about 3.4 to 3.9 percent of the Housing Register and 1.7 to 1.9 percent of national annual consents by the same calculation [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, April 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026); [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).

Budget 2026 is larger but later: HUD says it adds $69.2 million to the Flexible Fund to deliver 1,800 to 2,250 additional social and other homes over three years starting in 2028/29, which equals about 9.1 to 11.4 percent of the March 2026 Housing Register if treated as a one-off stock addition [HUD, Budget 2026 for the housing and urban system](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system); [MSD, Housing Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/housing-register.html). That comparison overstates near-term relief because the Budget 2026 homes start from 2028/29, while the register count is a March 2026 snapshot [HUD, Budget 2026 for the housing and urban system](https://www.hud.govt.nz/news/budget-2026-for-the-housing-and-urban-system); [MSD, Housing Register, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/housing-register.html).

### The programmes are targeted to high need, not just population growth

The Housing Investment Plan 2025 explicitly defines need as people's or households' ability to access suitable housing through the market, and says the allocation process compared locations using the Housing Register rate per 10,000 population, severe housing deprivation from Census 2023, planned-but-not-yet-delivered social-housing supply, population change, provider capability, market conditions, and local insight [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf). The plan's target-location shortlist is Far North District, Kawerau District, Māngere-Ōtāhuhu, Ōpōtiki District, Ōtara-Papatoetoe, Gisborne District, Manurewa, Hastings District, and Whakatāne District, while Hamilton, Tauranga, Wellington, and Christchurch receive smaller main-centre allocations [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).

That targeting matters because high-need places are not always high-consent places. In Stats NZ's year-ended-May-2026 territorial-authority data, Far North District consented 243 new dwelling units, Whakatāne District 139, Kawerau District 2, Ōpōtiki District 37, Gisborne District 216, and Hastings District 370, while Auckland consented 16,862 and Christchurch City consented 4,524 [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents downloadable data](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip). The three South Auckland local boards named in the Housing Investment Plan - Māngere-Ōtāhuhu, Ōtara-Papatoetoe, and Manurewa - consented 818, 679, and 845 new dwelling units respectively in the same year-ended-May-2026 Stats NZ local-board series, so South Auckland has substantial raw construction volume but is still treated by HUD as a target location because need and market access are not solved by aggregate consents alone [Stats NZ, May 2026 building-consents downloadable data](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Building-consents-issued/Building-consents-issued-May-2026/Download-data/building-consents-issued-may-2026.zip); [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).

### Delivery to date is smaller than the pipeline

HUD's April 2026 dashboard says Budget 2024 funded 1,500 new social homes over 2025/26 and 2026/27, Budget 2025 funded around 550 to 600 Auckland social homes to be ready for tenanting by November 2026, and $426 million had been approved since December 2023 for Māori-led delivery of around 1,000 homes [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, April 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026). The same dashboard shows 211 Budget 2024 homes delivered against 1,333 approved, 235 Budget 2025 Auckland homes delivered against 393 approved, and 97 Māori homes delivered against 754 contracted, which indicates an active pipeline but limited completed relief as at April/March 2026 [HUD, Budget 2024 and 2025 funded housing delivery dashboard, April 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/documents/budget-2024-and-2025-funded-housing-delivery-dashboard-for-april-2026).

Kāinga Ora is a large continuing stock manager rather than a simple expansion lever in this period. The Housing Investment Plan says Kāinga Ora will deliver an approximately 78,000-place social and supported housing portfolio by June 2026, but also says Kāinga Ora intends to maintain regional stock numbers unless requested by the Ministry, and that around 1,500 new-build homes and 400 retrofits per year will be offset by demolitions associated with redevelopment and sales [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf). That means Kāinga Ora construction can improve quality, bedroom mix, and suitability even where it does not produce a one-for-one net increase in social-housing places [HUD, Housing Investment Plan 2025](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Investment-plan-2025/Housing-investment-plan.pdf).

### Emergency-housing pressure is lower than the register, but still relevant

MSD's March 2026 monthly housing report says there were 471 households in emergency housing at the end of March 2026, up 21 from February 2026, and 417 children in emergency housing, up 72 from February 2026 [MSD, Monthly Housing Report, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/monthly-housing-update/2026/monthly-housing-report-march-2026.pdf). HUD's Homelessness Insights report says the most robust homelessness baseline remains the 2023 Census severe-housing-deprivation estimate of at least 112,500 people, and it cautions that it is not possible to conclude whether overall homelessness has increased, decreased, or stayed stable since Census 2023 [HUD, Homelessness insights to 31 March 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Homelessness/Homelessness-insights-report-to-end-March-2026.pdf?m=572f7739f02450ea54793fedd7ec85b57727299e). This makes emergency-housing exits a useful but incomplete yardstick: they are much smaller than the register, and neither captures all hidden severe deprivation [MSD, Monthly Housing Report, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/housing/monthly-housing-update/2026/monthly-housing-report-march-2026.pdf); [HUD, Homelessness insights to 31 March 2026](https://www.hud.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Documents/Homelessness/Homelessness-insights-report-to-end-March-2026.pdf?m=572f7739f02450ea54793fedd7ec85b57727299e).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current public dataset that joins funded-housing completions, Housing Register applications, emergency-housing exits, and private consents at the same territorial-authority or local-board geography would strengthen or overturn the place-by-place conclusion.
- Evidence that Budget 2025 and Budget 2026 Flexible Fund projects deliver faster, at lower subsidy per home, or unlock additional market supply beyond funded homes would make the programmes more material than the simple unit-count comparison implies.
- Evidence that purchases or leases from the existing market displace other low-income households would weaken the conclusion that every funded home is a net improvement to the local supply gap.
- I could not verify a complete current regional Kāinga Ora delivery dataset separating new builds, demolitions, sales, acquisitions, and net social-housing-place change.
- This finding uses public aggregate data only; frontline providers, iwi/Māori housing providers, councils, and people with lived experience should review whether the selected high-need locations and bedroom mixes reflect actual local need.
