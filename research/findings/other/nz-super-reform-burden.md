# NZ Super reform costs fall on different groups depending on whether the lever is age, rate, targeting, tax, or private saving

## Executive answer

- **Raising the NZ Super age concentrates the loss on future 65-66 year olds who cannot easily work longer.** NZIER says an age rise from 65 to 67 by 2050 would cut eligible numbers and NZ Super costs by about one-tenth, but would disproportionately affect groups with lower life expectancy, including Māori and Pacific peoples, and some physical labourers who may not be able to continue working past 65 [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
- **Indexation or rate restraint spreads the loss across all recipients over time, with the sharpest hardship risk for renters, single people, disabled people, and people without savings.** NZIER says CPI-only indexation rather than wage indexation could reduce 2050 NZ Super costs by about one-fifth but would let retiree living standards fall behind the general population, while the Retirement Commission says NZ Super is already insufficient for some renters and people without private savings [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- **Income or asset targeting shifts cost to better-off older people first, but the design risk is broader.** NZIER says higher tax on pensioners' other income could reduce net NZ Super costs by about one-tenth but would be complex and reduce incentives to work in old age, and asset-based means-testing could reduce incentives to save [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
- **Higher taxes or pre-funding shifts cost toward current workers and younger cohorts.** NZIER says unchanged settings could require tax revenue to rise by around 5-10% per worker, and that moving from pay-as-you-go toward a savings-based system creates a double burden on working-age people who must pay for current retirees while pre-funding future costs [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
- **More reliance on KiwiSaver or private saving shifts investment, earnings, contribution, and longevity risk onto groups with lower or interrupted earnings.** The Retirement Commission says KiwiSaver's voluntary and workplace-based design leaves gaps for self-employed people, women with caregiving gaps, Māori and Pacific peoples, and temporary migrants, while NZIER says reducing the role of NZ Super and strengthening KiwiSaver could perpetuate inequalities [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf); [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of who bears each reform burden is strongly supported by official and commissioned NZ sources, but the finding does not model a specific reform package, so exact dollar losses depend on policy design, transition rules, exemptions, and take-up.

## Evidence

### Baseline exposure

MSD counted 967,176 NZ Super recipients and 4,689 Veteran's Pension recipients in March 2026, so changes to current entitlements would affect a very large current cohort unless grandfathered [MSD NZS/VP data tables, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx). The same MSD workbook records 514,044 female NZ Super recipients, 453,060 male recipients, 49,833 NZ Super recipients receiving Accommodation Supplement, and 122,349 receiving Disability Allowance in March 2026, so women, renters or high-housing-cost recipients, and disabled people are visible in the current recipient base [MSD NZS/VP data tables, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx).

Stats NZ's 2024-base national projections say the 65+ population was 0.87 million, or 17% of the population, in 2024 and has a 90% probability of rising to 1.44-1.55 million, or 21-24% of the population, in 2051, so reforms aimed at long-run fiscal pressure primarily affect future cohorts and current workers rather than only today's retirees [Stats NZ, 2024-base national population projections](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/national-population-projections-2024base2078/). NZIER frames the core pressure as pay-as-you-go funding: retiree incomes are funded by taxes on current workers, and population ageing changes the ratio of workers to retirees [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).

### Reform-path burden map

| Reform path | Who bears the direct cost or risk | Why those groups are exposed | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raise eligibility age, for example 65 to 67 | Future cohorts aged 65-66; Māori and Pacific peoples; physical/manual workers; disabled people; carers; people with weak labour-market attachment | NZIER says a 65-to-67 rise by 2050 would reduce NZ Super costs by about one-tenth, but lower-life-expectancy groups and physical labourers would be disproportionately affected; the Retirement Commission adds that an age rise does not address people unable to work longer because of health or caregiving responsibilities [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). | High for direction; Medium for size |
| Index to CPI or otherwise restrain rates | All current and future recipients over time; single recipients; renters; disabled people; people without private savings | NZIER says CPI-only indexation could reduce 2050 costs by about one-fifth but would lower retirees' relative living standards and increase poverty and reliance on other support; the Retirement Commission says just over 40% of people aged 66+ in one-person or couple households receive less than $100 weekly from non-government sources, and that 64% of one-person households receive less than $100 weekly from non-government sources [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). | High for direction; Medium for distribution |
| Income-test, surcharge, or higher tax on pensioners' other income | Older people with employment income, private pensions, investment income, or other taxable income; potentially current retirees if not grandfathered | NZIER says taxing pensioners' other income at higher rates could reduce net NZ Super costs by about one-tenth but would be complex and reduce incentives to work in old age; New Zealand previously had a superannuation surcharge that was abolished in 1998, showing this is a real historical lever rather than a theoretical one [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Treasury/MSD, current retirement income framework background paper](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/prg-msd-dnzcrif.pdf). | Medium |
| Asset-test or broader means-test | Older people with assessable housing, financial assets, business assets, or savings; potentially savers and homeowners depending on exemptions | NZIER says asset-based means-testing could affect incentives to save, and the Retirement Commission says wealth among seniors varies widely because mortgage-free homes and substantial KiwiSaver balances give much greater security than having no assets [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). | Medium |
| Tighten or extend residence eligibility | Migrants, returning New Zealanders, and future cohorts with interrupted residence histories | Work and Income says the residence requirement is already increasing from 10 to 20 years based on date of birth, while people who lack enough New Zealand residence may need to wait or use a country with a social security agreement or a Realm country to qualify [Work and Income, residence criteria change](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/nz-super-and-veterans-pension-residency-changes-2024.html); [Work and Income, who can get NZ Super](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html). | High |
| Raise general tax, reduce other spending, or borrow to preserve NZ Super | Current workers and taxpayers; younger and future cohorts; users of crowded-out public services | NZIER says leaving retirement income policy settings unchanged could mean raising tax revenue by around 5-10% per worker, reducing public services, or allowing debt above 100% of GDP; the NZ Super Fund describes its mandate as reducing the tax burden of superannuation costs on future generations [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [NZ Super Fund, purpose and mandate](https://nzsuperfund.nz/about-the-guardians/purpose-and-mandate/). | Medium |
| Increase pre-funding through the NZ Super Fund | Current taxpayers if contributions rise; future taxpayers if pre-funding succeeds; public services if contributions crowd out other spending | NZIER says collective pre-funding through taxation invested in a government fund is one route toward a more savings-based system, but the transition creates a double burden because working-age people pay for current retirees while pre-funding future retirees [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf). | Medium |
| Increase reliance on KiwiSaver/private saving through compulsion, higher contributions, or a smaller NZ Super role | Low-income workers; women and carers; Māori and Pacific peoples; self-employed people; temporary migrants; people with volatile earnings; people who suffer poor investment returns | NZIER says reducing the role of NZ Super and strengthening KiwiSaver could perpetuate inequalities; the Retirement Commission says KiwiSaver is voluntary and workplace-based, and groups including part-time workers, the self-employed, caregivers, Māori and Pacific peoples may not benefit equally [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). | High for direction; Medium for package effects |

### Groups needing explicit protection in any reform design

Renters and mortgage-holders are exposed because NZ Super was historically built around a "golden assumption" of mortgage-free home ownership, and the Retirement Commission says mortgage-free ownership among older New Zealanders has fallen from 83% in the mid-1990s to 71% in recent years, while low-income 65+ private renters receiving Accommodation Supplement had a 68% rate of spending more than 40% of income on housing in 2024 [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). NZIER similarly says 66% of senior households own outright, 14% pay a mortgage, 20% pay rent, less than half of Māori seniors and about one-quarter of Pacific seniors own outright, and rent or mortgage costs for seniors are likely to exceed 40% of the NZ Super payment [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).

Women and carers are exposed to private-saving reform because the Retirement Commission says women have lower KiwiSaver balances because of lower lifetime earnings, part-time work, and caregiving, and men had 25% more in their KiwiSaver accounts than women in 2024 [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). The same review says paid parental leave disrupts retirement saving, and only 12,390 of 57,635 2025 paid-parental-leave recipients continued contributing to KiwiSaver and qualified for the government parental-leave contribution [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

Māori and Pacific peoples are exposed on multiple dimensions: NZIER says Māori life expectancy at age 65 is about four years lower than non-Māori life expectancy, that Māori and Pacific peoples tend to have lower incomes and shorter life expectancies, and that Pākehā/European employees contribute about 40% more to KiwiSaver each year than Māori and Pacific employees [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf). The Retirement Commission also says only 47% of Māori aged 65+ reported having enough to meet their needs, compared with 72% of Pākehā, and that Pākehā employees contributed around 40% more to KiwiSaver annually than Māori and Pacific employees [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

Disabled people and people in arduous work are exposed because an age rise or delayed pension assumes capacity to keep earning. MSD recorded 122,349 NZ Super recipients receiving Disability Allowance in March 2026, and the Retirement Commission cites OECD evidence that one in five workers aged 50-64 leave employment because of ill health [MSD NZS/VP data tables, March 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/statistics/benefit/2026/quarterly-benefit-fact-sheets-nzs-and-vp-tables-march-2026.xlsx); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf). The Retirement Commission says OECD analysis points away from broad job-title early-retirement rules and toward individual assessments based on health status and work conditions, with stronger disability and sickness insurance where early retirement is appropriate [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

Self-employed people are exposed because KiwiSaver does not give them automatic enrolment or employer contributions. A Retirement Commission/Hnry paper says the 2023 Census identified more than 420,000 self-employed people in New Zealand, that 44% of self-employed survey participants were contributing KiwiSaver members in 2024-25 compared with 78% of employees, and that self-employed people who contribute average just 2.6% of annual income because there is no employer contribution [Retirement Commission and Hnry, 2025](https://threefold.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/Improving-the-retirement-savings-of-the-self-employed_Final.pdf).

Migrants are exposed through both NZ Super eligibility and private-saving access. Work and Income says people born on or after 1 July 1977 need 20 years of New Zealand residence from age 20, including 5 years from age 50, while the Retirement Commission says temporary visa holders are currently not eligible to join KiwiSaver even if they are working and contributing to New Zealand's economy [Work and Income, who can get NZ Super](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/eligibility/seniors/superannuation/who-can-get-it/index.html); [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A worked Treasury or Retirement Commission distributional model for a specific reform package, including transition dates, grandfathering, tax interactions, Accommodation Supplement, Disability Allowance, and behavioural responses, would change the relative ranking of who loses most [NZIER, 2025](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Aotearoa-New-Zealand-in-2050-FINAL-v3.pdf).
- Linked administrative microdata on KiwiSaver balances, housing tenure, disability, ethnicity, sex, caregiving history, and employment type would sharpen the private-saving risk assessment; the Retirement Commission says better KiwiSaver data linked with income, employment, caregiving and housing would help identify how life events affect retirement savings [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- Human review with older renters, disabled people, Māori and Pacific organisations, carers, manual workers, migrants, and self-employed workers is needed before converting this burden map into reform advice, because the cited sources identify risk groups but do not substitute for lived-experience or Treaty-informed policy design [Retirement Commission, 2025 review](https://assets.retirement.govt.nz/public/Uploads/Retirement-Income-Policy-Review/2025-RRIP/Review-of-Retirement-Income-Policies-2025_WEB.pdf).
- I could not verify exact dollar losses for renters, disabled recipients, manual workers, or carers under any one reform because the issue asks about plausible reform paths rather than a legislated package with parameters.

## Open follow-up questions

- What distributional model would show after-housing-cost income changes by ethnicity, sex, disability, tenure, and employment history for a 65-to-67 age rise with transitional support?
- How would a revived income surcharge compare with an asset test once administration costs, work incentives, saving incentives, and political durability are included?
- What minimum KiwiSaver data fields should be available to policymakers to test whether private-saving reform worsens or reduces retirement inequities?
