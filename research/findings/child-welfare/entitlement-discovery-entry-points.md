# Where NZ families go to find out what support they qualify for, and where the journey breaks down

## Executive answer

- **There is no single front door.** Families discover child/family support through a scatter of *per-agency* channels — Inland Revenue's Working for Families pages and eligibility tool, MSD/Work and Income's "Check what you might get" and A–Z benefits, the Ministry of Education's ECE pages, plus cross-agency signposts like govt.nz and SmartStart. Each covers its own slice; no official tool screens a family across all agencies at once. [Finding #24](family-entitlements-nz.md); [IRD, WFF eligibility tool](https://www.ird.govt.nz/working-for-families/can-you-get-it/eligibility-tool); [Work and Income, Check what you might get](https://check.msd.govt.nz/); [SmartStart](https://smartstart.services.govt.nz/); [govt.nz, Financial help for your family](https://www.govt.nz/browse/family-and-whanau/financial-help-for-your-family/)
- **Community intermediaries are a high-volume entitlement-navigation channel — though how large a *share* of discovery they carry, versus government self-service, is not measured here.** Citizens Advice Bureau handles "over 500,000 enquiries per annum … many of which relate to navigating government to get their entitlements," and ~482,000 client interactions in a single year; FinCap's network of 700+ financial mentors across ~180 services and Whānau Ora navigators help people access services, with mentors advocating on clients' behalf. Families lean on these because the official system is hard to navigate alone — but no source here compares community versus self-service discovery volume for families, so treat these as important navigation infrastructure, not a measured "primary" channel. [CAB Sprint Report, digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/94-citizens-advice-bureau-sprint-report/html); [CAB, Face to Face with Digital Exclusion, 2020](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf)); [FinCap, Financial mentoring](https://www.fincap.org.nz/financial-mentoring/); [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora)
- **The journey breaks down at four points — three named directly in the primary qualitative evidence, the fourth structural.** The Welfare Expert Advisory Group's 2019 review found: "The system was seen as complicated. Some families and whānau were not aware of their entitlements and obligations. Some with a high need for support avoided engaging with MSD and other agencies." That single sentence directly names three of the failure modes — **complexity, awareness, and stigma/avoidance**. [WEAG, Whakamana Tāngata, 2019](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/research/weag/index.html) ([archived page](http://web.archive.org/web/20250505221147/https://www.weag.govt.nz/weag-report/whakamana-tangata/fundamental-change-is-needed/some-groups-are-disproportionately-negatively-affected/)) The fourth, **fragmentation**, is not a WEAG quote but a structural fact — child/family support is split across IRD, MSD, and the Ministry of Education, each with its own separate eligibility tool (see Breakdown 4). [Finding #24](family-entitlements-nz.md)
- **Non-take-up data shows the breakdown is both "didn't know it existed" and "thought I wasn't eligible."** In MSD's Income Support Survey, among people who appeared potentially eligible for the Accommodation Supplement, of those not receiving it 32.3% were unaware of the payment, and of aware non-recipients 35.9% believed they were ineligible — so the gap is roughly split between an *awareness* failure and a *self-assessed-eligibility* failure, not one or the other. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf); [Finding #93](take-up-gap-child-family-entitlements.md)
- **A cross-cutting fifth barrier is digital exclusion plus the withdrawal of in-person channels.** As government moved to "digital-first," CAB recorded 4,379 client interactions involving digital exclusion in just three months, disproportionately Māori (20%) and Pacific (14%) clients, and reports it is "filling a gap left by the withdrawal of a number of government counter and face-to-face services" — meaning the very families most likely to miss support are the ones the discovery channels serve worst. [CAB, Face to Face with Digital Exclusion, 2020](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf))

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the four breakdown points are each stated in current, official, and independent NZ sources (WEAG, MSD, CAB), and the "no single front door / community-sector-fills-the-gap" picture is well evidenced. It is Medium not High because much of the *frontline* evidence (how families actually experience discovery) comes from one organisation's casework (CAB), some take-up figures are dated or single-origin, and no source isolates which barrier is *dominant* for child/family entitlements specifically.

## Evidence

### Where families actually go — a fragmented map, half official and half community

**Official entry points are real but each is single-agency.** Finding #24 established that child/family support is administered across at least three agencies, and each publishes its own discovery surface: IRD has a Working for Families eligibility tool [IRD](https://www.ird.govt.nz/working-for-families/can-you-get-it/eligibility-tool); MSD/Work and Income has "Check what you might get" and A–Z benefit pages [Work and Income](https://check.msd.govt.nz/); the Ministry of Education owns 20 Hours ECE. [Finding #24](family-entitlements-nz.md); [Work and Income, A–Z benefits](https://www.workandincome.govt.nz/products/a-z-benefits/index.html) There are two cross-agency signposts — govt.nz's "Financial help for your family" page and SmartStart for the perinatal period — but these link *out* to each agency rather than screening a family in one place. [govt.nz](https://www.govt.nz/browse/family-and-whanau/financial-help-for-your-family/); [SmartStart](https://smartstart.services.govt.nz/) The prior framing finding reached the same conclusion: the gap "is not 'do pages exist?', but which families do not reach the right page … or fall between Inland Revenue, MSD, Ministry of Education, and community-service entry points." [Finding, family-support-navigation-framing](family-support-navigation-framing.md)

**Community intermediaries are a high-volume navigation channel.** The Citizens Advice Bureau's own account to government is that it fields "over 500,000 enquiries per annum where they assist customers with a wide range of issues, many of which relate to navigating government to get their entitlements or comply with regulations." [CAB Sprint Report, digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/dmsdocument/94-citizens-advice-bureau-sprint-report/html) CAB's 2020 report records ~482,000 client interactions in a year and describes volunteers as "on the frontline of helping people access their rights and entitlements." [CAB, 2020, Foreword](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf)) CAB even publishes a plain-language "How do I find out what benefits or payments I can get" guide, which itself illustrates that families cannot easily self-diagnose across the agency split. [CAB, KB00001845](https://www.cab.org.nz/article/KB00001845)

Other established community channels do the same brokering:
- **Financial mentors (FinCap / MoneyTalks):** "more than 700 financial mentors serving their communities from about 180 financial mentoring services," offering free, confidential support. Mentors "advocate for clients by negotiating with creditors, resolving debts, assisting in disputes," and the MoneyTalks helpline "connect[s] people with financial mentors and services to help them out of financial hardship." (These pages describe a brokerage/advocacy role but do not itemise entitlement-specific WINZ/IRD work, so I do not claim that specific.) [FinCap, Financial mentoring](https://www.fincap.org.nz/financial-mentoring/); [MoneyTalks](https://www.moneytalks.co.nz/)
- **Whānau Ora navigators:** practitioners who "work with whānau and families to identify their needs … and link and coordinate access to a range of other services or programmes" — an explicitly whānau-centred brokerage model, especially for Māori families. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Te Puni Kōkiri, Understanding whānau-centred approaches](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/documents/download/1025/understanding-whanau-centred-approaches.pdf)
- **Community Law:** publishes a step-by-step "how to apply for a benefit" manual and provides free advocacy for dealing with Work and Income. [Community Law Manual, Ch 23](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-manual/chapter-23-dealing-with-work-and-income/how-to-apply-for-a-benefit/some-general-requirements-for-benefits/)

### Breakdown 1 — Awareness: families don't know a support exists, or that they qualify

The WEAG consensus review (which consulted over 3,000 New Zealanders) found bluntly that "Some families and whānau were not aware of their entitlements and obligations." [WEAG, 2019](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/research/weag/index.html) ([archived](http://web.archive.org/web/20250505221147/https://www.weag.govt.nz/weag-report/whakamana-tangata/fundamental-change-is-needed/some-groups-are-disproportionately-negatively-affected/)) The take-up literature quantifies it: in MSD's Income Support Survey, of people apparently eligible for the Accommodation Supplement but not receiving it, 32.3% simply did not know the payment existed. [MSD Findings Pack 7, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf) Awareness is also unevenly distributed: the Ministry for Ethnic Communities' 2024 evidence report notes (citing Stats NZ) that only ~32% of New Zealand's unemployed people were enrolled for Jobseeker Support in 2021, and just 21% of unemployed Asian people, attributing lower uptake partly to "awareness of eligibility due to language barriers and accessibility of information, and cultural norms." [Ministry for Ethnic Communities, Ethnic Evidence Report 2024](https://www.ethniccommunities.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/63545/mecethnicevidencereport2024.pdf) ([archived PDF](http://web.archive.org/web/20260623132320/https://www.ethniccommunities.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/63545/mecethnicevidencereport2024.pdf))

### Breakdown 2 — Complexity: the "self-assessed ineligibility" trap and a system experienced as complicated

Even families who reach the right agency often mis-diagnose their own eligibility. In the same MSD survey, of *aware* Accommodation Supplement non-recipients, 35.9% believed they were ineligible (most because they thought they earned too much). [MSD Findings Pack 7, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf) This matters because Working for Families abates gradually and many entitlements have non-obvious thresholds, so "I earn too much" is frequently wrong. [Finding #24](family-entitlements-nz.md) WEAG's own words: "The system was seen as complicated." [WEAG, 2019](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/research/weag/index.html) The eligibility-precheck research in this stream reached the same point from the design side — many variables are discretionary or evidence-based and cannot be safely self-assessed. [Finding, eligibility-precheck-variables](eligibility-precheck-variables.md)

### Breakdown 3 — Stigma and trust: some families actively avoid the system

WEAG found that "Some with a high need for support avoided engaging with MSD and other agencies," describing the experience of the system as "unpleasant, time consuming, humiliating and frustrating," and noted that for some, needing support "is seen as shameful for individuals and the wider family. This shame serves as a barrier for people accessing the welfare system and contributes to engagement with predatory lenders who are seen as more approachable." [WEAG, 2019](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/research/weag/index.html) ([archived](http://web.archive.org/web/20250505221147/https://www.weag.govt.nz/weag-report/whakamana-tangata/fundamental-change-is-needed/some-groups-are-disproportionately-negatively-affected/)) Independent reporting corroborates the reluctance-to-approach barrier, particularly for migrant families: a Belong Aotearoa account describes people who "are hesitant to approach a Work and Income office" and lack familiarity with a welfare system unlike their country of origin. [RNZ, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/chinese/563621/unemployed-asians-battle-lazy-stigma-in-silence)

### Breakdown 4 — Fragmentation: falling between agencies

Because support is split across IRD, MSD, and the Ministry of Education — and childcare help *alone* is split three ways (MoE 20 Hours ECE, MSD childcare payments, IRD FamilyBoost) — a family's full entitlement rarely sits behind one door, one login, or one conversation. [Finding #24](family-entitlements-nz.md) A parent who qualifies for a Work and Income payment is not automatically told about an IRD credit, and vice versa; the existence of separate eligibility tools at each agency is the structural signature of this fragmentation. [IRD, WFF eligibility tool](https://www.ird.govt.nz/working-for-families/can-you-get-it/eligibility-tool); [Work and Income, Check what you might get](https://check.msd.govt.nz/) The prior framing finding could not locate "a single official cross-agency machine-readable ruleset for all child/family supports." [Finding, family-support-navigation-framing](family-support-navigation-framing.md)

### Cross-cutting — digital exclusion concentrates the harm on the families most at risk

The move to digital-first delivery adds a barrier that lands hardest on already-disadvantaged families. CAB's 2020 spotlight report, built from 4,379 recorded digital-exclusion interactions across more than 80 locations in a three-month period, found digital exclusion spread across all age groups (not just the elderly), with poverty the biggest driver and Māori (20%) and Pacific (14%) clients over-represented. [CAB, 2020](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf)) CAB documents specific agencies closing counters and removing paper channels, concluding that "the systems of government impede access to entitlements," and that the community sector is "filling a gap left by the withdrawal of a number of government counter and face-to-face services." [CAB, 2020](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf)) This is why the discovery problem cannot be solved by "publish a better webpage" alone — online-only discovery serves the most digitally-excluded families (over-represented among Māori, Pacific, and low-income clients) worst. Whether these are the *same* families who carry the largest child/family entitlement take-up gap is plausible, but the sources here do not establish that link — CAB documents *who* is digitally excluded, not that digital exclusion is the dominant driver of the highest non-take-up group.

### The community sector fills the gap — but its own capacity is unmeasured here

The consistent picture is that community intermediaries are important, high-volume infrastructure for entitlement navigation. Whether they have the capacity, coverage, and funding to actually close the gap is a separate question this finding does *not* answer — CAB itself reported it was "struggling to survive financially" while doing this work. [CAB, 2020, Foreword](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf) ([archived PDF](https://web.archive.org/web/20260218144939/https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/Face-to-Face-with-Digital-Exclusion-/FINAL_CABNZ-report_Face-to-face-with-Digital-Exclusion.pdf)) I have split that into a dedicated research issue (#173). [Issue #173](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/173)

## What would change this conclusion

- **Direct evidence on how families with children experience discovery.** Almost all the "breakdown" evidence here is either about the working-age benefit population broadly (WEAG, Income Support Survey, Jobseeker) or about CAB's whole client base — not specifically about parents seeking child/family entitlements. A qualitative study of families' actual discovery journeys (or MSD/IRD user-research) could re-weight which barrier dominates, or surface barriers not captured here (documentation burden, overpayment/debt fear, distrust after a bad experience). **This needs a human with lived experience or frontline navigation experience** — it cannot be settled from public documents.
- **A newer or child-specific take-up breakdown.** The 32.3%/35.9% awareness-vs-eligibility split is from one MSD survey of Accommodation Supplement recipients, not child/family payments; a Working for Families or Best Start "reasons for non-take-up" study could change the balance between the four breakdown points. [MSD Findings Pack 7, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)
- **Evidence that the official cross-agency signposts (govt.nz, SmartStart) work better than assumed.** If user data showed families routinely complete a cross-agency discovery journey through these, the "no single front door" framing would soften. I could not find usage/outcome data for those signposts. [govt.nz](https://www.govt.nz/browse/family-and-whanau/financial-help-for-your-family/); [SmartStart](https://smartstart.services.govt.nz/)
- **Community-channel capacity data (split to #173).** The claim that the community sector "fills the gap" only holds if those channels have headroom; CAB's own funding fragility suggests they may not. That is now a separate research question. [Issue #173](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/173)
- **What I could not verify:** (a) the full WEAG report PDF text — the primary `weag.govt.nz` site now 302-redirects to an MSD index and the archived full PDF was truncated on retrieval, so WEAG quotes are cited from the National Library / Wayback-archived HTML content pages (fetched and read successfully); (b) the Ministry for Ethnic Communities PDF returns HTTP 403 to plain HTTP fetchers and to WebFetch (bot protection, not a dead link), so it is cited via a working Wayback snapshot; (c) the underlying Stats NZ page for the 32% figure would not return body text to WebFetch, so that figure rests on the MEC report's citation of it; (d) the FinCap and MoneyTalks pages describe mentors' advocacy/brokerage role in general terms (negotiating with creditors, connecting to services) but do not itemise routine WINZ/IRD entitlement-correction work, so an earlier, stronger version of that claim was removed; and (e) no source I found quantifies the *share* of family entitlement discovery that flows through community channels versus government self-service, so the community-channel claims are framed as "high-volume/important," not "primary."

## Open follow-up questions

- **#173:** Do NZ community navigation channels (CAB, financial mentors, Whānau Ora) have the capacity and coverage to close the entitlement-discovery gap? [Issue #173](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/173)
- What does *child/family-specific* user research (MSD/IRD, or an NGO) say about where the discovery journey breaks down — and which of the four barriers dominates for parents specifically? (Needs a human with data access or lived experience.)
- What is the measured effect of the cross-agency signposts (govt.nz "Financial help for your family," SmartStart) — do families actually complete cross-agency discovery through them?
- For Māori and Pacific families with the highest material-hardship rates, does the Whānau Ora navigator model close the discovery gap more effectively than mainstream channels, and what is its reach? [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Stats NZ, child poverty year ended June 2025](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/child-poverty-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/)
