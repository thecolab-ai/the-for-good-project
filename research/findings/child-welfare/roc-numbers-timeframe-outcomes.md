# The numbers: NZ reports of concern — volume, professional-vs-public and further-action split, response-timeframe compliance, and care outcomes (aggregate only)

This finding assembles the **aggregate official statistics** on the reports-of-concern (ROC) pipeline for Stream #259 (see the framing doc [`nz-child-protection-gaps-framing.md`](nz-child-protection-gaps-framing.md), which flagged these exact fields as needing primary data). It uses **only published aggregate figures** — Oranga Tamariki's quarterly performance reports, its June 2025 ROC insights research, and the Independent Children's Monitor's outcomes reporting. **No case detail, no personal data, no small cells.** Where a field is only available in restricted IDI microdata or is not published at aggregate at all, that is flagged explicitly.

## Executive answer

- **Volume & trend:** Oranga Tamariki received **95,422 reports of concern for 59,391 distinct tamariki in calendar 2024 — a 34.5% (+24,494) jump on 2023.** This reverses a prior decline (ROC volumes fell ~28% between FY2018 and FY2022, then rose 5% in 2023). OT attributes **at least ~24%** of the 2024 jump to an internal recording-practice change (July 2024), ~5% to two specific events, and "the vast majority" of the remainder to broad social/economic factors it reads as consistent with rising actual harm. Quarterly volumes have since plateaued at a high level (~27,800–27,900/quarter through Q2 2025/26). [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf); [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf)
- **Professional-vs-public split:** intake is **professional-dominated** — Police, Education and Health together make up **~60%** of all ROCs, Police alone **~30–32%**, and Courts **<1.5%**; all notifier groups rose in 2024 with proportions stable to within 2%. But a single clean "professional vs public/family" percentage is **not published** in these aggregate sources (family/whānau and "other" are separate notifier lines, not a summed "public" total) — a precise two-way split needs an OT data request or IDI. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf)
- **Further-action vs no-further-action:** roughly **40% of ROCs require further action from a social worker** (Q2 2025/26: 9,821 of 27,866), i.e. a **majority do not** proceed to a statutory further-action response. The Monitor reported that **nationally 42% of 2023/24 ROCs resulted in no further action**, rising to **45% for health-sector notifiers and 44% for NGOs** (education was lower at 35%) — and flagged "a high proportion of reports of concern from professionals that do not result in further action." Whether that reflects appropriate triage or missed risk is **not resolvable from these aggregates**. [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki, *Reports of concern and early intervention* (2023/24 outcomes)](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-23-24/roc-intervention)
- **Timeframe compliance (the headline gap):** the statutory-style response target — respond to **critical ROCs within 24 hours and very urgent within 48 hours, at ≥95%** — is **being missed and worsening**. Compliance fell from **89% (Q1 2024/25) → 86% (Q2) → 85% (Q1 2025/26) → 80% (Q2 2025/26, Oct–Dec 2025)**, "generally between 80 and 93 percent" and "consistently falls short." OT marks this **"Off-track"** in its own report, citing high volumes and workforce pressure; Education is the single biggest source of critical/very-urgent ROCs (35%). [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf); [RNZ, "Oranga Tamariki reports it is 'off-track'…" (Q1 2025/26)](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/589522/oranga-tamariki-reports-it-is-off-track-dealing-with-critical-reports-of-concern)
- **Care outcomes:** despite the ROC surge, downstream volumes are **flat or falling** — Family Group Conference volumes stayed "relatively stable" 2022–2024; entries to care rose (+15% 2022→2023, +20% 2023→2024) but by Q2 2025/26 "**fewer children entered and exited care**" (342 entries that quarter; **4,077 children in care at 31 Dec 2025**). The one care-quality measure OT reports as **on-track** is social-worker visiting (**96–97%** of children in care visited within 8 weeks vs a 95% target). **Longer-term recovery/wellbeing outcomes after a ROC are not published at aggregate** — only substantiated-finding proportions and care entry/exit counts are. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf); [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** **High** for the published figures themselves — volume, the ~40% further-action rate, the timeframe-compliance decline, and care counts are all lifted directly from OT's own performance reports and the Monitor's outcomes reporting, and the load-bearing ones are double-sourced. It is **not** High that these numbers *mean* the system is failing children: the NFA rate, the professional-report NFA share, and the timeframe misses are inputs to that judgement, not proof of it, and OT itself notes some of the volume rise reflects *better recording*, not more harm. That judgement needs a human with authority (framing doc, Constitution Art. III).

## Evidence

### 1. Volume and trend

In calendar **2024, Oranga Tamariki received 95,422 reports of concern for 59,391 distinct tamariki — a 34.5% increase (+24,494) on 2023.** This followed a **~28% decrease between FY2018 and FY2022** and a **5% rise from 2022 to 2023** (citing Takada, 2024). [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.3, 8 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) The ~95,000 figure and "~35% more than 2023" were also reported contemporaneously. [RNZ, "Oranga Tamariki gets 95,000 reports…"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/545365/oranga-tamariki-gets-95-000-reports-about-possible-at-risk-children)

OT's own decomposition of the 2024 jump: a **recording-practice change** in July 2024 (all concerns now logged as ROCs rather than as contact records / "additional information") accounts for **at least ~24%**; the Te Pūkāea community call centre in Whakatāne (**~3%**) and a single mass-allegation investigation in Canterbury/Lower South (**~2%**) add ~5%; and "the vast majority" of the remainder is spread evenly across demographics, regions, notifier types and urgency, which OT reads as **general social/economic factors** consistent with rising actual harm. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.3–4, 8 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) This is the crucial caveat on the headline: **part of the "increase" is an artefact of better recording, not necessarily more children in danger** — OT says so directly.

Quarterly volumes have since **plateaued at a high level**: Q1 2025/26 = 27,813 and Q2 2025/26 (Oct–Dec 2025) = 27,866 ROCs, up ~57–64% on the same quarters of 2023/24 (17,731 and 16,988) and ~1–3% above 2024/25 — surpassing forecast demand for two consecutive quarters. [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, pp.3–4 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf) OT's demand outlook rates ROCs to OT as **LIKELY to increase** and system responsiveness as **ALMOST CERTAIN to be negatively impacted** over 1–2 years. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.4–5, 25 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf)

**Confidence: High.** OT's own research and quarterly reports agree; the volume is double-sourced. The *interpretation* of what drove the rise is OT's own and appropriately hedged by OT.

### 2. Professional-vs-public split (notifier mix)

OT classifies notifiers into categories: Police-FV, Police-Other, Education, Health, NGO, Court, Other Govt, Family, and Other. Across 2022–2024:

- **Police, Education and Health together ≈ 60% of all ROCs**, a share that "has remained largely unchanged."
- **Police (both categories) ≈ 30–32%** of annual ROCs; "police-other" saw the largest numeric rise.
- **Courts are the smallest, < 1.5%.**
- **All notifier types rose** in 2024, with each group's proportion stable to within 2%. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.9–10 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf)

So the intake is **professional-dominated**. **But there is no published single "professional vs public/family" percentage** in these aggregate sources: "Family" and "Other" are individual notifier lines, not a summed public total, and OT does not report a professional/non-professional dichotomy. Constructing one precisely would need an OT data request or IDI. A behavioural nuance OT flags: in some regions professionals report to Police rather than OT "as they feel the NZ Police are more responsive," which can move a report between notifier categories. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, p.23 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf)

**Confidence: High** on the notifier shares as published; **the clean two-way professional/public split is a documented data gap**, not a number I can state.

### 3. Further-action vs no-further-action split

In **Q2 2025/26, 40% of the 27,866 ROCs (9,821) required further action** from a social worker — meaning a **majority did not** proceed to a statutory further-action response (the remainder are "no further action," "additional information," "partnered response" or "not applicable"). [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, p.5 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf) The Independent Children's Monitor put **national no-further-action at 42% of ~75,000 ROCs in 2023/24**, and — critically for the "nobody could help them" pattern — broke it down by notifier: **45% of health-sector reports, 44% of NGO reports, and 35% of education reports resulted in no further action.** [Aroturuki Tamariki, *Reports of concern and early intervention* (2023/24 outcomes)](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-23-24/roc-intervention) The Monitor separately flagged "a high proportion of reports of concern from professionals that do not result in further action by Oranga Tamariki and where tamariki and rangatahi are not seen." [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, p.23 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) (corroborated as a Monitor finding in [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report))

OT's own research explicitly warns that a rising NFA count is **ambiguous**: it can reflect capacity saturation ("if there is capacity to record an increase in initial reports of concern correctly but not undertake an assessment, then findings of 'no further action' may increase") *or* appropriate triage of reports that never needed a statutory response. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.4, 28 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf)

**Confidence: High** on the published rates (double-sourced: OT ~40% further-action, Monitor 42% NFA); **the meaning of the NFA rate is expressly unresolved** in the sources.

### 4. Response-timeframe compliance — the headline shortfall

OT assigns each ROC an urgency: **critical (respond within 24 hours), very urgent (within 48 hours), urgent (within 10 working days).** [OT Practice Centre, ROC response timeframe](https://practice.orangatamariki.govt.nz/core-practice/practice-tools/intake-decision-response-tool/report-of-concern-response-timeframe); [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, p.17 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) The published performance measure — "**the percentage of critical or very urgent reports of concern addressed within operational timeframes will be at least 95%**" — has been **missed every recent quarter and is deteriorating**:

| Quarter | Critical/very-urgent within timeframe | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 2024/25 | 89% | below 95% target |
| Q2 2024/25 | 86% | below target |
| Q1 2025/26 (Jul–Sep 2025) | 85% (down from 86%) | **Off-track** |
| Q2 2025/26 (Oct–Dec 2025) | **80%** (a further 5-point fall) | **Off-track** |

OT reports the trend "generally between 80 and 93 percent" but "consistently falls short of the target of 95 percent," and marks it **"Off-track"** in its own dashboard, attributing the miss to high ROC volumes, workforce vacancies, email-referral delays, rural sites, and recording accuracy; **Education is the single biggest notifier of critical/very-urgent ROCs (35%).** [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, pp.3, 7–8 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf); [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, p.17 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) Independent reporting confirms the Q1 2025/26 point (85%, down from 86%, "OFF TRACK" in red capitals; the 24-hour/48-hour definitions; ~10% below target). [RNZ, "…'off-track' dealing with critical reports of concern"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/589522/oranga-tamariki-reports-it-is-off-track-dealing-with-critical-reports-of-concern)

Two caveats to state plainly: (a) this measure covers **critical + very urgent only** — timeframe compliance for the much larger "urgent / 10-working-day" tier is **not** published as a headline measure; and (b) it is an "addressed within timeframe" operational measure, **not** a measure of whether the child was ultimately safe.

**Confidence: High.** Double-sourced (OT primary + RNZ), consistent, and OT self-classifies it as off-track.

### 5. Care outcomes

Downstream of the ROC surge, statutory-intervention volumes are **flat or falling**, not rising with reports:

- **Family Group Conferences** stayed "relatively stable" 2022–2024 — the ROC increase "has not resulted in a greater number of care and protection family group conferences." Q2 2025/26: **1,774 children had a C&P FGC.** [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, pp.18–19 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf); [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, p.5 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf)
- **Entries to care** rose +15% (2022→2023) then +20% (2023→2024); **exits** fell 1.5% then rose 7%. [OT, *Understanding Reports of Concern*, June 2025, p.19 (PDF)](https://www.ot.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Research/Latest-research/Understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/Reports-of-concern-2024-increase-and-future-demand.pdf) But most recently, "despite the higher volumes of Reports of Concern, **fewer children entered and exited care**" — Q2 2025/26 saw **342 children enter care**, and **4,077 children and young people were in care at 31 December 2025.** [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, p.5 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf)
- **Care-quality (on-track):** social-worker visiting is the measure OT consistently meets — **96–97%** of children in care visited by a social worker within the last 8 weeks (target 95%). [OT Q2 2025/26 Performance Report, p.7 (PDF)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Quarterly-report/May-2026/2025-26-Quarter-2-Oranga-Tamariki-Performance-Report.pdf)
- **Annual context:** in 2024/25 OT reported completing over 44,000 investigations, more than 10,000 FGCs, and supporting 4,000+ children in care, with 95% visited at least eight-weekly. [OT, 2025 Annual Report summary](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/)

**What is *not* published at aggregate:** longer-term **recovery / wellbeing outcomes after a ROC** (e.g. re-notification-free rates, harm-recurrence, life outcomes). OT publishes substantiated-finding *proportions* and care entry/exit counts, but linked longitudinal outcome tracking sits in the **restricted IDI microdata** (accessed via a Stats NZ Data Lab code module, not a public dataset). [Stats NZ Integrated Data Commons, "Oranga Tamariki — Reports of Concern (ROC)"](https://idcommons.discourse.group/pub/oranga-tamariki-reports-of-concern-roc) The Monitor's Māori-outcomes reporting shows Māori overrepresentation persists (52% / ~39,400 of 2023/24 ROCs concerned tamariki Māori; Māori more likely to be referred for assessment). [Aroturuki Tamariki, *Reports of concern and early intervention* (2023/24 outcomes)](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-23-24/roc-intervention)

**Confidence: High** on the published counts; the recovery/wellbeing-outcome gap is a documented limitation.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A different reading of the volume rise.** If a future OT or Monitor analysis showed the 2024 jump was *mostly* the recording-practice change (rather than "at least ~24%" of it), the "reporting workload is surging" framing would soften toward "the same demand, better recorded." OT's ~24% figure is single-sourced (its own estimate) and it says the true recording-driven share is larger but unquantifiable — this is the weakest link in the volume story and is marked Medium.
- **The NFA and timeframe numbers are inputs, not verdicts.** A ~40% further-action rate and an 80% timeframe-compliance figure do **not** by themselves show children were failed: some NFA decisions are correct triage, and "addressed within timeframe" is an operational metric, not a safety outcome. Establishing whether these misses caused harm needs case-level review by people with clinical/legal authority — which this aggregate work deliberately does not do (framing doc; Constitution Art. III). If the Monitor's care-standards and safety-of-children-in-care reporting showed the NFA cohort was not experiencing measurable harm, the "gap" reading would weaken.
- **Could not verify / not publicly available:** (a) a clean **professional-vs-public** two-way percentage; (b) **timeframe compliance for the 10-working-day "urgent" tier** (only critical+very-urgent is a headline measure); (c) **per-notifier NFA over a time series** (only 2023/24 cross-section found); (d) **longitudinal post-ROC recovery/wellbeing outcomes** — these sit in restricted **IDI microdata**, not public aggregate. A researcher with a Stats NZ Data Lab project could reproduce (a)/(c)/(d) with small-cell suppression; this finding did not access the IDI.
- **Figures are point-in-time.** Quarterly numbers update ~quarterly; the 80% Q2 2025/26 figure was the latest published (report dated May 2026) at the access date and will move. All PDFs were fetched live and text-extracted; if OT restructures its measures, the compliance series may not be directly comparable across future quarters.

## Open follow-up questions
