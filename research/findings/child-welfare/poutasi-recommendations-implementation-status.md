# The government accepted all 14 Poutasi recommendations (Cabinet agreed Sept 2025, announced Oct 2025; further Cabinet decisions Dec 2025), but implementation is early-stage and the Monitor says the system has not materially changed

## Executive answer

- **The recommendations.** Dame Karen Poutasi's 2022 review of the children's sector — commissioned after the 2021 death of a five-year-old whose sole parent was in prison — identified **five critical gaps** and made **14 recommendations** to close them, ranging from vetting/checking carers of children whose sole parent is incarcerated (recs 1–2, 6), to multi-agency community teams (rec 3), linked health records (rec 4), **mandatory reporting of suspected abuse, legislated, with a supporting training/guidance package (recs 8–9)**, ECE child-protection-policy monitoring (rec 10), and an Independent Children's Monitor review after 12 months (rec 14). [MSD Cabinet paper — Appendix A: the 14 recommendations (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/appendix-a-dame-karen-poutasi-review-recommendations-to-address-gaps-in-the-children-s-system.pdf)
- **Formal acceptance: Cabinet agreed in September, announced in October.** After progressively responding since 2023, **the Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee agreed to accept all 14 recommendations on 17 September 2025** (minute SOU-25-MIN-0128, ref CAB-25-MIN-0330), and the same minute noted the Minister "intends to announce the Government acceptance … in October 2025." The Minister **publicly announced the acceptance on 9 October 2025**. The government adopted an "integrated all-of-Government" approach overseen by Child and Youth Ministers, counting at that point **two recommendations complete (11 and 14) and four underway (3, 4, 5, 10)**, with the rest to be implemented via four workstreams. [Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee minute SOU-25-MIN-0128 (17 Sep 2025, PDF)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/sou-25-min-0128-minute.pdf); [Beehive: "Strengthened system protects children from harm", 9 Oct 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/strengthened-system-protects-children-harm); [MSD Cabinet paper: Update on Government Response to the Poutasi Review (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf)
- **Mandatory reporting (recs 8–9) is the slowest and needs legislation.** It has **not started as a reporting regime**. Government is taking a deliberately **stepped approach — mandatory *training* first, mandatory *reporting* later** — to avoid flooding an under-resourced system. The return to Cabinet, foreshadowed for November 2025, happened on **10 December 2025**: the Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee (minute SOU-25-MIN-0178, released in MSD's 30 January 2026 "Next Steps" proactive release) **agreed to roll out foundational mandatory child-protection training from January 2026** to a prototype cohort of "approximately 500 core children's workers." The accompanying paper states "the training regime is our current implementation focus" and that its proposals "have no direct legislative implications" — i.e. the legislation mandatory *reporting* requires is still ahead. As of February 2026 the Monitor still graded both recs 8 and 9 **"Not achieved."** [MSD Cabinet paper: Next Steps on Government Response to the Poutasi Review (2026)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf); [Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee minute SOU-25-MIN-0178 (10 Dec 2025, PDF)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/sou-25-min-0178-minute.pdf); [MSD Cabinet paper (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki — second review, 18 Feb 2026 (PDF)](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025-FINAL-web.pdf)
- **The Monitor's verdict is that settings have not materially changed.** In its **second implementation review (18 February 2026)** the Independent Children's Monitor concluded: *"System settings have not changed, gaps remain and tamariki and rangatahi are still no more likely to be seen by Oranga Tamariki now than when [the child] was killed."* (the deceased child is named in the source; neutralised here) It counted **only two of the 14 recommendations complete**, and recorded that **24 further tamariki were killed by someone meant to be caring for them between December 2021 and June 2025.** This echoes its first review (August 2024), which found "tamariki are no safer now than when [the child] died." [Aroturuki Tamariki — second review (PDF), 18 Feb 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025-FINAL-web.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki — first review (PDF), Aug 2024](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/poutasi/Review-of-implementation-of-Poutasi-recommendations.pdf)
- **"Accepted" ≠ "measurably safer."** The government and the Monitor count progress differently, and the gap between them *is* the answer to this question: government reports **process milestones** (an inter-agency hub stood up, a training module piloted, ECE licensing changes) and by May 2026 claimed 3 of 14 complete; the Monitor grades against **outcomes** and finds most recommendations "Not achieved" and children no more visible or safer. The Minister herself conceded there had been "unacceptable delays." [Beehive: "Progress on improvements to children's system", 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system); [RNZ: "'Unacceptable' delays to improve child protection system", 11 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/586533/unacceptable-delays-to-improve-child-protection-system-government)

**Overall confidence:** High — the recommendation list, the government's acceptance and its own status counts, the legislative dependency of mandatory reporting, and the Monitor's critical verdict are all drawn from primary documents (the MSD Cabinet paper, Beehive releases, and the Monitor's own review PDFs), and the load-bearing figures are independently corroborated. It is not "Very High" only because implementation status is a fast-moving target and the counting of "complete" differs between government and the Monitor (explained below).

*No case detail is used. The deceased child's name is neutralised to "[the child]" everywhere in this finding's own prose, quotes and link labels; it remains only inside the raw source URLs, which are left intact as the genuine links. This finding does not analyse any individual case.*

## Evidence

### The 14 recommendations, grouped by the five critical gaps

Poutasi's review ("Joint Review into the Children's Sector: Identification and response to suspected abuse", reported to Cabinet December 2022) grouped its 14 recommendations under five gaps. Verbatim/near-verbatim from the government's own published Appendix A: [MSD Cabinet paper — Appendix A (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/appendix-a-dame-karen-poutasi-review-recommendations-to-address-gaps-in-the-children-s-system.pdf); original report: [Final report by Dame Karen Poutasi (2022, PDF, Oranga Tamariki)](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Reviews-and-Inquiries/System-review-Dame-Karen-Poutasi/CAB-22-MIN-0540-Final-report-by-Dame-Karen-Poutasi-on-the-death-of-Malachi-Subecz.pdf)

- **Gap 1 — needs of a dependent child when a sole parent is charged/prosecuted:** (1) Oranga Tamariki engaged in **vetting a carer** when a sole parent is arrested/taken into custody; (2) Oranga Tamariki **regular follow-up checks and support** for that carer; (6) MSD to **notify Oranga Tamariki** when a non-guardian caregiver of a child whose caregiver is in prison seeks a benefit or emergency housing.
- **Gap 2 — risk assessment too narrow:** (3) **Multi-agency teams** in communities with iwi/NGOs, resourced nationwide; (4) **link medical records** across the health sector; (5) add the **health sector to the Child Protection Protocol** between Police and Oranga Tamariki.
- **Gap 3 — agencies not proactively sharing information:** (7) **enhance understanding of the information-sharing regime** in the Oranga Tamariki Act 1989.
- **Gap 4 — under-reporting by some professionals:** (8) **mandate professionals to report suspected abuse, legislated**, defining "mandatory reporters"; (9) support mandatory reporting with a **package** — a reporting guide and a "High Report of Concern" category (modelled on NSW's "Risk of Significant Harm"), defined mandatory reporters who all receive regular training, undergraduate teaching of abuse signs, and mandatory refresher training with practising certificates conditional on it.
- **Gap 5 — the system allowed the child to remain invisible:** (10) **active monitoring by Education/ERO of ECE child-protection policies**; (11) **define the children's-system agencies in legislation**; (12) give those agencies a **statutory shared responsibility** for checking child safety; (13) **regular public-awareness campaigns** ("don't look away"); (14) the **Independent Children's Monitor to review implementation after 12 months** in its new system-wide role.

**Confidence: High** — quoted from the government's published Appendix A and the original report.

### Formal acceptance (October 2025) — late, and framed as "integrated"

The government had been responding to the recommendations since 2023. The **Cabinet decision** to accept all 14 was taken by the Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee on **17 September 2025**: minute SOU-25-MIN-0128 records that the committee "agreed to accept all recommendations from the Poutasi Review," noted two complete (11 and 14) and four underway (3, 4, 5, 10), and separately "noted that the Minister for Child Poverty Reduction intends to announce the Government acceptance of the recommendations, progress to date, and next steps in October 2025." [SOU-25-MIN-0128 minute (17 Sep 2025, PDF)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/sou-25-min-0128-minute.pdf) The **public announcement** followed on **9 October 2025**, when Child Poverty Reduction Minister Louise Upston said "the Government has accepted all the review's recommendations" and this "marks a clear line that child safety comes first." [Beehive, 9 Oct 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/strengthened-system-protects-children-harm) The accompanying Cabinet paper recommended Cabinet "agree to accept all recommendations" and recorded that, of the 14: **"one is complete (recommendation 11); one is complete but will be repeated (recommendation 14); and four are underway (recommendations 3, 4, 5, and 10)."** The remaining recommendations were organised into four workstreams — mandatory training/reporting (recs 8, 9, 13); identifying children of incarcerated sole parents (recs 1, 2, 6); information-sharing (rec 7); and collective accountability (rec 12) — with the Minister to "return to Cabinet in November 2025 to seek in principle policy decisions." [MSD Cabinet paper (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf) That return slipped to **10 December 2025** (SOU-25-MIN-0178, see the mandatory-reporting section below).

Two notable caveats in the government's own paper: **rec 12** (statutory shared responsibility in founding legislation) "is not possible to implement in the terms envisaged … because not all children's agencies have founding legislation"; and the paper states its proposals have "no direct legislative implications" at that stage — i.e. the legislation that mandatory reporting requires was still ahead, not in train. [MSD Cabinet paper (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf)

**Confidence: High** — Beehive release plus the primary Cabinet paper.

### Mandatory reporting and training (recs 8–9): the slowest reform, gated by legislation

Mandatory reporting is the reform the issue rightly flags as slow-moving. The government's position, stated in the Cabinet paper: introducing a mandatory-reporting regime "will be a significant change," and while international (Australian) evidence shows mandatory reporting "can significantly increase substantiated case identification" for child sexual abuse, "total reporting volumes can also increase significantly, putting pressure on systems." To mitigate that it is taking "a stepped implementation approach, **beginning with mandatory child protection training** for designated workforces," with reporting to follow once agencies can respond. [MSD Cabinet paper (2025)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2025/update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-update-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf) The Minister repeated this sequencing publicly: "mandatory training is our first step" and the government "will then move to introduce mandatory reporting" after considering other jurisdictions. [RNZ, 11 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/586533/unacceptable-delays-to-improve-child-protection-system-government)

The **December 2025 Cabinet decisions** put this on the record. On **10 December 2025** the Cabinet Social Outcomes Committee (minute SOU-25-MIN-0178) "agreed that foundational mandatory child protection training be initially rolled out through an electronic prototype, starting with a subset of approximately 500 core children's workers, as defined in the Children's Act 2014, from January 2026" (recs 8, 9, 13), and separately agreed to establish the in-person interagency hub from January 2026 (recs 1, 2, 6). The paper accompanying that minute — MSD's proactively-released "Next Steps on Government Response to the Dame Karen Poutasi Review" (published 30 January 2026) — is explicit that "the training regime is our current implementation focus" and that "the proposals in this paper have no direct legislative implications," confirming that the mandatory-*reporting* legislation is still a future step. [SOU-25-MIN-0178 minute (10 Dec 2025, PDF)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/sou-25-min-0178-minute.pdf); [MSD Cabinet paper: Next Steps (2026, PDF)](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf)

On the ground, the **training** step began as a pilot: an online child-protection module was being **tested with core children's workers across six agencies (Health NZ, Police, MSD, Ministry of Education, Corrections, Oranga Tamariki)** from early 2026 — over 400 workers, expanding toward 500 by the end of June 2026, ahead of wider phased rollout. [RNZ, 11 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/586533/unacceptable-delays-to-improve-child-protection-system-government); [Beehive, 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system) **Mandatory *reporting* itself has no announced legislation or commencement date** in any of the primary sources reviewed, and the Monitor graded recs 8 and 9 **"Not achieved"** as at February 2026, noting the same stepped approach. [Aroturuki Tamariki — second review (PDF), 18 Feb 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025-FINAL-web.pdf)

**Confidence: High** on the sequencing and the training pilot; **High** that no reporting-legislation date is yet public (absence corroborated across the Cabinet paper, both Beehive releases, and the Monitor review).

### The Monitor's implementation-review verdict: settings unchanged, children no more visible

Recommendation 14 tasked the Independent Children's Monitor (Aroturuki Tamariki) with reviewing progress. It has now done so **twice**, and both times reached a critical verdict.

- **First review — "Towards a stronger safety net to prevent abuse of children" (finalised May 2024, published August 2024).** Key finding: *"we are not confident that tamariki in similar situations to [the child] are any more likely to be seen, or kept safe by the system, than they were when [the child] died."* (the child is named in the source; neutralised here) It found "the recommendations of the Poutasi review have not been implemented and the system change Dame Karen called for has not happened," that "progress to date was disappointing," and that even recommendations within chief executives' control "we are yet to see any implementation." [Aroturuki Tamariki — first review (PDF), Aug 2024](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/poutasi/Review-of-implementation-of-Poutasi-recommendations.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki first-review landing page](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/poutasi-review)
- **Second review — published 18 February 2026.** Its overall conclusion: *"System settings have not changed, gaps remain and tamariki and rangatahi are still no more likely to be seen by Oranga Tamariki now than when [the child] was killed."* It found "little has changed," "a high proportion of reports of concern from professionals that do not result in further action," and that "Police and Oranga Tamariki, for the most part, undertake reviews in isolation from other agencies." It recorded that **between December 2021 and June 2025, 24 tamariki were killed by someone who was supposed to be caring for them** (with more since June 2025 not yet counted), and counted **only two of the 14 recommendations complete**. [Aroturuki Tamariki — second review (PDF), 18 Feb 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025-FINAL-web.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki second-review landing page](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net); [RNZ, 18 Feb 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report)

**Confidence: High** — both verdicts are quoted from the Monitor's own report PDFs and corroborated by national reporting.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The single most important limit is that "the system has not materially changed" is a value-laden judgement made by an oversight body, not a settled fact.** It is a strong, primary, independent judgement (the Monitor's) — but a human with clinical, lived-experience, Te Tiriti, or statutory authority should weigh it before it is treated as the final word. If a later independent review found children measurably more visible and safer, this conclusion would soften.
- **Implementation is a fast-moving target.** This finding is a snapshot to mid-2026. The **December 2025 Cabinet decisions are now incorporated** (SOU-25-MIN-0178 / the MSD "Next Steps" release), but any mandatory-reporting Bill and the Monitor's future annual reviews could each shift the status materially. In particular, **the actual legislative timetable for mandatory reporting is not yet public** and is the single biggest unknown — the December 2025 paper confirms training is the focus and that its own proposals have "no direct legislative implications," so the reporting legislation had still not been introduced as at that decision.
- **The two "counts of complete" use different thresholds** and I have not reconciled them to a single authoritative number — I have instead reported both and shown the divergence. A reader wanting one number should treat the Monitor's stricter "Not achieved / Complete" grading and the government's "underway" framing as answering different questions (outcome vs process).
- **Could not verify:** whether the inter-agency hub and mandatory training exist in every community (the Monitor flags they do not); the outcome/effectiveness of the new hub beyond its raw ROC/child counts; and any figure for children's-agency compliance with Part 2 of the Children's Act 2014 that the government said officials would enforce. *(Corrected from the previous draft: the post-acceptance policy decisions were located — they were taken on 10 December 2025, not November, and are published in MSD's "Next Steps" release with the SOU-25-MIN-0178 minute, now cited above.)*
- **De-identification is a hard limit, not a caveat.** No child, family, or worker is analysed here. Where a source title or URL contains the deceased child's name it is left intact as the genuine link, but this finding's own prose does not reproduce or analyse it.

## Open follow-up questions

- Beyond the December 2025 "Next Steps" decisions (training-first, hub from January 2026), what will the **mandatory-reporting design** ultimately be — definition of mandatory reporters, the "High Report of Concern" category, commencement — and is there now a Bill before Parliament? (Primary: MSD/Beehive proactive releases; Parliament.)
- Does the **inter-agency hub** for children of incarcerated sole parents change outcomes, not just report volumes, and is it being scaled beyond a pilot? (Primary: Oranga Tamariki, future Monitor reviews.)
- How does NZ's staged mandatory-reporting design compare with the **Australian (esp. NSW) regime** the government cites — including the documented downside of reporting-volume surges? (Primary: Australian Institute of Family Studies; academic literature.)
