# NZ's cross-agency child-protection handoffs still break down at visibility, proactive information sharing, joint review, and local coverage points

## Executive answer

- **The core "nobody's job" gap is real, current, and officially acknowledged.** Dame Karen Poutasi's 2022 review identified five critical gaps in the children's system, including that agencies and services were not proactively sharing information despite enabling provisions and that system settings enabled a child to be unseen at key moments; Aroturuki Tamariki's 2026 follow-up found that information sharing was still not being connected more effectively and that there was no evidence Oranga Tamariki guidance had improved frontline practice. [Poutasi final report, 2022](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Reviews-and-Inquiries/System-review-Dame-Karen-Poutasi/Final-report-Joint-Review-into-the-Childrens-Sector.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 3, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-3)
- **The law is not the main blocker; practice is.** The Office of the Privacy Commissioner says there is no legislative barrier to sharing information where there is a wellbeing or safety concern for a child or young person, and its Oranga Tamariki Act guidance says section 66C permits proactive sharing between child welfare and protection agencies including education, health, Corrections, MSD, Oranga Tamariki and Police. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, child wellbeing and safety guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/); [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, OT Act sharing guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/oranga-tamariki-act-1989/)
- **The handoff failures are not only at intake; they also sit in review and learning loops.** Aroturuki Tamariki found that Police and Oranga Tamariki, for the most part, undertake reviews in isolation from other agencies, focus on internal practice rather than broader systemic issues, and sometimes complete action plans without detail on whether the action changed anything. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Introduction, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/introduction)
- **The new national inter-agency hub is targeted, not a universal community hub.** Cabinet agreed to establish an in-person inter-agency hub from January 2026 at the Oranga Tamariki National Contact Centre for dependent children whose sole parent or carer is remanded in custody or imprisoned; by 29 April 2026 the Government said the hub had responded to 73 reports of concern for 110 tamariki. [MSD Cabinet paper, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf); [Beehive, 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system)
- **The Monitor says local multi-agency tables are not in every community and do not yet prove system-wide effectiveness.** It found that neither reports-of-concern tables nor family-violence tables are present in all communities, that there is no consistent operating model or risk-assessment approach, and that there is no consistent forum across communities for earlier collaboration before harm occurs; because this national coverage diagnosis mainly comes from the Monitor's evidence stream, the finding treats it as Medium confidence rather than a two-source High-confidence claim. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the existence and nature of the handoff gaps are High confidence because they are supported by Poutasi, the Monitor, the Privacy Commissioner, Cabinet material, and agencies' own responses. The conclusion about effectiveness is Medium because most current evidence measures implementation, activity, or local qualitative practice, not system-wide child-safety outcomes.

## Evidence

### The gap Poutasi named was "able to share, not proactively sharing"

Dame Karen Poutasi's 2022 system review was commissioned by six agencies with relevant responsibilities: Oranga Tamariki, Police, Corrections, MSD, Education and Health. [Poutasi final report, 2022](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Reviews-and-Inquiries/System-review-Dame-Karen-Poutasi/Final-report-Joint-Review-into-the-Childrens-Sector.pdf) Poutasi's review identified five critical gaps, including risk assessment being too narrow, agencies not proactively sharing information despite enabling provisions, lack of reporting of abuse risk by some professionals and services, and system settings that allowed a child to be invisible. [Poutasi final report, 2022](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/About-us/Performance-and-monitoring/Reviews-and-Inquiries/System-review-Dame-Karen-Poutasi/Final-report-Joint-Review-into-the-Childrens-Sector.pdf)

The 2026 Monitor review found the same information-sharing gap had not yet closed: its "Critical gap 3" section says the legal basis to share was not at issue, but frontline uncertainty meant information was not always shared across agencies as legislation envisaged. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 3, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-3) The Monitor also said many professionals still appeared to view referring concerns to Oranga Tamariki as satisfying their responsibilities, while agencies' responsibilities go beyond making a report of concern and require collaboration to understand risk. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 3, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-3)

**Confidence: High** that this is a current, official diagnosis; **Medium** on scale, because public sources do not quantify how many potentially useful information-sharing events are missed.

### The Privacy Commissioner is being used to remove the privacy-law excuse

The Office of the Privacy Commissioner published child wellbeing and safety information-sharing guidance on 24 November 2025 as part of the integrated government response to the Poutasi review. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, child wellbeing and safety guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/) The Commissioner states that there is no legislative barrier to information sharing when there is a wellbeing or safety concern for a child or young person, and that not sharing information when it can and should be shared can cause harm. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, child wellbeing and safety guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/)

The Privacy Commissioner's Oranga Tamariki Act guidance says section 15 permits sharing concerns directly with Oranga Tamariki or Police, section 66 enables Oranga Tamariki or Police to require relevant information, and section 66C permits proactive sharing for specified wellbeing and safety purposes. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, OT Act sharing guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/oranga-tamariki-act-1989/) The primary legislation confirms each of these provisions directly: section 15 is headed "Reporting of concerns to chief executive or constable" and lets any person report concerns about a child's or young person's wellbeing or safety to Oranga Tamariki or Police; sections 66 and 66A empower Oranga Tamariki or a constable to require and then disclose relevant information; and section 66C provides that a child welfare and protection agency or independent person "may, irrespective of the purpose for which that information was collected," disclose it "whether on request or on the agency's or independent person's own initiative" to another such agency to protect a child's or young person's safety or wellbeing. [Oranga Tamariki Act 1989, latest official Act text, legislation.govt.nz](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1989/24/en/latest/) The Privacy Commissioner guidance lists education, health, Corrections, MSD, Oranga Tamariki and Police among the child welfare and protection agencies covered by the section 66C sharing framework, consistent with the Act's own definition of "child welfare and protection agency." [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, OT Act sharing guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/oranga-tamariki-act-1989/); [Oranga Tamariki Act 1989, latest official Act text, legislation.govt.nz](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1989/24/en/latest/)

The Monitor says Government announcements in October 2025 stated that the Privacy Commissioner would work alongside children's agencies to reinforce expectations, participate in workshops, lead proactive messaging, and meet regional public service leadership groups to communicate safe information-sharing expectations. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 3, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-3) A May 2026 ministerial update said the Office of the Privacy Commissioner was rolling out sessions around the country to ensure workers know the Privacy Act does not prevent sharing information where child safety is involved. [Beehive, 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system)

**Confidence: High** that the Privacy Commissioner's role is guidance, messaging and assurance about legal settings; **Medium** that this will change practice, because the public evidence so far describes sessions and guidance rather than measured behaviour change.

### Police and Oranga Tamariki review loops still mostly learn inside agency boundaries

Aroturuki Tamariki requested child-death-review material from education, health, justice, Police, Oranga Tamariki and the Health Quality & Safety Commission, and Police and Oranga Tamariki provided copies of child death reviews undertaken between December 2021 and June 2025. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Our approach, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/our-approach) From those reviews, the Monitor found Police and Oranga Tamariki, for the most part, undertake reviews in isolation from other agencies and focus on internal practices rather than broader system issues. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Introduction, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/introduction)

The Monitor also found that most Police Family Violence Death Reviews did not make specific recommendations or introduce new systemic changes to Police practice, while some Oranga Tamariki reviews made recommendations and action plans but did not explain how or whether completed actions changed practice. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Introduction, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/introduction) Police's February 2026 response accepted the Monitor's findings and said Police acknowledges limited cross-agency collaboration in past Police Family Violence Death Reviews and is starting a shift toward more collaborative local family-violence death reviews involving agencies and service providers who had engaged with the relevant whanau, victim, or perpetrator. [NZ Police response to the Monitor, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/NZ-Police-response-to-Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025.pdf)

**Confidence: High** that past reviews have been siloed; **Medium** that the new collaborative review shift will fix the learning loop, because Police describes intended monitoring mechanisms but no published evaluation yet shows changed review quality or child-safety outcomes.

### Health is still not fully embedded in the Police-Oranga Tamariki child-protection protocol

Poutasi recommended adding the health sector as a partner to the Child Protection Protocol between Police and Oranga Tamariki so agencies could access health professionals experienced in identifying child abuse and undertake regular joint training. [Aroturuki Tamariki, 2024 response to Poutasi review](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/poutasi-review/response) The Monitor's 2026 review says Health NZ's board agreed in mid-October 2025 to join the protocol in a leadership and governance capacity as an initial step, while an updated draft protocol including Health NZ was being developed by Oranga Tamariki, Police and Health NZ. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

The Monitor reported that Health NZ could not confirm when or whether full partnership would happen until it understood frontline resourcing implications, and that full partnership was anticipated from 1 July 2026 only if scoping and approval supported it. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2) The Monitor concluded that until Health NZ provides child-protection expertise in all parts of Aotearoa, risks remain when Oranga Tamariki and Police make health decisions in isolation. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

**Confidence: Medium** — the current governance step is clear, but the practical availability of specialist child-protection health expertise across all regions is not yet demonstrated in the public evidence.

### The new national hub is a narrow handoff repair, not universal coverage

The December 2025 Cabinet paper on next steps proposed an in-person inter-agency hub located in the Oranga Tamariki National Contact Centre to share information about children whose sole parent or carer is remanded in custody or sentenced to imprisonment. [MSD Cabinet paper, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf) The Cabinet paper says privacy-safe information sharing under Oranga Tamariki Act provisions such as sections 66, 66C and 15 would inform an initial assessment within 48 hours, leading to statutory intervention, referral to community providers, or triage out where no concerns are identified. [MSD Cabinet paper, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf)

Cabinet agreed to establish the hub from January 2026, and the paper identifies Oranga Tamariki, Corrections, Education, MSD, Police and Justice as hub participants, with Health NZ considering its involvement at the time of the paper. [MSD Cabinet paper, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 1, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-1) A May 2026 ministerial update says the hub had responded to 73 reports of concern for 110 tamariki as of 29 April 2026. [Beehive, 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system)

The Monitor recognised the hub as progress and an opportunity to check safety and put support in place, but warned that if the hub is not sufficiently resourced it may fail to achieve Poutasi's intent or divert existing resources from other children for whom concerns have been raised. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 1, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-1) The Monitor also said it was unclear, at drafting, what role Health and Education would have in delivering supports through the hub and whether all affected tamariki and rangatahi would receive a welfare visit or only those where safety concerns were apparent. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 1, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-1)

**Confidence: High** on the hub's establishment and target cohort; **Low** on effectiveness, because the available public measure is activity count, not whether children were safer, seen faster, or better supported.

### Community multi-agency responses exist, but coverage and operating models are uneven

Poutasi recommended multi-agency teams in communities, working with iwi/Maori and NGOs and supported throughout the country, to prevent and respond to harm. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2) The Monitor's 2024 review found several multi-agency programmes across the country but not in every community, and its 2026 review again found that neither reports-of-concern tables nor family-violence tables are present in all communities, although family-violence tables cover every Police district. [Aroturuki Tamariki, 2024 response to Poutasi review](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/poutasi-review/response); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

The Monitor found these local tables are making a difference in communities where they operate, but they are not yet closing the Poutasi critical gap because there is no consistent operating model or risk-assessment approach, responses vary across communities, and most tables respond after harm rather than creating an earlier consistent forum for agencies to share information and prevent harm. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2) In community tables dealing with reports of concern, the Monitor heard that Oranga Tamariki and community agencies do not routinely hear what happened after whanau were referred to a community organisation or back to Oranga Tamariki, creating concern about whether anyone visited and whether tamariki were safe. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

There is some positive evidence from Integrated Safety Response pilots: Oranga Tamariki's research page says evaluations of the Waikato and Christchurch ISR model found improved safety, reduced revictimisation for Maori whanau, and better access to support services. [Oranga Tamariki, ISR evaluations](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/research/our-research/integrated-safety-response-isr-evaluations/) That positive evidence is narrower than Poutasi's child-protection handoff problem because ISR is a family-violence model established in Waikato and Christchurch, while the Monitor says current tables are not nationally present, not consistent, and not yet a system-wide early-prevention forum. [Oranga Tamariki, ISR evaluations](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/research/our-research/integrated-safety-response-isr-evaluations/); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)

**Confidence: Medium** that coverage and models are uneven, because the current national coverage diagnosis mainly comes from the Monitor's evidence stream; **Medium** on local effectiveness, because ISR evaluations support some benefits but do not establish national child-protection effectiveness for all community tables.

### Agencies acknowledge the gap, but their responses still mostly describe work programmes

The joint February 2026 agency response to the Monitor accepted that more needs to be done to address the risks Poutasi identified and said phased implementation is critical because of the scale of complex change and its impact on the Oranga Tamariki system. [Joint agency response to the Monitor, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Joint-Agency-response-to-Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025.pdf) The same response says MSD, as lead agency for the integrated response, will monitor and report the programme to responsible Ministers and Cabinet, and Oranga Tamariki and Police would also write individually under section 30 of the Oversight of Oranga Tamariki System Act 2022. [Joint agency response to the Monitor, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Joint-Agency-response-to-Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025.pdf)

Police's individual response accepts the Monitor's findings, says Police continues to participate in cross-agency implementation work, and notes that monitoring child-safety outcomes is challenging because outcomes reflect the whole child-protection system rather than Police-specific interventions alone. [NZ Police response to the Monitor, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/NZ-Police-response-to-Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025.pdf) This supports a cautious conclusion: agencies are now aligned around the need to fix cross-agency handoffs, but the public evidence still mostly shows plans, pilots, guidance, and activity measures rather than independent outcome evidence. [Joint agency response to the Monitor, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/assets/Reports/safety-net/Joint-Agency-response-to-Towards-a-stronger-safety-net-to-prevent-abuse-of-children-2025.pdf); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Key findings, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/key-findings)

**Confidence: Medium** — agency intent and governance are clear, but effectiveness is not yet proven.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A national, independent evaluation of the 2026 inter-agency hub would change the effectiveness conclusion** if it showed faster identification, timely welfare visits, better support referrals, fewer repeat reports of concern, or other child-safety outcomes for the target cohort compared with the previous process. [MSD Cabinet paper, 2026](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/publications-resources/information-releases/cabinet-papers/2026/next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review/paper-next-steps-on-government-response-to-the-dame-karen-poutasi-review.pdf); [Beehive, 5 May 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-improvements-children%E2%80%99s-system)
- **Measured frontline behaviour change would change the information-sharing conclusion** if agencies published evidence that workers in health, education, Corrections, MSD, Police and Oranga Tamariki were sharing relevant information more often, earlier, and in ways that improved risk assessment after the Privacy Commissioner's 2025 guidance and regional sessions. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, child wellbeing and safety guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 3, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-3)
- **A current map of reports-of-concern tables, family-violence tables, ISR/SAM coverage, and Enabling Communities tables would sharpen the coverage conclusion** because the public evidence says they are not in every community but does not provide a complete national coverage table. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)
- **Could not verify from public aggregate sources:** the proportion of child-protection cases where information was available in another agency but not shared; whether the Privacy Commissioner sessions have attendance, comprehension, or practice-change measures; the hub's 48-hour completion rate; whether hub referrals led to visits, supports, or statutory action; and whether Health NZ fully joined the Child Protection Protocol on or after 1 July 2026. [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 1, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-1); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Critical gap 2, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/critical-gap-2)
- **A human with child-protection practice, privacy-law, health, education, Police, Corrections, iwi/Maori provider, or lived-experience authority is needed** to test whether "share earlier and more often" is workable in frontline conditions without creating harmful surveillance, distrust, or unsafe disclosure for children and whanau. [Office of the Privacy Commissioner, OT Act sharing guidance, 2025](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/information-sharing-childrens-wellbeing-and-safety/oranga-tamariki-act-1989/); [Aroturuki Tamariki, Our approach, 2026](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/our-approach)
