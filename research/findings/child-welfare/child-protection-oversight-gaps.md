# NZ independent oversight bodies consistently identify systemic child-protection gaps, but their remits differ

## Executive answer

- The official independent record is not one single verdict: Aroturuki Tamariki monitors performance of the oranga tamariki system, including care-standards compliance and Māori outcomes; the Auditor-General examined Oranga Tamariki procurement and contract management; and the Ombudsman handles complaints and own-motion investigations about Oranga Tamariki and care or custody providers. [Aroturuki Tamariki: About us](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/about-us); [Ombudsman: oversight powers](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/oversight-powers-oranga-tamariki-system); [Auditor-General: Oranga Tamariki inquiry](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki)
- Aroturuki Tamariki's record is the broadest system-oversight record: it says its role expanded on 1 May 2023 from National Care Standards monitoring to monitoring the whole oranga tamariki system, and it says its monitoring is independent from Ministers and government agencies. [Aroturuki Tamariki: About us](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/about-us)
- The Monitor's recurring care-standards reports are consistently critical: the 2024/25 report says tamariki and rangatahi are still not receiving the minimum standard of care required by the National Care Standards Regulations, and its foreword says Oranga Tamariki continues not to comply with the regulations with limited improvement five years on. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25)
- The Monitor's 2024/25 Māori-outcomes report identifies over-representation and unequal timeliness: 57 percent of reports of concern with known ethnicity were for tamariki and rangatahi Māori, critical or very urgent reports for Māori were less likely to be responded to on time than those for non-Māori, and Māori made up 68 percent of care-and-protection custody and 80 percent of youth-justice custody. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system)
- The Auditor-General's May 2025 inquiry is narrower but material: it found weak procurement and contract-management foundations, inadequate planning, late and poorly documented decisions, insufficient evidence about effects on children and whānau, mostly non-competitive contracting, poor provider payment practice, and failure to factor Tiriti relationships into contracting decisions. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm)
- The Ombudsman's complaints record adds a rights and administrative-fairness lens: in a 2019-2023 complaints report, the Chief Ombudsman analysed 2,142 complaints and enquiries, found 109 formal deficiencies in 61 final opinions, and concluded he could not yet assure that Oranga Tamariki was consistently operating in accordance with good administrative practice. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** High — the conclusion rests on current official oversight-body publications, with each remit and finding cited to the relevant body's own record. It is not a judgement about any individual case, and it does not use identifying detail.

## Evidence

### The three oversight lenses are different

Aroturuki Tamariki says it was established in 2019 to monitor compliance with the National Care Standards Regulations, and that on 1 May 2023 its role expanded to monitor the whole oranga tamariki system, including early intervention, care, youth justice, and transitions out of care. [Aroturuki Tamariki: About us](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/about-us) The Monitor also says the Oversight of the Oranga Tamariki System Act requires it to act independently in its monitoring tools, approaches, and monitoring function, so its operations and findings are independent from Ministers and government agencies. [Aroturuki Tamariki: About us](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/about-us) Its corporate reporting describes an oversight system in which the Monitor works alongside the Children and Young People's Commission as advocate and the Ombudsman as the agency responsible for investigations and complaints. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Annual Report 2023/24](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/about-us/corporate-documents/annual-report)

The Ombudsman describes the separated roles similarly: the Children and Young People's Commission advocates, the Independent Children's Monitor monitors performance, and the Ombudsman investigates and monitors serious and significant issues and handles complaints about Oranga Tamariki and care or custody providers. [Ombudsman: oversight powers](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/oversight-powers-oranga-tamariki-system) The Ombudsman says the Oversight Act came into force on 1 May 2023 and gave the Chief Ombudsman additional powers and duties for complaints and investigations involving tamariki, rangatahi, and whānau in the Oranga Tamariki system. [Ombudsman: Oversight Act comes into force](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/oversight-oranga-tamariki-system-act-2022-comes-force) The Ombudsman also says that from 1 May 2023 he can receive complaints directly about Oranga Tamariki care or custody providers, rather than those complaints having to be dealt with through Oranga Tamariki. [Ombudsman: care or custody providers](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/agency-assistance/care-agencies-oranga-tamariki-system)

The Auditor-General's May 2025 inquiry was a public-sector management inquiry, not a whole-system child-safety review: the Auditor-General says concerns were raised in July 2024 about contract management, especially 2024/25 contracting decisions, and that the services under those contracts are critical for children and families, involve significant public spending, and could undermine public trust if poorly managed. [Auditor-General: Oranga Tamariki inquiry](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki)

**Confidence: High.** These role distinctions come from each oversight body's own current official description.

### Aroturuki Tamariki: care standards and wider system gaps

The Monitor's 2024/25 Experiences of Care report is its fifth full report on compliance with the National Care Standards Regulations and covers 1 July 2024 to 30 June 2025. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25) The report says tamariki and rangatahi are still not receiving the minimum standard of care required by the regulations, and that Oranga Tamariki had custody of nearly 99 percent of the 5,640 children in care during the reporting period. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25) Its foreword says the Monitor's reports are "saying pretty much the same thing" from year to year, that Oranga Tamariki continues not to comply with the regulations, and that improvement has been limited. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25)

The same report identifies a cross-agency gap inside the care-standards problem: the duty to meet the National Care Standards sits with Oranga Tamariki and other organisations with custody and care, but education and health support depends on the Ministry of Education, Ministry of Health, and Health NZ prioritising services and collaborating, and the Monitor says there is no regulatory requirement for those other agencies to do so. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25) The Monitor also reports hearing in every community it visited that tamariki and rangatahi were waiting for education and health services, including school enrolment, while agencies debated who would pay. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25)

The Monitor's in-depth review, _Towards a stronger safety net to prevent abuse of children_, is a second review of implementation of the Poutasi recommendations and was published on 18 February 2026. [Aroturuki Tamariki: safety net review](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net) Its key findings say gaps in the safety net remain, that the gaps identified in the Poutasi report have not closed, and that Oranga Tamariki is not always able to respond when it needs to. [Aroturuki Tamariki: safety net key findings](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/key-findings) The same key findings say later reviews remained mostly fragmented, not child-focused, and tended to look at one agency's practice rather than across multiple agencies or systemic issues. [Aroturuki Tamariki: safety net key findings](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/safety-net/key-findings)

**Confidence: High.** These are direct findings of the statutory Monitor, and the same care-standards concern appears in consecutive Monitor reports.

### Aroturuki Tamariki: Māori outcomes

The Monitor's 2024/25 Māori-outcomes report says the Oversight Act requires it to report annually on the performance of the oranga tamariki system in respect of outcomes for tamariki and rangatahi Māori and their whānau. [Aroturuki Tamariki: approach to Māori outcomes report](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/our-approach-to-this-report) The report says it uses a mixed-methods approach, gathering qualitative information from communities and quantitative data from agencies, and it draws on the Integrated Data Infrastructure for outcome indicators. [Aroturuki Tamariki: approach to Māori outcomes report](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/our-approach-to-this-report)

The 2024/25 Māori-outcomes report says reports of concern were made about 30,600 individual tamariki and rangatahi Māori and 22,900 non-Māori where ethnicity was recorded, meaning 57 percent of reports of concern with known ethnicity were for Māori. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system) It reports that 23 percent of tamariki and rangatahi Māori with a report of concern in 2024/25 had 10 or more previous reports of concern, compared with 2,960 non-Māori individuals in that group. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system)

The same report says critical and very urgent reports of concern for tamariki and rangatahi Māori had less timely responses than those for non-Māori in every region, with the national response rate for Māori dropping from 90 percent in 2023/24 to 84 percent in 2024/25 and the non-Māori rate dropping from 95 percent to 90 percent. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system) It also says 68 percent of care-and-protection custody and 80 percent of youth-justice custody in 2024/25 involved tamariki and rangatahi Māori, while the level of over-representation had not changed despite the care and custody population reducing over recent years. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system)

**Confidence: High.** The figures are from the Monitor's official annual Māori-outcomes report; the causal interpretation remains Medium because the Monitor itself notes some patterns may reflect need, complexity, or bias rather than a single cause. [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system)

### Auditor-General: procurement and contract management as a child-protection gap

The Auditor-General found that many aspects of Oranga Tamariki's 2024/25 contracting round were not in line with good procurement or contract-management practice, and that the failures significantly harmed trust and confidence in Oranga Tamariki from the social-services sector it relies on to provide services to vulnerable children and their families. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm) The Auditor-General found Oranga Tamariki did not have robust procurement and contract-management systems leading into the contracting round, routinely made contracting decisions with little analysis and late in the financial year, did not monitor performance meaningfully, and had not materially improved despite previous work programmes and frameworks. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm)

The Auditor-General found the contracting-round decisions were not adequately informed by evidence of how they would affect children and families or the sustainability of the social-services sector. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm) The report says the Auditor-General found no evidence that decision-making was informed by good information about the number of children and whānau affected, whether they could access needed services, or whether enough service levels existed nationally or regionally to compensate for changes. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm)

The Auditor-General also found that, with limited exceptions, contracts awarded in 2024/25 were not subject to an open, transparent, and competitive procurement process; provider performance measures were generally based on inputs or outputs rather than outcomes; Oranga Tamariki did not consistently pay providers on time; it did not adequately plan transitions for expiring, reduced, or terminated contracts; and it did not have a specific process to identify or manage conflicts of interest in the contracting round. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm) The same report says the Auditor-General did not see Oranga Tamariki factor Tiriti relationships into decision-making or engagement with kaupapa Māori, iwi, and hapū providers. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm)

**Confidence: High.** The findings are from the Auditor-General's official inquiry report and are narrow to procurement and contract management, not direct casework practice.

### Ombudsman: complaints, own-motion investigations, and administrative fairness

The Ombudsman reports that the Oversight Act strengthened the Ombudsman's complaint, resolution, and investigation system for Oranga Tamariki, and that the Ombudsman has increased monitoring of critical and serious incidents, complaint trends, and data, with an ability to investigate where necessary. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf) Under the strengthened provider role, the Ombudsman says complaints can be made directly about care or custody providers, and the Ombudsman can contact providers directly to resolve complaints. [Ombudsman: care or custody providers](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/agency-assistance/care-agencies-oranga-tamariki-system)

In _Children in care: complaints to the Ombudsman 2019-2023_, the Chief Ombudsman reports receiving 2,142 complaints and other enquiries about Oranga Tamariki from July 2019 to June 2023, completing or closing 1,964, resolving 86 complaints, completing final opinions in 117 investigations, and finding 109 formal deficiencies in 61 final opinions. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf) The same report lists recurring complaint themes including reports of concern not being acted on, recorded properly, or responded to adequately; inaccurate, outdated, incomplete, or biased information provided to the Family Court; failures to seek children's views; failures to engage with Māori in culturally appropriate ways; inadequate record-keeping and information transfer; inadequate communication and support for parents; and lack of natural-justice opportunities. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf)

The Chief Ombudsman concluded that he could not yet provide assurance that Oranga Tamariki was consistently operating in accordance with good administrative practice, while also noting positive outcomes and compassionate social work in some cases. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf) The Ombudsman's accompanying media release describes the call for changes as being on a scale rarely required of a government agency and says the report followed a review of 2,000 complaints and enquiries about Oranga Tamariki and predecessor agencies. [Ombudsman media release, 21 Feb 2024](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/chief-ombudsman-calls-changes-oranga-tamariki-scale-rarely-required)

**Confidence: High** on the complaint volumes, themes, and Ombudsman's conclusion; **Medium** on generalising the complaints record to whole-system prevalence, because complaints data shows what reached the Ombudsman rather than every interaction with the system.

### Oranga Tamariki's account of itself is materially more positive

Oranga Tamariki's 2024/25 annual-report news release says the year involved a sharp rise in service requests, with reports of concern up 44 percent overall and individual children with reports up 17 percent. [Oranga Tamariki: 2025 Annual Report release](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/) The same release says Oranga Tamariki completed more than 44,000 investigations, facilitated more than 10,000 Family Group Conferences, supported over 4,000 children in care, visited 95 percent of children in care at least once every eight weeks, and reported that 96 percent of children in care said they felt safe. [Oranga Tamariki: 2025 Annual Report release](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/)

That agency account should be treated as activity and self-reported performance context, not as an independent assessment of system adequacy. The Monitor explicitly says Oranga Tamariki cited unprecedented service pressure and a 17 percent increase in individual tamariki and rangatahi coming to its attention, but the Monitor did not accept that demand pressure explained ongoing non-compliance with regulations that have been in place since 2019. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25) The Auditor-General similarly says Oranga Tamariki publicly said it centred contracting decisions on children's needs, but the Auditor-General found records did not demonstrate how evidence was used and found no evidence of good information on affected children and whānau, service access, or replacement service capacity. [Auditor-General: Part 6 findings and recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/part6.htm)

**Confidence: High.** The contrast is between Oranga Tamariki's own published account and independent oversight-body findings about the same broad system.

## What would change this conclusion

- The conclusion would soften if future Monitor reports showed sustained compliance with the National Care Standards, reduced Māori disproportionality and timeliness gaps, and evidence that cross-agency health, education, and care responsibilities were working without children waiting while agencies disputed responsibility. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Experiences of Care 2024/25](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/eoc-24-25); [Aroturuki Tamariki: over-representation of Māori](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports/outcomes-24-25/over-representation-of-maori-in-the-oranga-tamariki-system)
- The procurement conclusion would change if Oranga Tamariki implemented the Auditor-General's recommendations and a later independent audit found a clear procurement strategy, documented child-impact analysis, outcome-linked performance measures, timely provider payment, and Tiriti-informed contracting decisions. [Auditor-General: recommendations](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/our-recommendations.htm)
- The Ombudsman conclusion would change if later complaints reporting showed lower complaint volumes, fewer formal deficiencies, fewer systemic remedies, and a positive assurance statement about Oranga Tamariki administrative practice. [Ombudsman: Children in care complaints 2019-2023](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-02/Children%20in%20care%20complaints%20to%20the%20Ombudsman%202019%E2%80%932023_Digital.pdf)
- I could not verify from public aggregate sources whether each oversight-body recommendation has been fully implemented after publication, or whether improvements claimed by Oranga Tamariki after 30 June 2025 have been independently confirmed. A human with child-protection, Māori provider, disability-rights, or lived-experience expertise should weigh the practical significance of these official findings before the stream moves from research to ideation.

## Open follow-up questions
