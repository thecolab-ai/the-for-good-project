# Community entitlement-navigation channels have broad NZ reach, but public evidence does not show enough spare capacity to close the entitlement-discovery gap alone

## Executive answer

- Citizens Advice Bureau, financial mentors/MoneyTalks, Whānau Ora navigators, and Community Law are real national or near-national help channels, but they are not a single reserve workforce that can be assumed to absorb all unmet entitlement-discovery demand. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf); [MoneyTalks](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks); [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)
- CAB has broad access infrastructure, with 80 locations and 328,070 client interactions in the 2024/25 year, and its 2025 welfare spotlight shows people come to CAB when they are unsure where to go, what support exists, how to access it, or what to do after a Work and Income decline. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf); [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf)
- Financial mentoring looks especially capacity-constrained, and this is corroborated by a source independent of the sector's own peak body: FinCap's 2025 Voices report says mentors were working within 176 organisations at the end of 2024, 16 fewer than in its first Voices report, while closed client cases rose to 25,140 in 2024, and — independently — the Ministry of Social Development, which funds Building Financial Capability services, cut that funding by 12.5% (to $19.5 million per year) from 1 July 2024, with RNZ reporting that 44 of 132 budgeting services lost funding amid rising cost-of-living demand. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994); [MSD, Successful financial mentoring providers confirmed, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/newsroom/2024/successful-financial-mentoring-providers-confirmed.html); [RNZ, budgeting services seek Auditor-General review, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/526056/budgeting-services-seek-auditor-general-review-of-msd-s-tender-process-after-funding-cuts)
- Whānau Ora is being explicitly redesigned for greater reach, more navigator services, workforce retention, data-driven investment, and more frontline funding, which is strong evidence that government and Te Puni Kōkiri see reach and workforce capacity as live constraints rather than solved conditions. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Te Puni Kōkiri RFP, Whānau Ora Commissioning Services](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/pages/download/pages-2945-A/RFP%20Wh%C4%81nau%20Ora%20Commissioning%20Services.pdf)
- Community Law has wide geographic coverage and strong volunteer leverage, but the best public national demand/capacity figures I found are dated, and I could not verify current national wait times, rejected requests, or entitlement-specific capacity for Community Law. [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/); [Community Law/NZIER media release, 2018](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-delivers-real-return-investment-nz-public/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the coverage and strain signals are well supported by current organisational and government sources, but wait-time, referral-acceptance, and unmet-demand data are missing or not public for several channels.

## Evidence

### What question this answers

This finding answers whether public evidence supports routing families to community entitlement-navigation channels as a capacity solution, not whether those channels are high quality or trusted by the families they already reach. [Issue #173](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/173)

### CAB is broad and useful, but already handling substantial welfare-navigation demand

CAB's 2025 annual report says its service operated from 80 locations around Aotearoa New Zealand, recorded 328,070 client interactions in 2024/25, and its website received 8,993,759 page views in that year. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf)

CAB's 2024 annual report, the latest I could fetch directly with extractable text, also describes a volunteer-heavy model with more than 2,000 CAB volunteers, 222,361 direct person-to-person information-and-advice contacts, 110,139 clinic contacts provided by other organisations in bureaux, and 80 supported service sites in 2023/24. [CABNZ Annual Report 2024](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2024-CABNZ-Annual-Report-8-Oct-2024.pdf)

CAB's welfare spotlight analysed 10,350 welfare-support enquiries from 1 November 2023 to 30 April 2025, and it says people often come to CAB when they are unsure where to go, want to know what support is available and how to access it, or need to understand options after being declined by Work and Income. [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf)

The same CAB welfare spotlight reports that welfare-support enquiries were more often face-to-face than all CAB enquiries, with 47.9% of welfare-support enquiries face-to-face compared with 38.5% of all CAB enquiries, and that Māori and Pacific Peoples were more represented in welfare-support enquiries than in all CAB enquiries. [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf)

That evidence supports CAB as a genuine entitlement-navigation front door, but it does not show spare capacity, because the public annual reports and spotlight report do not publish waiting times, turn-away rates, local vacancy rates, or how much additional casework each bureau could absorb. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf); [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf)

### Financial mentoring has national reach, but FinCap reports rising demand and service closures

MoneyTalks says it provides free support on money matters, has helped more than 175,000 New Zealanders, and can connect people with 180+ financial mentoring services. [MoneyTalks](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks)

FinCap's 2025 Voices report says its annual report presents data collected by more than 700 financial mentors across Aotearoa, and that financial mentors work with whānau throughout Aotearoa through free and confidential services. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994)

FinCap's 2025 Voices report also says financial mentors were working within 176 organisations at the end of 2024, 16 fewer than in its first Voices report, and that services continue to close because of sector funding constraints. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994)

The same report shows closed client cases increasing from 18,213 in 2022 to 21,388 in 2023 and 25,140 in 2024, and explicitly frames this as financial mentors meeting more demand despite a reduction in the number of mentors. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994)

FinCap reports that increased cost of living accounted for 29% of all recorded reasons for hardship in 2024, up from 18% in 2023, and was cited in 9,515 closed client cases. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994)

The capacity constraint is not only self-reported by the sector. The Ministry of Social Development, which funds Building Financial Capability services, re-contracted the sector for the first time since 2016 and reduced core funding by 12.5% from 1 July 2024, leaving $19.5 million per year. [MSD, Successful financial mentoring providers confirmed, 2024](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/newsroom/2024/successful-financial-mentoring-providers-confirmed.html) RNZ reported that 44 of 132 budgeting services lost MSD funding under that round, that twelve services asked the Auditor-General to review the tender process, and that services faced rising cost-of-living demand — for example a two-week wait to see one of Papakura Budgeting Services' eight financial mentors. [RNZ, budgeting services seek Auditor-General review, 24 August 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/526056/budgeting-services-seek-auditor-general-review-of-msd-s-tender-process-after-funding-cuts) MSD and RNZ are independent of FinCap, so the "already stretched" signal does not rest on a single origin.

This is the clearest current evidence that a referral-only entitlement tool could push demand into an already stretched channel unless it triages carefully, reduces mentor workload, or is co-designed with services that can control intake. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994)

### Whānau Ora has a dedicated navigator model, but the current redesign is itself evidence of reach, workforce, and data gaps

Te Puni Kōkiri says Whānau Ora is delivered through a devolved commissioning framework where commissioning agencies commission service providers and navigators to work with whānau to identify and access services and support. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora)

Te Puni Kōkiri says the commissioning agencies from 1 July 2025 are Rangitāmiro for the North Island north of and including Taupō, Māhutonga for the North Island south and east of Taupō, Te Tauraki for the South Island, and Tātou Collective for Pacific-focused delivery across Aotearoa New Zealand. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora)

Te Puni Kōkiri's 2024/25 annual report says the Whānau Ora Commissioning Outcomes appropriation was $187.385 million for 2024/25, that 13,990 whānau were supported by general commissioning agency navigators against a 12,000 to 15,000 target, and that 30,770 whānau were engaged through general commissioning against a 35,000 to 45,000 target. [Te Puni Kōkiri Annual Report 2025](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/documents/download/documents-4929-A/TPKAnnualReport-2025.pdf)

Te Puni Kōkiri's public Whānau Ora page says the next stage seeks greater service reach, an updated regional funding allocation model, increased funding to front-line delivery, a greater spread and number of navigator services, improved frontline workforce development and retention, and data-driven investment planning based partly on deprivation. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora)

The Whānau Ora commissioning RFP says the new funding model is centred on frontline navigator full-time-equivalent costs, that the regional allocation model is designed to deliver target navigator numbers proportional to whānau need and fair access, and that 2023 Census population and deprivation index measures are used to allocate funding according to the population in the three highest deprivation levels. [Te Puni Kōkiri RFP, Whānau Ora Commissioning Services](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/pages/download/pages-2945-A/RFP%20Wh%C4%81nau%20Ora%20Commissioning%20Services.pdf)

The RFP also says regional workforce plans should ensure an appropriately trained navigator workforce, target navigator profiles should evidence that reach can meet demand among those most in need, and the transition will take time because the qualified navigator workforce needs to grow. [Te Puni Kōkiri RFP, Whānau Ora Commissioning Services](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/pages/download/pages-2945-A/RFP%20Wh%C4%81nau%20Ora%20Commissioning%20Services.pdf)

The Auditor-General's 2023 Whānau Ora report found Te Puni Kōkiri was administering aspects of Whānau Ora well, but also found Te Puni Kōkiri had limited capacity to influence the wider public-sector change needed for whānau-centred approaches and that performance reporting was constrained because Te Puni Kōkiri received commissioning-agency reporting but did not have access to the underlying whānau data. [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2023](https://ao.parliament.nz/2023/whanau-ora/part3.htm)

Te Puni Kōkiri's 2024 response to the Auditor-General says investment increased from $30.484 million and 8,269 whānau engaged in 2014/15 to $156.858 million and more than 37,000 whānau engaged in 2023/24, and says the procurement aims to extend reach through increased investment in Whānau Ora commissioning. [Auditor-General follow-up, Te Puni Kōkiri response, 2024](https://ao.parliament.nz/2024/whanau-ora-follow-up/te-puni-kokiri-response.htm)

Taken together, Whānau Ora is a strong navigation model for eligible and reached whānau, but the public evidence does not support assuming that navigators can absorb all families missing child or family entitlements, because Te Puni Kōkiri is still scaling reach, workforce, measurement, and public-sector adoption. [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Auditor-General, Part 3, 2023](https://ao.parliament.nz/2023/whanau-ora/part3.htm)

### Community Law is geographically distributed, but current capacity data is thin

Community Law says it is all around the country from Kaitāia to Rēkohu/the Chathams, offers free legal help including information, workshops, and one-on-one legal help, and aims one-on-one help at people on benefits or low incomes and vulnerable people such as those struggling with housing or violence. [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)

Community Law's site lists local centres including Auckland, Blenheim, Canterbury and West Coast, Hawkes Bay, Manawatū/Horowhenua/Tararua, Nelson Bays, Otago, Porirua Kāpiti, Rotorua, Southland, Tairāwhiti, Taitokerau, Taranaki, Tauranga and Whakatāne, Waikato, Wairarapa, Wellington and Hutt Valley, Whanganui, plus specialist centres including Aotearoa Disability Law, Ngāi Tahu Māori Law Centre, and YouthLaw. [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)

A 2018 Community Law/NZIER media release says 24 Community Law Centres worked out of more than 140 locations, had about 170 staff and more than 1,200 volunteer lawyers, and provided more than 100,000 hours of advice, assistance, or representation to at least 48,000 clients on more than 53,000 legal issues in 2015/16. [Community Law/NZIER media release, 2018](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-delivers-real-return-investment-nz-public/)

The same 2018 release says Community Law clients came mainly from the bottom two socio-economic deciles, around 800,000 people, and that many thousands of people could be assisted with more resources if they cannot afford a lawyer or do not have access to legal aid. [Community Law/NZIER media release, 2018](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-delivers-real-return-investment-nz-public/)

Those figures support Community Law as a relevant rights-navigation channel, especially where entitlement issues become legal rights, housing, debt, employment, family, or benefit disputes, but they are too dated and too broad to prove current entitlement-navigation headroom. [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/); [Community Law/NZIER media release, 2018](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-delivers-real-return-investment-nz-public/)

### Practical implication for a future entitlement-discovery tool

The public evidence supports a tool that can help families identify likely questions, prepare for contact, and choose a suitable channel, because CAB, MoneyTalks, Whānau Ora, and Community Law all publish roles that include helping people identify rights, services, money options, or support pathways. [CABNZ Annual Report 2024](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2024-CABNZ-Annual-Report-8-Oct-2024.pdf); [MoneyTalks](https://fincap.org.nz/moneytalks); [Te Puni Kōkiri, Whānau Ora](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/en/nga-putea-me-nga-ratonga/whanau-ora); [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)

The public evidence does not support a tool that simply says "go to CAB / a financial mentor / a navigator / Community Law" as the main solution, because FinCap reports rising demand and funding-constrained service closures, Whānau Ora is being redesigned to improve reach and workforce capacity, CAB reports substantial welfare-support demand without publishing spare-capacity measures, and Community Law's current national capacity data is not publicly visible. [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994); [Te Puni Kōkiri RFP, Whānau Ora Commissioning Services](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/pages/download/pages-2945-A/RFP%20Wh%C4%81nau%20Ora%20Commissioning%20Services.pdf); [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf); [Community Law/NZIER media release, 2018](https://communitylaw.org.nz/community-law-delivers-real-return-investment-nz-public/)

## What would change this conclusion

- Current, region-level intake and wait-time data from CAB, financial mentoring services, Whānau Ora providers, and Community Law would change this finding if it showed consistent spare capacity in the places where low-income families with children are missing entitlements. [CAB Welfare Support Spotlight 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Social-Policy/Spotlight-Reports/2025-CAB-Welfare-Report-Final.pdf); [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994); [Te Puni Kōkiri Annual Report 2025](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/documents/download/documents-4929-A/TPKAnnualReport-2025.pdf); [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)
- A human or partner organisation with authority to share non-public service data could change the conclusion by verifying referral acceptance rates, average time to appointment, staff/navigator vacancies, and which services actively want more entitlement-screening referrals. [docs/STREAMS.md](../../../docs/STREAMS.md)
- I could not verify national current wait times, turn-away rates, appointment availability, or entitlement-specific volumes for CAB, MoneyTalks/financial mentors, Whānau Ora navigators, or Community Law from public sources. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf); [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994); [Te Puni Kōkiri RFP, Whānau Ora Commissioning Services](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/pages/download/pages-2945-A/RFP%20Wh%C4%81nau%20Ora%20Commissioning%20Services.pdf); [Community Law](https://communitylaw.org.nz/)
- I could not verify whether the same family is counted across multiple channels, so service-use volumes should not be added together as a national count of unique families helped. [CABNZ Annual Report 2025](https://www.cab.org.nz/assets/Documents/About-us/Annual-Reports/2025-CABNZ-Annual-Report-FINAL-30.09.25.pdf); [FinCap Voices 2025](https://fincap.org.nz/images/fincap-voices-report-2025.pdf?_cchid=d38904d07c9a450c991dfc33fc4c7994); [Te Puni Kōkiri Annual Report 2025](https://www.tpk.govt.nz/documents/download/documents-4929-A/TPKAnnualReport-2025.pdf)
