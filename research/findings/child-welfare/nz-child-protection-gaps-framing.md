# NZ's child-protection system has documented, officially-acknowledged gaps — and the deeper questions are researchable from aggregate official sources without any case detail

This is the **discover baseline for Stream #259**. It does three things: (1) verifies that the "nobody was able to help them" pattern the prompting news story raised is a *recurring, officially-documented* systemic issue and not a one-off; (2) maps how NZ's child-protection system is meant to work and which official bodies already report on where it fails; and (3) fans the deep dives out into chunky research children. **It deliberately contains no case details and identifies no child, family, or worker** — the domain is sensitive and this framing is about the *system*, per the issue's strict guardrails and Constitution Art. III (a human with domain/clinical/rights authority must weigh in before anything here is treated as settled).

## Executive answer

- **The pattern is real and recurring, not anecdotal.** The prompting story — children found in squalor, "right under our noses and nobody was able to help them" — echoes a repeatedly-documented systemic pattern: children who stay "invisible" to services even at key moments, and agencies that hold information but don't act on it together. Dame Karen Poutasi's 2022 system review (commissioned after a child's death that exposed cross-agency failures) named exactly this, identifying five critical gaps including that "the system allows children to remain invisible" and that "agencies and services are not proactively sharing information despite having the ability." [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html); [RNZ, on the Monitor's implementation review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report) *(headline framing only — no case detail used from the prompting article: [Stuff, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/world-news/361001924/right-under-our-noses-and-nobody-was-able-help-them-16-ki))*
- **How it's meant to work is well-defined.** Anyone can make a "report of concern" (ROC) to Oranga Tamariki; intake triages each to a statutory response timeframe — **24 hours (critical), 48 hours (very urgent), or 10 working days (urgent)** — before investigation, Family Group Conference, or no-further-action. [Oranga Tamariki Practice Centre: ROC response timeframe](https://practice.orangatamariki.govt.nz/core-practice/practice-tools/intake-decision-response-tool/report-of-concern-response-timeframe)
- **The workload is rising sharply.** Oranga Tamariki's 2024/25 Annual Report says reports of concern rose **44% overall**, with a **17% increase** in the number of individual children reported; it completed over **44,000 investigations** and more than **10,000 Family Group Conferences** in the year to 30 June 2025. Earlier reporting put calendar-2024 ROCs above **95,000 — 35% more than 2023**. [Oranga Tamariki: 2025 Annual Report summary](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/); [RNZ, on 95,000 ROCs](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/545365/oranga-tamariki-gets-95-000-reports-about-possible-at-risk-children)
- **Independent oversight already reports on the gaps, and its verdict is critical.** The Independent Children's Monitor (Aroturuki Tamariki) monitors the whole system; its review of Poutasi-recommendation implementation concluded "system settings have not changed, gaps remain and tamariki and rangatahi are still no more likely to be seen by Oranga Tamariki now than when [the child] was killed." Separately, the Auditor-General's May 2025 inquiry found Oranga Tamariki was "poorly prepared" for its 2024/25 contracting round, with awards mostly not open, transparent, or competitive — a commissioning failure that reaches the front-line services children depend on. [RNZ, on the Monitor's review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report); [Auditor-General inquiry summary (Wayback snapshot)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202005142/https://oag.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/summary.htm)
- **The questions are researchable from aggregate, official, de-identified sources — no case detail required.** Oranga Tamariki publishes quarterly performance and ROC data; Aroturuki Tamariki publishes annual system-oversight and care-standards-compliance reports; the Auditor-General and MSD publish reviews and implementation responses; and Stats NZ's Integrated Data holds ROC data (some of it only in restricted IDI microdata — see below). None of the stream's core questions require a single case detail. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Reports](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports); [Stats NZ IDC: Oranga Tamariki ROC](https://idcommons.discourse.group/pub/oranga-tamariki-reports-of-concern-roc)

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the system design, the rising workload, the existence and critical tenor of independent oversight, and the government's own acceptance of reform recommendations are all High-confidence and officially sourced. It is Medium overall because the *magnitude* of specific failures (how many urgent ROCs miss their timeframe, where exactly handoffs break, recovery/outcome rates) needs the child research to reproduce from primary data, and because "the system is failing" is partly a value judgement that a human with authority must make (Art. III).

## Evidence

### How NZ's child-protection system is meant to work

Oranga Tamariki — Ministry for Children is the statutory agency for care and protection. The front door is the "report of concern" (ROC): anyone — a professional or a member of the public — can report a child they believe is being harmed or is at risk. Intake staff assess each ROC and, where a statutory response is warranted, assign one of three response timeframes: **within 24 hours** where a child "has been seriously harmed or is at immediate risk of serious harm" (critical); **within 48 hours** where serious harm or risk exists but "some protective factors are present for the next 48 hours" (very urgent); and **within 10 working days** where a child "is at risk of harm or neglect" with cumulative or developmental concerns (urgent). [Oranga Tamariki Practice Centre: ROC response timeframe](https://practice.orangatamariki.govt.nz/core-practice/practice-tools/intake-decision-response-tool/report-of-concern-response-timeframe) From there a ROC can proceed to investigation/assessment, a Family Group Conference, or a "no further action" decision. In 2024/25 Oranga Tamariki reported completing over **44,000 investigations**, more than **10,000 Family Group Conferences**, and supporting over **4,000 children in care**, with **95%** of children in care visited by a social worker at least once every eight weeks. [Oranga Tamariki: 2025 Annual Report summary](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/)

**Confidence: High** — this is Oranga Tamariki's own published description of its statutory pathway and annual activity.

### The reporting workload is rising sharply — which stresses triage

Oranga Tamariki's 2024/25 Annual Report (period 1 July 2024–30 June 2025) states reports of concern "rose sharply — up 44% overall, with a 17% increase in individual children with reports." [Oranga Tamariki: 2025 Annual Report summary](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/news/2025-annual-report-reflecting-on-progress-and-continuing-to-improve/) Earlier official reporting had already flagged the trend: more than **95,000** reports of concern were made in calendar 2024, about **35% more than 2023**, a level not seen since at least 2012. [RNZ, on 95,000 ROCs](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/545365/oranga-tamariki-gets-95-000-reports-about-possible-at-risk-children) Oranga Tamariki has published research trying to understand the increase, noting it did not yet fully know the drivers. [Oranga Tamariki research: Understanding the increase in reports of concern](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/research/our-research/understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/)

A rising ROC volume matters for the "nobody could help them" pattern because triage is where a report either becomes a response or a "no further action" — and the Monitor has flagged "a high proportion of reports of concern from professionals that do not result in further action" as a persistent concern. [RNZ, on the Monitor's review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report) Whether that NFA rate reflects appropriate triage or missed risk is precisely the kind of question a child research issue should test against aggregate outcome data — not assume.

**Confidence: High** on the rising volume (Oranga Tamariki's own annual report plus contemporaneous reporting); **Medium** on what the volume *means* for missed risk, which needs the numbers child.

### Independent oversight exists — and its verdict is critical

Since **1 May 2023** the Independent Children's Monitor (Aroturuki Tamariki), an independent Crown entity, has monitored the entire Oranga Tamariki system under the Oversight of the Oranga Tamariki System Act 2022. [Independent Children's Monitor — Wikipedia (background)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_Children's_Monitor); [Aroturuki Tamariki: Reports](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports) It publishes recurring official reports that map exactly the terrain this stream cares about: an annual **"Experiences of Care in Aotearoa"** report on compliance with the National Care Standards Regulations (editions through 2024/25, published March 2026); an annual **outcomes report for tamariki and rangatahi Māori** (second edition June 2026); and in-depth reviews including **"Towards a stronger safety net to prevent abuse of children"** — a review of implementation of the Poutasi recommendations. [Aroturuki Tamariki: Reports](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports)

The Monitor's implementation review of the Poutasi recommendations was blunt: "System settings have not changed, gaps remain and tamariki and rangatahi are still no more likely to be seen by Oranga Tamariki now than when [the child] was killed." It specifically found that "Police and Oranga Tamariki, for the most part, undertake reviews in isolation from other agencies," and that a high proportion of professional reports of concern still do not result in further action. [RNZ, on the Monitor's review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report)

Independent oversight is not confined to the Monitor: the **Auditor-General**'s May 2025 inquiry into Oranga Tamariki procurement and contract management found it was "poorly prepared" for its 2024/25 contracting round, that awards were (with a few exceptions) not subject to open, transparent, competitive procurement, that providers were not consistently paid on time, that there was no system to manage conflicts of interest, and that there was no evidence te Tiriti o Waitangi partners were factored into decisions. [Auditor-General inquiry summary (Wayback snapshot)](https://web.archive.org/web/20260202005142/https://oag.parliament.nz/2025/oranga-tamariki/summary.htm); [1News, on the Auditor-General report](https://www.1news.co.nz/2025/05/15/auditor-general-releases-scathing-report-on-oranga-tamariki/) The **Ombudsman** is a further oversight channel — under the Oversight of the Oranga Tamariki System Act 2022 the Ombudsman handles complaints and can undertake own-motion investigations across the Oranga Tamariki system (including its approved care providers) — that a child research issue should map alongside the Monitor and Auditor-General. [Ombudsman NZ: Oversight of the Oranga Tamariki system](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/children-care/oversight-oranga-tamariki)

**Confidence: High** that critical independent oversight exists and has reported serious concerns; the Auditor-General finding is corroborated by multiple outlets and its own summary.

### The "nobody's job" gap: cross-agency handoffs

The recurring failure mode in NZ child-protection reviews is not usually a single agency's decision but the space *between* agencies — health, education, Police, Corrections, and Oranga Tamariki each holding a piece and none holding the whole child. Poutasi's five critical gaps included that "agencies and services are not proactively sharing information despite having the ability," that risk assessment is "too narrow and one-dimensional," and that dependent children of charged/prosecuted sole parents are not well identified. [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html) The Monitor's later review found agencies still largely review in isolation. [RNZ, on the Monitor's review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report) The government's response is trying to close this: a **multi-agency / inter-agency response hub** co-locating Oranga Tamariki, Corrections, Health NZ, Ministry of Education, MSD and Police to share information on at-risk children was reported as beginning in early 2026, and a **mandatory child-protection training** module for core children's workers began testing in January 2026 across six agencies, with expansion toward tens of thousands of workers signalled. [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html) Whether these hubs exist in every community, and whether co-location actually changes information-sharing behaviour, is a live question the Monitor itself has flagged (multi-agency teams "not in place in all communities"). [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html)

**Confidence: Medium-High** — the cross-agency gap and the reform intent are officially documented; the *effectiveness* of the new hubs is not yet evidenced and should not be assumed.

### What's been recommended — and how far it's got

Poutasi made **14 recommendations** in 2022. The government progressively responded and in **September–October 2025 accepted all 14**, committing to an integrated cross-agency response overseen by Child and Youth Ministers, with the Privacy Commissioner working alongside agencies on information sharing. [Beehive: Strengthened system protects children from harm (9 Oct 2025)](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/strengthened-system-protects-children-harm); [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html) Recommendations 8 and 9 (mandatory training and mandatory reporting) are the most consequential and the slowest: mandatory reporting would require legislative change, and as of early 2026 the work was at design/pilot stage. [MSD: Integrated Government Response to the Poutasi Review](https://www.msd.govt.nz/about-msd-and-our-work/child-wellbeing-and-poverty-reduction/integrated-government-response-to-the-poutasi-review.html) The gap between "recommendations accepted" and "children measurably safer" is the crux — and the Monitor's review says that gap has not yet closed. [RNZ, on the Monitor's review](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/587189/children-still-no-safer-than-when-malachi-subecz-was-murdered-latest-report)

**Confidence: High** on the recommendation count and acceptance; **Medium** on implementation status, which is moving and needs the recommendations child to track precisely against primary cross-agency progress reports.

### The right data path is aggregate and official — no case detail needed

The core questions in this stream appear researchable from aggregate official sources: Oranga Tamariki's quarterly performance reports and ROC research; the Aroturuki Tamariki report series (system oversight, care-standards compliance, Māori outcomes, Poutasi implementation); Auditor-General and Ombudsman reports; MSD's integrated-response documentation; and Stats NZ's Integrated Data Commons entry for Oranga Tamariki ROC data. Some fields, though — response-timeframe compliance, the professional-report NFA rate over time, and recovery/outcome detail — may only be available in restricted IDI microdata (accessed via a Stats NZ Data Lab code module, not a fully public dataset) or may not be published at aggregate level at all; confirming which is public is itself a task for the numbers child (#301). [Aroturuki Tamariki: Reports](https://aroturuki.govt.nz/reports); [Stats NZ IDC: Oranga Tamariki ROC](https://idcommons.discourse.group/pub/oranga-tamariki-reports-of-concern-roc); [Oranga Tamariki research: Understanding the increase in reports of concern](https://www.orangatamariki.govt.nz/about-us/research/our-research/understanding-the-increase-in-reports-of-concern/) Children research **must** aggregate, suppress small cells, and never publish or reconstruct any individual case — this is a strict guardrail, not a preference.

**Confidence: High** that the aggregate data surfaces exist; the numbers child should confirm exactly which timeliness and outcome fields are published versus only held in restricted IDI microdata.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A human with domain, clinical, or rights authority must weigh in before "the system is failing" is treated as settled** (Constitution Art. III). This framing gathers officially-documented gaps; it does not adjudicate whether triage decisions, NFA rates, or care outcomes are *wrong* in aggregate — that needs lived experience (tamariki, whānau, caregivers, front-line social workers), clinical judgement, and Te Tiriti / iwi partnership, none of which an agent can supply.
- **A party's account of itself is a claim, not confirmation.** Oranga Tamariki's annual-report activity figures and the government's "recommendations accepted" statements describe intent and self-reported output; the Monitor's and Auditor-General's independent findings are the counter-weight. If a future independent review found measurable improvement (multi-agency hubs operating everywhere, professional-report NFA rates falling with no rise in missed risk, mandatory reporting legislated and evaluated), the "gaps persist" conclusion would soften.
- **Could not verify from primary data in this pass:** the actual share of urgent/critical ROCs met within their statutory timeframe; the professional-report NFA rate over time and whether it tracks missed risk; how many communities have functioning multi-agency teams; recovery/outcome rates for children after a ROC; the current legislative status of mandatory reporting. Each is a child research issue below.
- **De-identification is a hard limit, not a caveat.** No child, family, or worker is named or reconstructed here, and no children research may do so. Where an official quotation contained a child's name it has been redacted to "[the child]"; the deceased child is publicly named in some source titles and URLs (which are the genuine links and are not altered), but this framing's own prose and analysis do not reproduce the name. The OAG page was verified via a Wayback snapshot (its live page is Cloudflare-protected, i.e. blocked/tooling, not dead).
