# Most eligible NZ families get child/family entitlements, but a persistent ~10–25% take-up gap sits with working (non-beneficiary) families

## Executive answer

- **Working for Families (WFF) take-up is high overall but not complete.** MSD estimates overall take-up was **87% of eligible families in the 2020 tax year**, and has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall" since 2007. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **The WFF gap is concentrated among families without a main benefit, not beneficiaries.** Take-up is "close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income" (because the payment is administered alongside the benefit — the study's own explanation) but "**between 75 and 85 percent** for eligible families without main benefit income" — i.e. up to a quarter of that group miss out. ("Without main benefit income" is largely, but not exactly, working families — it is the study's category, used as shorthand below.) [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **A broader benefits survey shows a similar beneficiary/non-beneficiary split (supporting, not child-specific).** In MSD's Income Support Survey (working-age people 18–64 on low-to-middle incomes — *not* only families with children), among those who appeared potentially eligible for the Accommodation Supplement only **43.9% self-reported receiving** it, and those **not** on a main benefit were statistically significantly *less* likely to report receiving it than beneficiaries. Non-take-up had three roughly comparable drivers: lack of awareness (~⅓), believing oneself ineligible despite appearing eligible (~⅓), and being aware but not applying. This corroborates the *direction* of the WFF pattern in a different entitlement, but covers a wider population than families with children. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)
- **System design moves the number.** The 2018 Best Start tax credit (paid via the same WFF channel) lifted take-up of the main WFF credits for families with very young children by **~5 percentage points** — evidence that proactive, streamlined enrolment closes part of the gap. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **Bottom line for the stream:** a real, measurable share of *eligible* families do not receive support they qualify for — so non-take-up (not just ineligibility) is part of the harm. This evidence does **not** on its own isolate the dominant cause, and does not rule out benefit adequacy, eligibility design, administrative churn, distrust, or application burden as larger factors. Whether the right remedy is a navigation/awareness tool or something else is a hypothesis to test next (see follow-ups), not a conclusion this finding can carry.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the WFF take-up estimates are from an official MSD administrative-data study, and the Accommodation Supplement survey shows a similar (though broader-population, non-child-specific) benefit-status split. The two most recent WFF figures stop at the 2020 tax year. The Accommodation Supplement figure (43.9%) is *self-reported* receipt among people who *appeared* potentially eligible — not an administrative take-up rate — with an uncertain direction of bias (potential-eligibility misclassification could push apparent take-up down; self-report error could go either way), so read it as directional, not exact.

## Evidence

### Working for Families: 87% overall, but 75–85% for working families

MSD's analysis of WFF eligibility and take-up over the 2007–2020 tax years, published on the Social Investment Agency's research Hub, estimates that **87% of eligible families took up the payments in the 2020 tax year**, and that the overall rate has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The headline number hides the important split. Take-up is "**close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income**" — for these families the family tax credit is administered alongside the benefit, so there is effectively nothing extra to claim. For "**eligible families without main benefit income**" — working families who must claim through Inland Revenue — take-up "ranged between **75 and 85 percent**". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The same study notes "families with one or more parent of Asian ethnicity had low estimated take-up in recent years", flagging possible drivers (data-linkage error, claiming barriers, income not captured in administrative data) that it says warrant further investigation — i.e. MSD does not treat the ethnic gap as fully explained. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Best Start: a design change that lifted take-up ~5pp

The study links a measurable improvement to system design, though it hedges the causal claim ("it appears that…"): "the 2018 introduction of the Best Start tax credit … increased take-up of the main Working for Families tax credits by families with very young children by around **five percentage points**." Because Best Start prompts a WFF registration around a birth, it pulled some previously non-claiming families into the main credits. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Accommodation Supplement: a take-up gap concentrated the same way

MSD's Income Support Survey (Findings Pack 7; 1,852 respondents, fieldwork June–December 2022) surveyed working-age people (18–64) and identified those "potentially eligible" for the Accommodation Supplement — **52.9% of respondents** met the combined criteria (in eligible housing, meeting residency, below cash and income limits, paying enough toward accommodation). [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

Among those potentially eligible, only **43.9% self-reported that they were receiving** the Accommodation Supplement (56.1% said no, didn't know, or preferred not to say). Crucially, self-reported receipt tracked benefit status the same way WFF take-up does: respondents **receiving a main benefit were statistically significantly *more* likely** to say they were receiving it, while those **not** on a main benefit were significantly *less* likely — the same beneficiary/working-family split. This 43.9% is self-report among people who *appeared* eligible (with wide margins of error and possible mis-identification of eligibility), so the *level* is uncertain and its direction of bias is not established — but the beneficiary/non-beneficiary *direction* of the split is statistically significant. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

The survey also quantifies *why* eligible people miss out, and it is not awareness alone. Of the 56.1% non-take-up, MSD attributes just over a third (33.6%) to people **not being aware** of the payment (81.0% of the potentially eligible had heard of it; 18.2% had not). The remaining two-thirds were aware but still didn't receive it: **35.9% of aware non-recipients thought they were not eligible** despite appearing eligible — most commonly (60.7%) because they believed they earned above the income threshold, even though their measured income was below it — and others were aware and possibly eligible but had not applied. Understanding that a non-beneficiary *can* receive the payment was actually high and consistent across sub-groups (67–91%), with no statistically significant difference between beneficiaries and non-beneficiaries. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

This echoes the WFF pattern from a different measurement angle: benefit recipients were significantly more likely to receive the payment than non-recipients. (Note the mechanism differs by entitlement — the WFF source describes near-automatic *administration* for main-benefit families, whereas the AS survey only shows main-benefit recipients were more likely to *self-report receiving* it, not that AS enrolment is automatic.) For AS the driver is a mix of awareness *and* mistaken self-assessed ineligibility, not awareness alone.

## Surprising or load-bearing claims

Supporting analogue only — not child-specific. | Medium |

Both claims are load-bearing for the stream's direction. The WFF benefit-status split is a single-source (MSD administrative) finding; the Accommodation Supplement survey is a *supporting analogue* in a different entitlement and a broader (non-child-specific) population, so it strengthens the direction of the pattern but does not independently establish it *for families with children*. The *specific 87%* figure rests on one study and should be treated as a good official estimate, not a hard fact.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A more recent WFF take-up estimate.** The figures stop at the 2020 tax year. Post-2020 changes (COVID income volatility, WFF threshold/abatement changes, IRD's automatic year-end assessments) could have moved take-up up or down. A refreshed MSD/IRD estimate would supersede this.
- **An administrative take-up *rate* for the Accommodation Supplement.** The 43.9% figure is self-reported receipt among survey respondents who *appeared* eligible, with wide margins of error and an uncertain direction of bias — not an administrative estimate. An MSD/IRD administrative take-up rate would supersede this and could be higher or lower; if it shows near-complete take-up, the AS gap would matter less than the survey implies.
- **Evidence that the "gap" is largely measurement error.** MSD itself flags that low estimated take-up (e.g. for Asian families) may partly reflect data-linkage error or income not captured in admin data. If most of the apparent gap is definitional, the real behavioural shortfall is smaller.
- **This needs a human with frontline/lived experience.** The survey already gives a quantified first pass at the mechanism (~⅓ unaware, ~⅓ wrongly believe they're ineligible, the rest aware-but-not-applying), but *why* eligible people wrongly self-assess as ineligible — income confusion, application complexity, or distrust — determines what tool would help. Someone from Citizens Advice Bureau, a budgeting service, or MSD frontline should sense-check this before anything is designed.
