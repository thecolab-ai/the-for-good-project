# Most eligible NZ families get child/family entitlements, but a persistent ~10–25% take-up gap sits with working (non-beneficiary) families

## Executive answer

- **Working for Families (WFF) take-up is high overall but not complete.** MSD estimates overall take-up was **87% of eligible families in the 2020 tax year**, and has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall" since 2007. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **The gap is concentrated among working families, not beneficiaries.** Take-up is "close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income" (because the payment is largely automatic once you're on a benefit) but "**between 75 and 85 percent** for eligible families without main benefit income" — i.e. up to a quarter of eligible working families miss out. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **The Accommodation Supplement shows the same pattern for a different reason: awareness.** In MSD's Income Support Survey, among people who appeared potentially eligible, only **81% had even heard of the payment** before the survey (18.2% were unaware), and understanding that non-beneficiary workers can qualify was far lower among low-income (~37%), middle-income (20.3%) and high-income (5.7%) non-beneficiaries than among beneficiaries. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)
- **System design moves the number.** The 2018 Best Start tax credit (paid via the same WFF channel) lifted take-up of the main WFF credits for families with very young children by **~5 percentage points** — evidence that proactive, streamlined enrolment closes part of the gap. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **Bottom line for the stream:** the avoidable harm isn't "families don't qualify" — it's that eligible *working* families either don't know they qualify or don't complete the claim. That points at a navigation/awareness tool, not a new entitlement.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the WFF take-up estimates are from an official MSD administrative-data study and are internally consistent with the Accommodation Supplement survey, but the two most recent WFF figures stop at the 2020 tax year, and no equivalent national take-up rate was found for the Accommodation Supplement itself (only self-reported awareness).

## Evidence

### Working for Families: 87% overall, but 75–85% for working families

MSD's analysis of WFF eligibility and take-up over the 2007–2020 tax years, published on the Social Investment Agency's research Hub, estimates that **87% of eligible families took up the payments in the 2020 tax year**, and that the overall rate has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The headline number hides the important split. Take-up is "**close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income**" — for these families the family tax credit is administered alongside the benefit, so there is effectively nothing extra to claim. For "**eligible families without main benefit income**" — working families who must claim through Inland Revenue — take-up "ranged between **75 and 85 percent**". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The same study notes "families with one or more parent of Asian ethnicity had low estimated take-up in recent years", flagging possible drivers (data-linkage error, claiming barriers, income not captured in administrative data) that it says warrant further investigation — i.e. MSD does not treat the ethnic gap as fully explained. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Best Start: a design change that lifted take-up ~5pp

The study attributes a measurable improvement to system design: "the 2018 introduction of the Best Start tax credit … increased take-up of the main Working for Families tax credits by families with very young children by around **five percentage points**." Because Best Start prompts a WFF registration around a birth, it pulled some previously non-claiming families into the main credits. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Accommodation Supplement: an awareness gap, concentrated the same way

MSD's Income Support Survey (Findings Pack 7) surveyed working-age people (18–64) and identified those "potentially eligible" for the Accommodation Supplement — **52.9% of respondents** met the combined criteria (in eligible housing, meeting residency, below cash and income limits, paying enough toward accommodation). [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

Among those potentially eligible, **81.0% were aware** of the Accommodation Supplement and **18.2% were not aware** of it before the survey. Understanding that a non-beneficiary worker can receive it was much lower than for beneficiaries: among aware-and-eligible respondents, the share who understood that people *not* on a main benefit could qualify was around **37% for low-income**, **20.3% for middle-income** and **5.7% for high-income** non-beneficiaries — versus much higher understanding among those already receiving a main benefit (85.3% aware). [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

This is the same structural story as WFF from a different measurement angle: people connected to the benefit system are enrolled almost automatically; working families outside it are far less likely to know the payment exists or that it applies to them.

The two claims above are load-bearing for the stream's direction. The *pattern* claim is corroborated by two independent MSD datasets (administrative-data take-up estimates and a self-report survey). The *specific 87%* figure rests on one study and should be treated as a good official estimate, not a hard fact.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A more recent WFF take-up estimate.** The figures stop at the 2020 tax year. Post-2020 changes (COVID income volatility, WFF threshold/abatement changes, IRD's automatic year-end assessments) could have moved take-up up or down. A refreshed MSD/IRD estimate would supersede this.
- **A real take-up *rate* for the Accommodation Supplement.** I found awareness and self-reported receipt framing, but not a clean "X% of eligible people receive it" administrative estimate. If MSD's eligible-population modelling shows near-complete take-up, the AS awareness gap would matter less than implied.
- **Evidence that the "gap" is largely measurement error.** MSD itself flags that low estimated take-up (e.g. for Asian families) may partly reflect data-linkage error or income not captured in admin data. If most of the apparent gap is definitional, the real behavioural shortfall is smaller.
- **This needs a human with frontline/lived experience.** Whether the barrier is *awareness*, *application complexity*, or *distrust* determines what tool would help. Someone from Citizens Advice Bureau, a budgeting service, or MSD frontline should sense-check the mechanism before anything is designed.
