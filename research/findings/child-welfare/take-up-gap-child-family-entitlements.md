# Most eligible NZ families get child/family entitlements, but a persistent ~10–25% take-up gap sits with families without a main benefit

## Executive answer

- **Working for Families (WFF) take-up is high overall but not complete.** MSD estimates overall take-up was **87% of eligible families in the 2020 tax year**, and has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall" since 2007. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **The WFF gap is concentrated among families without a main benefit, not beneficiaries.** Take-up is "close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income" (because the payment is administered alongside the benefit — the study's own explanation) but "**between 75 and 85 percent** for eligible families without main benefit income" — i.e. up to a quarter of that group miss out. (This is the study's measured category. It overlaps heavily with working families but is not identical — it can include non-beneficiary families not in paid work — so this finding uses "without a main benefit", not "working families", as the operative group.) [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **Best Start take-up is very high (~97%) — and shows the same benefit-status split.** A separate MSD administrative study estimates the Best Start tax credit was taken up for **96.9% of children in eligible families**, but with the same gradient as WFF: **99% for children in main-benefit families, 97% for paid-parental-leave families, and 94% for other eligible families**. So even for a near-universal payment, non-beneficiary families are the ones most likely to miss out — a child-specific corroboration of the pattern. [MSD via SIA Hub, Best Start take-up, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-take-up-of-the-best-start-tax-credit)
- **A broader benefits survey shows the same split for a third entitlement.** In MSD's Income Support Survey (working-age people 18–64 on low-to-middle incomes — *not* only families with children), among those who appeared potentially eligible for the Accommodation Supplement only **43.9% self-reported receiving** it, and those **not** on a main benefit were statistically significantly *less* likely to report receiving it than beneficiaries. Of those not receiving it, **32.3% were unaware** of the payment; of the aware non-recipients, **35.9% believed they were ineligible** (most because they thought they earned too much). Covers a wider population than families with children, so it is a supporting analogue. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)
- **System design *may* move the number (weak, single-source).** One MSD study suggests the 2018 Best Start tax credit (paid via the same WFF channel) lifted take-up of the main WFF credits for families with very young children by ~5 percentage points — but the source hedges this ("it appears that…") and it is *not* independently corroborated, so treat it as a lead, not established causality. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)
- **Bottom line for the stream:** a real, measurable share of *eligible* families do not receive support they qualify for — so non-take-up (not just ineligibility) is part of the harm. This evidence does **not** on its own isolate the dominant cause, and does not rule out benefit adequacy, eligibility design, administrative churn, distrust, or application burden as larger factors. Whether the right remedy is a navigation/awareness tool or something else is a hypothesis to test next (see follow-ups), not a conclusion this finding can carry.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — two independent MSD administrative studies (WFF and Best Start) show the same benefit-status split for child/family entitlements, and a third (the Accommodation Supplement survey) shows it for a broader population. The two most recent WFF figures stop at the 2020 tax year. The Accommodation Supplement figure (43.9%) is *self-reported* receipt among people who *appeared* potentially eligible — not an administrative take-up rate — with an uncertain direction of bias (potential-eligibility misclassification could push apparent take-up down; self-report error could go either way), so read it as directional, not exact.

## Evidence

### Working for Families: 87% overall, but 75–85% for families without a main benefit

MSD's analysis of WFF eligibility and take-up over the 2007–2020 tax years, published on the Social Investment Agency's research Hub, estimates that **87% of eligible families took up the payments in the 2020 tax year**, and that the overall rate has been "relatively stable over time, ranging between 83 and 90 percent overall". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The headline number hides the important split. Take-up is "**close to 100 percent for eligible families with main benefit income**" — for these families the family tax credit is administered alongside the benefit, so there is effectively nothing extra to claim. For "**eligible families without main benefit income**" — who must instead claim through Inland Revenue rather than having it administered automatically — take-up "ranged between **75 and 85 percent**". [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

The same study notes "families with one or more parent of Asian ethnicity had low estimated take-up in recent years", flagging possible drivers (data-linkage error, claiming barriers, income not captured in administrative data) that it says warrant further investigation — i.e. MSD does not treat the ethnic gap as fully explained. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Best Start: ~97% take-up, but the same benefit-status gradient

A separate MSD administrative study (also on the SIA Hub, Dec 2022) estimates that **96.5% of children born July 2018–September 2019 had a family eligible for Best Start**, and that Best Start "was taken up for an estimated **96.9 percent of the children in eligible families**". Take-up is very high but not uniform, and the gradient matches WFF: **99% for children in eligible families supported by a main benefit, 97% for those on paid parental leave (no benefit), and 94% for other eligible families**. So the families most likely to miss out are, again, those *not* connected to the benefit system. [MSD via SIA Hub, Best Start take-up, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-take-up-of-the-best-start-tax-credit)

The same WFF study also offers a hedged, single-source observation that Best Start lifted take-up of the *other* WFF credits: it says "it appears that" the 2018 introduction of Best Start "increased take-up of the main Working for Families tax credits by families with very young children by around **five percentage points**", the suggested mechanism being that Best Start prompts a WFF registration around a birth. This is a plausible design-effect lead but is **not independently corroborated** and the source itself hedges the causation, so it should not carry a solution-direction conclusion on its own. [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020)

### Accommodation Supplement: a take-up gap concentrated the same way

MSD's Income Support Survey (Findings Pack 7; 1,852 respondents, fieldwork June–December 2022) surveyed working-age people (18–64) and identified those "potentially eligible" for the Accommodation Supplement — **52.9% of respondents** met the combined criteria (in eligible housing, meeting residency, below cash and income limits, paying enough toward accommodation). [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

Among those potentially eligible, only **43.9% self-reported that they were receiving** the Accommodation Supplement (56.1% said no, didn't know, or preferred not to say). Crucially, self-reported receipt tracked benefit status the same way WFF take-up does: respondents **receiving a main benefit were statistically significantly *more* likely** to say they were receiving it, while those **not** on a main benefit were significantly *less* likely — the same beneficiary/non-beneficiary split. This 43.9% is self-report among people who *appeared* eligible (with wide margins of error and possible mis-identification of eligibility), so the *level* is uncertain and its direction of bias is not established — but the beneficiary/non-beneficiary *direction* of the split is statistically significant. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

The survey also quantifies *why* eligible people miss out, and it is not awareness alone — though the figures use different denominators and should not be read as equal thirds. Of the potentially-eligible people **not receiving** the payment, **32.3% said they were not aware** of it (across all potentially eligible, 81.0% had heard of it and 18.2% had not). Among the **aware non-recipients**, **35.9% thought they were not eligible** despite appearing eligible — and of those, **60.7% (± 15.6) said it was because they believed they earned above the income threshold**, even though their measured income was below it. The remainder were aware and possibly eligible but had not applied. Separately, understanding that a non-beneficiary *can* receive the payment was high and consistent across sub-groups (67–91%), with no statistically significant difference between beneficiaries and non-beneficiaries. [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf)

This echoes the WFF and Best Start patterns from a different measurement angle: benefit recipients were significantly more likely to receive the payment than non-recipients [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf). The mechanism differs by entitlement, though: the WFF study describes near-automatic *administration* of the family tax credit for main-benefit families [MSD via SIA Hub, 2022](https://thehub.sia.govt.nz/sitemap/estimates-of-working-for-families-eligibility-and-take-up-rates-2007-2020), whereas the Accommodation Supplement survey only shows main-benefit recipients were more likely to *self-report receiving* it — not that AS enrolment is automatic [MSD Income Support Survey, Findings Pack 7](https://www.msd.govt.nz/documents/about-msd-and-our-work/work-programmes/income-support-survey/findings-pack-7-accommodation-supplement-self-reported-take-up-and-other-findings.pdf). For AS specifically the driver is a mix of awareness *and* mistaken self-assessed ineligibility, not awareness alone.

The benefit-status split is corroborated by **two independent MSD administrative studies of child/family entitlements** (WFF and Best Start), which is why it carries Medium–High confidence; the Accommodation Supplement survey adds a third, broader-population signal but is not child-specific. The *specific 87%* WFF figure rests on one study and should be treated as a good official estimate, not a hard fact.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A more recent WFF take-up estimate.** The figures stop at the 2020 tax year. Unverified post-2020 developments (income volatility, any subsequent WFF settings changes, and changes to how IRD reconciles entitlements at year end) could have moved take-up up or down. A refreshed MSD/IRD estimate would supersede this.
- **An administrative take-up *rate* for the Accommodation Supplement.** The 43.9% figure is self-reported receipt among survey respondents who *appeared* eligible, with wide margins of error and an uncertain direction of bias — not an administrative estimate. An MSD/IRD administrative take-up rate would supersede this and could be higher or lower; if it shows near-complete take-up, the AS gap would matter less than the survey implies.
- **Evidence that the "gap" is largely measurement error.** MSD itself flags that low estimated take-up (e.g. for Asian families) may partly reflect data-linkage error or income not captured in admin data. If most of the apparent gap is definitional, the real behavioural shortfall is smaller.
- **This needs a human with frontline/lived experience.** The survey already gives a quantified first pass at the mechanism (of non-recipients, 32.3% unaware; of aware non-recipients, 35.9% believe they're ineligible; the rest aware-but-not-applying), but *why* eligible people wrongly self-assess as ineligible — income confusion, application complexity, or distrust — determines what tool would help. Someone from Citizens Advice Bureau, a budgeting service, or MSD frontline should sense-check this before anything is designed.
