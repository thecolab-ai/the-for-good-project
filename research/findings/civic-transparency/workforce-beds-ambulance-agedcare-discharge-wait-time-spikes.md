# Workforce, beds, ambulance demand and aged-care discharge constraints are real, but public NZ data does not align them to district wait-time spikes at the resolution needed to test the staffing hypothesis

This is research child **#736 of Stream #434**. It answers one question: **do workforce (Health NZ, Medical Council, Nursing Council), hospital bed capacity/occupancy, Hato Hone St John ambulance demand, and aged-care discharge-delay / ARC-capacity constraints — measured by district and quarter — explain the district-level wait-time spikes established by the sibling baseline (#732)?**

## Executive answer

- **The constraints are real and independently documented, but they are published at mutually incompatible granularities — so the staffing/bed/discharge hypothesis cannot be cleanly tested against district wait-time spikes with public data.** The wait-time baseline (#732) reports 20 districts × quarter. The Medical Council reports doctors by **19 regions** (Capital & Coast merged with Hutt Valley) and *annually*, not quarterly [MCNZ, 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf). Health NZ's nursing-vacancy figures are **national point estimates** (10.8% → 6.8% from 2023 to 2024), not a district-quarter series [Health NZ, workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context). National hospital-bed stock is reported to the OECD (2.5 beds/1,000 vs OECD average 4.2), while the Ministry of Health's historical **Acute Hospital Bed Days** district-quarter files stop at June 2023, before the 2024-2026 spike window this stream is testing [OECD, Health at a Glance 2025, NZ country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html) (archived: [Wayback, 5 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705160308/https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html)); [MoH Acute Hospital Bed Days, archived 26 Mar 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20250326221102/https://www.health.govt.nz/statistics-research/system-monitoring/planning-and-performance-data/acute-hospital-bed-days). St John's 2025 demand release is **national** (706,194 calls; 551,399 incidents), not a district-quarter breakdown or hospital-handover/ramping time series [Hato Hone St John, 27 Jan 2026](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/) (archived: [Wayback, 9 Feb 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260209204930/https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/)).
- **The only district-resolved constraint evidence is *narrative*, not quantitative.** Health NZ's own quarterly factsheets describe, district by district, what each is doing about ED flow, discharge, theatre capacity and workforce — e.g. Capital & Coast (the worst ED district, 57.6% in Q3 25/26) is "recruiting eight Clinical Nurse Specialists and streamlining discharge processes… to increase hospital capacity"; Southern faces "long-standing capacity challenges in Urology, Orthopaedics, and Ophthalmology while securing anaesthetist capacity for Southland" [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf). These narratives align qualitatively with the worst-performing districts but do not constitute a matched statistical test.
- **Government's own policy framing treats the constraints as causal** — the DPMC ED factsheet lists the 140 fast-tracked hospital beds as going to **exactly the worst-ED districts** (Middlemore, Waikato, Wellington, Nelson, Hawke's Bay) and allocates "$24 million over four years (Budget 2025) to increase Aged Residential Care capacity and ease hospital discharge delays" [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, Sep 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf) (archived: [Wayback, 7 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260707192557/https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf)). The targeting of beds at the worst-performing districts is itself circumstantial evidence that bed capacity is believed to bind — but it is a policy response, not a measured correlation.
- **Aged-care discharge delay is the one constraint with district-level quantitative data — and it runs the *wrong* way for a simple "ARC shortage causes ED spike" story.** The Sapere review for Health NZ found high-priority dementia/psychogeriatric residents waited on average nearly six months for ARC admission after assessment, ranging from 92 days (MidCentral) to 219 days (West Coast) [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review) (via the commissioned Sapere report, as cited in sibling finding #518). But the district with the *longest* ARC admission wait (West Coast) is the **best** ED performer (95.6%), and MidCentral (shortest ARC wait, 92 days) is the **worst** ED performer (59.2%) [#732 baseline; Health NZ ethnicity/rurality breakdown Q3 25/26](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality-breakdown-q3-25-26.pdf). This is a directional *contradiction* of the naive hypothesis at the two endpoints, though two points cannot settle it.
- **Nursing is the one workforce constraint with a plausible national mechanism, but it cannot be localised.** Health NZ estimates a current all-nurse shortage of 2,250 (6.8% of total need), rising to 4,120 by 2033, with specialist nursing needs persisting even as general vacancies closed [Health NZ, nursing analysis](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/workforce-plan-profession-specific-analysis/health-workforce-plan-profession-nursing-analysis). The Aged Care Association reports a 15% RN vacancy rate and over 1,200 ARC beds closed in the prior year due to RN shortages [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). But none of this is published by hospital district, so it cannot explain why Capital & Coast (57.6%) and MidCentral (59.2%) trail while West Coast (95.6%) leads.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The *existence* and *direction* of each constraint is well-evidenced (High for the national facts). The *district-level explanatory claim* — that these constraints explain the wait-time spikes — is Low-to-untestable with current public data, because the constraint variables are not measured at the same district-quarter resolution as the wait-time outcomes. The conclusion is therefore that the hypothesis is plausible and partly supported by policy targeting and qualitative narratives, but not statistically testable from public data, and is contradicted at its strongest two data points by the ARC-discharge evidence.

## Evidence

### What the question requires and what "test the staffing hypothesis" means

The framing (PR #730) graded H5 ("staffing shortfalls correlate with wait-time spikes") as **plausible but unproven** and explicitly said "a child issue should test staffing, beds, occupancy and demand together rather than treating vacancies as the sole driver" [framing, H5](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/analysis/nz-public-hospital-wait-times-and-ed-overcrowding-framing.md). Testing it requires aligning, by district and quarter: (a) wait-time outcomes — available at 20 districts × quarter from sibling #732; (b) workforce — doctors, nurses, vacancies; (c) hospital beds and occupancy; (d) ambulance demand; and (e) aged-care discharge delay / ARC capacity. The finding below establishes what exists at each, and where the granularities break down.

### Workforce — doctors (Medical Council)

The Medical Council 2025 workforce survey reports 20,530 practising doctors in 2025, up 2.6% from 20,012 in 2024. Vocational-scope doctors grew 15% since 2020; emergency medicine grew 37.1% (from 350 to 480) — the second-largest increase after diagnostic radiology (40%, 570→800) [MCNZ, 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf). Emergency medicine is also the scope most reliant on internationally qualified doctors (IMGs): 58.5% of emergency-medicine doctors are IMGs, the highest of any scope [MCNZ, 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf).

**Regional distribution (Table 11, by FTE, population from Stats NZ June 2024 estimates):** the three largest regions by doctor FTE are Auckland (3,904.8 FTE; 18.2% of total FTE), Capital, Coast and Hutt Valley (2,428.7; 11.3%), and Canterbury (2,520.7; 11.8%). Over three-quarters of doctors (76.9%) are in the North Island [MCNZ, 2025](https://www.mcnz.org.nz/assets/Publications/Workforce-Survey/Workforce-Survey-Report-2025.pdf).

**The granularity mismatch is decisive.** MCNZ reports 19 regions — merging Capital & Coast with Hutt Valley into one — while Health NZ's wait-time baseline reports 20 districts with the two separate. The two cannot be joined without splitting the merged region (which MCNZ does not publish). MCNZ is also annual (a November survey snapshot), not quarterly. So even at its most granular, the medical workforce data is one step coarser in geography and four steps coarser in time than the wait-time outcomes it would need to explain. There is no public source of *vacancies* by district for doctors at all — the survey reports FTE in post, not unfilled positions.

### Workforce — nurses (Health NZ / Nursing Council)

Health NZ's workforce plan reports nursing vacancies fell from an estimated 10.8% in 2023 (26,505 nursing FTE) to 6.8% in 2024 (29,400 nursing FTE), attributing the closure mainly to internationally qualified nurses joining, while flagging that "specialist nursing roles" still have need [Health NZ, workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context). The nursing analysis puts the workforce at ~78,700 practising-certificate holders (March 2024) and estimates a **current all-nurse shortage of 2,250** (6.8% of total need), rising to 4,120 by 2033 on top of the current pipeline [Health NZ, nursing analysis](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/workforce-plan-profession-specific-analysis/health-workforce-plan-profession-nursing-analysis).

These are **national** figures. Health NZ does not publish nursing FTE or vacancy rates by the 20 hospital districts. The growth is also acknowledged to be uneven across settings: "This growth has been greater in our hospitals than in primary and community settings" [Health NZ, workforce-plan context](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/planning-and-performance/health-workforce-planning/health-workforce-plan-2024-detailed-analysis-and-data/health-workforce-plan-2024-context) — a directional signal, but again not district-resolved.

The aged-care nursing constraint is quantified separately by the sector: the NZ Aged Care Association's 2023 survey found a 15% RN vacancy rate, 83% of facilities not fully RN-staffed, and 33% annual RN turnover; NZACA reported over 1,200 ARC beds closed in the prior year due to RN shortages [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). The mechanism is pay disparity: ARC RNs earn up to $20,000 less than Health NZ hospital nurses, and 36% of RNs/ENs who left ARC in 2023 went to a Health NZ hospital [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf). This is detailed in sibling finding #518 (`arc-staffing-constraints.md`); it establishes ARC nurse shortage → bed closure strongly, but is a sector-wide (not hospital-district) measure.

### Hospital bed capacity and occupancy

This is the largest current-period data gap. New Zealand's hospital bed stock is reported to the OECD as **2.5 beds per 1,000 population, less than the OECD average of 4.2** [OECD, Health at a Glance 2025, NZ country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html) (archived: [Wayback, 5 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260705160308/https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html)). Applied to Stats NZ's June 2024 population estimate of ~5.287 million (the population base MCNZ uses), this implies roughly 13,200 acute beds nationally — versus ~22,200 if NZ matched the OECD average. The same OECD source records 3.7 practising doctors/1,000 (OECD avg 3.9) and 11.7 practising nurses/1,000 (OECD avg 9.2) — i.e. NZ is near-average on doctors, above-average on nurses per capita, but well below average on beds [OECD, NZ country note, 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2025_15a55280-en/new-zealand_2154f240-en.html).

That does **not** mean no district-quarter bed-utilisation dataset ever existed. The Ministry of Health's archived **Acute Hospital Bed Days** page says it published historical data "up to June 2023" for districts and health-system partners, with Quarter 2 files **By District of Domicile** and **By District of Service** [MoH Acute Hospital Bed Days, archived 26 Mar 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20250326221102/https://www.health.govt.nz/statistics-research/system-monitoring/planning-and-performance-data/acute-hospital-bed-days). The live MoH page returned HTTP 403 to `scripts/fetch.mjs`; the script classified it as `BLOCKED` because plain HTTP was blocked and browser rungs were unavailable in this sandbox, so the archived snapshot is the verified citation path rather than evidence the source is dead.

The remaining gap is narrower and more actionable: I found no public **2024-2026** district-quarter bed-number or occupancy series that aligns with the wait-time-spike window in sibling #732. The data.govt.nz catalogue (queried via the vendored `data-govt-nz` skill for "hospital beds", "hospital occupancy", "hospital bed number") returns no current national acute-bed dataset — the closest hit is a LINZ emergency-management basemap that carries *occupancy figures* for rest homes and hospitals only as a mapping layer, not a time series [data.govt.nz catalogue search, accessed 8 Jul 2026](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/emergency-management-basemap). Health NZ's quarterly health-target factsheets describe theatre *utilisation* and *capacity challenges* in prose per district but publish no bed counts or occupancy percentages [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).

The one concrete bed number in the public record is a **flow** measure, not a stock: the DPMC ED factsheet states the Government's "fast-tracked hospital wards programme will deliver **140 new beds** in the second half of 2026 across Middlemore, Waikato, Wellington, Nelson, and Hawke's Bay hospitals" [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, Sep 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf) (archived: [Wayback, 7 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260707192557/https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf)). Nelson Hospital's ED treatment spaces also expanded from 23 to 29 [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, Sep 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf).

### Ambulance demand (Hato Hone St John)

St John's January 2026 release reports 2025 as the busiest year on record: **706,194 emergency 111 calls** (up nearly 30% over five years) and **551,399 emergency ambulance incidents** (up 17% since 2020), with August 2025 the highest monthly call volume ever at 63,888 [Hato Hone St John, 27 Jan 2026](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/) (archived: [Wayback, 9 Feb 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260209204930/https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/)). Demand pressure is attributed to "wider system pressures, an ageing population and rising acuity," and was "most acute over winter." Nearly half of all ambulance responses involved people aged 65 and over, and falls (52,559 incidents, up 8%) are "now one of the single largest drivers of ambulance demand" [Hato Hone St John, 27 Jan 2026](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/ambulance-workload-reaches-new-highs/).

**Crucially for this question, the cited St John release does not publish a district-quarter demand breakdown or any hospital handover-delay / ramping / offload time series.** St John has published some localised demand descriptors elsewhere — for example, its 2023 demand release named Auckland, Christchurch, Hamilton/Waikato, Manawatu, and Western Bay of Plenty as the busiest response locations [Hato Hone St John, 22 Jan 2024](https://www.stjohn.org.nz/news--info/news--articles/growth-in-demand-for-hato-hone-st-john-ambulance-services-continues/). But that is not a systematic district-quarter series that can be joined to Health NZ's 20-district wait-time panel. This confirms the framing's note that "the public ambulance source found here does not publish hospital handover-delay minutes or ramping hours for New Zealand" [framing, "What is already being done"](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/analysis/nz-public-hospital-wait-times-and-ed-overcrowding-framing.md). Without a ramping metric, ambulance demand can only be linked to ED waits through inference — and even the national demand number cannot be allocated to the 20 districts. (Health NZ's Q1 factsheet does note one ambulance-related flow intervention: MidCentral "partnered with Hato Hone St John Ambulance to provide Practice Plus vouchers for telehealth and after-hours care" to divert non-ED patients [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf) — a qualitative signal that ambulance-to-ED flow is a recognised problem in at least one district.)

### Aged-care discharge delay and ARC capacity

This is the one constraint with district-level quantitative data, drawn from the Sapere Research Group review commissioned by Health NZ for its Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review. The review found high-priority dementia/psychogeriatric residents waited **on average nearly six months** for ARC admission after assessment, **ranging from 92 days (MidCentral) to 219 days (West Coast)** [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review) (via the commissioned Sapere report; the district-day figures are documented in sibling finding #518, `arc-staffing-constraints.md`). The review's stated aim is a model "effective at reducing avoidable hospital admissions and bed days" [Health NZ, Aged Care Funding and Services Model Review](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review). Budget 2025 added "$24 million over four years… to increase Aged Residential Care capacity and ease hospital discharge delays" [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, Sep 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-11/gt-factsheet-target-1-sep25.pdf).

The ARC bed stock is under documented pressure: ~32,000 people live in ARC; the NZACA reports over 1,200 ARC beds closed in the year prior to the Infometrics report due to RN shortages [Infometrics for NZNO, 2025](https://www.nzno.org.nz/Portals/0/publications/Report%20-%20Infometrics%20report%20how%20many%20more%20nurses%20does%20New%20Zealand%20need%20August%202025%20FINAL.pdf); and earlier section-31 notice reporting tracked bed closures rising from ~200–300 (2020) to 841 (2021) and over 800 in the first five months of 2022 [Kaitiaki Nursing NZ, 2022](https://kaitiaki.org.nz/article/nurse-leader-pleads-for-funding-as-staffing-shortage-forces-closure-of-900-aged-care-beds/) (as cited in #518).

**The directional puzzle.** If ARC discharge delay were a primary driver of ED waits, one would expect districts with longer ARC waits to have worse ED performance. The two cleanest data points run the opposite way:

| District | ARC admission wait (high-priority, days) | ED ≤6h performance (Q3 25/26) |
|---|---|---|
| West Coast | 219 (longest) | 95.6% (best) |
| MidCentral | 92 (shortest) | 59.2% (worst) |

Sources: ARC wait from [Sapere review via Health NZ / #518](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/aged-care-funding-and-services-model-review); ED performance from [Health NZ, Q3 25/26 ethnicity/rurality breakdown](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality-breakdown-q3-25-26.pdf).

Two points cannot settle a relationship, and the ARC wait measures *admission* delay for high-priority dementia/psychogeriatric residents (a specific sub-stream), not general hospital-to-ARC discharge delay. But it is the strongest available signal that the simple "ARC shortage → blocked hospital beds → ED spike" chain is not monotonic across districts, and that something else (ED process, hospital size, rural casemix, the rural-generalist model) dominates in West Coast. This deserves a proper panel analysis once (or if) the underlying discharge-delay data is released.

### Qualitative district narratives — the only district-resolved constraint evidence

Health NZ's Q1 2025/26 factsheets give a paragraph per district on operational causes and responses. These are the closest thing to district-level constraint evidence, and they align qualitatively with the worst performers:

- **Capital & Coast** (worst ED, 57.6%): "improving patient flow through recruitment of eight Clinical Nurse Specialists and streamlining discharge processes—particularly on weekends and after-hours—to increase hospital capacity" [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).
- **MidCentral** (worst ED, 59.2%): partnered with St John for telehealth/after-hours diversion; ran Saturday theatre lists for lengthy-wait specialties [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).
- **Southern** (elective 53.3%): "long-standing capacity challenges in Urology, Orthopaedics, and Ophthalmology while securing anaesthetist capacity for Southland" [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).
- **West Coast** (best ED, 95.6%): attributes strong performance to "effective communication between ED and ward teams… increased discharge rates… criteria-led discharge," plus a rural-generalist model that "reduces the need for patient travel by keeping care delivery local" [Health NZ, Q1 2025/26 factsheets](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/health-targets-factsheets-Q1-2025-26.pdf).

These narratives consistently name **discharge, capacity, anaesthetist/theatre staffing, and ED-to-ward flow** as the operational levers — matching the constraint variables in this question. But they are prose, not a matched quantitative panel, and they describe *responses* and *self-diagnoses*, which are not the same as measured causal links.

## What would change this conclusion

- **Reinstating or extending the historical Acute Hospital Bed Days district-quarter series beyond June 2023** would let a real panel regression test whether bed utilisation predicts ED six-hour, FSA and elective-treatment performance during the 2024-2026 spike window. This is the single highest-value data release for this question, and its current-period absence is the binding constraint on the analysis — not the absence of a hypothesis.
- **A district-level nursing-vacancy series** (not just the national 10.8%→6.8% point estimates) would let the staffing hypothesis be localised. Health NZ holds this operationally; publishing it by district and quarter would directly enable the test this issue was set up to run.
- **A public hospital handover-delay / ramping time series by hospital** from St John or Health NZ would connect ambulance demand to ED access block at the point the framing (#434) identifies as the ED-overcrowding mechanism. Its continued absence means the ambulance leg of the hypothesis is inference-only.
- **Release of the underlying Sapere discharge-delay data** at district resolution (the 92–219 day range is a summary; the per-district detail is presumably in the commissioned report) would turn the two-point contradiction above into a real test. If a fuller panel showed the ARC-wait/ED-performance relationship is genuinely non-monotonic or confounded by rurality, the "ARC shortage causes ED spikes" framing would need to be narrowed or dropped.
- **Reconciliation of MCNZ's 19 regions with Health NZ's 20 districts** (publishing Capital & Coast and Hutt Valley separately in the workforce survey) would remove a mechanical join failure that currently prevents matching doctors-per-capita to wait-time outcomes even at annual resolution.
- **A reviewer may fairly challenge** the claim that the data gap is the binding constraint rather than the hypothesis being wrong. The counter is that the directional evidence (policy targeting of beds at worst districts; district narratives naming discharge/capacity/anaesthetists; the ARC-discharge contradiction) is consistent with constraints mattering but not settleable either way — which is exactly why the test needs the missing panel.

What I could NOT verify: the exact national acute-bed count (the ~13,200 figure is an arithmetic inference from the OECD rate and Stats NZ population, not a separately published Health NZ count); any 2024-2026 hospital-district occupancy percentage (none found publicly); any district-quarter nursing vacancy; any hospital-level ambulance ramping metric; and whether the Capital, Coast and Hutt Valley doctor-FTE figure splits unevenly between the two districts in a way that matters for the wait-time join. A human with Health NZ operational access, or an OIA request for district-level bed/occupancy/vacancy data, could close most of these.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Why did public Acute Hospital Bed Days district-quarter reporting stop after June 2023, and can Health NZ/MoH reinstate it or release the 2024-2026 continuation via OIA?** This is now the sharpest data-access question because the historical reporting infrastructure demonstrably existed.
- **Does hospital bed occupancy predict ED six-hour performance at district-quarter resolution?** Answerable only if Health NZ releases the underlying current-period occupancy data — a strong candidate for an OIA request or a skills-repo request for a Health NZ bed/occupancy CLI.
- **What explains West Coast's combination of the longest ARC discharge delay AND the best ED performance?** Likely a rural-generalist / low-throughput / low-access-block effect, but it deserves its own narrow study — it is the cleanest natural experiment in the baseline.
- **Is the medical-workforce growth in emergency medicine (350→480) keeping pace with ED attendance growth?** ED attendances rose (Q1 25/26 saw district-level increases of +0.7% to +15.3% year-on-year per the Q1 factsheet) while EM doctors grew 37.1% over five years — a throughput-per-EM-doctor question that needs matched denominators.
- A **data-govt-nz / skills-repo request** for a Health NZ hospital-bed-and-occupancy skill would turn this from a "current-period data is not public/aligned" finding into a testable one for the next researcher. (Not opened here, as it concerns a separate repo's backlog, but flagged as the obvious next step.)
