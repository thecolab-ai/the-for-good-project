# A 78-council desk census finds statutory finance documents everywhere in principle, but very little council-published structured spending data

## Executive answer

- This desk census used LGNZ's official list of all 78 regional, territorial, and unitary council websites as the frame, because LGNZ says Aotearoa New Zealand has 78 councils and publishes a comprehensive council-website list. [LGNZ council types](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/local-government-in-nz/councils-in-aotearoa/); [LGNZ council websites](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/local-government-in-nz/councils-in-aotearoa/council-websites-and-maps/)
- National, machine-readable finance baselines exist: Stats NZ publishes local-authority financial/statistical releases in CSV/XLSX form, DIA's Local Councils site publishes historic long-term-plan financial tables in CSV/XLSX, and MBIE says GETS award notices are published as CSV open data. [Stats NZ local authority statistics](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [DIA Local Authority Long-Term Plans](http://www.localcouncils.govt.nz/lgip.nsf/wpg_URL/Resources-Download-Data-Local-Authority-Long-Term-Plans?OpenDocument); [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data)
- A reproducible, committed script ran the bounded homepage + root-sitemap keyword census over all 78 councils. It surfaced finance, budget, procurement, grants or open-data keyword pages for **75 of 78 councils** (the other 3 could not be scanned from this environment's IP — 2 WAF-blocked, 1 a near-empty reader stub — not confirmed negatives), so front-door discoverability of finance/procurement/grants *pages* is close to universal, not scarce. What the census did **not** surface is a council-published *structured* supplier-payment or transaction-spending ledger: none of the matched front-door URLs carried a CSV/XLS/JSON/GeoJSON extension. That structured-data negative is **Low confidence**, because a structured file can sit behind a document library, JS download, portal, or search box the front door can't see. [Audit artifact](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/data/council-spending-census-78-audit.tsv); [census script](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/scripts/council-spending-census.mjs)
- The strongest directly verified council-published structured spending-adjacent examples remain narrow: Christchurch City Council's ArcGIS Capital Works Programme has project, activity, budget-band and date fields under CC BY 4.0, while Wellington City Council's ArcGIS `FundingDataWCCbyfund` is an Excel grants workbook last updated in 2018 and has unclear item-level licensing. [Christchurch ArcGIS metadata](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/content/items/a96f3f9e87c84608b164557d87874ecb?f=json); [Christchurch ArcGIS layer fields](https://gis.ccc.govt.nz/arcgis/rest/services/CorporateData/Administrative/MapServer/23?f=pjson); [WCC FundingDataWCCbyfund metadata](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/content/items/eef4e55911fe40e39a9ec0c0d2640479?f=json)
- The practical answer for a civic tool is: start with national aggregates and named council document pages, treat GETS as procurement-notice evidence rather than a spending ledger, and pilot only where a council has a directly verified structured dataset such as Christchurch capital works. [NZ Government Procurement data and reporting](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/data-and-reporting/)

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The existence of national datasets and named structured examples is High confidence; the "not found" claim about full transaction-level council data is Low confidence because it is based on a reproducible public-web desk search, not council confirmation or LGOIMA requests.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This finding answers issue #187 by checking the supply side across all 78 councils named by LGNZ, rather than only Auckland, Wellington and Christchurch as in the earlier limited scan. [Issue #187](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/187); [earlier data census](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-spending-data-census.md); [LGNZ council websites](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/local-government-in-nz/councils-in-aotearoa/council-websites-and-maps/)

I treated "spending, budget, and procurement data" as four different evidence levels: statutory financial documents such as long-term plans, annual plans and annual reports; structured all-council finance datasets; procurement/tender/award-notice data; and council-published line-item, project, grants, supplier, or transaction datasets. That separation is necessary because LGNZ describes Long-term Plans, Annual Plans and Annual Reports as the core council planning/reporting documents, while MBIE's GETS data is procurement award-notice data rather than a consolidated finance ledger. [LGNZ how councils work](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/local-government-in-nz/councils-in-aotearoa/how-councils-work/); [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data); [NZ Government Procurement data and reporting](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/data-and-reporting/)

The council-by-council census is run by a committed, reproducible script, [`scripts/council-spending-census.mjs`](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/scripts/council-spending-census.mjs), over LGNZ's official council-website list (committed as the input frame [`data/council-spending-census-78-councils.tsv`](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/data/council-spending-census-78-councils.tsv)). For each council it fetches (a) the homepage and (b) the root `sitemap.xml` — **following redirects**, and if that is a sitemap *index*, fetching every child sitemap it references (capped at 30) — then scans every sitemap `<loc>` URL and every homepage link/nav-text string against a bounded keyword list grouped as **finance/budget** (`annual report`, `long term plan`/`ltp`, `annual plan`, `budget`, `finance`), **procurement** (`procurement`, `tender`, `contract`, `supplier`), **spending** (`spending`, `expenditure`, `rates`, `invoice`, `payment`) and **open-data/grants/capital** (`open data`, `grant`, `capital works`, `capital programme`). Matching is case- and separator-insensitive. The exact per-council raw output — every fetch's HTTP status, byte count, fetch method, sitemap URL count, and matched sample URLs — is committed as the audit artifact [`data/council-spending-census-78-audit.tsv`](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/data/council-spending-census-78-audit.tsv), so every classification in the table below is auditable and reproducible with `node scripts/council-spending-census.mjs research/findings/civic-transparency/data/council-spending-census-78-councils.tsv`. This is a front-door census: homepage + root sitemap only. It is not a full crawl, a site-search/document-library query, or a JS-rendered fetch, so it cannot see search-only endpoints, portal/ArcGIS services, files whose URLs carry no matching token, or PDFs behind a search box. [LGNZ council websites](https://www.lgnz.co.nz/local-government-in-nz/councils-in-aotearoa/council-websites-and-maps/); [ADR-0006 fetch ladder](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/adr/0006-fetch-proxy-browser-management.md)

Fetches use the ADR-0006 ladder: a direct browser-shaped HTTP request first, and on a WAF block (403/405/406/bot-challenge) a retry through the `r.jina.ai` reader proxy, which egresses from a different IP and returns the real current page or sitemap. This matters because, from the census machine's datacenter IP, the majority of council sites returned a blanket WAF block to *both* plain HTTP and the local browser rungs — `node scripts/fetch.mjs` returned HTTP 406 for Auckland Council even through stealth Chromium — a block no local browser fingerprint can clear. Bot-challenge pages (Cloudflare, AWS WAF "confirm you are human") that leak through the reader proxy are detected and the council is classified **BLOCKED**, never counted as a negative. Each council lands in one of four buckets, in decreasing evidential strength: **HIT** (a keyword matched a sitemap URL or homepage link/nav text — a positive result); **NO-HIT** (the root sitemap was fetched and scanned and no keyword matched — the strong negative, since the sitemap is the comprehensive URL list); **PARTIAL** (only the homepage could be scanned, sitemap absent or blocked, and no keyword matched there — a *weak* negative); and **BLOCKED** (neither homepage nor sitemap yielded scannable content through the whole ladder — a tooling/IP artefact per ADR-0006, **not** evidence of absence). A residential IP will convert many of this environment's BLOCKED rows into direct HITs or NO-HITs. Data.govt.nz and MBIE asset downloads were likewise bot-protected to command-line fetches and were read via the built-in web fetch/search rung; a bot-protection response is not treated as a dead citation. [Data.govt.nz local councils showcase](https://data.govt.nz/catalogue-guide/showcase/local-councils); [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data); [ADR-0006](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/docs/adr/0006-fetch-proxy-browser-management.md)

Across the 78 council websites, the reproducible bounded census surfaced a finance/budget, procurement, spending, or open-data/grants/capital keyword — in a sitemap URL or homepage link/nav text — for **75 councils (HIT)**. **1** returned only a homepage-level partial with no keyword (Northland Regional Council, whose reader response was a near-empty 114-byte stub — inconclusive, not a true negative), and **2** (Porirua City Council and Hurunui District Council) were **BLOCKED** by a WAF at every rung from this environment's datacenter IP and could not be scanned at all. No council was fully scanned and found keyword-free (0 NO-HIT). The honest headline is therefore that front-door discoverability of finance/procurement/grants *pages* is close to universal wherever a council site is fetchable. This **corrects the earlier draft of this finding, which reported 28 hits / 50 "no hits"**: that split was an artifact of fetches that silently failed — for example an unfollowed `sitemap.xml` redirect at Wellington City Council, and datacenter-IP WAF blocks — being miscoded as keyword-negatives rather than reflecting any real absence of finance pages. Every row above is now backed by the committed audit artifact rather than by LGNZ, which supplies only the council frame. What the census does **not** surface is structured, machine-readable spending data at the front door: none of the matched sample URLs recorded in the audit artifact carried a CSV, XLS/XLSX, JSON, or GeoJSON extension for a finance/procurement/spending page. That is consistent with a **Low confidence** read that council-published *structured* spending data is not easily discoverable from council website front doors — while explicitly not ruling out JavaScript-generated downloads, extensionless files, search-only document libraries, ArcGIS/portal services, or PDFs, none of which this front-door method can see. ### Licences and reuse terms

The national finance data has clearer reuse terms than many council-level examples: Stats NZ uses CC BY 4.0 unless indicated otherwise, DIA Local Councils pages display CC BY 3.0 NZ, and data.govt.nz says content is CC BY 4.0 unless indicated otherwise. [Stats NZ local authority statistics](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [DIA download index](http://www.localcouncils.govt.nz/lgip.nsf/wpg_url/Resources-Download-Data-Index); [data.govt.nz requests CSV result/footer](https://www.data.govt.nz/requests/csv)

Council portal licences are item-specific enough to require per-dataset checks: Christchurch's capital-works ArcGIS item states CC BY 4.0, while the Wellington grants item has no licence value in its item metadata even though WCC's general GIS open-data terms refer to CC BY 4.0. ## What would change this conclusion

- A council-by-council confirmation request asking each council whether it publishes reusable supplier-payment, procurement-register, capital-project, grants, chart-of-accounts, LTP spreadsheet, or annual-report data would raise or overturn the Low-confidence negative claim from this web-only census.
- A full crawl of each council document library, including JavaScript search endpoints and PDFs, could find structured downloads that the homepage/root-sitemap method missed.
- Re-running the committed census script from a residential IP would convert this environment's 2 BLOCKED councils (Porirua, Hurunui) and the 1 near-empty PARTIAL (Northland Regional) into direct HIT/NO-HIT results, and would let a reviewer independently reproduce or challenge every row; the datacenter-IP WAF blocks here are tooling per ADR-0006, not evidence of absence.
- A broadened keyword list or a per-council document-library scrape could still turn up a structured spending ledger this front-door method cannot see — the Low-confidence structured-data negative should be treated as a lead, not a settled fact.
- A current DIA replacement for Local Councils 2021-31 or 2024-34 LTP tables would change the "historic/dates through 2018-28" assessment for all-council budget data.
- A successful non-bot-blocked download of MBIE GETS CSV files would allow a council-name count of GETS award notices; this pass verified the official MBIE page, but command-line CSV downloads returned Incapsula pages and were treated as blocked tooling, not dead links.
- A direct licence opinion is needed before reusing item-level council data at scale, because general website copyright terms and ArcGIS item metadata can diverge.
- I did not contact councils, lodge LGOIMA requests, or verify whether internal finance systems can export the missing ledgers; this finding is a public-source desk census only.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which councils, if any, will confirm by email or LGOIMA that they publish supplier-payment or transaction-level spending data, and under what reuse terms?
- Can the 2024-34 LTP financial tables be reconstructed into a common CSV/XLSX from council-published plans without hand-keying?
- Which citizen question types from the demand-side finding are answerable from national aggregates alone, and which require council-specific project, contract, or supplier data?
- Would Christchurch's capital-works schema generalise to other councils' capital-programme reporting, or is it too Christchurch-specific?
