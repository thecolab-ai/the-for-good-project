# Council-spending civic tech: what exists, what to build on, and what failed

## Executive answer

- **The duplication risk is real but narrow (this bullet synthesises the cited sections below).** There is prior art in this space, but the NZ efforts each occupy only a slice of it — public-records requests [FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); an advocacy-framed council league table [Ratepayers' Report methodology](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rp_methodology); official aggregate datasets/charts [Figure.NZ](https://figure.nz/chart/okcV4PqsEUxjxWQy-nHozrMb2KSVsSfqn) — and the dominant *working* international model is government- or vendor-run spending portals [Checkbook NYC](https://www.checkbooknyc.com/); [OpenGov](https://opengov.com/); [USAspending.gov](https://www.usaspending.gov/), not independent citizen civic tech. Within the scope I searched (see caveats below), I did not find a tool that does the specific thing this stream contemplates — turning already-public council spending into plain-language answers to the questions residents actually ask — so I record this as *no matching tool found within scope*, not a proof of absence. My judgement follows: building is therefore not automatically duplication, but three existing pieces (FYI, the Ratepayers' Report, the official aggregates) are load-bearing enough that a new tool should **reuse or reconcile against them, not rebuild them**.
- **In NZ, build *on* existing infrastructure.** FYI.org.nz is a live, volunteer-run platform for lodging and publishing OIA/LGOIMA requests, built on mySociety's Alaveteli (the same lineage as the UK's WhatDoTheyKnow) [FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); [NZ Herald, 2015](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/oia-requests-website-relaunched-by-the-herald/WISYVCCDSFKT7KW6R26VG47FOI/). The Taxpayers' Union's Ratepayers' Report already publishes annual all-council "league tables" of rates and spending, but through an explicit lower-tax advocacy frame [Ratepayers' Report methodology](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rp_methodology); [Taxpayers' Union, RPR 2026](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rpr_2026). Official aggregate finance data (Stats NZ, DIA, MCERT) is the data backbone the stream's prior findings already identified [framing finding](council-spending-legibility-framing.md); [data census finding](council-spending-data-census.md).
- **The clearest cautionary tale is international and well-evidenced:** the UK mandated publication of every council transaction over £500 and predicted an "army of armchair auditors" would use it — that army never materialised; usage was low and thin, blocked by data-quality/comparability problems and an information-to-action gap [gov.uk, "More than 60 councils open books to armchair auditors"](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/more-than-60-councils-open-books-to-armchair-auditors); [Worthy, "Where are the Armchair Auditors?", 2014](https://opendatastudy.wordpress.com/2014/01/12/where-are-the-armchair-auditors/); [Waugh et al., 2022](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4144895).
- **The clearest sustainability lesson (an inference, flagged as such):** independent or large civic aggregators that scraped or centralised many councils' data have struggled to last — OpenlyLocal's UK council-spending service no longer operates (its domain now hosts an unrelated business blog) [OpenlyLocal.com](https://openlylocal.com/), and OpenSpending, the flagship global platform, was rebuilt from scratch once and now runs under a named maintaining organisation [OpenSpending platform update, 2017](https://blog.okfn.org/2017/08/16/openspending-platform-update/); [OpenSpending, About](https://www.openspending.org/about). I found no cost figures, so treat "these are expensive to sustain" as a caution rather than a measured fact. Prudent takeaway: avoid "scrape all 78 councils into one central database forever" unless there is a funded maintenance model.
- **The model that scaled internationally was resourced and official (or vendor-supplied)**, not volunteer: NYC's Checkbook NYC [Checkbook NYC](https://www.checkbooknyc.com/) and the federal USAspending.gov [USAspending.gov](https://www.usaspending.gov/) are run by comptrollers or federal agencies with budgets and legal mandates, and many US localities license the OpenGov commercial platform, which reports serving 2,000+ communities and is listed as an ICMA partner [OpenGov](https://opengov.com/); [ICMA, OpenGov](https://icma.org/page/opengov). That is context for what "success" has required elsewhere, not a claim it transplants to NZ.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the existence, status, and lineage of the named efforts are directly sourced, and the "raw dumps didn't create citizen auditors" lesson has multiple independent academic and official sources. It is Medium not High because the field scan is not exhaustive (I did not audit all 78 NZ councils for local tools, and NZ-specific evidence on *usage* of any of these is thin), and because whether UK/US lessons transfer to NZ is judgement, not evidence.

## Evidence

### Scope of this finding

This answers the stream's long-open prior-art question (framed as #17, re-opened as #188 because the planned research produced no finding): *what NZ and international civic-tech efforts on council spending legibility exist or have been tried, and what should this stream build on or avoid?* [Issue #3](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/3). I treat "civic tech" broadly — volunteer/NGO tools, official transparency portals, and mandated-publication regimes — because the useful lessons come from all three. I did **not** attempt a per-council audit of local tools across all 78 NZ authorities; that remains open. The stream's earlier findings already established the *data* landscape (Stats NZ/DIA/MCERT aggregates; three council open-data portals), so this finding focuses on *efforts and tools*, not raw data sources [data census finding](council-spending-data-census.md).

### New Zealand: what exists (mostly "build on", not "duplicate")

**FYI.org.nz — public-records requests, live and volunteer-run.** FYI lets New Zealanders "publicly lodge Official Information Act (OIA) and Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act (LGOIMA) requests online," and automatically publishes the requests and the agencies' responses; it is "an independent project maintained completely by volunteers" and "powered by Alaveteli," the mySociety open-source platform, adapted from the UK's WhatDoTheyKnow.com [FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about). It was set up in 2009 after an Open Government event by developer Rowan Crawford, as the first WhatDoTheyKnow-style deployment outside the UK, and is run by Open New Zealand [NZ Herald, 2015](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/oia-requests-website-relaunched-by-the-herald/WISYVCCDSFKT7KW6R26VG47FOI/); the codebase is a public NZ fork of Alaveteli [GitHub, fyiorgnz/alaveteli](https://github.com/fyiorgnz/alaveteli). Implication: FYI is the *request/disclosure* layer — a legibility tool should link to and build on it (e.g. surface answered spending requests) rather than reinvent a request system. The stream's framing finding already noted FYI's "Local and regional councils" authority category [framing finding](council-spending-legibility-framing.md).

**Ratepayers' Report — an existing all-council comparison, but advocacy-framed.** The Taxpayers' Union publishes the Ratepayers' Report annually so "New Zealanders [can] see how their local councils compare," ranking councils on average residential and commercial rates, staffing costs, third-party payments and liabilities, using each council's most recent annual report plus LGOIMA requests; it presents figures per-household to make councils comparable [Ratepayers' Report methodology](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rp_methodology); [Ratepayers' Report 2026](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rpr_2026). Critically, its methodology page concedes that an "apples to apples" residential-rates figure "is difficult" because councils mix rates, levies and user charges differently — the same comparability problem the stream's comparability finding independently found [Ratepayers' Report methodology](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/rp_methodology); [comparability finding](council-spending-comparability.md). The Taxpayers' Union states its mission as "lower taxes, less waste, more accountability" [Taxpayers' Union, mission](https://www.taxpayers.org.nz/our_mission). Implication: this is real prior art on cross-council comparison and on the LGOIMA-gathering method — worth learning from — but its framing is contested, which is arguably an *opening* for a neutral, source-linked tool rather than a reason not to build.

**Figure.NZ — charting of official local-authority statistics.** Figure.NZ republishes local-authority financial statistics as charts (e.g. operating expenditure of all local authorities) drawn from Stats NZ data [Figure.NZ, operating expenditure of local authorities](https://figure.nz/chart/okcV4PqsEUxjxWQy-nHozrMb2KSVsSfqn). It is a *visualisation* layer over aggregate national data, not a per-council or per-project spending explorer.

**GovHack NZ / data.govt.nz — a recurring hackathon and open-data catalogue (not verified as prior art).** GovHack is a civic hackathon run annually, and NZ councils (e.g. Wellington City Council) have contributed open datasets through it; data.govt.nz's GovHack group and wider catalogue host local/regional government datasets [data.govt.nz, WCC open-data journey through GovHack](https://www.data.govt.nz/blog/wellington-city-councils-open-data-journey-through-govhack); [GovHack NZ group, data.govt.nz](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/group/govhack). I did **not** identify a specific GovHack (or other hackathon) prototype targeting council-spending legibility, nor confirm the maintenance status of any output, so I do not count this as prior art — only as an unaudited lead that a targeted search could pursue.

### International: efforts and, more usefully, their lessons

**OpenSpending / "Where Does My Money Go?" (Open Knowledge Foundation) — the flagship, and a sustainability warning.** OpenSpending is "a free, open and global platform to search, visualise and analyse fiscal data in the public sphere," one of the longest-running open-data projects, having started in 2009 as "Where Does My Money Go?" [OpenSpending, About](https://www.openspending.org/about). Its trajectory is the lesson: in 2017 OKF publicly launched "a completely new iteration," formally "replacing the old OpenSpending platform" that had run since 2011, while admitting "a noted lack of community engagement goes back to before we started on the new platform" [OpenSpending platform update, 2017](https://blog.okfn.org/2017/08/16/openspending-platform-update/). Today the About page states the platform is "Maintained by Datopian" and "Built with PortalJS" [OpenSpending, About](https://www.openspending.org/about). *Inference, not a documented cost/governance finding:* that a global, well-known platform needed a from-scratch rebuild and now runs under a single named maintaining organisation is a reasonable caution that a centralised, scrape-everything fiscal-data platform carries an ongoing maintenance burden. I did not find figures on that cost, nor the terms of the Datopian arrangement (e.g. whether it is a paid contract, a steward, or a successor project), so I do not claim OpenSpending was "outsourced to a contractor."

**OpenlyLocal (UK) — aggregated council spending, transparency service no longer operating.** OpenlyLocal aggregated UK council data, including spending, and pioneered features like one-click FOI requests on individual spending payments [countculture, "one-click FoI requests for spending payments", 2010](https://countculture.wordpress.com/2010/10/04/new-feature-one-click-foi-requests-for-spending-payments/); a 2010 OKF post describes it opening up local spending data across many councils [OKF blog, "Opening up UK local spending data", 2010](https://blog.okfn.org/2010/01/05/opening-up-uk-local-spending-data/). The openlylocal.com domain today serves an unrelated small-business blog, not the transparency service [OpenlyLocal.com](https://openlylocal.com/), and the project's founder Chris Taggart subsequently went on to OpenCorporates [Open Rights Group, Chris Taggart bio](https://www.openrightsgroup.org/advisory-council/chris-taggart/). I could not find a dated post-mortem stating when or why the service closed, so I treat the closure date and reason as **unverified** and draw only a cautious lesson: an independently-run, multi-council scraper did not remain a going concern. I do **not** claim to have established *why* — funding, maintenance load, or the founder's move to OpenCorporates are plausible but unconfirmed causes.

**UK Local Government Transparency Code (£500 spend) + "armchair auditors" — the central "what to avoid" lesson.** The UK required local authorities to publish all spending transactions over £500, all procurement-card spend, and contracts over £5,000 [Local Government Transparency Code 2015, gov.uk](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/local-government-transparency-code-2015/local-government-transparency-code-2015). The political promise was an "army of armchair auditors" scrutinising the books, a phrase from David Cameron's transparency agenda [gov.uk, "More than 60 councils open books to armchair auditors"](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/more-than-60-councils-open-books-to-armchair-auditors). The evidence is that this promise failed:
- Ben Worthy's review concluded "the army has not appeared," with only isolated successes; he identified the barriers as inconsistent/non-comparable data, the rare combination of skills/time/knowledge required, a gap between finding something and knowing how to act on it, and dependence on there already being a high-profile controversy [Worthy, "Where are the Armchair Auditors?", 2014](https://opendatastudy.wordpress.com/2014/01/12/where-are-the-armchair-auditors/).
- A peer-reviewed field study by Waugh, Malinen, Benjamin and Hodkinson found citizens' ability to act as armchair auditors under England's reformed audit regime "is minimal," attributing this to systemic barriers (no clear oversight mechanism, no proper escalation channel) rather than lack of citizen willingness or expertise [Waugh et al., "'Armchair Auditors' – Rhetoric Versus Reality", 2022 (SSRN)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4144895).
- A 2024 peer-reviewed review of two decades of UK local-government transparency likewise found that "rather than the hoped for 'army' of auditors, low level use was spread broadly, or thinly, across businesses, a few members of the public, journalists and NGOs," and that presentation "frames" interpretation while councils "sit on a huge amount of data but lack the capacity and resources to use it" [Worthy et al., "More open, more democratic or better at hiding?", *Local Government Studies*, 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/03003930.2024.2417222).

The convergent lesson for this stream: **publishing raw transactions is not the intervention.** Legibility — answers, context, comparability, and a clear path from "I found something" to "here's what to do" — is the gap that mere disclosure left open. That directly supports the stream's "make it legible, not just public" framing.

**USA — Checkbook NYC, OpenGov, USAspending: the resourced/official (or paid-vendor) model.** Where citizen-facing spending transparency has scaled and persisted, it has generally been government- or vendor-run: NYC's Comptroller runs Checkbook NYC (transaction-level city spending) [Checkbook NYC](https://www.checkbooknyc.com/), the federal USAspending.gov publishes award-level federal spending data [USAspending.gov](https://www.usaspending.gov/), and many US localities license the OpenGov commercial platform — which reports serving over 2,000 communities and is listed as an ICMA partner [OpenGov](https://opengov.com/); [ICMA, OpenGov](https://icma.org/page/opengov). Implication: the sustained successes had budgets, staff, and legal mandates, or a paid vendor — a caution against assuming a volunteer or small-grant NZ project can maintain a comprehensive spending portal indefinitely.

### Synthesis: build on vs avoid

- **Build on:** FYI.org.nz (request/disclosure layer, link don't rebuild); official aggregate datasets (Stats NZ/DIA/MCERT) as the finance backbone; the Ratepayers' Report's LGOIMA-gathering method and its honest admission of the comparability problem. (GovHack is a possible *idea source*, but I did not verify any relevant prototype exists — see the GovHack note above.)
- **Avoid:** a "publish/scrape everything and they will come" model (UK £500 code; OpenlyLocal; OpenSpending's rebuild) — the UK evidence shows it under-delivers on citizen use, and the platform histories caution (as an inference) that it carries real maintenance cost. Avoid cross-council comparisons that ignore the definitional differences both the Ratepayers' Union and MCERT flag. *Design judgement, not an evidenced consequence:* a neutral, source-linked framing is likely to travel further across a politically mixed audience than an explicit advocacy frame like the Ratepayers' Union's — but I have no direct evidence that advocacy framing reduces trust for this specific use case.
- **The unfilled niche** the evidence points to: not more raw data, but a *legibility* layer — plain-language answers to recurring resident questions, always source-linked, honest about comparability limits — which aligns with the stream's demand finding [demand finding](council-data-questions.md).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A maintained NZ tool I missed.** I did not audit all 78 councils, nor exhaustively search NZ civic-tech projects; a live, well-used NZ council-spending legibility tool (e.g. from a council, a university, or a media outlet) would move this from "unfilled niche" toward "occupied." A per-council scan is the obvious next research step.
- **Evidence that transparency dumps *did* drive citizen use somewhere comparable.** The "armchair auditors failed" finding is UK-centric. Rigorous evidence of sustained, broad citizen use of a raw-transaction portal (rather than journalist/NGO use) would weaken the "legibility, not disclosure" framing.
- **A funded, durable maintenance model.** The sustainability caution assumes volunteer/small-grant resourcing. A committed multi-year funder or a council partner willing to host would change the risk calculus on a comprehensive portal.
- **NZ usage data for FYI / Ratepayers' Report.** I found no evidence on how much New Zealanders actually *use* these for spending questions. Usage numbers could show the space is either more saturated or more wide-open than "existence" implies.
- **Unverified specifics I flag honestly:** OpenlyLocal's exact closure date/reason (inferred from the repurposed domain, not a dated post-mortem); and whether NZ citizens would behave differently from the UK "armchair auditor" pattern — a question that needs a human with local-journalism or community-organising experience, and ideally light user research, before any build (a G2 concern).

## Open follow-up questions

- Per-council scan: do any of the 78 NZ councils (or NZ media/universities) run a citizen-facing spending explorer today, and is it maintained? (Natural pairing with the outstanding data-census follow-up.)
- Usage evidence: how much are FYI.org.nz and the Ratepayers' Report actually used for council-*spending* questions specifically, and by whom (residents vs journalists vs advocacy)?
- Transfer question: is there any jurisdiction where a *legibility* layer (answers + context), not raw disclosure, measurably raised citizen engagement with council spending — a positive model to copy rather than a failure to avoid?
