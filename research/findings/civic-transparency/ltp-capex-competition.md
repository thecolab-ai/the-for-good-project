# In sampled high-risk council LTPs, water, stormwater, transport and ordinary renewals dominate capital budgets while seismic remediation is a small, poorly standardised named layer

## Executive answer

- I coded four official LTP/capital-programme documents for councils with visible seismic and climate or water-risk exposure: Wellington City, Hutt City, Christchurch City, and Dunedin City; Dunedin is included as a deferred-plan comparator because it adopted a 2025-34 nine-year plan and its capital report also presents a 2024-34 ten-year comparison. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en); [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf); [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)
- Across the sample, the big competition is not "seismic versus climate" as two symmetric budget categories; it is named seismic projects competing inside larger capital envelopes dominated by three waters, stormwater/flood protection, transport, and renewals. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)
- Wellington budgeted $4.897 billion of 2024-34 capex, with $1.441 billion in three waters and $1.144 billion in transport, while seismic items such as the Town Hall, Te Matapihi, Kilbirnie Recreation Centre and other earthquake-prone venues sit inside "Other Activity Groups" rather than a separable seismic line. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)
- Hutt's plan says it will spend just over $2.7 billion over 10 years, with 60% in water services and 21% in transport, while the directly labelled seismic items I found were much smaller: $1.082 million for critical-pipeline seismic upgrades and $3 million for the Petone Recreation Ground Grandstand demolition/ground-floor strengthening option. [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)
- Christchurch is the clearest case where flood/stormwater dwarfs labelled seismic: in a $6.511 billion capital programme, water supply, wastewater, stormwater and flood protection total about $2.756 billion, while direct "earthquake/strengthening/base isolation" rows I found total about $36.7 million. [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the headline pattern is supported by official council documents and the Audit Office's national pressure analysis, but the seismic figures are conservative because councils often embed strengthening inside broader building, heritage, renewal, insurance or precinct projects without a standard "seismic" tag.

## Evidence

### Coding method

I used the official adopted LTP or council capital-programme document for each council and coded only public capital expenditure figures that could be traced to an activity table or named project line in that document. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en); [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf); [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)

The categories are conservative and non-overlapping for comparison: "water" means water supply plus wastewater where the council exposes those lines; "stormwater/flood" means stormwater drainage plus flood protection/control where exposed; "ordinary renewals" means the council's own renewal category; "seismic remediation" means rows explicitly labelled earthquake, seismic, strengthening, or base isolation, excluding broader heritage or building renewals unless the row itself names seismic work. [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf)

That method undercounts seismic where a project combines heritage restoration, refurbishment, fire, access, asbestos, roof, HVAC and structural work, but it avoids counting every council building renewal as seismic remediation. [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf); [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)

### How the competition shows up

The Audit Office's national 2024-34 LTP analysis shows the background pressure: 58 councils forecast $91.9 billion of capital expenditure, including $39.5 billion for renewing existing assets, and planned three-waters capex rose to $38.6 billion from $24.6 billion in the same councils' 2021-31 LTPs. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)

The same Audit Office report says stormwater renewal expenditure is much lower relative to depreciation than other water assets in 2024-34, with stormwater/drainage renewals at 43% of depreciation compared with 113% for drinking water and 87% for wastewater, and says that is concerning given more severe weather events associated with climate change. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)

In Wellington, the competition appears as prioritisation inside a very large capital envelope: the plan says it includes only a proportion of required renewal investment, defers 25% of renewals spend in transport, property, housing and other community assets, and separately prioritises three waters while investigating buildings and other assets to address earthquake-prone issues. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)

In Hutt, the competition is explicit in the overview text: Council says fixing pipes and other water infrastructure is its top priority and is driving much of the rates increase, while it has "dialled up" water investment and "dialled down" other activities after a savings exercise. [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)

In Christchurch, the capital programme shows stormwater and flood protection as a major climate-facing spend because stormwater drainage totals $214.5 million and flood protection/control totals $703.5 million over 2024-34, while the infrastructure strategy frames climate resilience and natural hazards as one of the long-term infrastructure challenges. [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf); [Christchurch City Council infrastructure strategy](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP-2024-2034/Infrastructure-Strategy.pdf)

In Dunedin, the capital report makes the trade-off mechanism unusually visible: staff considered affordability, debt limits, renewals over new capital, market delivery, climate/Zero Carbon targets and legal requirements, and then excluded or rephased $272.780 million from the unconstrained capital budget. [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)

### Interpretation

On this sample, seismic remediation competes mostly as an embedded building, heritage, lifeline or venue obligation, not as a standard LTP activity line comparable with water, stormwater, transport or renewals. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf)

That means a council can face real seismic remediation decisions while the LTP table still makes water and renewals look like the dominant pressure, because water and renewals are standard activity/expenditure categories and seismic remediation is usually a project descriptor. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm); [Hutt City Council LTP page](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)

The strongest practical conclusion is that a volunteer team could build a useful transparency tool by combining two layers: a standard activity-level capex comparison from LTP tables, and a conservative keyword-coded named-project layer for seismic, climate adaptation, flood protection and resilience. [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf); [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- A council-provided project export that tags every capital line by primary driver and secondary driver, including "seismic", "climate adaptation", "growth", "level of service", "renewal" and "compliance", would change this from conservative keyword coding to a stronger comparable dataset.
- A structural engineer or council asset manager could reclassify mixed building projects where seismic work is present but not named in the project title, which would likely increase the seismic totals.
- A full review of annual reports, business cases, project pages and asset-management plans could move some currently unclassified "heritage", "building renewal", "resilience" and "facility" spend into seismic or climate-adaptation categories.
- I could not verify a complete, council-approved seismic total for Wellington or Hutt from the LTP tables alone, and I did not code all 67 territorial authorities.
- I did not open sub-issues because the narrowed four-council sample was enough to answer the core question for #482.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which councils publish project-level LTP data in machine-readable form, and can a repeatable classifier code all 67 territorial authorities?

- Can climate adaptation be separated from ordinary stormwater/flood renewals in LTP documents without council staff input?
