# NZ acute mental-health inpatient capacity: beds, closures, and the infrastructure pipeline

This is research child **#233 of Stream #224** ([discover baseline](./mh-inpatient-inspections-baseline.md)). It answers one question: **how many acute mental-health (and secure intellectual-disability) inpatient beds NZ has, how that has changed, and what is actually being added or lost** — including ward closures, build delays, and what OPCAT reports say about occupancy pressure. It inherits the baseline's corrections (the "Waikato ward slips to 2027" story is a *different, general* ward, not the mental-health facility) and verifies the mental-health facility's own timeline directly.

## Executive answer

- **There is no single clean national bed count — the number depends on scope, and that ambiguity is itself the finding.** The most-cited figure for **adult acute inpatient beds is ~608**, and it did **not change between 2017 and 2022** [RNZ, 23 Nov 2022 (Health Minister Andrew Little)](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/479350/mental-health-system-struggling-to-keep-ahead-of-demand-for-acute-services-little). By December 2024 Health NZ was citing a broader "**more than 1,300 funded mental health and addiction hospital beds, including more than 900 acute mental health beds**" [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). These are not contradictory so much as different denominators (adult-only acute vs all-age acute vs all funded MH&A beds) — the absence of a single, defined, published time series is a genuine transparency gap. **Confidence: Medium.**
- **Whatever the count, it is chronically undersized and static.** NZ sits well below the OECD average on psychiatric beds per capita; an NZMJ editorial argues acute beds should rise to **at least 50 per 100,000** against an **OECD average of ~69/100,000** [NZMJ editorial, 2024](https://nzmj.org.nz/media/pages/journal/vol-137-no-1588/it-is-unethical-to-incarcerate-people-with-disabling-mental-disorders-is-it-also-unlawful/c178c33f0b-1705528200/1588-editorial.pdf). Despite the $1.9b announced for mental health in Budget 2019, acute bed numbers "remained around 600 with persistent shortages" and **no Budget bid for more psychiatry beds was made in 2024** [RNZ, 2023 review coverage](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review); [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity).
- **The pipeline — the Mental Health Infrastructure Programme (MHIP) — is real: 16 projects, ~$997.3m.** As of mid-2026 the current project list has ~6 completed and ~6 in construction/design, replacing ageing units and adding *modest* capacity, mostly like-for-like [Health NZ MHIP page](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/infrastructure/national-infrastructure-projects/mental-health-infrastructure-programme) ([archived 3 Jul 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100603/https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/about-us/what-we-do/programmes-and-initiatives/infrastructure/national-infrastructure-projects/mental-health-infrastructure-programme)). **But the programme was formally reset for cost and schedule blowouts:** an independent 2022 review found **9 of 16 projects had significant budget/timeline problems (5 needing a complete reset, 4 urgent action)**, with cost escalations like MidCentral +70% over its initial $35m [RNZ, 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review). **Confidence: High** on the reset; the current per-project status is the operator's own claim.
- **Waikato's flagship — a 64-bed, $131.2m adult acute facility replacing Henry Rongomau Bennett Centre (HRBC) capacity — is on track per the operator but has already slipped, and adds only ~4 net beds.** Health NZ reported it weather-tight on 30 April 2026 and "expected to open in late December 2026" [Health NZ via Scoop, 30 Apr 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/GE2604/S00085/major-milestone-reached-for-waikatos-new-adult-acute-mental-health-facility.htm) — but the MHIP project page still lists practical completion as "Quarter 2 2026", and earlier reporting variously had it opening "mid-2026" then "September 2026", so the timeline has drifted by roughly six months [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). At 64 beds it is "**one less than was needed**" and only ~4 more than the unit it replaces [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **The "slips to 2027" story from the stream's starting facts is a different ward** (a 28-bed general assessment/diagnostic unit near ED) — that correction from the baseline holds.
- **Beds are being lost at the front while the pipeline delivers slowly, and OPCAT has documented severe over-capacity for years.** Wakari's **Ward 10A — a 12-bed secure intellectual-disability unit — closed to new admissions on 24 April 2026 with all patients relocated from May/June 2026**, and no decision made on refurbishment or replacement; the Public Service Association warned the Southern district may be left "**without any local care facility**" for this population [Stuff/Health NZ, Jun 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/nz-news/361001848/health-nz-welcomes-ombudsman-investigation-wakari-ward-10a-closure-begins); [ODT, 12 Jun 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/health/anger-wakari-decision). Meanwhile acute wards run **115% (2024) to 140% (Aug 2025) of capacity**, with patients sleeping on mattresses on floors in interview and seclusion rooms — conditions the Ombudsman has repeatedly found to breach the torture convention [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse); [Ombudsman, 2020/2021](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/chief-ombudsman-critical-overcrowding-henry-bennett). **The binding constraint reported repeatedly is not just acute beds but the lack of community/residential beds to discharge into** — split off as a follow-up (see below).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the *direction* is well-evidenced and mostly double-sourced (static/undersized capacity, a real-but-reset pipeline delivering modest net beds, closures at the front, sustained over-capacity). The *precise national bed count* is Medium because the published figures use different scopes and I could not locate a single authoritative, defined national time series; per-project MHIP status and opening dates are operator claims, not confirmed delivery.

## Evidence

### 1. How many acute beds — and why the number won't sit still

The cleanest, most-quoted figure for **adult acute inpatient beds is 608**, and the striking fact is its stasis: "there were reportedly 608 beds for acute mental health patients in 2017 and are still [608] in 2022," in coverage of then-Health Minister Andrew Little conceding the system "has struggled to keep ahead of the demand for acute services" (23 Nov 2022) [RNZ, 23 Nov 2022](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/479350/mental-health-system-struggling-to-keep-ahead-of-demand-for-acute-services-little). Coverage of the 2022 infrastructure review likewise states acute bed numbers "remained around 600 with persistent shortages" despite the $1.9b Budget 2019 package [RNZ, 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review).

By late 2024, Health NZ was quoting a **larger, differently-scoped** figure: "New Zealand has more than 1,300 funded mental health and addiction hospital beds, including more than 900 acute mental health beds" [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). The ~608 (adult acute inpatient) and ~900 (all acute mental-health) figures are best read as **different denominators**, not a real +300-bed jump — the ~900 likely folds in child/youth, older-persons and forensic acute beds. I could not find a single published document that defines the categories and gives a consistent time series, so **the true "national acute bed count" is scope-dependent and the transparency gap is real.** On the number of *units*: a 2023 national location study identified **22 publicly funded hospitals with acute adult inpatient mental-health units** [Still in the shadows, NZMJ/PMC, 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9830750/); other coverage refers to "20 publicly funded adult inpatient acute wards." **Confidence: Medium** (figures are real and sourced, but not reconciled to one definition).

### 2. Undersized against the OECD, and not growing

NZ has "a chronic low number of beds per capita compared to other countries in the OECD" [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). An NZMJ editorial (2024) argues NZ's inpatient psychiatric beds should be increased to **at least 50 per 100,000** population against an **OECD average of ~69 per 100,000** [NZMJ editorial, 2024](https://nzmj.org.nz/media/pages/journal/vol-137-no-1588/it-is-unethical-to-incarcerate-people-with-disabling-mental-disorders-is-it-also-unlawful/c178c33f0b-1705528200/1588-editorial.pdf). Crucially, capacity is not being expanded to close that gap on the operating side: in December 2024 Health NZ confirmed **no Budget bid for more psychiatry beds was made that year**, "despite being over capacity" [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). **Confidence: Medium** (the 50/69 targets come from one authoritative editorial; the "no bid / undersized" framing is multiply sourced).

As of mid-2026 the current project list (all bed numbers and dates as stated on the operator's own page — **claims, not confirmed delivery**):

**The pattern is replacement, not expansion.** Most projects rebuild ageing units at similar or marginally higher bed counts (Tauranga 26→29, Waikato ~60→64, Grey replacing Maanakitanga at 6). Net new acute capacity across the whole programme is modest relative to the ~600-bed base and the ~115–140% occupancy pressure. **Confidence: High** on the programme's existence, size and project list; **Medium** on precise per-project net-bed deltas (baselines for several replaced units are not stated on the page).

**The programme was formally reset.** Independent experts reviewed the MHIP in 2022; of the 16 projects, **9 had significant budget/timeline problems — 5 needing a complete budget-and-timeline reset and 4 needing urgent action** — while 4 were on track despite cost rises, 2 were complete and 1 was privately funded [RNZ, 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review) ([archived](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100812/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review)). The review found original plans "lacked detail on cost and timelines, leading to unrealistic schedules and budgets," with escalations such as Northland $12m→$19.5m, Taranaki reaching ~$90m, and MidCentral rising ~70% over its initial $35m; multiple completion dates were pushed back by around a year [RNZ, 2023](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/486311/mental-health-building-projects-face-soaring-costs-delays-review). A formal **Mental Health Infrastructure Programme Review** was also carried out via Te Waihanga / Health NZ (published technical review) [Te Waihanga review page](https://tewaihanga.govt.nz/our-work/reviews/mental-health-infrastructure-programme-review); [Te Whatu Ora publication](https://www.tewhatuora.govt.nz/publications/mental-health-infrastructure-programme-review-technical-review-for-te-whatu-ora/). **Confidence: High** that the programme hit systemic cost/schedule problems and was reset.

### 4. Waikato: the flagship, verified against the operator's own words

Health NZ's own 30 April 2026 update describes the new **64-single-room adult acute mental-health facility** as a **$131.2m** investment, ~8,000 m², reached "weather-tight," and "expected to open in **late December 2026**" [Scoop/Health NZ, 30 Apr 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/GE2604/S00085/major-milestone-reached-for-waikatos-new-adult-acute-mental-health-facility.htm). This **verifies the mental-health facility's timeline directly** (as the baseline instructed) and confirms it is **not** the "slips to 2027" ward — that was a separate 28-bed general assessment/diagnostic unit near ED. Note the drift, though: the MHIP project page still shows "Quarter 2 2026" practical completion, and 2025 reporting variously said "mid-2026" then "September 2026," so the operator's own timeline has slipped roughly six months over 2025–26 [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). The 64 beds replace the ageing HRBC wards and were described in 2025 as "**one less than was needed**," i.e. ~4 net additional beds [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: High** on cost/bed count/late-Dec-2026 as the operator's stated position; the opening itself is unconfirmed (future-dated).

### 5. Beds lost at the front: Wakari Ward 10A

Following the Ombudsman's March 2026 inspection, Health NZ **halted new admissions to Ward 10A on 24 April 2026**, began relocation planning in May, and the Health NZ board **agreed to close Ward 10A as a forensic/secure intellectual-disability unit**, relocating all current patients "to facilities better suited to their needs" [Stuff/Health NZ, Jun 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/nz-news/361001848/health-nz-welcomes-ombudsman-investigation-wakari-ward-10a-closure-begins). Ward 10A is a **12-bed secure unit** for adults (18+) with an intellectual disability requiring compulsory assessment/care [Stuff, Jun 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/nz-news/361001848/health-nz-welcomes-ombudsman-investigation-wakari-ward-10a-closure-begins); [ODT, 12 Jun 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/health/anger-wakari-decision). Health NZ (Craig Ashton) said **"no decisions have been made on the progression of refurbishment works for Ward 10a,"** i.e. the capacity is offline with no confirmed replacement, and the **Public Service Association warned the Southern district may now be "without any local care facility"** for this vulnerable population [ODT, 12 Jun 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/health/anger-wakari-decision). Earlier, Health NZ had announced a mid-2026 **refurbishment** plan for Ward 10A that would close the ward during construction, move patients temporarily to Ward 9A, and complete Ward 9A improvement works by June 2026 [Health NZ, "Improving care and recovery: Wakari Hospital Ward 10A Upgrade"](https://www.healthnz.govt.nz/news-and-updates/improving-care-and-recovery-wakari-hospital-ward-10a-upgrade); [ODT, 18 Dec 2025](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/health/wakari-ward-10a-makeover-confirmed). That refurbishment path was overtaken by the closure decision. **Where the 12 patients actually went, and whether equivalent secure ID capacity now exists in the South Island, I could not verify** (withheld for patient privacy) — a placement-capacity question flagged for child #234. **Confidence: High** on the closure, bed count and PSA warning; the net loss of secure ID capacity is **unverified** because destinations are undisclosed.

### 6. What OPCAT reports say about occupancy pressure

Over-capacity in acute mental-health units is a **long-standing, repeatedly-documented** OPCAT finding, not a new phenomenon:

- **Henry Rongomau Bennett Centre (Waikato):** the Chief Ombudsman found in early 2020 that patients were subjected to **degrading treatment including overcrowding**, high seclusion and restraint use [Ombudsman, 2020](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/chief-ombudsman-critical-overcrowding-henry-bennett); [NZ Herald, 2020](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/henry-bennett-centre-patients-subjected-to-overcrowding-degrading-treatment-chief-ombudsman/NWLJVUYOCNN3XAKZEEWADPG63I/). During a 2019 inspection the centre was running "well over 100% of its funded bed numbers," with seclusion rooms, day rooms and whānau rooms turned into makeshift bedrooms [RNZ, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/517433/waikato-mental-health-facility-patient-forced-to-sleep-on-floor-due-to-overcrowding).
- **Te Whare o Matairangi (Wellington):** a follow-up OPCAT inspection reported clients "sleeping on mattresses on the floor in the TV room and seclusion lounge," and a low-stimulus seclusion area "regularly over occupancy" [Ombudsman OPCAT report, 2021 (PDF)](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2021-11/OPCAT%20Report%20-%20Report%20on%20an%20unannounced%20follow%20up%20inspection%20of%20Te%20Whare%20o%20Matairangi%20Mental%20Health%20Inpatient%20Unit%2C%20Wellington%20Hospital%2C%20under%20the%20Crimes%20of%20Torture%20Act%201989.pdf).
- **Torture-convention breach:** the Chief Ombudsman has characterised mental-health-unit overcrowding as a **breach of the UN Optional Protocol to the Convention Against Torture (OPCAT)** [Ombudsman, "Mental health units breach UN convention"](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/mental-health-units-breach-un-convention); [Newsroom, 8 Jun 2021](https://newsroom.co.nz/2021/06/08/mental-health-unit-overcrowding-a-torture-convention-breach/).
- **Still happening in 2024–25:** the HRBC was at **up to 115% occupancy** in April–May 2024, with at least one patient on a floor mattress for two nights [RNZ, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/517433/waikato-mental-health-facility-patient-forced-to-sleep-on-floor-due-to-overcrowding); by **August 2025 a nurse reported acute wards running at 140% capacity**, with patients "sleeping on mattresses on the floor in interview rooms, seclusion rooms and sometimes other wards," and the key bottleneck being "a lack of beds in mental health and addiction residential services" to discharge into [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703100728/https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: High** on the pattern of documented over-capacity; the 140% figure is a single frontline-nurse account and should be read as illustrative of pressure rather than an audited system-wide occupancy rate.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A defined national bed time series.** The single biggest weakness is that "the acute bed count" has no clean, published, category-defined series. A Health NZ / MoH data pull specifying adult-acute vs all-age acute vs forensic vs secure-ID beds, year by year, could confirm or overturn the "static ~600 / broader 900" reading. Until then the count is Medium.
- **Confirmed delivery of MHIP projects.** Every future date here (Waikato late Dec 2026, Tauranga Q4 2026, Grey Q3 2026, Hillmorton 2027–28, Hutt Q3 2027) is the operator's own claim. Given the programme's documented history of ~1-year slips, opening confirmations (not milestones) would materially change the "what is actually being added" picture — in either direction.
- **The full 2026 Ombudsman Ward 10A report** (not yet published as of 3 Jul 2026 per the baseline) and disclosure of where Ward 10A patients were placed. If equivalent secure ID capacity was created elsewhere, "12 beds lost" becomes "12 beds moved"; if not, the South Island secure-ID capacity gap is real and larger than this finding can prove.
- **Could not verify:** a single authoritative national acute-bed total for 2025/26; the exact net-new-bed sum across the whole MHIP (baselines for several replaced units aren't published); the destinations/placement capacity for relocated Ward 10A patients; whether the 140% Waikato figure reflects a sustained system-wide rate or a peak. The precise scope of the "900+ acute" figure is my inference, not a stated definition.
- **A human with clinical / health-infrastructure authority should sense-check the bed-scope reconciliation** before any of these numbers are used in a decision — the definitional ambiguity is exactly the kind of thing a sector insider would resolve in one sentence.
