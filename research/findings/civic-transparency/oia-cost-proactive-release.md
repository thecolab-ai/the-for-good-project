# NZ's published OIA cost baseline is about $184m, but the defensible proactive-release savings claim is only low single-digit millions until repeat-request data is collected

## Executive answer

- The best current primary baseline is Tregaskis Brown's November 2025 model for the Ministry of Justice: responding to OIA requests cost an estimated $183.6m in FY2024/25, within a $175m-$250m range, from 158,862 completed requests in the Public Service Commission data scope. [Ministry of Justice, regulatory stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/); [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf); [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)
- The model is useful as a workload baseline, but not as a savings number: MoJ and TBL both say the underlying PSC reporting does not record request type, complexity, requester demographics, resources used, or cost drivers, and the Ministry commissioned further 2026 research because the cost drivers are not well understood. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf); [Ministry of Justice, regulatory stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/)
- Police is the largest confound: in TBL's FY2024/25 model Police accounts for 74,526 of 158,862 completed requests, or 46.9% of volume, but $50.0m of $183.6m, or 27.2% of modelled cost, because Police is classified as a high-volume routine agency. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)
- A defensible proactive-release savings claim should not use the full $183.6m. The observable FY2024/25 proxy is 5,417 OIA responses published on agency websites in the TBL model; at TBL's routine marginal cost of $353, one avoided repeat request per published response would be about $1.9m, while each additional 1,000 measured avoidable routine requests would add about $0.35m and each 1,000 measured avoidable complex requests would add about $3.53m. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx); [PSC OIA statistics planning, Jan-Jun 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-Planning-Jan-June-2026.pdf)
- Bottom line: "proactive release saves money" is plausible, but the public evidence supports a low single-digit-million annual claim as a conservative working estimate, not a tens-of-millions claim, unless agencies produce repeat-request counts or time-and-motion data. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the baseline and model mechanics are primary-source and reproducible from the released PDF/spreadsheet, but the proactively-releasable repeat-request slice is not collected in public statistics.

## Evidence

### What TBL/MoJ actually modelled

The Ministry says it commissioned Tregaskis Brown Ltd in late 2025 to model official-information-system costs from data provided to the Public Service Commission, and says the November 2025 report estimated over $183m in FY2024/25 within a $175m-$250m range depending on request complexity. [Ministry of Justice, regulatory stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/)

The released ministerial briefing gives the precise preferred estimate as $183.6m for FY2024/25, says reported OIA requests grew from 40,273 in FY2015/16 to 158,862 in FY2024/25, and forecasts $342m in FY2030/31. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

The model scope is narrower than the whole OIA universe: it covers entities listed under Schedule 1 of the Ombudsmen Act 1975 as government departments and departmental agencies, plus Police, NZDF, and statutory Crown entities, but excludes entities such as school boards and also excludes requests handled by Ministers' offices. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

TBL's preferred FY2024/25 scenario is "Scenario Five", which assumes a two-tier model: routine requests cost $352.97 each, complex requests cost $3,529.66 each, routine-response agencies are modelled as 90% routine and 10% complex, and more-complex agencies are modelled as 75% complex and 25% routine. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

TBL derived the complex-response cost from Australian FOI cost data adjusted to New Zealand salary levels, a 60% overhead factor, and indirect costs, and the released report states that routine responses are costed at 10% of the complex response cost. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf); [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

The top FY2024/25 modelled cost agencies in Scenario Five are Police at $50.0m, Health NZ at $16.5m, Natural Hazards Commission at $12.1m, Corrections at $10.8m, MBIE at $7.9m, NZTA at $7.6m, Education at $7.2m, Health at $5.9m, MPI at $5.8m, and MSD at $5.7m. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

### Why the $183.6m is not a savings ceiling

MoJ's briefing says the model cannot show what drives costs, cannot show whether cost pressures differ by agency, and relies on data collected for a different purpose; the briefing says care is needed when considering the findings, while also saying the model is likely conservative because it lacks detailed internal cost capture. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

The PSC's OIA statistics are response-compliance statistics, not cost-driver statistics: PSC says the collection covers completed request counts, timeliness, publication of OIA responses, complaints, extensions, transfers, refusals, and average response time. [Public Service Commission, OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics)

The PSC's 2026 collection planning confirms that the "responses published" statistic counts how many OIA responses were published on an agency website during the period, not how many later requests were avoided by those releases. [PSC OIA statistics planning, Jan-Jun 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-Planning-Jan-June-2026.pdf)

DPMC's Cabinet proactive-release policy is also not a direct savings measure: it requires Cabinet papers and minutes to be proactively released within 30 business days unless there is good reason not to publish or to delay, and states that this is transparency publication rather than release in response to an OIA request. [DPMC, CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements)

### Police reporting-change confound

TBL says total OIA volumes jumped particularly between FY2018 and FY2019, from 44,960 to 101,555, and says Police OIA volume increased from 13,127 to 63,576, likely because of under-recording before a new system and the inclusion of media requests. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

MoJ separately notes that a significant increase in reported Police OIA requests was due to a change in Police reporting processes in 2018/19, and TBL says historical volume has also been affected by PSC/Ombudsman guidance on including media information requests. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

The current PSC FAQ does not say every media query should be counted; it says a media query should be logged and counted when it requires considered application of the OIA, while media seeking comment rather than information is an example of a query that would not necessarily be counted. [PSC OIA Statistics FAQs 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-FAQs-2026.pdf)

The public data therefore supports an upper-bound adjustment, not a precise media-enquiry subtraction: if Police FY2024/25 volume had remained at TBL's FY2017/18 figure of 13,127 instead of 74,526, the apparent excess would be 61,399 requests, or 38.6% of all FY2024/25 modelled requests, and at TBL's routine unit cost that excess is about $21.7m. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf); [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

That $21.7m should not be described as "media enquiries cost": it bundles reporting change, previous under-recording, genuine Police workload growth, and any media-query inclusion into one upper-bound counterfactual. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf); [PSC OIA Statistics FAQs 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-FAQs-2026.pdf)

### Proactively releasable marginal cost

TBL explicitly distinguishes high-volume transactional requests from repeated requests for information of the same type: it says six high-volume agencies mostly handle routine or personal/situation-specific information, while other agencies and Ministerial offices may receive fewer but more complex or repeated requests for the same kind of information, such as briefing titles. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

For the high-volume agencies, MoJ's own example suggests savings may come from cheaper service channels for personal/situation-specific information, such as camera footage, rather than from open-by-default publication of reusable public records. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

In TBL's FY2024/25 workbook, agencies reported 5,417 OIA responses published on their websites; using TBL's routine response cost of $352.97, one avoided repeat request per published response would equal about $1.91m. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

That $1.91m is a ceiling proxy rather than a measured saving because the PSC statistic records publication of responses, not whether anyone reused them, whether they prevented a future request, or what due-diligence cost was needed to publish them. [PSC OIA statistics planning, Jan-Jun 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-Planning-Jan-June-2026.pdf); [DPMC, CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements)

The safest reusable marginal-cost rule from the TBL model is therefore: every 1,000 measured avoidable routine requests are worth about $0.35m of gross response cost avoided, and every 1,000 measured avoidable complex requests are worth about $3.53m before publication and maintenance costs. [TBL model spreadsheet, Appendix 1](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/TBL-Report-November-2025-Appendix-1-OIA-Estimated-Cost-and-Forecast-Model.xlsx)

## What would change this conclusion

- A release of TBL's 2026 follow-up report, which MoJ says will survey about 40 agencies and examine cost drivers, could replace the proxy estimate with agency-reported repeat-request and cost-driver evidence. [Ministry of Justice, regulatory stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/)
- Agency-level data showing how many requests repeat already-published information, briefing-title lists, ministerial diaries, datasets, or other reusable records would change the savings estimate from a proxy to a measured marginal-cost calculation. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)
- Police data separating media information requests, non-OIA media queries, personal information requests, and other OIA requests would replace the $21.7m upper-bound confound estimate with a real number. [PSC OIA Statistics FAQs 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-FAQs-2026.pdf)
- I could not verify a public count of repeatedly-requested information across agencies, a public count of Police media requests included in OIA statistics, or a measured avoided-cost evaluation of a New Zealand proactive-release programme.
- This finding needs a human policy/legal reviewer before being used to design OIA reform, because cost-saving interventions can change requester rights and agency incentives under constitutionally significant legislation. [MoJ proactive release PDF, 3 June 2026](https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Publications/Proactive-release-Relating-to-Official-Information-Act-1982.pdf)

## Open follow-up questions

- When TBL's 2026 follow-up report is released, what share of OIA cost is attributable to repeated requests for the same public information rather than personal/situation-specific information?
- Which five agencies have the largest measured stock of repeatable public-information requests, excluding Police/NZDF/EQC personal or transactional requests?
- How many visits, downloads, or search referrals do published OIA-response libraries receive, and do those correlate with lower repeat-request volume?
