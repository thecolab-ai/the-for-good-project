# Hutt leads sampled councils on asset-level seismic transparency, while Wellington leads on major-project delivery visibility and the rest are patchier

## Executive answer

- **Materially ahead on asset-register transparency: Hutt City.** Hutt publishes a council-owned building register with asset names, assessment type, %NBS or grade, comments, and many linked engineering PDFs; it also exposes live delivery risk on the Petone Grandstand, including a 20% NBS rating, partial closure, a $3 million LTP budget, $223,539 already spent, and updated strengthening options of $2.93 million to $3.16 million that exclude several full-refurbishment costs [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings); [Hutt City Council Petone Grandstand project, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/pito-one-projects/petone-grandstand).
- **Materially ahead on major-project delivery visibility: Wellington City.** Wellington does not expose a single council-owned seismic asset register in the sources found, but it gives unusually current status for large public-facing projects: Te Matapihi Central Library reopened on 14 March 2026 after base-isolation remediation, and the Town Hall project page says the Town Hall will complete construction in July 2026, the annex in September 2026, and public reopening in February 2027 [Wellington City Council Central Library page, accessed 2026-07-05](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/te-ngakau-civic-precinct-programme/central-library); [Wellington City Council Town Hall project page, published 18 June 2026](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/earthquake-strengthening-projects/town-hall-strengthening/about-the-project).
- **Middle of the pack: Dunedin City and Palmerston North City.** Dunedin has useful regulatory-process disclosure and a detailed but old 2017 LGOIMA release of council-building seismic assessments; its current page says DCC is reviewing register entries and pausing new potentially earthquake-prone identifications until new legislation is in place, while its 2025-34 capital report names seismic-related renewals for the Town Hall/Municipal Chambers and Moana Pool [Dunedin City Council EPB page, last updated 30 March 2026](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/services/building-services/planning-your-project/earthquake-prone-buildings); [Dunedin City Council LGOIMA release, 2017](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/794698/Earthquake-assessment-of-Council-Buildings-LGOIMA-297641.pdf); [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report, 28 January 2025](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf). Palmerston North is a smaller high-risk comparator because MBIE lists Palmerston North as a high seismic-risk example location, and PNCC publicly says it owns several earthquake-prone buildings needing remediation by 2039, but it does not name those council-owned buildings or publish %NBS values on that page [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [Palmerston North City Council EPB page, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.pncc.govt.nz/Rates-Building-Property/Property-housing/Earthquake-prone-buildings).
- **Behind on comparable council-owned asset disclosure in this sample: Christchurch City.** Christchurch publishes LTP material, and a 2025 Newsline item disclosed a live civic-office seismic review and staff relocation, but I could not verify a current public council-owned asset register comparable to Hutt's; the available civic-office disclosure is asset-specific rather than portfolio-wide [Christchurch City Council Newsline, 20 May 2025](https://www.newsline.ccc.govt.nz/news/story/review-of-christchurch-city-council-te-hononga-civic-offices-underway); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1, accessed 2026-07-05](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the ranking is well supported for the specific public pages checked, but a full adversarial audit would need council property teams or LGOIMA exports to confirm whether unpublished internal registers exist.

## Evidence

### Comparison method

I compared five councils requested or implied by #484: Wellington City, Hutt City, Christchurch City, Dunedin City, and Palmerston North City as a smaller high-risk territorial authority because MBIE lists Palmerston North among high seismic-risk example locations [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works).

The scoring criteria were public availability of a council-owned asset register, %NBS or equivalent rating disclosure, project status, budget certainty, and whether service-critical or high-consequence assets are distinguishable from ordinary community assets; these criteria come from the issue text and are applied only to public sources I could fetch or verify [Issue #484](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/484).

### Interpretation

Hutt is materially ahead for a transparency tool because its page is close to a machine-readable starting dataset: it names individual council assets, shows assessment type and %NBS, links reports, and includes comments that distinguish "unlikely to be earthquake prone", "new build", completed NBS improvement work, and earthquake-prone assets [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings).

Wellington is materially ahead for public delivery tracking because its major civic assets have current project pages with dates and status, including a reopened Central Library and a Town Hall programme with construction and public reopening milestones, but the absence of a single current council-owned register makes it weaker than Hutt for portfolio comparison [Wellington City Council Central Library page, accessed 2026-07-05](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/te-ngakau-civic-precinct-programme/central-library); [Wellington City Council Town Hall project page, published 18 June 2026](https://wellington.govt.nz/your-council/projects/earthquake-strengthening-projects/town-hall-strengthening/about-the-project).

Dunedin's transparency is split between an old but unusually detailed LGOIMA asset release and current pages that disclose regulatory posture and selected capital lines; this is useful evidence of council work, but it is not a current public portfolio register [Dunedin City Council LGOIMA release, 2017](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/794698/Earthquake-assessment-of-Council-Buildings-LGOIMA-297641.pdf); [Dunedin City Council EPB page, last updated 30 March 2026](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/services/building-services/planning-your-project/earthquake-prone-buildings); [Dunedin City Council capital expenditure report, 28 January 2025](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf).

Christchurch should not be read as "doing nothing": the live Te Hononga example shows precautionary relocation and engineering review, and the LTP contains earthquake/strengthening/base-isolation capital lines, but public portfolio transparency is materially behind Hutt because the evidence is scattered by project, LTP row, Newsline item and LGOIMA release rather than exposed as a current council-owned register [Christchurch City Council Newsline, 20 May 2025](https://www.newsline.ccc.govt.nz/news/story/review-of-christchurch-city-council-te-hononga-civic-offices-underway); [Christchurch City Council LTP volume 1, accessed 2026-07-05](https://ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/The-Council/Plans-Strategies-Policies-Bylaws/Plans/Long-Term-Plan/LTP2024/LTP-2024-34-document-VOL-1.pdf).

Palmerston North is a useful high-risk comparator because it discloses the existence of several council-owned earthquake-prone buildings and a 2039 remediation deadline, but it is materially behind Hutt on public usability because it withholds the asset names, ratings, project stages and budget lines from the checked page [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [Palmerston North City Council EPB page, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.pncc.govt.nz/Rates-Building-Property/Property-housing/Earthquake-prone-buildings).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current council-owned seismic asset register from Wellington, Christchurch, Dunedin or Palmerston North with asset names, %NBS, assessment dates, EPB status, delivery status and budgets would change their ranking against Hutt.
- A council property-team confirmation that an unpublished register is intentionally withheld for security, privacy, commercial or operational reasons would change the transparency interpretation but not the public-usability score.
- A full LGOIMA sweep could reveal that Christchurch or Wellington has stronger internal delivery tracking than the public pages show; this finding only ranks public transparency.
- I could not verify Christchurch's 2025 Civic Building Reports LGOIMA PDF directly because plain `curl` returned an 883-byte HTML shield page, Jina returned only metadata, and the official Newsline page did not include the exact %NBS values visible in search snippets; I therefore used the Newsline item for official status and did not treat the LGOIMA PDF as a verified citation.
- This finding does not assess engineering adequacy. A structural engineer or council asset manager would be needed to judge whether a given remediation option is technically sufficient.
