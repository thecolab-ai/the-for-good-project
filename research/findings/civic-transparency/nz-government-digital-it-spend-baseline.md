# No single published actuals figure exists for NZ government digital/IT spend; current billion-dollar numbers are projections, not audited actuals

## Executive answer

- **There is no current, single published figure for total NZ government digital/IT *actual* spend.** The last (and only) time Treasury/SSC published an all-of-government ICT expenditure total was a 2006 review, which estimated **~$3.0 billion a year** (opex $1.8bn + capex $1.2bn, FY2005/06 data). [Treasury/SSC, *Report of Expenditure Review: Government ICT Spending*, Sept 2006, para 38](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/ssc-exgrev-ict-sep06.pdf) — **High confidence this is real and says this; Low confidence it reflects current spend**, since it is now 20 years old and government has grown substantially since.
- **The two forward-looking figures the discover issue (#255) flagged — "~$13 billion over 5 years" and "almost $6 billion over 10 years" — are government claims about projected future spend and hoped-for savings, not measured actuals.** The $13bn figure was publicly stated by Minister Judith Collins on 22 September 2025 as the **projected** public-sector technology spend over the next five years, with a claimed 30% (**$3.9bn**) saving achievable through centralised procurement. [Beehive, "Government digital changes to bring big savings", 22 Sept 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-digital-changes-bring-big-savings) A Cabinet paper released 11 February 2026 — *Driving Down the Cost of Digital in Government – Implementation* — shows the same $13bn figure is actually "Baselined against the 2024 investment intentions of $13 billion" [Te Kawa Mataaho Public Service Commission / Office of the Minister for Digitising Government, Cabinet paper, 11 Feb 2026, p.3](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Driving-down-the-cost-of-digital-in-government-Implementation.pdf) — i.e. the underlying number derives from 2024 agency investment-intentions data, not a fresh five-year calculation. Whether the 22 Sept 2025 Beehive release was the *first public statement* of the $13bn figure, versus a restatement of an existing internal 2024 figure, could not be confirmed either way; the earlier "first stated" framing has been corrected below. **High confidence** the $13bn is a projection/investment-intentions figure, not audited spend; **Medium confidence** on exactly when it first became public.
- **The "almost $6 billion over 10 years" figure IS traceable to a primary document** — a Cabinet paper missed in the original pass. *Driving Down the Cost of Digital in Government – Implementation* (Te Kawa Mataaho Public Service Commission / Office of the Minister for Digitising Government, released 11 Feb 2026) states the GCDO modelled cumulative savings from the Digital Government Target State and agency digital clustering programme at "between $200 and $330 million" by year three, rising to "between $2.3 and $3.7 billion" by year seven, and "between $3.7 and $5.9 billion" by year ten [same Cabinet paper, pp.2, 5](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Driving-down-the-cost-of-digital-in-government-Implementation.pdf) (fetched directly by plain HTTP, confirmed with `pdftotext`) — "almost $6 billion" is the top of the government's own year-ten range. RNZ's 2 July 2026 report links the figure straight through to its own earlier story, published 26 May 2026, which reports the same $5.9bn/10-year figure alongside "digital clusters" and the "digital government target state." [RNZ, "Government looking at centralising back-office operations to save billions", 26 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/596368/government-looking-at-centralising-back-office-operations-to-save-billions) **High confidence** — now backed by a primary Cabinet document plus two independent RNZ reports, though it remains a *modelled savings projection*, not savings realised to date.
- **The review itself confirms the core problem this issue was asked to investigate: no consolidated baseline of current digital spend exists inside government either.** The *Digital Reset Plan 2026* explicitly recommends the centre "establish consistent baseline and trends of current spend" as a fix, and separately reports that a central digital function ("GDDA"/GCDO) itself costs **$42 million a year** to run with 170 FTE. [Public Service Commission, *Digital Reset Plan 2026*, pp.7-8](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf) — **High confidence** (obtained and read the primary PDF directly).
- **The one large, well-documented, closed and reconciled programme is IRD's Business Transformation** — a 2013–2022 overhaul of Inland Revenue's tax platform. IRD's own 2022 completion announcement describes it as a **$1.5 billion** transformation programme that had officially closed. [Inland Revenue, "Sharing the secrets of successful business transformation", 15 Jul 2022](https://www.ird.govt.nz/media-releases/2022/sharing-the-secrets-of-successful-business-transformation) A broader Treasury accounting figure — **$2.61 billion** — includes depreciation, capital charges and inflation adjustment on top of the direct capital/operating spend; both figures are genuine and describe different scopes of "cost." [NZ Herald/BusinessDesk, "IRD insists transformation project tracking below budget", 14 Jul 2016](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/tax/ird-insists-transformation-project-tracking-below-budget/TCS4CUM6SU3NRGHBFDP2HYCRTM/); [Office of the Auditor-General, "Our intentions: …IRD's Business Transformation programme", 27 Aug 2019](https://ao.parliament.nz/about-us/news/2019/ird-business-transformation) (Wayback capture, see Sources). **High confidence** — three independent primary/near-primary sources (IRD itself, Treasury documents reported by NZ Herald, and the OAG) are mutually consistent once the different accounting scopes are accounted for.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The negative finding — "no current published all-of-government actuals total exists" — is High confidence, positively confirmed by the government's own review. The historical 2006 baseline and the IRD case study are High confidence. The forward-looking $13bn and $6bn/10yr figures are both now High confidence as *government claims*, traced to a primary Cabinet paper, but remain explicitly Low/no confidence as *evidence of actual spend or realised savings* — they are modelled projections, not audited outcomes. The still-unverified figure is the KPMG-reported $12.5bn (2024) number, which remains single-sourced.

## Evidence

### The question this answers

*How much does the NZ government actually spend on digital/IT, on what, and what are the biggest programmes?* This narrows the issue to what is publicly verifiable: (1) whether any all-of-government actuals total exists, (2) what the widely-quoted billion-dollar figures actually measure, and (3) the best-documented individual programme as a worked example, since no consolidated multi-agency actuals ledger could be found to exist at all (see below).

### There is no current all-of-government ICT/digital actuals total

I searched Treasury's Investment Management System, Gateway and Major Projects pages, the Government Chief Digital Officer's (GCDO, now folded into the Government Digital Delivery Agency, GDDA) published material, data.govt.nz, and NZ Government Procurement's reporting pages. None publish a current total. [Treasury, Investment management system](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management-system); [Digital Investment Office](https://www.digital.govt.nz/standards-and-guidance/governance/investment/digital-investment-office); [NZ Government Procurement, Government procurement insights](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/about-us/system-leadership/government-procurement-insights/)

The only official all-of-government ICT expenditure total I could locate is a 2006 State Services Commission/Treasury expenditure review, which estimated total government ICT spend at **approximately $3.0 billion per annum** for FY2005/06 (operating $1.8bn + capital $1.2bn), based on a sample of ~27 agencies covering ~22% of total government output expenditure, extrapolated to the whole sector. [Treasury/SSC, *Report of Expenditure Review: Government ICT Spending*, Sept 2006, paras 5, 35–38, 48](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2007-11/ssc-exgrev-ict-sep06.pdf) I could not find any subsequent all-of-government total published with comparable methodology in the nearly 20 years since — the GCDO's later "System Investment" and "Digital Investment Office" work tracks *intentions* and *cloud uptake*, not a reconciled actuals total (see next section).

Confirming this gap is not just my own search failing: the government's own July 2026 review of its digital delivery function makes the same finding. Interviewing agency leaders and reviewing GDDA/GCDO materials, the reviewers found it was "hard to get fundamental metrics to judge performance of tech investment across the agencies" with "limited understanding on baseline tech spend funded through appropriations vs. project funding," and that "reports/surveys on tech investment intentions across agencies were thinly populated." The review's own recommended fix is to "establish consistent baseline and trends of current spend" — i.e., as of July 2026, government confirms this baseline does not yet exist. [Public Service Commission, *Digital Reset Plan 2026*, pp.7-8](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf) (obtained directly and read in full; commissioned by PSC Commissioner Sir Brian Roche, released 2 July 2026, per [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds))

### What the headline billion-dollar figures actually measure

Three distinct billion-dollar figures circulate in NZ media and government commentary on this topic, each measuring something different — none is an actuals total:

1. **"$13 billion projected technology spend over the next five years"** — a forward planning estimate publicly stated by Minister for Digitising Government and the Public Service Hon Judith Collins KC on 22 September 2025, alongside a claim that centralised procurement could cut this by up to 30% ($3.9bn). The Beehive release does not establish that this was the first public statement of the figure, and the later Cabinet paper says the modelling was "Baselined against the 2024 investment intentions of $13 billion," so the safest reading is that this is a centrally aggregated projection/investment-intentions figure for the next five years — not measured spend. [Beehive, "Government digital changes to bring big savings", 22 Sept 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/government-digital-changes-bring-big-savings); [PSC/Digitising Government, Cabinet paper, 11 Feb 2026, p.3](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Driving-down-the-cost-of-digital-in-government-Implementation.pdf); repeated in [Beehive, "Public service digital transformation accelerates", 16 Dec 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/public-service-digital-transformation-accelerates) and covered by [The Post, "Centralising $13 billion tech purchases could cut costs by 30%"](https://www.thepost.co.nz/politics/360830393/government-centralise-13-billion-tech-buy-scythe-costs-30).
2. **"$12.5 billion" agency investment intentions figure** — an earlier (2024) version of the same kind of forward-looking survey number, described by KPMG's public-sector team as agencies' own five-year digital investment intentions, which DIA itself reportedly called "unaffordable and unrealistic." [KPMG NZ, "A digital dawn for New Zealand", May 2024](https://kpmg.com/nz/en/insights/government-public-sector/nz-budget-2024/digitising-government.html) — verified by fetching the live page directly; the underlying primary DIA/Treasury document this KPMG figure traces back to could not be located. **Low confidence, single (secondary/vendor) source** — flagged for further verification rather than treated as confirmed.
3. **"Almost $6 billion in savings over 10 years"** — the top of a $3.7–$5.9 billion year-ten savings range the GCDO modelled for the Digital Government Target State/agency-clustering programme, set out in the Cabinet paper *Driving Down the Cost of Digital in Government – Implementation* (11 Feb 2026) and reported by RNZ (2 Jul 2026, tracing through to RNZ's own 26 May 2026 story). [PSC/Digitising Government, Cabinet paper, 11 Feb 2026, pp.2, 5](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Driving-down-the-cost-of-digital-in-government-Implementation.pdf); [RNZ, 26 May 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/596368/government-looking-at-centralising-back-office-operations-to-save-billions) **High confidence** this is what the figure represents and where it originates; it is a modelled savings *forecast*, not savings measured/realised to date.

None of 1–3 is a measured actuals figure. All are planning estimates or aspirational savings targets — even where (as with #3) a primary source now exists, the primary source itself is a modelled forecast, not an audited outcome.

### The one well-documented, closed, and reconciled major programme: IRD Business Transformation

Inland Revenue's Business Transformation (2013–2022) is the best-documented example located in this pass of a NZ government digital programme with a clear, closed-out, reconciled public cost trail — this is not a claim that it is the *only* such programme, since no systematic comparison across all government digital programmes was attempted — and the Digital Reset Plan 2026 review itself cites it as the system's best example of a large-scale transformation done well. [Public Service Commission, *Digital Reset Plan 2026*, p.10](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf)

- **In progress (Aug 2019):** the Office of the Auditor-General described IRD as "spending between $1 billion and $1.7 billion on its Business Transformation programme." [OAG, "Our intentions: …IRD's Business Transformation programme", 27 Aug 2019](https://ao.parliament.nz/about-us/news/2019/ird-business-transformation)
- **In progress (2016):** Treasury documents reported by NZ Herald/BusinessDesk put "the total cost of the project" at "as much as $2.61 billion, when depreciation, capital charges and inflation adjustment are included" — a broader accounting measure than direct capex+opex. [NZ Herald, "IRD insists transformation project tracking below budget", 14 Jul 2016](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/business/personal-finance/tax/ird-insists-transformation-project-tracking-below-budget/TCS4CUM6SU3NRGHBFDP2HYCRTM/)
- **At completion (Jul 2022):** IRD's own media release describes the work as a "successfully completed" "$1.5 billion transformation programme" that was officially closed that month. [Inland Revenue, "Sharing the secrets of successful business transformation", 15 Jul 2022](https://www.ird.govt.nz/media-releases/2022/sharing-the-secrets-of-successful-business-transformation)
- **Origin (2014):** Treasury's Budget 2014 Cabinet paper on the programme shows the funding request process began with a $19.5 million operating tranche for FY2014/15 mobilisation work, with most cost detail withheld under the Official Information Act at time of release. [Treasury, "Inland Revenue Business Transformation: Delivering the First Steps" (Budget 2014 Information Release)](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2018-01/b14-2904662.pdf)
- **Benefits, as reported in IRD's own FY2023/24 annual report:** cumulative reduction in IRD's administration costs reached $519 million, and cumulative additional Crown revenue attributed to the programme reached $2,880 million against target. [Inland Revenue, Annual Report 2024, "Outcomes of the Transformation Programme"](https://www.ird.govt.nz/about-us/publications/annual-corporate-reports/annual-report/annual-report-2024/our-performance/our-services/residual-activities-following-the-transformation-programmes-substantive-closure/outcomes-of-the-transformation-programme)

These figures are consistent with each other once the accounting scope is understood: $1.5–1.7bn is the direct capital+operating delivery cost; $2.61bn is the fuller cost including depreciation, capital charges and inflation adjustment over the ten-year appropriation period (2014–2024).

### Other government-scale digital spend context found (not independently verified as actuals)

- Government agencies spend approximately **$51.5 billion annually** on all goods and services (not just ICT); ICT-category tenders made up **12.1%** of the *number* of tenders advertised in FY24/25 — this is a tender-count share, not a confirmed dollar share of the $51.5bn. [NZ Government Procurement, Government procurement insights](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/about-us/system-leadership/government-procurement-insights/) (fetched directly)
- All-of-Government (AoG) common-capability contracts (which include, but are not limited to, ICT) recorded **$3.2 billion spent** and **$182 million saved** in the same reporting period, per the same source. This is a subset of total ICT spend (mandated common contracts only), not the whole.
- The central digital function (GCDO/GDDA) itself costs **$42 million a year to operate**, with 170 FTE and ~$20m of that revenue from internal chargebacks. [Public Service Commission, *Digital Reset Plan 2026*, p.7](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf)
- Kāinga Ora self-funded an approximately **$170 million** technology upgrade through internal cost reallocation — cited by the Reset Plan review as a rare example of self-funded digital delivery outside the standard Budget process. [Public Service Commission, *Digital Reset Plan 2026*, p.8](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf)

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published Treasury/GCDO/GDDA all-of-government ICT actuals total for any recent fiscal year** would directly overturn the "no current baseline exists" finding — this is exactly the gap the Digital Reset Plan 2026 review itself flags, so if the GDDA acts on its own recommendation, this could change within the life of this stream.
- **The primary source for the $12.5bn (2024) KPMG-reported figure**, if located, would let it be upgraded from Low to High confidence, or could show it was a journalistic/vendor restatement that shouldn't be treated as a distinct government claim. (The $6bn/10yr figure's primary source was located during review — it is the modelled year-ten savings range in the Cabinet paper *Driving Down the Cost of Digital in Government – Implementation*, 11 Feb 2026 — so this open question is now resolved for that figure.) A next agent should try: an Official Information Act request angle (flagging this needs a human, since agents shouldn't fabricate having filed one), and the DIA/PSC media team's original 2024 briefing pack for the $12.5bn figure.
- **Whether the $13bn/$3.9bn and $3.7–5.9bn savings forecasts are actually realised** as the Target State/clustering programme proceeds (target dates through to ~2036) would let the "projection, not actual" framing throughout this finding be revisited against outturn data — this is exactly what the Digital Reset Plan 2026's recommended "baseline and trends of current spend" would need to track.
- **A line-by-line reconstruction of digital/IT appropriations from Vote Estimates and Supplementary Estimates** across the major digital-spending Votes (Internal Affairs, Revenue, Business/MBIE, Public Service, Health) for the last 3–5 fiscal years was out of scope for this pass given time — this is exactly the kind of "chunky" sub-task that could produce a genuine bottom-up actuals estimate to cross-check against the government's top-down $13bn projection. I have split this off as a sub-issue rather than attempt it partially here (see below).
- **Could not verify:** the primary source underlying the KPMG-reported $12.5bn figure; the exact date the $13bn figure was first made public (versus first modelled internally against 2024 investment intentions); a reconciled multi-year time series of actual (not projected) government digital spend of any kind post-2006.
- **This needs a human with domain/legal authority**, not an agent: an actual OIA request to Treasury/GDDA for a current all-of-government ICT actuals total, since none is published — an agent should not represent itself as having made such a request, but a human contributor or partner with standing could usefully do so.
