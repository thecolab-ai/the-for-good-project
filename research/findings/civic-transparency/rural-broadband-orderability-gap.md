# NZ cannot publicly identify rural addresses that fail a usable broadband standard

## Executive answer

- The best public answer to "which rural addresses still cannot order usable broadband?" is negative: New Zealand has public rural coverage, price, performance and programme-output evidence, but I could not find a public address-level dataset that combines live orderability, speed, latency, data allowance, price and reliability into one usable-service test [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [data.govt.nz catalogue search for rural broadband](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=rural+broadband&rows=10), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- A defensible public service standard for this research would be: orderable at the address, at least 50 Mbps down and 10 Mbps up, unlimited or effectively uncapped data, latency low enough for voice/video calls, and not materially more expensive than comparable urban broadband. Canada uses a 50/10 unlimited universal-service objective, the Commerce Commission uses MBNZ speed/latency evidence and cites 150 ms one-way latency as usable for VoIP, and the Commission separately reports rural price premiums [CRTC high-speed broadband trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [ISED Canada progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Public data can show risk groups, not a definitive address list. The Commission's rural denominator is about 352,000 addresses outside local fibre-company fibre areas, with 2025 coverage of 50% copper, 96% 4G FWA, 16% 5G FWA, 71% WISP non-cellular FWA, 1% HFC and 1% rural WISP fibre; those percentages overlap by technology and do not say whether a service is live-orderable, affordable, reliable, or fast at a specific dwelling [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- The strongest public lower-bound signal is narrow: about 4% of rural addresses were outside the reported 4G FWA footprint in 2025, which is about 14,000 addresses if applied to the Commission's 352,000 rural-address denominator, but this is not a "cannot order usable broadband" count because WISP, copper, satellite or other options may exist at some of those addresses [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).
- The likely unresolved classes are: addresses outside terrestrial footprints, addresses inside a wireless footprint but blocked by stop-sell or capacity, addresses where the only practical non-satellite service is ADSL/VDSL or overloaded FWA, addresses where satellite is blocked by line of sight or upfront cost, and low-income households facing rural price premiums. Public sources support these classes, but not their national address count [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the non-existence of a public all-criteria address count is well supported by current official sources and catalogue checks, but the exact number of failing addresses remains Low confidence because it requires non-public provider orderability, stop-sell, installation, price and outage data.

## Evidence

### Scope and standard

I interpreted "rural addresses" in this issue as the Commerce Commission's rural broadband denominator: addresses outside areas where local fibre-company fibre is available, which the Commission says covered about 352,000 addresses or 15% of New Zealand addresses in 2025 [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). This differs from Stats NZ rural household geography, so it should not be mixed with the previous Census rural-household estimate without a reconciliation step [research finding for #454](../other/rural-connectivity-underserved-baseline.md), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

I used a working "usable broadband" standard of orderable fixed-location broadband at 50/10 Mbps, unlimited or effectively uncapped data, voice/video-usable latency, a price not materially worse than comparable urban service, and enough reliability that the service is not just theoretical. The 50/10 unlimited element is not an official NZ rural obligation; it is a comparator standard because Canada's CRTC target is 50/10 unlimited to 100% of households by 2031 and Canada's ISED dashboard reports rural/urban progress against 50/10 Mbps [CRTC high-speed broadband trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [ISED Canada progress dashboard](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/high-speed-internet-canada/en/progress-toward-universal-access-high-speed-internet). The latency element is grounded in the Commerce Commission's MBNZ discussion, which says latency affects gaming and video calls and cites VoIP below 150 ms one-way as usable, while the price element is grounded in the Commission's rural price tables and affordability discussion [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Claim confidence: Medium.** The benchmark is explicit and decision-useful, but it is a research standard assembled from comparator and regulator evidence rather than an adopted NZ universal-service rule [CRTC high-speed broadband trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

### Public coverage data cannot answer live orderability

The Commerce Commission Connectivity Map is the main public NZ tool for technology coverage, but the Commission says providers supply the data, the data is a point-in-time 30 June 2025 view, the map is updated annually rather than live, wireless parcel coverage is assigned when at least 50% of the parcel falls inside a provider coverage area, satellite is excluded because obstacles can block a nominally national service, not all providers are included, provider names are not shown, and the map may differ from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). Those caveats mean the map can identify coverage-risk places, but it cannot identify every rural address that can place an order today or receive an installation that meets a service standard [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

The current public coverage figures still matter. In 2025 the Commission reported rural technology coverage of 50% for copper, 96% for 4G FWA, 16% for 5G FWA, 71% for WISP non-cellular FWA, 1% for HFC and 1% for WISP rural fibre, and it says white areas in its rural coverage figure are mostly rural areas not covered by any mapped technology [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). A simple lower-bound arithmetic check gives roughly 14,000 rural addresses outside the 4G FWA footprint, using 4% of 352,000 rural addresses, but that is only a 4G FWA coverage gap, not a multi-technology usable-service count [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The public address base exists, but the service-classification overlay does not. LINZ's NZ Addresses layer is public, downloadable, updated weekly and contained 2,419,237 address features in the metadata I checked, and LINZ's Building Outlines layer is public and downloadable, but neither contains broadband orderability, price, latency, outage, stop-sell or data-cap fields [LINZ NZ Addresses API metadata](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/123113/), [LINZ NZ Building Outlines API metadata](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/101290/). The public data.govt.nz catalogue returned zero datasets for "rural broadband", "broadband connectivity", and "telecommunications coverage" in live CKAN searches, so I did not find a government open-data package that fills the gap [data.govt.nz rural broadband search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=rural+broadband&rows=10), [data.govt.nz broadband connectivity search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=broadband+connectivity&rows=10), [data.govt.nz telecommunications coverage search](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/api/3/action/package_search?q=telecommunications+coverage&rows=10).

**Claim confidence: High** that the public map and address datasets do not themselves answer live orderability; **Low** for any inferred count beyond the narrow 4G FWA coverage arithmetic [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [LINZ NZ Addresses API metadata](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/123113/).

### The strongest unresolved address classes

Addresses outside terrestrial mapped coverage are the clearest group to investigate first, but satellite prevents treating them as automatically unserved. The Commission excludes satellite from the map because trees, buildings and other obstacles can block the signal, while its 2025 report says LEO satellite is reshaping rural broadband and measured Starlink Residential at 226 Mbps and Residential Lite at 200 Mbps in April 2025 [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). That means a terrestrial white-space address may pass the speed test with satellite, fail because of line-of-sight or upfront cost, or pass technically but not be affordable to the household [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Addresses inside wireless coverage but affected by stop-sell or capacity are a second unresolved group. NIFF says demand on rural broadband towers caused some towers to go into stop-sell where no new users can be supported, and says stop-sells and congestion restrict working from home, home schooling, e-commerce, whānau connection, event information and entertainment [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/). NIFF's December 2025 connectivity update reports regional stop-sells upgraded, including 3,517 in Waikato, 1,950 in Bay of Plenty and 2,369 in Canterbury, and defines stop-sell as a customer with mobile network coverage being unable to access a new broadband connection because the relevant tower has no spare capacity [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf). This proves the class exists and has been material enough for a public programme, but it does not publish the current remaining stop-sell address list [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).

Addresses whose only practical service is legacy copper or congested FWA are a third unresolved group under a 50/10 plus latency standard. The Commission measured rural ADSL at 10 Mbps down and 1 Mbps up, VDSL at 48 Mbps down and 13 Mbps up, and 4G FWA at 56 Mbps down in April 2025, while reporting upstream-load latency of 1,290 ms for ADSL, 493 ms for VDSL and 484 ms for 4G FWA [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). Those averages suggest many ADSL addresses and some VDSL/FWA addresses would fail a strict 50/10 plus loaded-latency test, but MBNZ is a volunteer performance sample and does not publish every failing rural address [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission MBNZ programme](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/).

Addresses facing high rural prices or high satellite setup costs are a fourth unresolved group if "usable" includes affordability. The Commission reports rural households paid a weighted average $85.77 per month for broadband versus $72.65 for urban households, rural 4G FWA unlimited plans averaged $130 per month versus $76 urban, Starlink Residential was $159 per month and Starlink Residential Lite was $79 per month, and LEO satellite upfront equipment costs were $449-$599 in its provider-website data [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). That evidence supports an affordability-risk class, but it does not identify which rural households cannot afford service because household income, plan eligibility, subsidies, equipment deals and take-up choices are not linked in the public data [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The Remote Users Scheme is the strongest official admission of a hard residual group, but its public page is an eligibility and process source rather than a live residual count. NIFF says RUS focused on rural and remote New Zealand where broadband was not currently available, only voice/text existed, or internet coverage was poor; eligible principal residences could receive support, including a one-off grant of up to $2,000 if they remained unable to receive coverage after coverage extensions [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/). The page says letters were sent to those known to have no or poor connectivity and applications required name, address and household details, which implies NIFF/provider-held address information exists, but the page does not publish the address list, application count, approval count or still-unserved count [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).

**Claim confidence: Medium.** The classes are well supported; the address counts inside each class are not public [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/), [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Commerce Commission, MBIE or NIFF table joining LINZ/parcel or address identifiers to provider live orderability, technology, stop-sell status, expected speed, measured or modelled latency, data allowance, monthly price, installation cost, outage history and satellite obstruction status would replace this evidence-gap finding with an actual failing-address count [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [LINZ NZ Addresses API metadata](https://data.linz.govt.nz/services/api/v1/layers/123113/).
- RUS application, eligibility, approval and grant-completion statistics by area would sharpen the residual no-service count; the public RUS page confirms address-level applications existed, but I could not verify public counts of applications, approvals, remaining eligible unserved addresses or post-install performance [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- Provider stop-sell/open-capacity data by tower, address or service area would change the orderability conclusion because NIFF and ComCom already show that coverage can differ from orderability, but the remaining live stop-sell footprint is not public [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).
- A formal NZ rural broadband service standard would change the threshold used here; I used Canada's 50/10 unlimited standard and ComCom latency/price evidence because I could not verify an equivalent current NZ rule that combines speed, latency, data, price and reliability [CRTC high-speed broadband trends](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/publications/reports/policymonitoring/ban.htm), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- I could not verify provider-specific address results from broadbandmap.nz, live RSP order portals, Starlink obstruction checks, household income-linked affordability, or outage histories without using individual addresses or non-public data; those are exactly the fields needed for a high-confidence answer and should be handled by an authorised agency or human-led data-sharing process, not by publishing identifiable household examples.

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues were opened; the answer is narrow enough to complete in this finding.
- Could ComCom publish a privacy-preserving aggregate table of rural addresses failing a defined standard by SA2, technology and reason code without revealing household-level data?
- What NZ-specific affordability threshold should be paired with a rural broadband service standard: urban-equivalent price, price-to-income share, or a regulated/social tariff?
- Should RUS be evaluated as "served after grant" only when post-install speed, latency, data allowance and ongoing price meet a defined standard?
