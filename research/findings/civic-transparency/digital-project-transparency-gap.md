# NZ should publish a major digital-project register with status, assurance, spend, benefits, and vendor fields

## Executive answer

- New Zealand has internal reporting and assurance machinery for major investments, but it does **not** routinely publish a live, project-level digital/IT register with green/amber/red status, cost-to-date, approved budget, latest forecast, schedule variance, Gateway/assurance ratings, benefit tracking, write-offs, and linked vendors/contracts. Treasury says Quarterly Investment Reporting gives Ministers and Cabinet visibility of significant investments, risks, issues, timing, expected funding, and whether investments are on scope, on time, and on budget, while Gateway reviews rate high-risk investments' likelihood of successful delivery; those are decision-system fields, not a standing public digital-project dataset [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting), [Treasury Gateway reviews](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/gateway-reviews).
- The transparency gap is not just theoretical. The 2026 Digital Reset Plan says the system lacks complete and timely information, that basic metrics on technology-investment performance were hard to obtain, that investment-intention reporting was thin and input-focused, and that central visibility of actual technology spend is limited because some projects are hidden in budgets and financial reporting [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf).
- New Zealand used to publish something closer to a public major-project dashboard: Treasury's Major Projects Performance Reports ran from November 2015 to June 2017 and included challenging-project assessments before being discontinued because of changed ministerial priorities [Treasury MPPR archive](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/major-projects-performance-reports). The July 2016 report published project names, agencies, responsible Ministers, delivery-confidence ratings, cost, schedule, benefits commentary, and a five-point delivery-confidence scale [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf).
- The open-by-default standard should therefore be a **major digital-project register**, not ad hoc OIA disclosure: one row per project or programme above a threshold, updated quarterly, machine-readable, linked to Cabinet/business-case releases, Gateway/independent-assurance summaries, procurement notices, and closure/benefits reports, with narrow redactions for security, privacy, active negotiations, and genuine commercial prejudice [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles), [data.govt.nz open-data policies](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy), [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting).
- Overseas comparators show the bar is realistic. The UK publishes annual Government Major Projects Portfolio reporting and says departments publish detailed data plus Senior Responsible Owner information; the US IT Dashboard publishes budgetary data, key performance indicators, CIO risk ratings, and downloadable data feeds for federal IT investments [UK major projects data](https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/major-projects-data), [UK NISTA 2024-25 annual report](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/nista-annual-report-2024-2025/nista-annual-report-2024-25), [US IT Dashboard](https://www.itdashboard.gov/), [US IT Dashboard data feeds](https://www.itdashboard.gov/data-feeds).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the existence of NZ internal reporting, Gateway assurance, the discontinued public MPPR model, and overseas comparator fields is well sourced. It remains Medium because I could not prove that no agency publishes every field for every major digital project somewhere fragmented across agency sites, and because a live all-of-government register would require legal/official-information judgement about what can be released by default.

## Evidence

### What is published now, and what is missing

Treasury's current Quarterly Investment Reporting (QIR) is the closest central mechanism I found. Treasury says QIR identifies and tracks investments through their lifecycle, gives the Minister of Finance and Cabinet visibility of the investment pipeline, and seeks to improve visibility of significant investments, risk escalation, prioritisation, sequencing, Budget advice, and investment-system performance [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting). Treasury also says agencies verify progress each quarter, reporting is signed off by chief executives or delegates, and the framework captures stage-gate timing, expected funding requirements, and whether investments are delivered to scope, on time, and on budget [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting).

The public QIR material is partial and Cabinet-paper-shaped, not a project register. Treasury's QIR page lists proactively released Cabinet papers and OIA releases, and the September 2025 Cabinet paper says the annexed dashboards include expenditure, High-Profile High-Risk investments, upcoming delivery, cost pressures, delays, planning decisions, agency maturity, and contract conversion [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting), [September 2025 QIR Cabinet paper](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/cabinet-paper-cab-25-sub-0475-qir-sep2025-v2.pdf). That paper also says three Gateway reviews in the September 2025 quarter had Amber/Red or Red ratings and required ministerial intervention, but it does not publish a reusable all-project table naming every digital project and giving each project's latest assurance rating, approved budget, cost-to-date, forecast final cost, schedule variance, vendor links, or benefit status [September 2025 QIR Cabinet paper](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/cabinet-paper-cab-25-sub-0475-qir-sep2025-v2.pdf).

Gateway assurance is also visible internally but not open by default at project level. Treasury says Gateway is an independent peer-review method for high-risk investments, required for all investments with a moderated high Risk Profile Assessment rating, and that review outcomes are shared with Ministers, Cabinet, and relevant system leaders [Treasury Gateway reviews](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/gateway-reviews). Treasury says Gateway reports are official information subject to the OIA, and that if Treasury receives a request it transfers it to the sponsoring agency as document owner [Treasury Gateway reviews](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/gateway-reviews). That means the default publication model is request-and-transfer, not a public assurance-rating register [Treasury Gateway reviews](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/gateway-reviews).

The central system itself now identifies the missing data problem. The Digital Reset Plan says the government digital system is fragmented and lacks complete and timely information, that it was hard to get fundamental metrics to judge technology-investment performance across agencies, that reports and surveys on technology-investment intentions were thinly populated and focused on raw spend and activity rather than value or impact, and that there is limited central visibility of actual technology spend because "skunk-works" projects can be hidden in budgets and financial reporting [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). The same plan recommends consistent reporting of technology cost, performance outcomes, and benefits across agencies, and proposes a whole-of-government digital investment roadmap and asset register plus internal/external scrutiny of digital spend and performance [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf).

### What NZ has previously published

Treasury's Major Projects Performance Reports show that public project-level reporting is not alien to New Zealand's investment system. Treasury says it published Major Projects Performance reports from November 2015 until June 2017, including assessments of the most challenging projects, and discontinued the report because of changed ministerial priorities [Treasury MPPR archive](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/major-projects-performance-reports). The July 2016 MPPR covered 55 major projects across 33 agencies, with a whole-of-life cost of $36b, and described major projects as 11% of the government investment portfolio but 45% of its cost [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf).

The July 2016 MPPR's fields are a useful minimum precedent. It published a five-point monitoring delivery confidence scale from Green through Red, defined the scale in terms of budget, schedule, scope, and benefits risk, published a summary table with sector, agency, project/programme name, delivery confidence, change, responsible Minister, and page reference, and said dashboards used agency-provided financial, schedule, benefits, and progress commentary plus Corporate Centre monitoring assessments [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf). It also included an explicit pre-investment pipeline dashboard with lead agency, project name, delivery confidence, indicative cost range, and whether business case clinics and Gateway reviews had been used [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf).

### Failure examples show why ad hoc release is not enough

The halted Civil Registration replacement under Te Ara Manaaki illustrates the project-cost and write-off gap. RNZ reported in April 2024 that the Department of Internal Affairs terminated its Civil Registration system replacement vendor contract and refused to say how much the failure had cost taxpayers, citing confidential vendor discussions and other programme matters [RNZ, 4 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project). RNZ later reported in June 2025 that DIA had written off $22.9m on the abandoned project, returned $58m of unspent funding to the Crown, and was starting again with uncertainty about funding and the replacement plan [RNZ, 5 Jun 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/563154/internal-affairs-to-have-another-go-at-modernising-births-deaths-and-marriages-registry). The load-bearing point is not RNZ's editorial framing; it is that public understanding depended on episodic reporting rather than a standing field for "terminated/paused", "write-off", "unspent funding returned", "vendor dispute", and "reuseable work products" [RNZ, 4 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project), [RNZ, 5 Jun 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/563154/internal-affairs-to-have-another-go-at-modernising-births-deaths-and-marriages-registry).

The Biometric Capability Update (BCU) failure illustrates the assurance/status gap. MBIE said in June 2026 that the $33m BCU project failed to deliver anything since starting in November 2018, was discontinued in November 2025, and failed because of decisions, oversight, and governance failings; MBIE also said the independent review criticised over-optimism, inadequate governance, and ignoring quality-assurance and staff advice that questioned viability [MBIE, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/mbie-welcomes-public-service-commission-investigation-after-critical-independent-review). Immigration New Zealand's public page says BCU was intended to replace and modernise ageing biometric systems for identity checks and immigration risk management, and points readers to MBIE's media release and independent review [Immigration NZ, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/biometric-capability-update-project/). Parliament's own House Happenings page records that the Speaker referred a matter of privilege to the Privileges Committee about evidence given by MBIE to the Education and Workforce Committee, and that an urgent debate on Immigration New Zealand's Biometric Capability Upgrade project was held that week [NZ Parliament, 26 Jun 2026](https://www.parliament.nz/news/a4bc5b8f-8f65-42d4-93b7-03c9e6e9736d/House-Happenings?lang=en&page=1).

### Contracts and vendors are partly published, but not linked to delivery status

New Zealand Government Procurement already requires award-notice publication. Rule 32 says agencies must publish a contract award notice on GETS within 30 business days of signing, including agency and supplier names, a description of goods/services/works, award date, contract term, expected spend or evaluated offer range, procurement process, opt-out/exemption rationale if relevant, NZBN where available, and any other requested information [Procurement Rule 32](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/government-procurement-framework/government-procurement-rules/awarding-the-contract/contract-award-notice/). MBIE also publishes New Zealand award notice data from GETS as procurement open data [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data).

The gap is linkage, not absolute absence. A public digital-project standard should link each major digital project to its GETS notices, supplier NZBNs, panel/secondary procurement where publishable, contract term, expected spend, major variations, and closure/termination status, while preserving current exceptions for genuinely sensitive supplier safety, security, and commercially sensitive content [Procurement Rule 32](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/government-procurement-framework/government-procurement-rules/awarding-the-contract/contract-award-notice/), [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data).

### What an open-by-default standard should include

New Zealand's data policy already supplies the release principle. Data.govt.nz says Cabinet's 2011 Declaration on Open and Transparent Government set a government-wide approach to actively releasing high-value public data collected and held on behalf of taxpayers [data.govt.nz open-data policies](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy). NZDIMP says government-held data and information should be open for public access unless OIA or other limits apply, released proactively online, trusted and authoritative where possible, free or reasonably priced, reusable, machine-readable, granular, and released in aggregate or modified form where original-form release is not possible [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles).

For major digital/IT projects, the standard should publish these fields quarterly in CSV/API and human-readable form:

| Field group | Minimum fields | Why it belongs |
|---|---|---|
| Identity and scope | project/programme name, lead agency, responsible Minister, Senior Responsible Owner role/title, digital category, short plain-language purpose, Cabinet/business-case stage, linked Cabinet releases and public business-case summaries | Treasury QIR already tracks lifecycle phase and stage gates, and old MPPR published project, agency, Minister, and purpose fields [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting), [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf). |
| Money | approved budget, original baseline, cost-to-date, forecast final cost, latest quarterly spend, forecast-versus-actual variance, write-offs/impairments, unspent funding returned, and whether spend is capital, operating, baseline-funded, or project-funded | The Digital Reset Plan says central visibility of actual technology spend is limited and QIR already tracks expected funding and spend against budget [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf), [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting). |
| Delivery | start date, approved end date, current forecast end date, schedule variance, delivery phase, milestone status, paused/terminated/restarted flags, reason for status change | QIR already covers whether investments are on time and stage-gate timing, and old MPPR's confidence scale explicitly covered schedule, scope, and benefits [Treasury QIR](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/quarterly-investment-reporting), [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf). |
| Assurance and risk | latest delivery-confidence rating, latest Gateway gate/type and RAG rating, independent-quality-assurance status, top public risks/issues, date of next review, and a publishable summary of management action | Gateway exists to rate likelihood of successful delivery, and old MPPR published delivery-confidence ratings [Treasury Gateway reviews](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/gateway-reviews), [Treasury MPPR July 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf). |
| Benefits and value | expected benefits, benefit owner, baseline metric, target metric, realised-to-date metric, post-implementation review date, and closure benefits report link | Treasury says investment realisation reviews actual performance, benefits, asset performance, and portfolio performance, while OAG says benefits need to be explicit in business cases and embedded in tracking once the asset is in use [Treasury investment realisation](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-realisation), [OAG asset reflections](https://ao.parliament.nz/2017/asset-reflections/part4.htm). |
| Vendors and procurement | linked GETS notices, supplier names/NZBNs where publishable, expected spend, contract term, procurement method, opt-out/exemption rationale, major variation/termination flag, and contract document or redacted summary link | Rule 32 already requires award-notice fields; the project register should connect those notices to project status and outcomes [Procurement Rule 32](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/government-procurement-framework/government-procurement-rules/awarding-the-contract/contract-award-notice/). |
| Release controls | field-level withholding reason, review date for withheld fields, and whether information is unavailable, not collected, withheld, or not applicable | NZDIMP allows protection where OIA or other limits apply, but still favours release in aggregate or modified form when original-form release is not possible [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles). |

The UK and US comparators support those fields. GOV.UK's major-projects collection says NISTA publishes an annual report as part of a transparency commitment, that the report tracks progress of projects in the Government Major Projects Portfolio, and that departments publish more detailed project data plus Senior Responsible Owner lists and appointment letters [UK major projects data](https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/major-projects-data). The UK 2024-25 NISTA annual report says the 31 March 2025 GMPP included 213 projects with £996b whole-life cost and £742b whole-life benefits, including 24 ICT projects with £36b whole-life cost and £42b benefits [UK NISTA 2024-25 annual report](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/nista-annual-report-2024-2025/nista-annual-report-2024-25). The US IT Dashboard says its IT Portfolio contains budgetary data for federal IT investments and key performance indicators, metrics, and data points to monitor investment health; its data-feed page says current IT Dashboard datasets can be selected for offline review and analysis [US IT Dashboard](https://www.itdashboard.gov/), [US IT Dashboard data feeds](https://www.itdashboard.gov/data-feeds).

## What would change this conclusion

- A current, authoritative all-of-government digital-project register that publishes the field groups above would narrow this conclusion from "missing" to "quality/discoverability gaps"; I did not find one through Treasury, PSC/GDDA, digital.govt.nz, data.govt.nz, or existing findings.
- A legal or Ombudsman review could change which Gateway, commercial, security, or procurement fields should be default-public versus summary-only; this finding proposes a transparency standard, not a binding OIA judgement.
- A complete inventory of agency-level digital project pages might show that some fields are published in scattered form, but that would still leave a machine-readability and joinability problem unless each project can be linked to assurance, spend, benefits, and vendor data.
- The `data-govt-nz` vendored skill was checked before generic search, but its CKAN calls returned non-JSON responses in this environment for "major projects", "government procurement", and "investment"; I therefore used direct official pages and search instead.
- I could not verify the full cost-to-date of DIA's terminated Civil Registration component from an official DIA source, and I did not rely on RNZ alone for a total-cost claim beyond the published $22.9m write-off and $58m returned figure that RNZ attributed to DIA.
- I could not verify whether all Gateway review ratings can safely be published without undermining candour in assurance interviews; a human official-information expert should decide the default-public summary level.

## Open follow-up questions

- What threshold should trigger inclusion in the register: dollar value, risk rating, privacy/security sensitivity, public-facing service impact, or any digital project requiring Cabinet approval?
- Which Gateway fields can be published automatically without weakening candid peer review: rating only, rating plus themes, or a redacted review summary?
- Should the register cover only central government first, or also high-value Crown entity and local-government digital projects?
- Can GETS, QIR, Gateway, Cabinet-release, and agency annual-report data be joined using existing identifiers, or is a new project ID needed?
