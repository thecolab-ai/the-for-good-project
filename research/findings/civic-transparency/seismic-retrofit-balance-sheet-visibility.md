# Seismic-retrofit obligations are visible as project and risk disclosures, not as a comparable council balance-sheet liability

## Executive answer

- The public all-council balance-sheet spine can show which councils carry land and buildings, fixed assets, current debt, term debt, total liabilities, additions to fixed assets, depreciation and a broad "valuation changes, provisions, and write-offs" expense line, but the Stats NZ June 2025 local-authority financial-statistics CSV has no seismic, earthquake, strengthening or retrofit series label. [Stats NZ LAFS release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)
- Council LTPs make some seismic work visible as named capital projects and activity commitments: Wellington's 2024-34 LTP commits to Town Hall earthquake strengthening, Te Matapihi foundation strengthening, Kilbirnie Recreation Centre remediation, and options for other earthquake-prone venues. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1, 2024-34](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)
- Asset-level risk can be much more visible than balance-sheet liability: Hutt City publishes a council-owned seismic register with %NBS grades and assessment reports, and separately says the Petone Grandstand has a $3 million LTP budget against strengthening options that are close to or above that budget. [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings); [Hutt City Council Petone Grandstand project](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/pito-one-projects/petone-grandstand)
- Insurance and debt headroom are visible in financial strategies, but not as retrofit obligations: Wellington forecasts net debt of $2.9 billion including $272 million of insurance headroom by 2033/34, while the Audit Office says councils are planning historically high borrowing and that higher-risk earthquake regions have faced particularly sharp insurance-cost increases. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1, 2024-34](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)
- The practical answer is "partly visible, not comparable": a reader can often find named projects, asset ratings, insurance headroom and broad debt capacity, but cannot roll those disclosures into a reliable national seismic-retrofit liability without hand-coding council documents and checking whether each item is already funded, deferred, insured, de-scoped, or only a regulatory role. [MBIE EPB Register](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register); [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the absence of a seismic-specific Stats NZ series is directly checked against the June 2025 CSV package, and the case examples are official council sources; confidence is not High because I did not audit all 67 territorial-authority LTPs, annual reports and asset registers.

## Evidence

### What the Stats NZ spine can and cannot show

Stats NZ's June 2025 Local authority financial statistics release is the right common spine for comparing council balance sheets because its CSV package gives council-level series for financial position, income and expenditure, and income and expenditure by activity. [Stats NZ LAFS release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/); [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)

In the June 2025 CSV package, the financial-position group includes labels such as cash and bank deposits, land and buildings, fixed assets, infrastructural assets, additions to fixed assets, current debt, term debt, current and non-current liabilities, total liabilities and total equity, while the income-and-expenditure groups include depreciation and amortisation, interest expenditure, purchases and other operating expenditure, total operating expenditure, and a broad "Expenditure on valuation changes, provisions, and write-offs" line. [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package, fetched by curl and parsed locally on 2026-07-05](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)

The same parsed CSV did not contain series labels matching seismic, earthquake, retrofit, strengthening, contingent or insurance, so the Stats NZ spine can identify where a council has debt, assets, depreciation or broad provisions/write-offs, but cannot say which part of those lines is caused by seismic remediation. [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package, fetched by curl and parsed locally on 2026-07-05](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)

The Audit Office's 2025 LTP analysis reinforces why this distinction matters: councils are forecast to spend $91.9 billion of capex during 2024-34, including $39.5 billion of renewals, and are planning historically high borrowing, with debt excluding Auckland forecast to peak at $28.8 billion in 2033 and debt including Auckland forecast to peak at $50.9 billion in 2032. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)

### Council examples

Wellington City Council is the clearest example of partial visibility: its 2024-34 LTP says the council plans $4.9 billion of capex over 10 years, and the same document names ongoing earthquake strengthening for the Town Hall, Te Matapihi foundation strengthening, remediation of the earthquake-prone Kilbirnie Recreation Centre by 2028, and options for earthquake-prone venues including the Michael Fowler Centre, Bond Store and Opera House. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1, 2024-34](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)

Wellington's financial-strategy disclosure is visible at the risk-headroom level rather than the retrofit-liability level: the LTP says increasing earthquake and climate-disaster risk has changed council insurance risk, that some public assets cannot be covered by insurance, and that net debt is expected to increase to $2.9 billion including $272 million of insurance headroom by 2033/34. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1, 2024-34](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)

Hutt City Council is the clearest example of asset-register visibility: its council-owned register lists council buildings, %NBS ratings, seismic grades and assessment links, including the Petone Recreation Ground grandstand at 20% NBS and earthquake-prone, Petone Wharf as earthquake-prone, and several assets marked as unlikely to be earthquake-prone. [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings)

Hutt's Petone Grandstand project page shows why a visible capital budget still needs interpretation: the page says a detailed seismic assessment rated the grandstand at 20% NBS, lists three strengthening options with updated estimates of $2.93 million to $3.16 million, says the LTP allocated $3 million including planning/design/construction, and says the estimates exclude items such as cosmetic repairs, asbestos removal, detailed design, project management and consents. [Hutt City Council Petone Grandstand project](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/our-projects/pito-one-projects/petone-grandstand)

Dunedin City Council shows a different channel: its public earthquake-prone-buildings page mostly exposes the regulatory process and owner-facing fees rather than council-owned capital obligations, saying the Building Act requires councils to identify potentially earthquake-prone buildings, request and review assessments, determine whether buildings are earthquake-prone, issue notices and maintain records, with building-services costs recovered by fees. [Dunedin City Council earthquake-prone buildings page](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/services/building-services/planning-your-project/earthquake-prone-buildings)

### Why this is not yet a national liability number

The EPB system creates roles for territorial authorities and duties for building owners: MBIE says territorial authorities identify potentially earthquake-prone buildings, determine earthquake-prone status, issue notices and publish register information, while owners display notices and remediate within specified timeframes. [MBIE, how the EPB system works](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)

Those roles do not by themselves create a council balance-sheet liability for every private earthquake-prone building in a district, because the owner remediation duty and the territorial-authority regulatory duty are different obligations. [MBIE, how the EPB system works](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)

The current legal and timing baseline is also moving: MBIE says non-lapsed EPB remediation deadlines as at 2 April 2024 are extended by four years during the review, and the EPB Register page says buildings with EPB notices deadline on or after 2 April 2024 have a four-year extension with a possible further two-year government extension. [MBIE, how the EPB system works](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [MBIE EPB Register](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register)

For financial transparency, the consequence is that the next step is not to add up Stats NZ debt, depreciation or provisions as if they were seismic obligations; it is to build a project-and-asset register that reconciles each council-owned exposure to its LTP budget, annual-report note treatment, insurance/headroom disclosure, and EPB status. [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip); [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings)

## What would change this conclusion

- A current official MBIE, DIA, Stats NZ, LGFA or Audit Office dataset tagging seismic-remediation cost, debt or provisions by council would change the conclusion from "not comparable nationally" to "comparable subject to validation." [Stats NZ LAFS release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025/)
- A council-by-council annual-report note review showing that councils consistently disclose seismic strengthening as provisions or contingent liabilities would raise confidence in an accounting-note aggregation. [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)
- Passage of the Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Bill or another reform materially changing which buildings require mandatory remediation would change which council-owned assets remain in scope. [MBIE, how the EPB system works](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)
- I could not verify a complete national list of council-owned earthquake-prone assets, a standard annual-report note treatment for seismic obligations, or how each council allocates debt to specific seismic projects. [MBIE EPB Register](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register); [Stats NZ LAFS CSV package](https://www.stats.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/Local-authority-financial-statistics/Local-authority-financial-statistics-Year-ended-June-2025/Download-data/local-authority-financial-statistics-year-ended-june-2025.zip)
- A human local-government finance specialist or council asset manager could materially improve the interpretation of whether a project is already funded, deferred, scoped only to life-safety strengthening, or likely to become a provision under public-benefit-entity accounting standards.
