# The EPB Bill narrows mandatory remediation to high-risk building types, but council asset effects depend on construction type and zone

## Executive answer

- The current earthquake-prone building (EPB) system remains the live legal baseline on 5 July 2026 because the Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Bill is still active at Committee of the whole House, after second reading on 30 June 2026, and has not reached third reading or Royal assent. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en)
- Under the current system, territorial authorities identify potentially earthquake-prone buildings, require owner engineering assessments, determine EPB status, issue notices and publish register information; owners then carry out seismic work within risk-area deadlines, currently extended by four years for non-lapsed deadlines as at 2 April 2024. [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)
- If the bill passes in the form described by Parliament and MBIE, mandatory EPB regulation will narrow to high-risk building types in medium and high seismic zones, with low-zone buildings and buildings outside the high-risk construction profile moving out of EPB designation. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en); [MBIE proposed changes, last updated 16 October 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system)
- Asset-level effects are not a simple "council asset in or out" switch: Hutt City's Petone Recreation Ground grandstand is currently listed as earthquake-prone at 20% NBS, but whether it remains a mandatory EPB after reform depends on whether the new methodology classifies it as a high-risk building type and what mitigation tier applies. [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings); [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)
- Dunedin/coastal Otago is the clearest "moves into scope" geography: MBIE and Parliament both say coastal Otago, including Dunedin, would move from low to medium seismic risk, while Dunedin City Council is already reviewing existing register entries, pausing new potentially-EPB identifications until the new legislation is in place, and says records outside current or proposed criteria will be marked "Outside Profile Categories". [New Zealand Parliament submissions page, 17 December 2025](https://www3.parliament.nz/en/pb/sc/make-a-submission/document/54SCTIN_SCF_350162E5-1747-474B-30BB-08DE385625F0/building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill); [Dunedin City Council, last updated 30 March 2026](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/services/building-services/planning-your-project/earthquake-prone-buildings)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction of law reform is well supported by official sources, but final legal obligations can still change during Committee of the whole House and final asset classifications require the enacted Act, regulations and MBIE methodology.

## Evidence

### Current legal baseline

The EPB system in force on 5 July 2026 is the Building Act 2004 system introduced by the Building (Earthquake-prone Buildings) Amendment Act 2016, which MBIE describes as regulating how seismic risk is identified and remediated in vulnerable existing buildings. [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)

Under that current system, territorial authorities identify potentially earthquake-prone buildings, owners obtain engineering assessments after notification, territorial authorities determine EPB status and issue notices, and determined buildings are published in the EPB Register. [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [MBIE EPB Register, last updated 18 May 2026](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register)

The current system uses high, medium and low seismic risk areas to set identification and seismic-work timeframes: high-risk areas have owner seismic-work deadlines of 7.5 years for priority buildings and 15 years for other EPBs; medium-risk areas have 12.5 and 25 years; low-risk areas have no priority-building timeframe and 35 years for other EPBs. [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)

The live timing baseline is already softened because MBIE says all non-lapsed EPB remediation deadlines as at 2 April 2024 are extended by four years, with a possible Ministerial extension of up to two further years while the review proceeds. [MBIE Building Performance, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works)

### What the bill would change if passed as reported

Parliament describes the active bill as establishing a more proportionate and risk-based system that will regulate only high-risk building types in medium and high seismic zones; the same Parliament page records Bill No. 238-2, second reading on 30 June 2026, and the current Committee of the whole House stage. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en)

MBIE's proposed-changes page says the reforms would remove low-risk buildings and buildings in low seismic zones from the EPB system, introduce tiered mitigation requirements based on location and building type, allow building-owner deadline extensions, and remove the requirement for concurrent fire and accessibility upgrades. [MBIE proposed changes, last updated 16 October 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system)

MBIE estimates the proposed changes would remove around 55% of EPBs, about 2,900 buildings, from the EPB system; give around 1,440 EPBs more affordable remediation requirements; leave 840 EPBs with no mandatory remedial-work requirement; and require full retrofit for only around 80 buildings. [MBIE proposed changes, last updated 16 October 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system)

The MBIE approval-for-introduction paper gives the most specific official construction-type signal found in this pass: the bill would regulate only buildings built of unreinforced masonry, or concrete and other heavy-material buildings that are at least three storeys high; current heavy-material EPBs of three or more storeys would remain EPBs regardless of pre- or post-1976 design date. [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)

The same MBIE paper describes the mitigation tiers as risk-register only, facade securing, targeted retrofit, and full retrofit, with full retrofit reserved for three-or-more-storey unreinforced masonry buildings in urban centres. [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)

Parliament's select-committee submissions page says the bill would remove earthquake ratings from the EPB system, regulate only high-risk building types in high- and medium-risk zones, prevent buildings in low-risk zones and buildings outside high-risk types from being designated earthquake-prone, reclassify coastal Otago including Dunedin from low to medium, and let territorial authorities grant seismic-work deadline extensions of up to 15 years. [New Zealand Parliament submissions page, 17 December 2025](https://www3.parliament.nz/en/pb/sc/make-a-submission/document/54SCTIN_SCF_350162E5-1747-474B-30BB-08DE385625F0/building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill)

[Dunedin City Council seismic-strengthening guidelines, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0011/796952/Seismic-Strengthening-Guidelines.pdf); [New Zealand Parliament submissions page, 17 December 2025](https://www3.parliament.nz/en/pb/sc/make-a-submission/document/54SCTIN_SCF_350162E5-1747-474B-30BB-08DE385625F0/building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill)

### What legal obligations remain

If the bill passes as reported, territorial authorities would still have EPB administrative obligations for in-scope buildings: Parliament says the bill regulates high-risk building types in medium/high zones, and MBIE says the bill preserves territorial-authority involvement while narrowing the buildings and mitigation requirements. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en); [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)

Owners of remaining in-scope EPBs would still face mitigation duties, but the duty would not always be "strengthen to 34% NBS or demolish": MBIE says %NBS would not be used in the new system, demolition would remain an option, and mitigation would be tiered by risk. [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)

Buildings outside the new geographic and construction-type scope would lose mandatory EPB status, but that does not prove the building is structurally low-risk for every purpose; it only means the mandatory EPB regime would no longer designate it, and commercial, insurance, health-and-safety or council asset-management decisions may still use seismic information. [New Zealand Parliament submissions page, 17 December 2025](https://www3.parliament.nz/en/pb/sc/make-a-submission/document/54SCTIN_SCF_350162E5-1747-474B-30BB-08DE385625F0/building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill); [DLA Piper, 19 December 2025](https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2025/12/a-first-look-at-the-earthquake-prone-building-regime-reform-no-surprises)

I treat the final sentence above as a legal-risk interpretation rather than settled law because the official sources define the Building Act EPB regime, while the interaction with health and safety duties, leases, insurance and asset-management standards was not resolved in the official sources checked. [DLA Piper, 19 December 2025](https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2025/12/a-first-look-at-the-earthquake-prone-building-regime-reform-no-surprises)

## What would change this conclusion

- Royal assent, Committee of the whole House amendments, a Supplementary Order Paper, or final regulations that change the bill's scope, zones, transition dates or mitigation tiers would change the legal-obligations answer. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en)
- The final MBIE EPB methodology would change asset-level calls because the current evidence identifies broad construction types and mitigation tiers, not final assessment rules for each council asset. [MBIE approval-for-introduction paper, February 2026](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/dmsdocument/31789-building-earthquake-prone-buildings-amendment-bill-approval-for-introduction-proactiverelease-pdf)
- A council-owned asset register with construction type, storeys, current EPB notice status and intended mitigation tier for Wellington, Hutt and Dunedin would raise confidence from Medium to High. [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register, accessed 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings)
- I could not directly verify the full 238-2 bill text through legislation.govt.nz because direct `curl` returned an AWS WAF challenge and the repo `scripts/fetch.mjs` returned the same challenge page; I therefore relied on Parliament's bill-status page, Parliament's submissions page, MBIE's proposed-changes page, and MBIE's approval-for-introduction paper for the bill description. [New Zealand Parliament, accessed 2026-07-05](https://bills.parliament.nz/v/6/350162e5-1747-474b-30bb-08de385625f0?Tab=history&lang=en); [MBIE proposed changes, last updated 16 October 2025](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/building-and-energy/building/earthquake-prone-building-and-seismic-risk-management-review/proposed-changes-to-the-earthquake-prone-building-epb-system)
- This is not legal advice; a building-law practitioner, territorial-authority building-control officer or structural engineer should confirm final duties for a specific asset once the Act, regulations and MBIE methodology are final.
