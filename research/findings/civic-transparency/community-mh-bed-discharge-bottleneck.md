# NZ community and residential mental-health bed capacity and the acute discharge bottleneck

This is research child **#252 of Stream #224**. It answers one question: **how many community, residential, and step-down mental-health and addiction beds NZ funds, what the waitlists look like, and how the shortage of discharge placements drives acute-ward over-capacity.**

## Executive answer

- **No one knows the national community MH&A bed count.** There is no published, defined national time series for community, residential, or step-down mental-health and addiction beds — in stark contrast to the acute inpatient series where at least partial figures exist (~608 adult acute; ~900+ acute across all age groups). Health NZ, MoH, and Stats NZ do not publish a consolidated community-bed inventory. **Confidence: High** that the gap exists; **Low** on any specific national community-bed figure.
- **The discharge bottleneck is the binding constraint on acute flow.** Acute wards run 115% to 140% of capacity, and the most-repeatedly cited reason patients cannot leave is **"a lack of beds in mental health and addiction residential services"** — many facilities "already full and had a waitlist" — not a lack of acute beds themselves [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: High** on the pattern; the 140% figure is a single frontline account.
- **Community providers will not disclose numbers.** RNZ contacted major Waikato providers (Pathways, Manaaki Trust, Ember Korowai Takitini) about demand and waitlists; none would comment, and one referred back to Health NZ. The PSA described a **"culture of fear"** among providers that they would be punished by Health NZ or government if they spoke publicly [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: High** on the refusal to comment and culture-of-fear claim.
- **Community mental health is acknowledged as under-resourced.** The Public Service Association (main union for mental health nurses) said community mental health is "resource-short" and called for "major investment and commitment" from government in community mental health [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). Health NZ admitted acute wards "still experienced spikes in demand" despite "working with community partners over the past year to improve the flow of patients to community settings" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: High** on the under-resourcing pattern; **Medium** on the one-year timeline.
- **Budget 2024 made no bid for more psychiatry beds, and Budget 2025 did not include a dedicated community MH&A bed expansion programme.** Health NZ confirmed in December 2024 that "no Budget bid for more psychiatry beds was made that year" despite being over capacity [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). I could not find evidence of a targeted community-bed expansion in Budget 2025 or 2026. **Confidence: Medium** on the no-bid finding; **Low** on the Budget 2025/26 conclusion because key government sources (Beehive, Health NZ data pages) were bot-blocked and I could not verify exhaustively.
- **Respite services are also maxed out.** Beyond residential beds, patients need emergency housing options, and respite capacity was described as fully stretched [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). **Confidence: Medium** (single-source account, but consistent with the broader resource-shortage narrative).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the *direction* (community bed shortage drives acute over-capacity; providers won't disclose numbers; community sector is under-resourced; no recent targeted bed-expansion bid) is well-evidenced and multiply attested. The *precise numbers* are absent — not because they don't exist, but because they are not published and providers refuse to share them. This is itself a finding.

## Evidence

### 1. The core finding: no published national community MH&A bed count

In sharp contrast to acute inpatient beds — where partial figures exist (~608 adult acute inpatient, unchanged 2017→2022; ~900+ acute by a broader Health NZ count by Dec 2024) [RNZ, 23 Nov 2022](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/479350/mental-health-system-struggling-to-keep-ahead-of-demand-for-acute-services-little); [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity) — there is **no published, defined national count of community, residential, or step-down mental-health and addiction beds.**

I searched for:
- Health NZ community mental health data pages (bot-blocked; 403)
- MoH publications on community MH&A (redirected to generic publications page; 403)
- Stats NZ hospital statistics (no community-bed series found)
- Mental Health Commissioner reports (domain returned 404)
- Beehive.govt.nz Budget 2025 releases (Distil bot protection blocked; 403)

The absence is notable. If the community-bed count were a tracked metric, it would appear in annual reports, data portals, or budget statements. Its absence across all three suggests it is simply **not collected or published.** This does not mean the beds don't exist — they clearly do (providers like Pathways, Manaaki Trust, and Ember Korowai Takitini operate residential services) — but means there is no authoritative denominator. **Confidence: High** that no consolidated public figure exists.

### 2. The discharge bottleneck: community beds are the binding constraint

The central mechanism driving acute ward over-capacity is not insufficient acute capacity (which is already over-subscribed) but the **inability to discharge patients to appropriate community settings:**

- A mental health nurse at Waikato Hospital reported (Aug 2025) that **"one of the key factors contributing to problems transitioning patients to the community was a lack of beds in mental health and addiction residential services."** Patients often lack a supportive household and need 24/7 care and medication oversight. "Many facilities were already full and had a waitlist, and respite services were maxed out" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse).
- The same nurse reported acute wards at **140% capacity** with patients sleeping on mattresses on the floor in interview rooms, seclusion rooms, and other wards [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse).
- Earlier in August 2025, RNZ reported an **entire ward at Auckland's North Shore Hospital** that "could not be discharged as they had nowhere to go for non-hospital care" [cited in RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). (Note: I attempted to locate and read the original North Shore article directly but could not find the correct article URL — RNZ's URL structure changed and the specific article ID was not recoverable from the Wayback Machine. The claim is cited within the Waikato article as a specific RNZ report.)
- In April–May 2024, the HRBC was at **up to 115% occupancy**, with patients sleeping on floor mattresses [RNZ, 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/517433/waikato-mental-health-facility-patient-forced-to-sleep-on-floor-due-to-overcrowding).

This is a **flow problem, not just a stock problem.** Adding acute beds (as the MHIP does) is necessary but not sufficient — if patients can't leave acute wards, new acute beds fill up too. The sibling finding (#233) documented the stock problem; this finding documents the flow constraint. **Confidence: High** on the pattern (multiply attested across 2024 and 2025, from frontline and institutional sources); **Medium** on the specific 140% and North Shore figures (the former is single-frontline-account, the latter is cited indirectly).

### 3. Why we don't know the numbers: providers won't disclose

RNZ contacted several major Waikato mental-health and addiction residential providers about current demand and waitlists:

> "RNZ asked some of the major providers of mental health and addiction residential care in Waikato about current demand and waitlists — including Pathways, Manaaki Trust and Ember Korowai Takitini — but none would comment, and one referred RNZ back to Health NZ" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse).

The PSA described a **"culture of fear"** among community providers:

> "Fitzsimons said there was a 'culture of fear' among many community providers that they would be punished by HNZ or the government if they spoke out about the reality they were facing. Fitzsimons said she wanted HNZ and the government to be clear with providers that they could present their realities, so New Zealand could know how many beds it was short of." [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse).

Health NZ's response was to decline to speak for community providers:

> "Any decision to make comment to media is one for our community providers to make and is not for Health New Zealand to speak to" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse).

This creates a **double blind spot:** providers won't disclose, and Health NZ won't publish. The result is that the exact community bed deficit is unknowable from public sources. **Confidence: High** on the refusal to comment and the culture-of-fear claim (directly sourced from the RNZ article with named actors).

### 4. Community mental health is under-resourced

Multiple sources converge on community mental health being under-resourced:

- **PSA (main union for mental health nurses):** "Community mental health was resource-short, with workers having their pay equity claims scrapped by the government, and a shortage of beds and purpose-built facilities. 'Major investment and commitment is needed by the government in community mental health, by the government.'" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)
- **Labour's mental health spokesperson Ingrid Leary:** "What was happening in Waikato showed underfunding in both the public health and community provider systems." [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)
- **Health NZ's own admission:** Vicki Aitken (HNZ mental health and addictions lead for Te Manawa Taki) said HNZ "had been working with community partners over the past year to improve the flow of patients to community settings, but admitted that the acute wards still experienced spikes in demand" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse). This implicitly acknowledges that community placement capacity has not increased enough to absorb the demand.
- **Health NZ confirmed in Dec 2024** that no Budget bid for more psychiatry beds was made that year "despite being over capacity" [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity).

**Confidence: High** on the pattern of under-resourcing (converging from union, opposition, and government sources).

### 5. Budget 2024/25/26: no targeted community-bed expansion found

- **Budget 2024:** Health NZ confirmed in December 2024 that **no Budget bid for more psychiatry beds was made** that year [RNZ, 20 Dec 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/537203/no-budget-for-psychiatric-beds-despite-being-over-capacity). This was a system-wide statement — not limited to acute vs community — but the absence of any psychiatric bed bid suggests no community-bed expansion either.
- **Budget 2025 and 2026:** I could not verify the specific Budget 2025 and 2026 mental health allocations because:
  - Beehive.govt.nz is behind Distil bot protection (403)
  - Health NZ data pages are CloudFront-blocked (403)
  - Wayback Machine snapshots of these pages were not available for the relevant dates
  - I could not locate a Health NZ press release specifically announcing community MH&A bed investment

The mental health budget is large overall (Budget 2019 allocated ~$1.9b for mental health, though much went to acute infrastructure), but a targeted community-bed expansion programme was not identifiable in accessible sources. **Confidence: Low** on the Budget 2025/26 conclusion specifically — this is an acknowledged gap, not a confident negative finding.

### 6. The broader context: respite, emergency housing, and systemic barriers

The discharge bottleneck is not just about residential beds — it extends to:

- **Respite services** described as "maxed out" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)
- **Emergency housing** also lacking, with patients who "do not have a supportive household to go to" after hospital [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)
- **Staffing pressures** compounding the problem: the same nurse reported "short staffing and the shortage of senior nurses" on acute wards, with staff on ACC leave from patient assaults [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)
- **Methamphetamine-related admissions** increasing demand: HNZ's Vicki Aitken attributed rising demand partly to "meth abuse and wider system issues" [RNZ, 14 Aug 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/569914/waikato-mental-health-patients-sleeping-on-floor-mattresses-because-of-bed-shortage-nurse)

**Confidence: Medium** on the broader context (consistent with the primary source but largely single-account).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published national community MH&A bed inventory.** If Health NZ or MoH released a defined count of community residential/step-down beds, the entire "we don't know" gap closes. The PSA explicitly asked for this: "New Zealand could know how many beds it was short of." An Official Information Act request to Health NZ for community bed numbers and waitlist data would resolve this.
- **Budget documents confirming or denying community-bed expansion.** Access to Budget 2025 and 2026 Beehive releases (currently behind Distil protection) or the underlying Budget Books would confirm whether any targeted community-bed funding exists.
- **A second journalist or researcher verifying the "culture of fear" among providers.** The RNZ account is detailed and named, but one corroborating source would elevate this from Medium to High.
- **Data from the Mental Health Commissioner's annual reports.** The Mental Health Commissioner's office should have systemic data on placement capacity — but the `justice.govt.nz/mental-health-commissioner/` page returned 404 and `mhcom.govt.nz` returned no content. A human with access to these reports would resolve this.
- **An OIA request.** The single most impactful next step: an OIA request to Health NZ asking for (1) total funded community MH&A residential bed count, (2) waitlist data by district, and (3) whether Budget 2025/26 included targeted community-bed expansion funding.
- **Could not verify:** the exact community bed deficit (providers won't tell us, Health NZ won't publish); whether Budget 2025/26 included community-bed funding (bot-blocked government sources); the original RNZ North Shore Hospital article (URL not recoverable); the Mental Health Commissioner's position on community capacity; whether the "culture of fear" extends beyond Waikato to a national pattern.
- **A human with sector authority or an OIA request is needed** to get the actual community bed numbers — this is data that exists inside Health NZ and community providers, but is not public.

## Open follow-up questions

- **What is the actual national community MH&A residential bed count?** An OIA request or sector insider could resolve this. This is the single biggest data gap.
- **How does community MH&A funding compare per patient to acute inpatient funding?** If community beds are cheaper but underfunded relative to need, that's a policy question, not just a data gap.
- **What is the Mental Health Commissioner's view on the community capacity gap?** The Commissioner's office should have this data — accessing their reports (currently 404/unavailable) would be valuable.
- **Are there regional variations in the community bed deficit, or is it national?** Waikato and Auckland are documented; the pattern in other districts is unknown.
