# Of six recurring council-spending question types, the general questions are answerable from currently published data (the formal decision record is at least legally accessible); the specific-instance ones largely require LGOIMA

## Executive answer

- This finding connects the stream's demand side (six recurring question types about council spending, from [`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)) to its supply side (national aggregates plus a 78-council front-door census and a comparability review, from [`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md), [`council-spending-data-census.md`](council-spending-data-census.md), and [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). It grades each question type as **Answerable / Partial / Not** from *currently published* material — meaning a member of the public could get the answer at the granularity the question implies, without lodging a Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act (LGOIMA) request or doing PDF extraction beyond a layperson's reach.
- The split is consequential: the **general** form of the *money and service* questions ("what does my council spend on, by activity", "is the water/roads service meeting its measures", "what are elected members paid") is **answerable** from statutory documents and national datasets. The general "what was formally decided and why" question (Q4) is a step weaker — it is **only *legally accessible* (graded Partial)**: the decision record can be inspected and copied under LGOIMA's open-meeting rules, but I did **not** verify it is reliably *published online* across councils, so it is not counted among the questions a published-data answer layer can reliably serve. The **specific-instance** form ("what did *this* trip / rebrand / legal response cost", "what were the *terms* of *this* contract and is delivery monitored", "is *this named* official misbehaving", "where are the CCTV cameras and what's the retention policy") was **not found in the national and common published data reviewed here** (national aggregates, statutory documents, and a 78-council front-door census) and is LGOIMA-shaped. That last claim is *"not reliably answerable from the published data reviewed"*, not a proven universal negative: the supply census's structured-data negative is itself explicitly **Low confidence** ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)), and a per-council publication audit could move some cells.
- The demand corpus is a supporting signal, not proof. Every title in the coded FYI.org.nz sample that underpins the six question types *is a formal LGOIMA request* — i.e. a question its author either could not answer from published material or chose to route as a request ([FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); [`council-data-questions.md` Appendix A](council-data-questions.md)). Routing a question as a request is not the same as the answer being unavailable, so this is corroborating, not dispositive. What *is* auditable from that sample is a **lower bound**: at least ~45 of the 200 titles (~23%) are, on their face, specific-instance requests the published-data supply side cannot serve — the 13 CCTV/ANPR requests, staff-conduct/senior-pay/settlement requests, internal-communications/correspondence requests, one-off named-cost requests, and named-contract/SLA/supplier requests (FYI IDs enumerated in §"The demand corpus is itself an answerability signal"). I did **not** do a full per-title answerability recode, so I make no claim about which end *dominates* — that recode is the key follow-up.
- **Implication for the candidate outcome (a plain-language answer layer).** A layer built on currently published data can reliably answer the general "where does the money go" and "is the service performing" questions using Stats NZ and MCERT aggregates, statutory annual-plan/long-term-plan/annual-report documents, and mandatory service-performance measures. The formal-decision question (Q4) is **only *legally accessible*, not established as reliably online** — the agenda/report/minutes trail can be inspected and copied under LGOIMA, but reliable online publication across councils is unverified here, so an answer layer cannot yet count Q4 as a published-data answer without a per-council online-publication audit. It **cannot**, from published data alone, answer the specific-cost, contract-terms, and conduct questions — a demonstrable, substantial share of observed formal-request demand (the ~45-title lower bound above) — for which the realistic product is *routing/templating an LGOIMA request* (what FYI already does), not answering from a dataset. The steward should weigh the answer-layer option knowing it serves the *general/aggregate* questions and must hand off, not answer, a substantial specific-instance component whose exact weight in real resident demand is not yet measured.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The answerability grade for the *general* form of each question is High **only where it rests directly on statute** (rates/activity-group reporting, mandatory performance measures, elected-member remuneration, the *legal* right of public access to the meeting record). The claim that *specific-instance* questions are not answerable and require LGOIMA is downgraded to **Medium**, and is stated as *"not found in the national/common published data reviewed here"* rather than a proven universal negative: it leans on the census finding's own **Low-confidence** structured-data negative (front-door scanning that can miss files behind document libraries, JS, portals, search boxes, or non-keyword URLs — [`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)) plus the comparability finding's Medium-confidence "no common chart of accounts". The demand corpus corroborates but does not prove this, because a routed request is not evidence the answer was unpublished. The inference about *how much* formal demand skews toward the non-answerable end is **Low** — I establish only an auditable *lower bound* (~45/200 titles), not a majority, and the demand finding deliberately does not rank question types by frequency.

## Evidence

### Scope, method, and what "answerable" means here

This answers issue #241 (Part of #3, Stream #3): which of the six recurring citizen question types about council spending can be answered from *currently published* data, and which cannot. It is a synthesis/bridge finding — it does not re-run the supply census or re-code the demand sample, but reasons over the four completed stream findings and adds primary statutory sources for each answerability claim.

I define **"answerable from currently published data"** as: a member of the public could obtain the answer, at the granularity the question implies, from proactively published material (statutory finance/planning documents, national open datasets, council open-data portals, or the published meeting record) **without** lodging an LGOIMA request and without extraction skills beyond reading a document or downloading a table. I grade each question type **Answerable**, **Partial**, or **Not** on that test, and name the fallback (usually LGOIMA) where it fails. The six types and their evidence base are taken verbatim from the demand finding ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

The supply facts I rely on are established in the three supply findings and summarised here: national machine-readable baselines exist (Stats NZ local-authority financial statistics; DIA long-term-plan financial tables; MBIE/GETS procurement award notices; MCERT council-performance profiles), statutory documents (long-term plans, annual plans, annual reports) exist everywhere in principle, but **very little council-published structured or transaction-level spending data** was found, and there is **no public common chart of accounts** — comparability holds at statement/activity-group level only, not at project or transaction level ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md); [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md); [Stats NZ local authority statistics](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html)).

### Why the "general" questions are answerable (statutory basis)

**Q1 — rates and what they fund.** The Local Government Act 2002 requires long-term plans and annual reports to report by *groups of activities* and to include an audited funding impact statement for each group, so "what does my council fund, and how is it rated for" is published at activity level ([Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10, clauses 23–26](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html); [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). At property level, the Local Government (Rating) Act 2002 requires councils to keep a **rating information database** that must be available for **free public inspection**, and councils must give public notice each May of where it can be inspected ([Local Government (Rating) Act 2002](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/6/en/latest/); [Taituarā, *Rating Know-How* guide to the Act](https://taituara.org.nz/Folder?Action=View+File&Folder_id=90&File=Rating+Guide+final+-+web+version.pdf)). National aggregates (Stats NZ local-authority financial statistics; MCERT council-performance profiles) answer the cross-council "where does the money go / how does my council compare on rates and expenditure" version ([Stats NZ, March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/)).

**Q4 — the formal decision trail (legal access, not necessarily online).** LGOIMA does more than create a request right: Part 7 sets standards of openness for local-authority meetings, requiring meetings to be publicly notified and open to the public, agendas and associated reports to be open to public inspection, and the public to be able to **inspect the minutes and obtain copies**, with the public excludable only by resolution for specified reasons ([Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act 1987](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/0174/latest/DLM122242.html); [Ombudsman, LGOIMA guide for local-government agencies](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/lgoima-local-government-agencies-guide-processing-requests-and-conducting-meetings)). Crucially, the statutory guarantee is *access* — inspection at the local authority's office during ordinary office hours and copies on request — **not** a requirement that the full decision trail be published online in a usable, connected form. So the formal decision record is *legally accessible without lodging an LGOIMA request*; whether an answer layer can actually *pull* it from published data depends on each council's online publishing, which I did not test across councils (see "What would change this conclusion"). What is *not* proactively available either way is the internal correspondence and communications behind the decision, which is why the demand sample's "internal communications" and "correspondence" requests are LGOIMA requests ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)). I therefore grade Q4 **Partial**, not Answerable.

**Q5 — service performance.** Mandatory non-financial performance measures require local authorities (and relevant council-controlled organisations) to report standard measures for five activity groups — water supply, wastewater, stormwater, flood protection, and roads and footpaths — in their long-term plans and annual reports, and the statement of service provision is audited ([Office of the Auditor-General, on the mandatory performance measures](https://oag.parliament.nz/2015/ltps/part5.htm); [DIA local government policy — mandatory non-financial performance measures](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy); [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html)). The current rule set is the **Non-Financial Performance Measures Rules 2024**, which came into force on **21 August 2024**; that update was a technical one that revised only water-supply Performance Measure 1 (safety of drinking water — replacing references to the revoked 2005 standards with the Drinking Water Quality Assurance Rules 2022) and left the other activity groups' measures unchanged, so the earlier 2013 rules still describe the substance for wastewater, stormwater, flood protection, and roads ([NZ Gazette, *Non-Financial Performance Measures Rules 2024* (2024-go3549), in force 21 August 2024](https://gazette.govt.nz/notice/id/2024-go3549)). So "is the water/roads service meeting its measures" is answerable; asset-condition and renewal-backlog detail (e.g. the Tasman water-renewal backlog that surfaced through Local Democracy Reporting) is more variable and often not in a queryable published form ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

**Q6 — elected-member remuneration.** The Remuneration Authority determines the pay of local-government elected members (mayors, councillors, regional chairs) under a published methodology and determination, so "what are elected members paid" is answerable from a published source ([Remuneration Authority — local government members](https://www.remauthority.govt.nz/local-government-members); [Remuneration Authority — pay for local government members](https://www.remauthority.govt.nz/local-government-members/pay-local-government-members)). Staff conduct, investigation costs, and specific senior-staff pay — the governance items that appear in the demand sample as "staff conduct" and "$200k job titles" requests — are not proactively published and are LGOIMA-shaped ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

### Why the "specific-instance" questions are not answerable from published data

The census across all 78 councils found statutory finance documents everywhere in principle but **very little council-published structured spending data**, and *no matched URL carried a CSV/XLS/JSON structured extension* in the front-door scan; the strongest directly verified structured example (Christchurch's capital-works programme) still only exposes budget *bands*, not exact spend, suppliers, invoices, or contracts ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)). **Two honesty caveats bound how hard this negative can be pushed.** First, that census's own authors mark the structured-data negative **Low confidence**, because the scan is homepage + root-sitemap keyword crawling and a structured file can sit behind a document library, a JS download, a portal, a search box, an extensionless URL, or a URL without one of the bounded keywords ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)). Second, the comparability finding is **Medium-confidence** evidence about cross-council *comparability*, not direct proof that any given trip, rebrand, contract, or conduct record is un-published by every council. So the correct statement is *not found in the national and common published data reviewed here*, not *proven unpublished anywhere*. The comparability finding independently establishes that NZ has **no public common chart of accounts or transaction taxonomy** — reporting is comparable at statement/activity-group level only, and LGOIMA "is reactive access to information held by each council, not evidence of a proactive national open-data standard" ([`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). Together these mean the item-level, contract-term, supplier-level, and conduct questions are **not reliably answerable from currently published national/common data** and typically require a request — though a per-council publication audit could flip specific cells. The GETS award-notice dataset partly answers "who won a tendered contract" for councils that use GETS, but NZ Government Procurement warns its GETS information "should not be used to consolidate financial data", so it is procurement-notice evidence, not a spending or contract-terms answer ([MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data); [NZ Government Procurement, data and reporting](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/data-and-reporting/); [`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)).

### The demand corpus is itself an answerability signal

This is a supporting bridge — but a bounded one. The six question types are derived from a coded sample of FYI.org.nz request titles, and FYI publishes *Official Information Act / LGOIMA requests* and their responses ([FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); [`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)). Every one of those titles is a question its author *routed as a formal request* rather than answering from published material — which is corroborating, not conclusive, because a requester may route a question they could have answered otherwise.

**What can be audited without a full recode is a lower bound.** The demand finding's Appendix A codes titles by *broad topic* (service 38, money 26, reg 22, gov 21, decision 17, cctv 13, contract 9, ambiguous 54), and — as the reviewers correctly note — those topic buckets *mix* answerable and non-answerable forms (e.g. `service` spans published GTFS feeds *and* one-off asset failures; `money` spans rating policy *and* named project costs). I did **not** re-code all 200 titles by answerability. What I can defend by inspection is that **at least ~45 of the 200 titles (~23%) are, on their own wording, specific-instance requests the published national/common data cannot serve** — a floor, not a majority claim (FYI IDs from [`council-data-questions.md` Appendix A](council-data-questions.md)):

- **CCTV/ANPR (13):** 33756, 34776, 33776, 29319, 27343, 29320, 29071, 28108, 32029, 30219, 30480, 29148, 29316.
- **Staff conduct / senior-staff pay / settlements (~6):** 34661, 34662, 34663 (the same request bulk-filed to three councils — one demand signal, three titles), 34599 (job titles over $200k), 34215 (termination/settlement payments), 30159 (staff benefits).
- **Internal communications / correspondence behind a decision (~8):** 33823, 32553, 33027, 33026, 28955, 33656, 8774, 16510.
- **One-off named costs (~8):** 34798 (chamber fit-out), 34656 (Spragg Bush bridges), 34208 (Wycola skatepark), 28065 / 27842 (Claudelands grandstand), 30275 (coffee machines), 34657 (Fairy Falls path), 29154 (pool WorkSafe records).
- **Named contract / SLA / supplier lookups (~10):** 34383 (Whiteside SLA), 34276 (Te Kaha naming-rights), 33112 (Deloitte/contract mgmt), 34600 (long-term contractors), 34640 (Cheops contract), 32886 (payments to Agite), 26860 (Joyce Advisory), 15963 (QAC agreement), 33768 (service agreements), 30971 (council payments made).

By contrast, the titles the published data *clearly does* answer (statutory rating policy 34375/34848/35000/10443, elected-member/governance statutory documents 34342/31712, published open datasets like GTFS 34720) are a smaller, though real, set. The honest reading is therefore: **both ends are present; a substantial specific-instance component is demonstrable; which end *dominates* real resident demand is not established** — the demand finding itself declines to rank types by frequency, and its clearest rates signal comes from *council-solicited* consultation, not organic requests ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)). A full per-title answerability recode is the load-bearing follow-up (see below).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A per-council answerability census.** Answerability is graded here at the *question-type* level using national rules plus a front-door supply census. Individual councils may publish more than the national/common baseline — Christchurch's structured capital-works dataset is one *verified* example ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)) — and the audit should test, per council, for surfaces I have **not** confirmed exist in NZ and am flagging as hypotheticals to check, not as established facts: a public **contract register**, a **proactive LGOIMA disclosure log**, project-level capital data, and an online rates calculator. Wherever such a surface exists, it would move that council's "Partial/Not" cells toward "Answerable" and change where an answer-layer pilot is feasible. This is the single most valuable follow-up and a clean fan-out candidate.
- **A full per-title answerability recode of the demand sample.** This finding establishes only an auditable *lower bound* (~45/200 titles specific-instance); it does **not** recode all 200 FYI titles as answerable-vs-not, because the upstream topic categories mix both forms and a second coding layer compounds subjectivity. Re-coding every title in Appendix A by answerability (with a second coder to measure disagreement) is what would let anyone claim which end *dominates*. Until then, treat "a substantial specific-instance component exists" as supported and "the non-answerable end dominates" as **not** established.
- **A frequency-weighted demand study.** Even a clean recode of the FYI sample would still rest on a small, self-selecting corpus the demand finding refuses to rank by frequency. A representative survey of residents (or council call-centre / elected-member inbox themes) could show the *most common* questions are actually the answerable aggregate ones, which would strengthen the answer-layer case rather than weaken it.
- **A shift toward proactive disclosure.** If NZ adopted a UK-style transparency-code duty to publish spending over a threshold (noted as international prior art in [`council-spending-legibility-framing.md`](council-spending-legibility-framing.md)), or councils widely published disclosure logs, the specific-instance questions would migrate from "Not" to "Answerable" over time.
- **What I could not verify.** I did not verify online availability/coverage of council rates calculators or of the RID online (the statutory *inspection* right is verified; convenient online access is not). I did not confirm whether individual councils publish employee-remuneration bands in annual reports (a common practice, but I found no statutory requirement to cite, so I omitted the claim). I did not test the *quality* of published meeting portals (whether minutes reliably connect to reports and votes in a usable way). A council democracy-services officer, a working local-government journalist, or an FYI power-user should sanity-check the answerability grades against day-to-day reality.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Per-council answerability census:** which of the 78 councils publish a contract register, a proactive LGOIMA disclosure log, project-level capital data, or an online rates calculator — i.e. where do the "Partial/Not" cells flip to "Answerable"? (Chunky; a strong fan-out candidate.)
- For the specific-instance half of demand that published data can't serve, would an **LGOIMA-routing/templating** layer (what FYI already does, plus council-specific templates) be a better product than an answer layer — and could proactive-disclosure logs turn repeat requests into a published answer over time?
- Does the aggregate answerable layer (Stats NZ + MCERT + activity-group funding impact statements + published minutes + mandatory performance measures) actually satisfy a resident's *plain-language* question, or does the group-of-activities granularity fall between "national aggregate" and "the specific thing I asked about"?
