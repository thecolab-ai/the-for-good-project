# Of six recurring council-spending question types, the general questions are answerable from currently published data; the specific-instance ones largely require LGOIMA

## Executive answer

- This finding connects the stream's demand side (six recurring question types about council spending, from [`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)) to its supply side (national aggregates plus a 78-council front-door census and a comparability review, from [`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md), [`council-spending-data-census.md`](council-spending-data-census.md), and [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). It grades each question type as **Answerable / Partial / Not** from *currently published* material — meaning a member of the public could get the answer at the granularity the question implies, without lodging a Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act (LGOIMA) request or doing PDF extraction beyond a layperson's reach.
- The split is clean and consequential: the **general** form of a question ("what does my council spend on, by activity", "what was formally decided and why", "is the water/roads service meeting its measures", "what are elected members paid") is **answerable** from statutory documents and national datasets. The **specific-instance** form ("what did *this* trip / rebrand / legal response cost", "what were the *terms* of *this* contract and is delivery monitored", "is *this named* official misbehaving", "where are the CCTV cameras and what's the retention policy") is **largely not answerable** from proactively published data and is LGOIMA-shaped.
- The demand corpus is itself evidence of this split. Every title in the coded FYI.org.nz sample that underpins the six question types *is a formal LGOIMA request* — i.e. a question its author either could not answer from published material or chose to route as a request ([FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); [`council-data-questions.md` Appendix A](council-data-questions.md)). That sample skews heavily toward the specific-instance, LGOIMA-dependent end (one-off costs, named contracts, staff conduct, CCTV), which is precisely the demand a "plain-language answer layer over published data" would *least* be able to serve.
- **Implication for the candidate outcome (a plain-language answer layer).** A layer built on currently published data can reliably answer the general "where does the money go / what was decided / is the service performing" questions using Stats NZ and MCERT aggregates, statutory annual-plan/long-term-plan/annual-report documents, published meeting agendas/reports/minutes, and mandatory service-performance measures. It **cannot**, from published data alone, answer the specific-cost, contract-terms, and conduct questions that dominate the observable formal-request demand — for those, the realistic product is *routing/templating an LGOIMA request* (what FYI already does), not answering from a dataset. The steward should weigh the answer-layer option knowing it addresses the *general/aggregate* half of demand and must hand off, not answer, the *specific-instance* half.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. The answerability grade for the *general* form of each question is High where it rests on statute (rates/activity reporting, the public meeting record, mandatory performance measures, elected-member remuneration). The claim that *specific-instance* questions require LGOIMA is High (supported both by the census finding's structured-data negative and by the fact that the demand corpus is made of LGOIMA requests). The inference that formal demand skews toward the non-answerable end is Medium, because FYI is a small, self-selecting sample and the demand finding deliberately does not rank question types by frequency.

## Evidence

### Scope, method, and what "answerable" means here

This answers issue #241 (Part of #3, Stream #3): which of the six recurring citizen question types about council spending can be answered from *currently published* data, and which cannot. It is a synthesis/bridge finding — it does not re-run the supply census or re-code the demand sample, but reasons over the four completed stream findings and adds primary statutory sources for each answerability claim.

I define **"answerable from currently published data"** as: a member of the public could obtain the answer, at the granularity the question implies, from proactively published material (statutory finance/planning documents, national open datasets, council open-data portals, or the published meeting record) **without** lodging an LGOIMA request and without extraction skills beyond reading a document or downloading a table. I grade each question type **Answerable**, **Partial**, or **Not** on that test, and name the fallback (usually LGOIMA) where it fails. The six types and their evidence base are taken verbatim from the demand finding ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

The supply facts I rely on are established in the three supply findings and summarised here: national machine-readable baselines exist (Stats NZ local-authority financial statistics; DIA long-term-plan financial tables; MBIE/GETS procurement award notices; MCERT council-performance profiles), statutory documents (long-term plans, annual plans, annual reports) exist everywhere in principle, but **very little council-published structured or transaction-level spending data** was found, and there is **no public common chart of accounts** — comparability holds at statement/activity-group level only, not at project or transaction level ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md); [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md); [Stats NZ local authority statistics](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html)).

### Why the "general" questions are answerable (statutory basis)

**Q1 — rates and what they fund.** The Local Government Act 2002 requires long-term plans and annual reports to report by *groups of activities* and to include an audited funding impact statement for each group, so "what does my council fund, and how is it rated for" is published at activity level ([Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10, clauses 23–26](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html); [`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). At property level, the Local Government (Rating) Act 2002 requires councils to keep a **rating information database** that must be available for **free public inspection**, and councils must give public notice each May of where it can be inspected ([Local Government (Rating) Act 2002](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/6/en/latest/); [Taituarā, *Rating Know-How* guide to the Act](https://taituara.org.nz/Folder?Action=View+File&Folder_id=90&File=Rating+Guide+final+-+web+version.pdf)). National aggregates (Stats NZ local-authority financial statistics; MCERT council-performance profiles) answer the cross-council "where does the money go / how does my council compare on rates and expenditure" version ([Stats NZ, March 2026 quarter](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/local-authority-statistics-march-2026-quarter/); [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/)).

**Q4 — the formal decision trail.** LGOIMA does more than create a request right: Part 7 sets standards of openness for local-authority meetings, requiring meetings to be publicly notified and open to the public and their **agendas, reports, and minutes** to be available to the public, with the public excludable only by resolution for specified reasons ([Local Government Official Information and Meetings Act 1987](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/0174/latest/DLM122242.html); [Ombudsman, LGOIMA guide for local-government agencies](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/lgoima-local-government-agencies-guide-processing-requests-and-conducting-meetings)). So the *published* decision record — the agenda item, the officer's report, the resolution, the minutes — is answerable from council meeting portals without a request. What is *not* published is the internal correspondence and communications behind the decision, which is why the demand sample's "internal communications" and "correspondence" requests are LGOIMA requests ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

**Q5 — service performance.** The Non-Financial Performance Measures Rules 2013 require local authorities (and relevant council-controlled organisations) to report standard mandatory measures for five activity groups — water supply, wastewater, stormwater, flood protection, and roads and footpaths — in their long-term plans and annual reports, and the statement of service provision is audited ([Office of the Auditor-General, on the mandatory performance measures](https://oag.parliament.nz/2015/ltps/part5.htm); [DIA local government policy — mandatory non-financial performance measures](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy); [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html)). So "is the water/roads service meeting its measures" is answerable; asset-condition and renewal-backlog detail (e.g. the Tasman water-renewal backlog that surfaced through Local Democracy Reporting) is more variable and often not in a queryable published form ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

**Q6 — elected-member remuneration.** The Remuneration Authority determines the pay of local-government elected members (mayors, councillors, regional chairs) under a published methodology and determination, so "what are elected members paid" is answerable from a published source ([Remuneration Authority — local government members](https://www.remauthority.govt.nz/local-government-members); [Remuneration Authority — pay for local government members](https://www.remauthority.govt.nz/local-government-members/pay-local-government-members)). Staff conduct, investigation costs, and specific senior-staff pay — the governance items that appear in the demand sample as "staff conduct" and "$200k job titles" requests — are not proactively published and are LGOIMA-shaped ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

### Why the "specific-instance" questions are not answerable from published data

The census across all 78 councils found statutory finance documents everywhere in principle but **very little council-published structured spending data**, and *no matched URL carried a CSV/XLS/JSON structured extension* in the front-door scan; the strongest directly verified structured example (Christchurch's capital-works programme) still only exposes budget *bands*, not exact spend, suppliers, invoices, or contracts ([`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)). The comparability finding independently establishes that NZ has **no public common chart of accounts or transaction taxonomy** — reporting is comparable at statement/activity-group level only, and LGOIMA "is reactive access to information held by each council, not evidence of a proactive national open-data standard" ([`council-spending-comparability.md`](council-spending-comparability.md)). Together these mean the item-level, contract-term, supplier-level, and conduct questions cannot be answered from what is currently published; they require a request. The GETS award-notice dataset partly answers "who won a tendered contract" for councils that use GETS, but NZ Government Procurement warns its GETS information "should not be used to consolidate financial data", so it is procurement-notice evidence, not a spending or contract-terms answer ([MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data); [NZ Government Procurement, data and reporting](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/data-and-reporting/); [`council-spending-census-78.md`](council-spending-census-78.md)).

### The demand corpus is itself an answerability signal

This is the load-bearing bridge. The six question types are derived from a coded sample of FYI.org.nz request titles, and FYI publishes *Official Information Act / LGOIMA requests* and their responses ([FYI, About](https://fyi.org.nz/help/about); [`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)). Every one of those titles is therefore a question that its author routed as a formal request rather than answering from published material. Reading the demand finding's Appendix A through the answerability grades above, the sample is dominated by the *specific-instance* end — one-off project/trip/rebrand costs (`money`), named-supplier and contract lookups (`contract`), staff-conduct and pay requests (`gov`), CCTV/ANPR policy and location requests (`cctv`), and internal-communications requests (`decision`) — i.e. exactly the questions the supply side cannot answer from published data. The narrowly aggregate questions that published data *does* answer (activity-level rates, cross-council comparison) are under-represented in the request corpus, plausibly *because* they are already answerable and so seldom become a formal request. This is consistent with the demand finding's own framing that its rates signal comes mostly from *council-solicited* consultation, not organic requests ([`council-data-questions.md`](council-data-questions.md)).

## What would change this conclusion

- **A per-council answerability census.** Answerability is graded here at the *question-type* level using national rules plus a front-door supply census; some individual councils publish far more (Christchurch's structured capital-works data; councils that maintain public **contract registers** or **proactive LGOIMA disclosure logs**; councils with online rates calculators). A council-by-council audit could move several "Partial/Not" cells to "Answerable" for those councils and would materially change where an answer-layer pilot is feasible. This is the single most valuable follow-up and a clean fan-out candidate.
- **A frequency-weighted demand study.** The load-bearing "demand skews toward the non-answerable end" inference rests on a small, self-selecting FYI sample that the demand finding explicitly refuses to rank by frequency. A representative survey of residents (or council call-centre / elected-member inbox themes) could show the *most common* questions are actually the answerable aggregate ones, which would strengthen the answer-layer case rather than weaken it.
- **A shift toward proactive disclosure.** If NZ adopted a UK-style transparency-code duty to publish spending over a threshold (noted as international prior art in [`council-spending-legibility-framing.md`](council-spending-legibility-framing.md)), or councils widely published disclosure logs, the specific-instance questions would migrate from "Not" to "Answerable" over time.
- **What I could not verify.** I did not verify online availability/coverage of council rates calculators or of the RID online (the statutory *inspection* right is verified; convenient online access is not). I did not confirm whether individual councils publish employee-remuneration bands in annual reports (a common practice, but I found no statutory requirement to cite, so I omitted the claim). I did not test the *quality* of published meeting portals (whether minutes reliably connect to reports and votes in a usable way). A council democracy-services officer, a working local-government journalist, or an FYI power-user should sanity-check the answerability grades against day-to-day reality.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Per-council answerability census:** which of the 78 councils publish a contract register, a proactive LGOIMA disclosure log, project-level capital data, or an online rates calculator — i.e. where do the "Partial/Not" cells flip to "Answerable"? (Chunky; a strong fan-out candidate.)
- For the specific-instance half of demand that published data can't serve, would an **LGOIMA-routing/templating** layer (what FYI already does, plus council-specific templates) be a better product than an answer layer — and could proactive-disclosure logs turn repeat requests into a published answer over time?
- Does the aggregate answerable layer (Stats NZ + MCERT + activity-group funding impact statements + published minutes + mandatory performance measures) actually satisfy a resident's *plain-language* question, or does the group-of-activities granularity fall between "national aggregate" and "the specific thing I asked about"?
