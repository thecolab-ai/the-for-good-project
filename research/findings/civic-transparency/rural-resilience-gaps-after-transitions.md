# Rural mobile and emergency resilience gaps remain visible after copper and 3G transitions, but public data still cannot rank exposed communities

## Executive answer

- The best public answer is not a ranked list of exposed communities; it is a risk register with hard lower-bound counts. Public sources quantify rural copper exposure, mobile landmass coverage, Mobile Black Spot Fund outputs, 3G-dependent-address exposure, and registered 111-vulnerable landline consumers, but they do not publish a combined map of rural communities, roads, devices, vulnerable users, outage history, and backup-power duration [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf), [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code 2025 Compliance Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/111-Contact-Code-Compliance-Report-20242025.pdf).
- Copper withdrawal still leaves a material managed-transition task: the Commerce Commission counted about 223,100 premises in rural copper areas, 56,000 remaining copper broadband connections at 30 June 2025, and 12,000 remaining copper voice-only connections at 30 June 2025 [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf).
- The 3G shutdown did not create a large address-level coverage hole in the public data, but it did create device and edge-coverage risk: ComCom estimated that 500 addresses, or 0.02% of New Zealand addresses, relied entirely on 3G coverage as at June 2025, while TCF says 3G-reliant phones, tablets, medical and security alarms, vehicle trackers, and business devices no longer work after shutdown, including for 111 calling [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown).
- Rural mobile safety remains geographic even when population coverage is high: ComCom reported 99.0% 4G population coverage but only 54.6% average 4G landmass coverage in 2025, and said coverage models do not always reflect real-world experience [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Emergency resilience is still power- and tower-dependent: the 111 Contact Code exists for landline consumers whose newer home-phone technologies may fail in a power cut, and NEMA warns that Emergency Mobile Alerts may not be received outside mobile coverage, when towers are damaged, or when power is out [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code page](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/telecommunications-for-consumers/commission-111-contact-code/), [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the residual risk categories and official counts are well supported, but the exposure ranking that #588 asks for would require non-public or unpublished joined data from providers, emergency-management agencies, road authorities, electricity networks, device vendors, councils, iwi, and rural communities.

## Evidence

### What can be quantified now

**Copper and voice exposure.** ComCom defines rural copper areas as rural premises with a copper line enabling access to a copper-based service, and counted about 223,100 premises in those areas as at 30 June 2024 [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf). ComCom says rural copper broadband connections fell from 74,000 at 30 June 2024 to 56,000 at 30 June 2025, and that rural copper voice-only connections fell to 12,000 at 30 June 2025 [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf). ComCom also says Chorus plans to stop using copper by 2030 and that a managed withdrawal process is important to protect rural consumers during change [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf).

**Mobile coverage exposure.** ComCom reported that 4G mobile population coverage reached 99.0% in 2025, while average 4G landmass coverage was 54.6%, and noted that MNO propagation models do not always match real-world experience [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). ComCom also says 4G coverage is not a like-for-like replacement for 3G coverage for every consumer, because the technologies differ and some consumers may experience changed ability to connect [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**3G shutdown exposure.** ComCom estimated that 500 addresses, or 0.02% of New Zealand addresses, relied entirely on 3G coverage at June 2025, and said it was monitoring impacted devices and expected to discuss impacts in the next report [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). TCF says all New Zealand mobile providers had shutdown timetables ending with the last MNO on 31 March 2026, and that 3G-reliant devices would stop working for calls, texts, data, and emergency 111 calling [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown). One NZ said on 23 March 2026 that its 2G and 3G networks were fully retired and that remaining customers using older equipment would be unable to call, text, use data, or contact 111 on those devices [One NZ, 2G and 3G shutdown complete](https://media.one.nz/2g-3g-shutdown-complete). 2degrees says its 3G services have not been available since February 2026 and that devices relying on 3G cannot make emergency 111 calls on its network [2degrees, 3G shutdown guidance](https://www.2degrees.nz/help/mobile-help/troubleshooting/things-to-know-about-our-3g-shutdown).

**Road and tourism exposure.** ComCom says the Mobile Black Spot Fund had completed coverage to 192 tourism sites and about 2,000 km of state highways and local roads by 30 June 2025, but also says MBSF does not publish public quality metrics such as "good", "moderate" or "limited" coverage for each site [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). NIFF's December 2025 connectivity update states programme targets of about 2,000 km of state-highway and local-road mobile coverage, more than 200 tourism sites, 108 battery-capacity upgrades, and 15 settlements with voice coverage [NIFF Connectivity Update, December 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf). NIFF describes the rural mobile programme as supporting safety on state highways, visitor experience at tourist destinations, and emergency response capability in rural areas [NIFF, Rural Mobile](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-mobile/).

**111-vulnerable consumers.** The 2025 111 Contact Code compliance report says there were 891 registered vulnerable consumers at 30 June 2025, 441,969 residential landline services, and 394 vulnerable-consumer applications during the disclosure year [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code 2025 Compliance Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/111-Contact-Code-Compliance-Report-20242025.pdf). The same report says alternative means supplied included 166 mobile phones and 45 battery backups, and that One NZ was ordered to pay $1.1 million after admitting ten breaches of the Code [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code 2025 Compliance Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/111-Contact-Code-Compliance-Report-20242025.pdf). ComCom's public 111 Contact Code page says the Code protects residential landline consumers who cannot call 111 in a power cut after moving to fibre or fixed wireless home-phone technologies that need household power to work [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code page](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/telecommunications-for-consumers/commission-111-contact-code/).

### What still cannot be ranked from public data

The highest-exposure places are likely where several risks overlap: remaining rural copper voice or broadband dependence, weak or model-only mobile signal, road or tourist black spots, older 3G-dependent devices, medically or safety-vulnerable landline users, and long power or access-restoration times [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf), [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema). I found no public official dataset that joins those factors at community, road-corridor, marae, farm, household, tower, or device level [Commerce Commission Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Connectivity Update, December 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).

The official broadband and mobile evidence also uses different denominators: the copper report uses premises in rural copper areas, the mobile chapter uses population and landmass coverage, the MBSF reports road kilometres and tourism sites, and the 111 Contact Code compliance report uses residential landline services and registered vulnerable consumers [Commerce Commission Copper Services Investigation final report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code 2025 Compliance Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/111-Contact-Code-Compliance-Report-20242025.pdf). Because those denominators do not line up publicly, a community cannot be ranked by simply adding the published figures [Commerce Commission Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

Power resilience remains the largest unjoined emergency variable. NEMA says Emergency Mobile Alerts are broadcast from targeted cell towers and may not be received outside coverage, when towers are damaged, or when power is out, while TCF says Cyclone Gabrielle cut power, damaged fibre, destroyed bridges, and affected mobile services through power outages and cable cuts [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema), [TCF, One year on from Gabrielle](https://www.tcf.org.nz/news/one-year-on). I found public NIFF counts of battery-capacity upgrades and ComCom discussion of 111 power-failure protections, but not a public national table of tower backup duration, generator access constraints, outage duration, or fuel/logistics exposure by rural community [NIFF Connectivity Update, December 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf), [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code 2025 Compliance Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/111-Contact-Code-Compliance-Report-20242025.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- A public joined dataset or official dashboard combining rural copper connection status, mobile coverage quality, MBSF site quality, road exposure, 3G-device migration, 111-vulnerable-consumer uptake, outage history, tower backup power, and electricity-restoration dependencies would change this finding from a Medium-confidence risk register into a High-confidence prioritisation [Commerce Commission Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Connectivity Update, December 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).
- A post-shutdown ComCom or provider report quantifying actual 3G-dependent phones, alarms, vehicle trackers, EFTPOS terminals, telemetry devices, and business devices that stopped working after shutdown would sharpen the device-risk conclusion [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown).
- Provider or lifelines-sector publication of rural tower backup-power duration, generator refuelling constraints, and outage restoration times would change the emergency-resilience ranking, especially for places exposed to storms, slips, flood isolation, and long road closures [TCF, One year on from Gabrielle](https://www.tcf.org.nz/news/one-year-on), [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema).
- I could not verify a public national list of rural communities, road corridors, farms, marae, medically dependent users, alarms, trackers, or towers most exposed after copper withdrawal and the 3G shutdown; that information may exist inside providers, emergency-management agencies, councils, electricity distributors, or lifelines groups, but it was not visible in the public sources checked.
- A human review by rural emergency managers, hauora providers, disability advocates, farmers, marae/iwi representatives, WISPs, councils, and local civil-defence groups is still needed because public network reports cannot show whether vulnerable people can actually use the supplied backup options during a local outage [Commerce Commission 111 Contact Code page](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/telecommunications-for-consumers/commission-111-contact-code/), [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema).

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues opened; this follow-up issue was answerable as a public-data gap assessment without fan-out.
- Can ComCom, MBIE/NIFF, NEMA, NZTA, electricity distributors, providers, and councils publish an aggregate "rural emergency communications exposure" layer without revealing personal, commercially sensitive, or security-sensitive infrastructure data?
- How many registered 111-vulnerable landline consumers are rural, and how many received a mobile-phone solution in a place where local mobile coverage and tower backup power are robust enough during outages?
- Which road corridors have the worst overlap of no/limited mobile coverage, high crash or isolation risk, tourist use, and long emergency-service response time?
