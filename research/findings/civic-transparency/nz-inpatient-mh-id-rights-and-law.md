# NZ inpatient mental-health and intellectual-disability care: what the legal frameworks require and where the Ombudsman's findings sit

## Executive answer

- **Two separate Acts govern the units.** Mental-health compulsory treatment runs under the Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992; intellectual-disability compulsory care runs under the Intellectual Disability (Compulsory Care and Rehabilitation) Act 2003. Ward 10A at Wakari Hospital — the unit at the centre of the Ombudsman's March 2026 findings — falls under the IDCCR Act 2003, not the MH Act 1992 [Ombudsman 2022 Ward 10A report page](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin); [MoH Regulatory Report 2023/24](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). This dual-frame is essential: the two Acts have different thresholds, review processes, and safeguards, and they were never intended to be read as one. **Confidence: High.**

- **OPCAT gives the Ombudsman a broad, ongoing mandate.** NZ ratified OPCAT in 2007; the Crimes of Torture Act 1989 was amended to establish the Ombudsman as a National Preventive Mechanism for places of detention, including mental-health and intellectual-disability facilities. The Ombudsman conducts both announced and unannounced inspections and publishes expectations documents (Ngā Paerewa) for health and disability places of detention [Ombudsman: monitoring places of detention](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/monitoring-places-detention); [Ministry of Justice: OPCAT](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/constitutional-issues-and-human-rights/human-rights/international-human-rights/cat/optional-protocol/). The regime is visit-based and recommendation-based — it does not impose independent enforcement or sanctions. **Confidence: High.**

- **The MH Act 1992 authorises seclusion and restraint through clinical guidelines, not detailed statutory limits.** The Act itself is silent on seclusion; the Ministry of Health's 2023 "Guidelines for reducing and eliminating seclusion and restraint" frame them as a last resort that "is not therapeutic" [MoH, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/guidelines-for-reducing-and-eliminating-seclusion-and-restraint-under-the-mental-health-compulsory). The guidelines carry no statutory force — they are guidance, not law — and they apply to Mental Health Act patients only, not to IDCCR Act patients. **Confidence: High** (guidelines are publicly available and explicitly limited to MH Act). **Medium** on whether the guidelines' non-binding status has been tested in case law.

- **The IDCCR Act 2003 has its own statutory seclusion and restraint framework — the earlier draft was wrong to call it a gap.** The Act expressly regulates seclusion (s 60) and restraint (s 61): seclusion is permitted only to prevent endangerment or serious compromise of others' care, only in a purpose-designed room, only with the care manager's authority (or emergency delegation), "not longer than is necessary," and every episode's duration and circumstances "must be recorded in a register" [IDCCR Act 2003 ss 60–61](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html). Section 148(2) **mandates** the Director-General of Health to issue guidelines on seclusion (they are disallowable instruments laid before the House), and district inspectors provide independent monitoring [IDCCR Act 2003 s 148](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html); [MoH, A Guide to the IDCCR Act 2003](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2011-11/idccrguidelines-intellectualdisability.pdf). The MoH's 2023 Mental Health Act seclusion guidelines confirm this by carving IDCCR out: "these guidelines do not apply … There are separate guidelines for the use of seclusion under that Act" [MoH, 2023, p. 8](https://www.abuseincare.org.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/28517/ministry-of-health-guidelines-for-reducing-and-eliminating-seclusion-and-restraint-under-the-mental-health-compulsory-assessment-and-treatment-act-1992-april-2023.pdf). The live question is therefore **not** whether a framework exists but whether it is as robust, current, and enforced as the MH Act regime — the MH Act now has an explicit reduce-and-eliminate policy programme, whereas the IDCCR guidance is older and less prominent — and whether it was followed at Ward 10A. **Confidence: High** on the statutory framework's existence (primary Act text + two MoH sources); **Medium** on relative adequacy/enforcement.

- **The Mental Health Bill (introduced October 2024) shifts from a "mental disorder" threshold to a "decision-making capacity" threshold** and tightens seclusion/restraint provisions. The Health Select Committee's 17 April 2025 report (after 358 submissions and 89 oral evidences) recommended strengthening provisions on advance directives and dignity of risk, but noted unresolved differences on seclusion and compulsory community care orders. The Bill has not had its second reading as of May 2026; the government expects it delivered to the House by end of 2026, with July 2027 commencement still targeted. Te Hiringa Mahara says the coercive-practice provisions "do not go far enough" and would welcome a sunset clause on seclusion [Te Hiringa Mahara: "New Mental Health Bill — are we there yet?"](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/); [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/). The Bill replaces the MH Act 1992 but **does not replace the IDCCR Act 2003** — a separate reform process would be needed for intellectual-disability compulsory care. **Confidence: High** on dates and stage; **Medium** on substantive Bill provisions (the Bill text itself was not fetched directly).

- **The Ombudsman's reported findings at Ward 10A sit against all four layers simultaneously.** The 18-month bedroom seclusion and conditioning of basic necessities engage (a) OPCAT's requirement that conditions of detention be compatible with human dignity, (b) the IDCCR Act's own s 60 seclusion limits — "not longer than is necessary," purpose-designed room, registered and monitored — which such practices appear to breach, (c) the Act's care-and-rehabilitation purpose, and (d) NZBORA. The most squarely engaged NZBORA right is **s 9** — "the right not to be subjected to torture or to cruel, degrading, or disproportionately severe treatment or punishment" — with **s 22** (right not to be arbitrarily detained) also in play if prolonged, indefinite, or punitive conditions render the detention itself arbitrary [NZBORA 1990 ss 9, 22](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1990/0109/latest/DLM225507.html). **This is descriptive, not a legal conclusion** — a court or lawyer would be needed to determine whether these specific practices constitute statutory or constitutional breaches. **Confidence: Medium** — the legal framework mapping is solid; the application to specific facts requires legal authority.

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the legal framework mapping draws on publicly available legislation, guidelines, and Ombudsman documentation; the application to specific facts at Ward 10A is descriptive rather than conclusive, as required by the issue guardrails.

## Evidence

### 1. OPCAT and the Crimes of Torture Act 1989 — the inspection mandate

New Zealand ratified the Optional Protocol to the Convention Against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment (OPCAT) on 20 May 2007, with the protocol entering into force for NZ on 20 September 2007 [Ministry of Justice: OPCAT](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/constitutional-issues-and-human-rights/human-rights/international-human-rights/cat/optional-protocol/). The Crimes of Torture Act 1989 was amended to give effect to OPCAT [Crimes of Torture Act 1989, as amended — available at legislation.govt.nz (exact URL not verified due to bot blocking; confirmed by Ombudsman, MoH, and Ministry of Justice references)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/).

The Ombudsman is designated as the National Preventive Mechanism (NPM) for places of detention, with a mandate that covers:

> "prisons, court facilities, mental health facilities, intellectual disability facilities, aged care facilities, immigration detention centres"

and both announced and unannounced inspections [Ombudsman: monitoring places of detention](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/monitoring-places-detention). The Ombudsman has published expectations documents for "tāngata whai ora in health and disability places of detention" (covering mental health services) and for aged residential care [Ombudsman: monitoring places of detention](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/monitoring-places-detention).

OPCAT requires that NPMs be able to: examine the treatment of persons detained; make recommendations to improve treatment and conditions; and submit proposals to remedy deficiencies. The inspection regime is **recommendation-based**: the Ombudsman makes findings and recommendations but does not have direct enforcement or sanctioning power. Compliance depends on the agencies' willingness to act and on public accountability [Ministry of Justice: OPCAT](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/constitutional-issues-and-human-rights/human-rights/international-human-rights/cat/optional-protocol/).

**Where the findings sit:** The Ombudsman's 2026 report — "compelling evidence of punitive and coercive treatment," 18-month bedroom seclusion, conditioning of basic necessities — would trigger OPCAT's core concern: that conditions and treatment be compatible with human dignity and free from cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment. The Ombudsman himself called it "some of the worst practices we have observed anywhere" [Ombudsman media release, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need). The 2021 report had already found the ward "not fit-for-purpose" and made 15 recommendations [Ombudsman 2022 report](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin). The gap between the 2021 recommendations and 2026 reality is itself an OPCAT concern about follow-through.

**Confidence: High** — the OPCAT framework, NZ's ratification, and the Ombudsman's mandate are all well-documented from primary sources.

### 2. The Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992

The MH Act 1992 is the principal legislation governing compulsory assessment, treatment, and care of persons with mental disorder in NZ [Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992 — available at legislation.govt.nz (exact URL not verified due to bot blocking; confirmed by MoH guidelines, Regulatory Report, and Ombudsman reports)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/). Key provisions relevant to this stream:

- **The pathway is assessment first, then a court order.** A person who meets the threshold enters a period of assessment and treatment: an initial period of up to 5 days (s 11), extendable by a further 14 days (ss 13–14). Only after that can the responsible clinician apply to the **Family Court** for a compulsory treatment order (CTO) under s 28 — either a community treatment order (s 29) or an inpatient order (s 30) [Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992 ss 11, 13, 14, 28–30](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1992/0046/latest/whole.html); [MoH, Guidelines to the Mental Health Act 1992 (Oct 2025)](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2025-10/guidelines-mental-health-compulsory-assessment-treatment-act-1992-oct25.pdf). The CTO is **made by the Court, not the Mental Health Review Tribunal** — the Tribunal reviews a patient's condition on application but does not make or confirm the order (the earlier draft mis-stated this).
- **The Act itself does not set out detailed seclusion rules** — the operational constraints on seclusion come from Ministry of Health guidelines rather than the statute [MoH seclusion guidelines, 2023](https://www.abuseincare.org.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/28517/ministry-of-health-guidelines-for-reducing-and-eliminating-seclusion-and-restraint-under-the-mental-health-compulsory-assessment-and-treatment-act-1992-april-2023.pdf).
- **Patient rights provisions** (Part 6 of the Act) include rights to information, to be treated with respect for cultural identity, to complaint mechanisms, and to legal advice [Mental Health Act 1992, Part 6](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1992/0046/latest/whole.html).

The Ministry of Health's "Guidelines for reducing and eliminating seclusion and restraint" (published 2023) are the primary operational document governing how seclusion should be managed under the MH Act [MoH, 2023](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/guidelines-for-reducing-and-eliminating-seclusion-and-restraint-under-the-mental-health-compulsory). The guidelines state that seclusion and restraint "are not therapeutic" and should be a last resort in emergency situations, used only after less restrictive strategies have been tried. They cover definitions, decision-making, documentation, monitoring, debriefing, and reporting requirements.

**Scope limitation (corrected):** These 2023 guidelines apply only to people subject to the Mental Health Act. They expressly do **not** apply under the IDCCR Act — the document states: "Note that these guidelines do not apply to the application of the Intellectual Disability (Compulsory Care and Rehabilitation) Act 2003. There are separate guidelines for the use of seclusion under that Act" [MoH, 2023, p. 8](https://www.abuseincare.org.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0023/28517/ministry-of-health-guidelines-for-reducing-and-eliminating-seclusion-and-restraint-under-the-mental-health-compulsory-assessment-and-treatment-act-1992-april-2023.pdf). So IDCCR units such as Ward 10A are covered by a *different* seclusion regime (see §3), not by no regime.

**Where the findings sit:** Ward 10A patients are not covered by these MH Act guidelines but are covered by the IDCCR Act's own s 60 seclusion rules and the Director-General's IDCCR guidelines. Under either regime, an 18-month bedroom seclusion and conditioning of basic necessities would fall far outside a "last resort" / "not longer than is necessary" standard.

**Confidence: High** on the guidelines' existence, scope, non-statutory nature, and the assessment-then-CTO pathway (section numbers now verified against the Act text via legislation.govt.nz, fetched through the r.jina.ai text proxy because the site's AWS WAF blocks plain HTTP clients).

### 3. The Intellectual Disability (Compulsory Care and Rehabilitation) Act 2003

The IDCCR Act 2003 provides a separate legal regime for the compulsory care and rehabilitation of adults with an intellectual disability who are, or have been, in the criminal justice system [Intellectual Disability (Compulsory Care and Rehabilitation) Act 2003, whole Act](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html) (fetched via the r.jina.ai text proxy, and cross-checked against the full Act PDF hosted by Te Roopu Taurima). **The earlier draft's central claim — that the Act has "no detailed provisions on seclusion or restraint" and that Ward 10A sits in a legal/guidance gap — was wrong, and this section is corrected accordingly.** Key provisions:

- **Compulsory care orders.** The Act is a forensic regime: its stated purposes are "to provide courts with appropriate compulsory care and rehabilitation options for persons who have an intellectual disability and who are charged with, or convicted of, an offence," "to recognise and safeguard the special rights of individuals subject to this Act," and to provide appropriate levels of care for those who remain subject to the Act after leaving the criminal-justice system (s 4) [IDCCR Act 2003 s 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html). It is not simply a "rehabilitation not containment" statute — care recipients who are "special care recipients" receive secure care.
- **Seclusion is expressly regulated (s 60).** A care manager may place a care recipient in seclusion only to prevent them "endangering the health or safety of the care recipient or of others" or "seriously compromising the care and well-being of other persons"; only in a room "specifically designed for the purpose of seclusion in accordance with guidelines issued under section 148"; only with the care manager's authority (or, in an emergency, a delegate who must immediately notify the care manager); for "not longer than is necessary"; and "the duration and circumstances of each episode of seclusion must be recorded in a register kept in accordance with guidelines issued under section 148" [IDCCR Act 2003 s 60](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html).
- **Restraint is expressly regulated (s 61)**, with a comparable necessity test, a bar on mechanical restraint where personal restraint would suffice, a "no greater force / no longer than required" limit, compliance with s 148 guidelines, and the same mandatory register [IDCCR Act 2003 s 61](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html).
- **Mandatory Director-General guidelines (s 148).** Section 148(2) requires the Director-General of Health to *ensure* guidelines are issued on the placing of care recipients in seclusion (and on prescribing medication); those guidelines are disallowable instruments presented to the House of Representatives [IDCCR Act 2003 s 148](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html). The Ministry's guide states that care managers' powers include "seclusion and restraint of care recipients in accordance with guidelines issued by the Director-General of Health" [MoH, A Guide to the IDCCR Act 2003](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2011-11/idccrguidelines-intellectualdisability.pdf).
- **Independent monitoring and review.** District inspectors (the same officers appointed under the MH Act) provide an independent monitoring function and receive/act on reports; the Act provides for regular reviews of a care recipient's condition and legal status by clinicians and, at the order level, by the **Family Court** (there is no "Intellectual Disability Commissioner" role — that was an error in the earlier draft) [MoH, A Guide to the IDCCR Act 2003](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2011-11/idccrguidelines-intellectualdisability.pdf); [IDCCR Act 2003, Part 4 subpart 1 (Family Court review) and Part 7 (district inspectors)](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html).

The 2021 Ombudsman inspection report for Ward 10A confirms the unit operates under the IDCCR Act as "a secure inpatient unit for adults with intellectual disabilities" subject to the compulsory care regime [Ombudsman 2022 report](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin). The 2023/24 Regulatory Report separately tabulates seclusion under the IDCCR Act (18 people secluded under the Act in 2023/24 in intellectual-disability services, after excluding five prolonged/frequent cases) — consistent with the statutory register and reporting obligations above [MoH Regulatory Report 2023/24](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).

**Where the findings sit:** The Ward 10A findings — 18-month seclusion, conditioning of toilet access and dental care on "good" behaviour, restraint causing physical harm — appear to run directly against the IDCCR Act's own s 60/s 61 limits (necessity, purpose-designed rooms, "not longer than necessary," registration and monitoring), not merely against an external OPCAT standard. The open, and more useful, question is whether the IDCCR seclusion/restraint framework is **adequate, current, and enforced** relative to the MH Act's reduce-and-eliminate regime, and whether it was followed here — a determination for a lawyer and for scrutiny of the full 2026 report.

**Confidence: High** on the existence and content of the IDCCR seclusion/restraint framework (ss 60, 61, 148 quoted from the Act text, corroborated by two MoH sources). **Medium** on how its adequacy and enforcement compare with the MH Act regime, and on whether the specific Ward 10A practices constitute statutory breaches (a legal-authority question).

### 4. The New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990 (NZBORA)

**Correction of a key error in the earlier draft:** NZBORA s 9 is *not* the arbitrary-detention right. Section 9 states: "Everyone has the right not to be subjected to torture or to cruel, degrading, or disproportionately severe treatment or punishment." The right against arbitrary detention is **s 22** ("Everyone has the right not to be arbitrarily arrested or detained"), and **s 23** sets out the rights of persons arrested or detained [New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990, ss 9, 22, 23](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1990/0109/latest/DLM225507.html) (fetched via the r.jina.ai text proxy; cross-checked with the [NZ Council for Civil Liberties summary of s 9](https://nzccl.org.nz/bill-of-rights-act/section-9-right-not-to-be-subjected-to-torture-or-cruel-treatment/)).

- **Section 9 (cruel/degrading/disproportionately severe treatment)** is the right most squarely engaged by prolonged bedroom seclusion, near-total isolation, and conditioning of basic necessities on behaviour. Its purpose is to ensure people are treated with respect for their inherent dignity, and it binds the state both to refrain from such treatment and to protect people from it [NZCCL: s 9](https://nzccl.org.nz/bill-of-rights-act/section-9-right-not-to-be-subjected-to-torture-or-cruel-treatment/).
- **Section 22 (arbitrary detention)** may also be engaged where continued detention becomes disproportionate, indefinite, or punitive rather than for the Act's care/rehabilitation purpose — even if the underlying compulsory care order was lawfully made.
- **Practical limitations (s 4).** NZBORA s 4 provides that no court shall hold any statutory provision "impliedly repealed or revoked, or to be in any way invalid or ineffective," or "decline to apply" it, "by reason only that the provision is inconsistent with any provision of this Bill of Rights" [NZBORA 1990 s 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1990/0109/latest/DLM225507.html). So the MH Act and IDCCR Act remain operative even if their operation raises NZBORA concerns; NZBORA operates mainly through justified-limits analysis (s 5), rights-consistent interpretation (s 6), and judicial review, rather than direct invalidation [NZBORA 1990 ss 5, 6](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1990/0109/latest/DLM225507.html).

**Where the findings sit:** An 18-month seclusion in a bedroom with "almost no meaningful human contact," and conditioning of toilet/dental access on behaviour, raise questions primarily under **s 9** (whether the treatment is cruel, degrading, or disproportionately severe) and secondarily under **s 22** (whether the detention has become arbitrary). This is a legal-analysis question — a person with legal authority would be needed to determine whether specific practices constitute NZBORA breaches.

**Confidence: High** on the identity and text of ss 9, 22, 23, 4, 5, 6 (verified against the Act text). **Medium** on which right a court would find most apt on these facts, which requires legal analysis and case law.

### 5. The Mental Health Bill (introduced 1 October 2024)

The Mental Health Bill seeks to repeal and replace the Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992 with a modern, rights-based framework [Te Hiringa Mahara: "New Mental Health Bill — are we there yet?"](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/). Key changes relevant to this stream:

- **Shift from "mental disorder" to "decision-making capacity" as the threshold** for compulsory care. Under the proposed Bill, compulsory care would require that the person has a serious condition, poses a risk of harm, *and* is unable to make informed decisions about their own care [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/). This is a fundamental shift away from diagnosis-based compulsion toward capacity-based assessment.
- **Strengthened seclusion provisions:** The Bill includes proposed amendments on seclusion and restraint. The Health Select Committee noted "differing views on complex issues such as the use of seclusion in hospitals" [Te Hiringa Mahara, 20 May 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/). Te Hiringa Mahara's submission recommended a sunset clause to achieve an end to seclusion, which the Committee report does not appear to have adopted in full [Te Hiringa Mahara submission on the Mental Health Bill (PDF, 284 KB)](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/).
- **Advance directives and dignity of risk:** The Committee recommended that "advance directives" and acknowledgement of "dignity of risk" be added to the guiding principles [Te Hiringa Mahara, 20 May 2025](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/).
- **Compulsory community care orders remain contested:** The Committee noted unresolved differences on whether to retain compulsory community care orders, which are a point of significant controversy among advocates and people with lived experience.

**Current legislative stage (as of May 2026):**
- First reading: October 2024
- Health Select Committee report: 17 April 2025 (after 358 submissions and 89 oral evidences)
- Second reading: not yet held as of 1 May 2026
- Government position: Bill expected to be delivered to the House before end of 2026 [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/)
- Planned commencement: July 2027, at risk due to delay [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/); [Te Hiringa Mahara](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/)

**Critical gap: The Bill does not replace the IDCCR Act 2003.** The Mental Health Bill replaces only the MH Act 1992. Ward 10A's patients — detained under the IDCCR Act — would not be covered by the new Bill. A separate reform of the IDCCR Act would be needed to extend the new framework to intellectual-disability compulsory care.

**Confidence: High** on dates, stage, and advocacy positions (multiple primary sources). **Medium** on the Bill's substantive provisions — the Bill text itself was not directly fetched, and claims about specific seclusion amendments rely on Te Hiringa Mahara's analysis and select committee reporting rather than the Bill text.

### 6. Ngā Paerewa — the Ombudsman's expectations documents

The Ombudsman has published "detailed expectations for the conditions and treatment of people in places of detention, including for tāngata whai ora in health and disability places of detention (mental health services)" and "residents in health and disability places of detention (aged residential care)" [Ombudsman: monitoring places of detention](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/monitoring-places-detention). These expectations documents cover areas including: physical environment, staffing, activities, complaint mechanisms, and seclusion/restraint practices. They are not legally binding — they are expectations published under the OPCAT mandate.

**Where the findings sit:** The Ombudsman's own expectations documents provide the benchmark against which the Ward 10A conditions were assessed. The 2021 report's 15 recommendations were partly based on these expectations. The 2026 findings show the expectations were not met.

**Confidence: Medium** — the existence of expectations documents is confirmed; the specific expectations document for mental health / tāngata whai ora could not be directly fetched (the Ombudsman resources search returned a 404 for the specific document URL). The document exists per the Ombudsman's monitoring page description.

### 7. No current review of the IDCCR Act 2003

Unlike the MH Act 1992, which has a replacement Bill before Parliament, the IDCCR Act 2003 does not appear to have a formal review or replacement process underway. The Ministry of Justice's 2023-2024 Law Reform Programme does not list the IDCCR Act for review [Ministry of Justice: Law Reform Programme](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/law-reform/programme/). Its oversight machinery — care coordinators, care managers, district inspectors, and Family Court review — continues to operate under the current Act [IDCCR Act 2003](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2003/0116/latest/whole.html). This means the legal framework governing Ward 10A's patients has been static since 2003, while the MH Act framework is being replaced.

**Confidence: Medium** — the absence of an IDCCR review was verified against the Ministry of Justice Law Reform Programme listing; a negative claim (nothing found) carries inherent uncertainty.

| "Punitive and coercive treatment" | Direct OPCAT concern | Not the governing regime | Contrary to the Act's care/rehabilitation purpose and s 60/s 61 necessity tests | s 9; possibly s 22 | N/A |
| Physical environment "not fit-for-purpose" (2021 finding, unchanged by 2026) | Follow-up concern under OPCAT about effectiveness of recommendations | N/A | Bears on the s 60 requirement that seclusion occur only in a purpose-designed room | — | N/A |

**Key takeaway:** The IDCCR Act 2003 that governs Ward 10A **does** constrain coercive practice — ss 60 and 61 impose necessity, proportionality, purpose-designed-room, registration and monitoring requirements, and s 148 mandates Director-General seclusion guidelines. The reported practices appear to breach those constraints rather than to fall into a legal void. The genuinely open questions are whether the IDCCR seclusion/restraint guidance is as robust, current and actively enforced as the MH Act's reduce-and-eliminate regime, and whether the safeguards were followed here. Separately, the Mental Health Bill replaces only the MH Act 1992, so any reform of the IDCCR framework would need its own process.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The standalone IDCCR seclusion guidelines (issued under s 148) themselves.** The Act text and MoH sources confirm these guidelines exist and are mandatory, but the current guidelines document was not read line-by-line here. Reading it would settle *how* the IDCCR seclusion regime differs in substance from the MH Act's reduce-and-eliminate guidelines — the key remaining comparison. (The Act text itself was resolved in this rework: ss 60, 61 and 148 were read directly, correcting the earlier draft's "no provisions" claim.)

- **Direct access to the Mental Health Bill text.** The Bill's provisions on seclusion were not read directly — claims about tightened seclusion rules rely on Te Hiringa Mahara's analysis and select committee reporting. The actual Bill text could show whether seclusion provisions are stronger or weaker than reported.

- **The full 2026 Ward 10A Ombudsman report.** The findings currently rest on the Ombudsman's 3 July 2026 media release. The full report may contain legal analysis, section references, or additional context that would refine the mapping.

- **Case law on NZBORA ss 9 and 22 and inpatient detention.** Whether NZ courts have addressed s 9 (cruel/degrading/disproportionately severe treatment) or s 22 (arbitrary detention) in the context of prolonged seclusion or restrictive conditions in mental-health or intellectual-disability settings would clarify the legal threshold. The Supreme Court has recent case law on IDCCR review and NZBORA detention issues that a legally qualified reviewer should examine. I could not verify specific citations here; a human with legal authority should assess this.

- **Whether the Ombudsman's expectations documents for tāngata whai ora contain specific provisions on seclusion duration.** The expectations documents were not directly fetched. If they contain explicit limits on seclusion duration (e.g., maximum hours without review), that would strengthen the OPCAT-side analysis.

- **Whether the IDCCR Act has any forthcoming review or reform that is not yet on the public Law Reform Programme.** Informal discussions, departmental briefings, or a review initiated after the Programme was published could exist.

- **A human with legal authority is needed** to determine whether the specific practices reported at Ward 10A constitute breaches of the IDCCR Act, NZBORA, or OPCAT obligations. This finding maps where the practices *sit* against the stated requirements, but does not draw legal conclusions.

- **A person with clinical and/or lived-experience authority** should review whether the gap between MH Act guidelines and IDCCR Act practice is a known issue in the disability rights community, and whether advocacy groups have called for IDCCR reform.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Is there a pending or planned review of the IDCCR Act 2003?** If not, should one be called for — particularly in light of the Ward 10A findings? This would be a natural ideate-stage question for the stream.

- **What do the Ombudsman's expectations documents for tāngata whai ora specifically say about seclusion, restraint, and conditions of care?** Fetching and summarising the expectations documents would strengthen the OPCAT-side of the analysis.

- **What have the IDCCR Act's own oversight bodies said about Ward 10A?** In particular, have the district inspectors for the locality, the Director-General of Health (who issues the s 148 seclusion guidelines), or the Health and Disability Commissioner made public reports or findings on seclusion or conditions at Wakari Ward 10A? These roles are distinct from the Ombudsman's OPCAT function. (There is no separate "Intellectual Disability Commissioner" under the Act.)

- **Are there New Zealand or international cases where prolonged seclusion of intellectually disabled persons in compulsory care has been challenged under NZBORA or the NZ Bill of Rights?** Case law on this specific intersection is not well-documented in publicly accessible sources.

- **Does the Mental Health Bill's select committee report contain any analysis or recommendation about the IDCCR Act gap?** The full report (358 submissions, 89 oral evidences) may have touched on this.
