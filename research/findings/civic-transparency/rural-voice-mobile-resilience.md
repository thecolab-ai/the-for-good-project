# Rural voice and emergency resilience is becoming a managed-transition problem, not just a broadband coverage problem

## Executive answer

- Rural broadband choices have improved, especially through 4G/5G fixed wireless and low-earth-orbit satellite, but that does not by itself solve rural voice, 111, road-corridor, farm-safety, or outage resilience needs; the Commerce Commission separates rural home broadband from mobile "on the move" services and says consumers now have to compare coverage, performance, reliability, device compatibility, upfront costs and monthly price across different technologies [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Copper retreat is real but not complete: the Commerce Commission's copper investigation reported about 223,100 rural-copper-area premises, 56,000 rural copper broadband connections and 12,000 copper voice-only connections as at 30 June 2025, and noted general submitter consensus that a managed withdrawal process would be important if copper is deregulated and removed [Commerce Commission, Copper Services Investigation final report, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf).
- The 3G shutdown mainly changes device and local coverage risk rather than nominal national coverage: TCF says any 3G-reliant device will stop working for emergency 111 calling, 2degrees says non-upgraded 3G-reliant phones will be affected for voice including 111, and ComCom says providers expanded 4G to replicate 3G coverage as far as practicable, with 4G available to more rural addresses than 3G ever was at June 2025 [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown), [2degrees, 3G shutdown guidance](https://www.2degrees.nz/help/mobile-help/troubleshooting/things-to-know-about-our-3g-shutdown), [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- Mobile black spots remain a resilience issue even after public investment: ComCom reports 99% 4G population coverage but warns coverage models do not always reflect real-world experience, while NIFF says remote state-highway coverage is delivered as signposted "islands" because continuous coverage is difficult in rugged terrain [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [NIFF, Rural Mobile](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-mobile/).
- Power and disaster resilience is the weak point in a fibre/fixed-wireless/mobile world: the 111 Contact Code exists because fibre and fixed wireless may not work in a power failure, and NEMA says Emergency Mobile Alerts may not be received when people are outside mobile coverage, cell towers are damaged, or power is out [Commerce Commission, 111 Contact Code 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/pdf_file/0025/356650/Commission-111-Contact-Code-2024-26-June-2024.pdf), [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the direction is strongly supported by current ComCom, NIFF, TCF, provider and emergency-management sources, but public data still does not give a clean rural denominator for households, farms, roads, devices, medically dependent users, and outage duration.

## Evidence

### The question is not just "can a rural household buy broadband?"

ComCom's 2025 monitoring report explicitly separates fixed rural home connectivity from mobile "on the move" services: its rural chapter covers residential fixed-location broadband where local fibre company fibre is unavailable, while its mobile chapter covers personal mobile texting, calling and data [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). That distinction matters because a household can have adequate home broadband through satellite or fixed wireless while still lacking reliable mobile coverage across the farm, road, track end, or tourist location where an emergency occurs [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [NIFF, Rural Mobile](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-mobile/).

ComCom says rural connectivity choices are changing quickly as fixed wireless, low-earth-orbit satellite and direct-to-cell services expand, but it frames that as more complex consumer choice across coverage, performance, reliability, device compatibility, upfront costs and monthly price rather than as a solved resilience problem [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). Its report also says LEO satellite can provide strong rural broadband where line-of-sight to the sky is sufficient, while direct-to-cell satellite services were initially limited to SMS and were primarily complementary to mobile networks at the time of reporting [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Claim confidence: High** for the access-versus-resilience distinction, because it is explicit in ComCom's report structure and reinforced by NIFF's road/tourism safety framing.

### Copper withdrawal removes a resilience backstop for some homes unless managed carefully

The Commerce Commission made a final 2025 recommendation that rural copper services be deregulated; the report says rural copper areas still covered about 223,100 premises, with 56,000 rural copper broadband connections and 12,000 copper voice-only connections as at 30 June 2025 [Commerce Commission, Copper Services Investigation final report, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf). ComCom also recorded that submitters generally agreed a managed withdrawal process would be important to protect rural consumers if copper were deregulated and removed, including education and protection for more vulnerable consumers [Commerce Commission, Copper Services Investigation final report, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf).

The 111 Contact Code shows why copper withdrawal has an emergency dimension: the code says fibre and fixed wireless may not work in a power failure, requires providers to inform residential landline consumers about technologies and devices that may fail, and requires providers to supply vulnerable consumers with an appropriate no-cost means to contact 111 during a power failure [Commerce Commission, 111 Contact Code 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/pdf_file/0025/356650/Commission-111-Contact-Code-2024-26-June-2024.pdf). The code also treats a copper landline, adequate mobile coverage with unrestricted mobile-phone access, a UPS, or a managed medical/safety alarm as examples of ways a consumer may already have an emergency-contact means during a power failure [Commerce Commission, 111 Contact Code 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/pdf_file/0025/356650/Commission-111-Contact-Code-2024-26-June-2024.pdf).

The risk is not hypothetical compliance theatre: in December 2025 ComCom said the High Court ordered One NZ to pay $1.1m after admitting ten breaches of the 111 Contact Code, and the Commissioner described telecommunications services as a vital lifeline during natural disasters and power failures [Commerce Commission, One NZ 111 Contact Code penalty, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/one-nz-ordered-to-pay-1-1-million-for-breaching-111-obligations/).

**Claim confidence: High** that copper withdrawal needs explicit emergency-calling management for vulnerable landline users; **Medium** on how many rural households would be worse off after migration, because the public data gives copper connection counts but not household-level backup-power, mobile-coverage and vulnerability status.

### 3G shutdown is mostly a device and edge-coverage problem, not proof that rural mobile resilience is solved

The industry-wide TCF page says New Zealand mobile providers began shutting down 3G from the end of 2025, the last network operator shut down on 31 March 2026, and 3G-reliant devices will no longer work after shutdown, including for emergency 111 calling [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown). 2degrees' customer guidance says its 3G services are no longer available as of February 2026, non-upgraded 3G-reliant phones will be affected for voice including emergency 111 calls, and coverage changes were not expected to be significant overall but could occur in limited locations because technologies differ [2degrees, 3G shutdown guidance](https://www.2degrees.nz/help/mobile-help/troubleshooting/things-to-know-about-our-3g-shutdown).

ComCom's June 2025 monitoring report gives the system-level counterweight: before shutdown, providers expanded 4G coverage to replicate 3G coverage as far as practicable, and 4G was available to more rural addresses than 3G ever was at June 2025 [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). One NZ's March 2026 shutdown release says customers still using older equipment after the 2G/3G shutdown would be unable to call, text or use data on existing devices, including for contacting 111, while the retired spectrum would be reused on 4G and 5G [One NZ, 2G and 3G shutdown complete, 2026](https://media.one.nz/2g-3g-shutdown-complete).

**Claim confidence: Medium-High** that the main residual 3G-shutdown risk is non-upgraded devices and local edge cases; **Medium** rather than High because public material does not quantify how many rural people, farm devices, alarms, vehicle trackers or tourism users remained 3G-dependent at shutdown.

### Road, tourism and farm-safety needs remain local and geographic

ComCom reports 4G mobile coverage reached 99% of population in 2025, but also says mobile coverage is generally modelled by operators and those models do not always reflect real-world experience [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report says the Mobile Black Spot Fund completed 74 new 4G towers in the year to 30 June 2025, adding coverage to 28 tourism sites, 16 marae and 481km of state highways and local roads, and that the programme had reached 192 tourism sites and about 2,000km of state highways and local roads by 30 June 2025 [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

NIFF's Rural Mobile page explains the practical shape of the remaining problem: continuous state-highway coverage is difficult in remote rugged areas, so the programme creates signposted "islands" of coverage where drivers can pull over to call 111; NIFF also says towers have been built in more than 180 tourist locations, prioritised by visitor numbers and safety needs [NIFF, Rural Mobile](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-mobile/). ComCom similarly states that MBSF coverage is designed for usable safety-grade road-corridor coverage rather than just theoretical signal presence, but says black spots are still likely to remain because of New Zealand geography and low population density [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Farm-safety evidence is more weakly quantified in public official data. Federated Farmers has a live 2025 Rural Connectivity Survey page, but the public page exposed only the survey title and PDF presence in this environment; secondary reporting of that survey says more than 600 farmers responded and that mobile coverage averaged 57% of farmland, but I treat that as a stakeholder signal rather than an official national statistic because I could not extract the underlying PDF directly [Federated Farmers, Rural Connectivity Survey 2025 page](https://www.fedfarm.org.nz/Web/Web/Resources/Rural-Connectivity-Survey.aspx), [Farmers Weekly, Federated Farmers survey report, 2025](https://www.farmersweekly.co.nz/news/connectivity-frustrations-deepen-for-farmers/).

**Claim confidence: High** that road/tourism black spots remain a local safety issue; **Low-Medium** for the exact farm-coverage estimate until the Federated Farmers PDF or another farm-level dataset is directly extractable.

### Emergency communications depend on power, towers and local coverage

The 111 Contact Code exists because retail landline consumers on newer home-phone technologies can lose 111 access in a power cut, and the code requires providers to supply accepted vulnerable consumers with an appropriate emergency-contact means that works for the minimum period at the premises [Commerce Commission, 111 Contact Code 2024](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/pdf_file/0025/356650/Commission-111-Contact-Code-2024-26-June-2024.pdf). ComCom's One NZ enforcement release reinforces that provider compliance matters in practice, because the admitted breaches related to information disclosure, record keeping and customer outreach obligations under the code [Commerce Commission, One NZ 111 Contact Code penalty, 2025](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/news-and-media/news-and-events/2025/one-nz-ordered-to-pay-1-1-million-for-breaching-111-obligations/).

NEMA's Emergency Mobile Alert page says alerts are broadcast from targeted cell towers to capable phones and SIM-connected devices, but warns people may not receive an alert if they are outside mobile coverage, mobile phone towers are damaged, or power is out [Get Ready, Emergency Mobile Alert](https://getready.govt.nz/prepared/ema). NEMA's Easy Read "no phone or internet" guidance separately tells households to plan for how they will contact each other, keep a battery- or solar-powered radio and important phone numbers, and keep phone lines clear for 111 calls in an emergency [Get Ready, No phone or internet](https://er.getready.govt.nz/prepared/impacts/no-phone-or-internet).

**Claim confidence: High** that backup power and tower availability are central to emergency resilience; **Medium** on rural exposure because public sources do not publish a combined map of mobile black spots, tower backup power duration, rural medically dependent users and road/tourism exposure.

## What would change this conclusion

- A public dataset combining rural address technology, actual mobile signal quality, farm-boundary mobile coverage, road/tourism corridor coverage, outage history, tower backup-power duration, and 111-vulnerable-consumer uptake would change this from a Medium-confidence resilience diagnosis to a High-confidence geographic prioritisation [Commerce Commission, Telecommunications Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF, Rural Mobile](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-mobile/).
- A post-shutdown provider or regulator report quantifying how many 3G-dependent phones, alarms, trackers, EFTPOS terminals, farm devices and roaming devices stopped working after 31 March 2026 would sharpen the 3G impact conclusion [TCF, Understanding the 3G shutdown](https://www.tcf.org.nz/digital-living/understanding-the-3g-shutdown).
- Evidence that direct-to-cell satellite provides reliable, affordable, emergency-capable voice/data to ordinary rural users across black spots would reduce the concern about mobile black spots; current ComCom evidence describes D2C as early, selectively available and complementary [Commerce Commission, 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- I could not verify the Federated Farmers 2025 survey PDF contents directly from the live page, so the exact 57% farmland mobile-coverage figure should be checked against the original PDF before being used as a headline statistic [Federated Farmers, Rural Connectivity Survey 2025 page](https://www.fedfarm.org.nz/Web/Web/Resources/Rural-Connectivity-Survey.aspx).
- ComCom PDF links returned bot-protection/blocked responses to raw `curl` and `scripts/fetch.mjs` in this environment, but the built-in web fetch rendered/extracted the PDFs successfully; under ADR-0006 I treated the raw 403/browser-tool failure as tooling, not as a dead citation.
