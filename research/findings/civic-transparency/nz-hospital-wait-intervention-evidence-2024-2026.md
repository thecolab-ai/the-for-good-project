# Which NZ hospital-wait interventions since 2024 have credible evidence of improving waits or throughput

This is research child **#734 of Stream #434**. It answers one question: **of the interventions Health New Zealand and DPMC themselves name as driving the 2024–2026 health-target results — ED staffing/beds, urgent after-hours care, Elective Boost, cataract surgery sessions, theatre productivity, MSK/orthopaedic physiotherapist triage, and outsourcing/FSA volume pushes — which have credible, checkable evidence that they actually moved waits or throughput, versus being asserted without independent verification?**

The sibling baseline finding ([#732](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/732), [hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md](hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md)) established *what* the national/district/specialty trends are. This finding does not re-derive those trends; it asks whether the specific interventions government releases credit for the trend have evidence beyond the government's own say-so.

## Executive answer

- **Every intervention this research could verify is named as a driver in the government's own primary-source "Target Delivery Actions" panels** — the DPMC quarterly factsheets for Target 1 (ED) and Target 2 (elective treatment), and Health NZ/Beehive quarterly health-target releases for FSA — not in independent third-party material. That is the central finding: **the causal claims in this brief overwhelmingly originate from the same government agencies being measured**, and only one intervention (a single-region cardiology pathway) has a government release with an isolated before/after number tied to its own start date. [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, quarter ending Dec 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/gt-factsheet-target-1-dec25.pdf); [DPMC, Target 2 factsheet, quarter ending Mar 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf).
- **"FSA Accelerate clinics" as named in this issue could not be verified as an actual programme name** — no Health NZ, DPMC, or news source found using that exact term. The closest matches are ad hoc "additional clinics" set up by individual districts to work through long-wait FSA backlogs (documented for Nelson Marlborough and Northland, below), not a named national programme. This is flagged, not assumed.
- **Two interventions have output/volume evidence but no isolated wait-time attribution**: Elective Boost (16,005 procedures delivered Feb–June 2025, 51.3% over its 10,579 target) and the MSK/orthopaedic physiotherapist pathway (7,200+ FSAs and 9,500+ follow-ups since August 2024) both report *volumes delivered*, which is a real, countable output, but neither the government nor any source found here separates "what the wait-time percentage would have been without this intervention" from the other things happening in the same quarters (general volume funding, outsourcing, coding changes). [Beehive, "More Kiwis receiving surgery as Elective Boost exceeds first target", 8 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/more-kiwis-receiving-surgery-elective-boost-exceeds-first-target); [Beehive/Scoop, "Health Targets: Physiotherapist Boost Will Cut Orthopaedic Wait Times", 1 Oct 2025](https://auckland.scoop.co.nz/2025/10/health-targets-physiotherapist-boost-will-cut-orthopaedic-wait-times/).
- **One intervention has a genuinely quantified regional before/after result**: a Northland cardiology FSA pathway (rural clinics, coordinated assessments, longest-wait-first triage, expanded catheterisation lab capacity) is credited with a 36% fall in the region's cardiology FSA waitlist and a 68% fall in long-wait (4+ month) patients between November 2023 and March 2025 — but this is still a single self-reported Beehive release with no independent audit found, and it predates the MSK pathway's national rollout, so it is evidence for a *specific regional cardiology pathway*, not for the national programmes this issue asks about. [Beehive/Scoop, "Faster Heart Specialist Access in Northland", Aug 2025](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/PA2508/S00065/faster-heart-specialist-access-in-northland.htm).
- **Independent scrutiny that does exist points the other way on outsourcing equity, is genuinely mixed on FSA volumes, and shows a cataract waitlist snapshot that has to be read carefully rather than as a clean "delivered but still failed" story.** The Auditor-General's June 2025 report explicitly found Health NZ has **not yet assessed** whether outsourcing to the private sector helps or worsens equitable access, and flagged it as a risk, not a proven benefit [OAG, "Providing equitable access to planned care treatment", 25 Jun 2025](https://oag.parliament.nz/2025/planned-care/summary.htm). On FSA volumes specifically, the record does not show a delivery shortfall: Health NZ's own reported figures show FSA acceptances *rising* (648,000 people accepted in 2024, up from 595,000 in 2022) and the FSA waitlist *falling* (down over 8,000 in the weeks to mid-April 2025); ASMS's actual, documented concern in the same reporting is **referral suppression** — patients not being referred at all because GPs doubt they will be seen — not a fall in FSAs delivered, and the separate warning that private-sector capacity "may be insufficient" comes from Health NZ's own internal briefing papers to the Minister, not from ASMS [RNZ, "Health NZ aims to cut surgery waitlists by outsourcing to private hospitals, extending doctors' hours", 13 May 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/in-depth/560757/health-nz-aims-to-cut-surgery-waitlists-by-outsourcing-to-private-hospitals-extending-doctors-hours). On cataracts: New Zealand's national cataract waitlist had grown by 696 patients as of **March 2024** — a snapshot that predates, by three months, the 30 June 2024 completion of the related 3,000-surgery delivery milestone, so it shows demand outpacing capacity added *so far* rather than a delivered milestone failing to shrink the list [NZ Optics, "Cataract waitlist grows, despite CPAC rethink", 1 Nov 2024](https://www.nzoptics.co.nz/live-articles/cataract-waitlist-grows-despite-cpac-rethink/). And the Auditor-General separately confirmed (Jan 2026) that ASMS/NZNO concerns about FSA waitlist patients being administratively reclassified in Nelson did **not** amount to reporting misrepresentation, but did surface real cross-district process variability in how the FSA measure is administered — a reminder that a rising FSA percentage is a measurement as well as a clinical outcome, and worth reading with that caveat [OAG, "Concerns about first specialist appointment waiting lists", 30 Jan 2026](https://oag.parliament.nz/2026/specialist-appointment).

**Overall confidence:** Medium. High confidence that the interventions exist, are dated, and are officially credited (primary-source factsheets are unambiguous on this). Low-to-Medium confidence that any of them, individually, has been shown — by evidence independent of the agency running it — to have *caused* a specific share of the observed wait-time improvement, because (a) several interventions land in the same quarters as each other and as broader volume/outsourcing funding, making single-intervention attribution hard even in principle from public data, and (b) the one body that has looked independently (the Auditor-General) has so far audited *equity and reporting-integrity* questions, not a counterfactual attribution study of "how much did X% improvement, if any, come from initiative Y."

## Evidence

### What "the current releases" actually say, verbatim, and when

The issue references "current releases" listing these interventions. This research traced that claim to two primary-source document types, both of which name the interventions explicitly as "Target Delivery Actions":

**DPMC Target 1 (ED), quarter ending December 2025 (data as at September 2025, published March 2026)** lists, verbatim, as "Initiatives to help improve results for this target":
- "$20 million in 2025/26 to improve flow through EDs and wards, and to enable improved weekend decision making."
- "A $14.455 million project to expand and upgrade Palmerston North Hospital's emergency department," (+~40% ED capacity, 15 additional points of care)
- "The Government's fast-tracked hospital wards programme will deliver 140 new inpatient beds in 2026 across Hawke's Bay, Nelson, Middlemore, Wellington Regional and Waikato Hospitals."
- "$24 million over four years (Budget 2025) to increase Aged Residential Care capacity and ease hospital discharge delays."
- "$164 million over four years (Budget 2025) for new and improved urgent and after-hours healthcare" — with named go-live dates: Lower Hutt daytime urgent care (1 Dec 2025), Dunedin 24/7 (17 Dec 2025), Counties Manukau extended hours (17 Dec 2025) moving to 24/7 (19 Jan 2026).
[DPMC, Target 1 factsheet, quarter ending Dec 2025](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/gt-factsheet-target-1-dec25.pdf) (fetched via plain HTTP, PDF text-extracted with `pdftotext`).

**DPMC Target 2 (elective treatment), quarter ending March 2026 (published June 2026)** lists, verbatim:
- "Health New Zealand continues to partner with the private sector to deliver the Elective Boost through to June 2026. This includes an additional 21,000 hip, knee, cataract and other procedures to be provided."
- "A focus on long waiting patients (including those that will be waiting > four months by the time they receive surgery)."
- "Expanded eye care services for the Hutt Valley and Porirua are in place to deliver 20 to 25 cataract surgery sessions each month, freeing up theatre capacity at Kenepuru and Wellington Hospitals for more complex eye procedures."
- "Improving internal hospital theatre productivity – there has been a notable reduction in late starts and early finishes in elective theatres in February [2026]. Theatre utilisation is also improving and close to meeting 2025/26 milestones."
[DPMC, Target 2 factsheet, quarter ending Mar 2026](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf) (fetched and PDF-extracted the same way).

This is the primary source for **theatre productivity** and **cataract surgery sessions** as named, dated initiatives — and it is worth being precise about how thin the theatre-productivity evidence is: the only quantification offered is qualitative ("notable reduction," "close to meeting … milestones"), with no percentage, no baseline, and no district breakdown given in this factsheet or any other document this research located.

**FSA has no equivalent DPMC factsheet** (confirmed in the baseline finding — FSA is a Health NZ-only target, not a cross-government DPMC Target). The nearest equivalent "initiatives" text is Health NZ/Beehive's own quarterly release: "FSAs were delivered at 104 per cent of plan to June [2025], meaning more patients are being seen than planned," attributed in the same release to the MSK physiotherapist pathway and to outsourcing volume. [Beehive, "Health targets: reducing wait times and putting patients first", 30 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/health-targets-reducing-wait-times-and-putting-patients-first) (full text retrieved and parsed from the live page).

### Elective Boost — dated, volumed, but not isolated from concurrent outsourcing

Elective Boost was committed to in March 2025 (target: 10,579 additional procedures before 30 June 2025) and delivered, per Health NZ's own count, **16,005 procedures from February to June 2025** — 51.3% above target. The top procedures were cataract surgery (5,058), knee surgery (751) and hip surgery (638); regionally, Northern region delivered 4,766, Midcentral 5,051, Central 2,961, South Island 3,227. [Beehive, "More Kiwis receiving surgery as Elective Boost exceeds first target", 8 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/more-kiwis-receiving-surgery-elective-boost-exceeds-first-target) (full release text retrieved via `curl` and parsed).

Cross-referencing against the baseline finding's national quarterly trend: elective-treatment performance rose from 65.9% (Q2 25/26, Oct–Dec 2025) — wait, more precisely the relevant window is Q4 24/25 (Apr–Jun 2025), which is exactly when Elective Boost procedures were being delivered. The baseline finding's district table shows large single-quarter jumps in several districts in that window — e.g., West Coast elective performance up 17.9 percentage points quarter-on-quarter, Whanganui up 12.2, Wairarapa up 11.5 — reported in the same Beehive release that credits Elective Boost. [Beehive, "Health targets: reducing wait times and putting patients first", 30 Sep 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/health-targets-reducing-wait-times-and-putting-patients-first). The timing is consistent with Elective Boost contributing to the Q4 24/25 improvement. It is not *proof* of a specific magnitude of contribution, because Elective Boost ran concurrently with the separate, larger outsourcing-to-private-sector programme (below) — both are "more procedures done," and the public data does not separate their individual shares of the same quarter's percentage change.

**The record on the adjacent outsourcing strategy is mixed, not uniformly negative** (Elective Boost explicitly "partners with the private sector" to deliver, per the Target 2 factsheet, making the two hard to fully separate). Health NZ's own reported FSA figures, cited in the same RNZ piece, point to progress: 648,000 people accepted for a first specialist assessment in 2024, up from 595,000 in 2022, and the number of patients waiting for an FSA reportedly down by over 8,000 in the weeks to mid-April 2025. Separately, ASMS executive director Sarah Dalton raised a different concern in the same reporting — not a fall in FSAs delivered, but **referral suppression**: some patients are not being referred for a specialist assessment at all, because GPs doubt they will be seen given how long waitlists have grown. Separately again, Health NZ commissioner Lester Levy's own briefing papers to the Minister (January 2025) on accelerating outsourcing flagged "a risk that capacity in the private sector may be insufficient" due to workforce availability — an internal risk flag Health NZ raised about its own plan, not an ASMS finding or an external audit result. The Royal Australasian College of Surgeons' NZ chair, Ros Pochin, publicly questioned the premise, saying most surgeons already work long hours and there is no "cache of private surgeons sitting there twiddling their thumbs." [RNZ, "Health NZ aims to cut surgery waitlists by outsourcing to private hospitals, extending doctors' hours", 13 May 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/in-depth/560757/health-nz-aims-to-cut-surgery-waitlists-by-outsourcing-to-private-hospitals-extending-doctors-hours) (fetched and parsed directly; the article was subsequently updated by RNZ to add a Health NZ statement clarifying FSA waiting had reduced). This is a genuine tension in the record, but it is a tension between a referral-suppression concern (patients not referred at all) and rising official acceptance/falling-wait numbers — this research found no source showing FSA delivery volumes flat or falling in 2025 versus 2024, and an earlier version of this finding's claim to that effect has been corrected as unsupported by its cited source.

### MSK/orthopaedic physiotherapist triage pathway — real, dated, output-measured, wait-time effect not isolated

The Musculoskeletal (MSK) orthopaedic pathway was introduced in **August 2024**, and as of the October 2025 announcement had completed "more than 7,200 first specialist assessments (FSAs) and over 9,500 follow-up appointments," delivered by Advanced Clinical Practice Physiotherapists (21.47 FTE nationally at the time, with a further 15.6 FTE being recruited and up to 50 physiotherapists funded for postgraduate training at AUT and Otago in Semester 2 2025). As at 30 June 2025 there were 34,395 patients waiting for an orthopaedic FSA nationally, of whom Health NZ estimates ~70% could be managed through this pathway; orthopaedics is ~18% of the national FSA and elective waitlists. [Beehive/Scoop, "Health Targets: Physiotherapist Boost Will Cut Orthopaedic Wait Times", 1 Oct 2025](https://auckland.scoop.co.nz/2025/10/health-targets-physiotherapist-boost-will-cut-orthopaedic-wait-times/) (full text retrieved and parsed).

This lines up in time with what the baseline finding shows: orthopaedics remains the single worst-performing FSA specialty nationally (43.5% seen within 120 days at the December 2025 snapshot, with the largest overdue count of any specialty — see [hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md](hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md)), which is consistent with the pathway targeting real, unresolved pressure rather than a specialty that was already recovering. But "7,200 FSAs completed by physiotherapists since Aug 2024" is an **output count**, not a wait-time-before/after comparison for orthopaedics specifically — no source located here publishes an orthopaedics-specific FSA percentage-within-target time series broken out before and after August 2024 (the baseline finding's specialty table is a single December 2025 snapshot, not a trend).

The closest independent, peer-reviewed evidence located is a qualitative outcome evaluation of an earlier, regional precursor: the **Community Orthopaedic Triage Service (COTS)** piloted at Bay of Plenty DHB, evaluated via semi-structured interviews with patients, physiotherapists, managers and GPs and published in the *New Zealand Journal of Physiotherapy* (2025). It reports **perceived** benefits — "improved experience, easier access, and reduced hospital pressures" — via thematic analysis, explicitly *not* a quantitative wait-time study, and cautions that "attention must be taken not to utilise a triage model as another barrier to accessing services." [Stilwell, Reid & Larmer, "Implementation of an Orthopaedic Triage Service for Osteoarthritis in the New Zealand Public Health System," *NZ Journal of Physiotherapy* 53(2), 2025](https://openrepository.aut.ac.nz/server/api/core/bitstreams/5ceea075-7bcf-4c7e-aeaa-90f8d64ba173/content) (PDF fetched via `r.jina.ai` proxy after a direct WebFetch attempt returned unparsed binary; full text extracted and confirmed as the correct paper). This is genuine independent academic evidence for the *model of care* (physiotherapist-led MSK triage) working as intended from the perspective of the people using it, at a regional pilot predating the national pathway — it is not evidence, one way or the other, about the national 2024+ pathway's effect on the published wait-time percentage.

### Cataract surgery sessions — a case where a delivered, named intervention coincided with the target *waitlist growing*

The Target 2 factsheet cites new Hutt Valley/Porirua eye-care services delivering "20 to 25 cataract surgery sessions each month" as a current initiative (undated start in the factsheet, but consistent with the earlier, separately reported **Hutt Hospital procedure-suite cataract service** and Kenepuru vitreoretinal equipment, together projected to add 300–500 treatments a year). [DPMC, Target 2 factsheet](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-06/gt-factsheet-target-2-mar26.pdf); cataract-specific volume context from [Beehive, "Cataract surgery kicks off waitlist action"](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/cataract-surgery-kicks-waitlist-action) and [RNZ, "Government expands cataract surgery eligibility, targets waitlists"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/political/493096/government-expands-cataract-surgery-eligibility-targets-waitlists).

Government sources confirm an earlier, separate 2023/24 cataract commitment (3,000 additional surgeries by 30 June 2024) was delivered. **Independent trade-press reporting shows the national cataract waitlist had grown by 696 patients as of March 2024** — an increase over the prior year, against the backdrop of a promised 3,500 additional cataract surgeries over the 18 months from July 2023 [NZ Optics, "Cataract waitlist grows, despite CPAC rethink", 1 Nov 2024](https://www.nzoptics.co.nz/live-articles/cataract-waitlist-grows-despite-cpac-rethink/) (the 696 figure and the March 2024 date both come from a Te Whatu Ora statement quoted in the article). The chronology here matters: March 2024 is roughly nine months into the 18-month window, and it **predates the 30 June 2024 completion of the 3,000-surgery delivery milestone by three months**. So this is not evidence that a completed delivery failed to shrink the waitlist — it is a mid-window snapshot, taken before that milestone's surgeries were finished, showing demand growing faster than the capacity added so far (Te Whatu Ora's own statement attributed the growth to "continuing demand" even while confirming the 3,000-surgery target was met). It is still a genuine data point against reading "X procedures delivered" as equivalent to "waitlist got shorter," but the finding should not be read as showing the waitlist was measured *after* the promised volumes were delivered. No independent, more recent (post-June-2024, or post-2025 Hutt/Porirua-session) waitlist-growth figure was located in this research — flagged as a gap below.

### Urgent and after-hours care, ED staffing and beds — funded and dated, but too recent for a wait-time verdict

Budget 2025 committed $164 million over four years to urgent and after-hours care, aiming for 98% of New Zealanders to be within a one-hour drive of in-person urgent care, including new/extended services named with specific go-live dates in December 2025–January 2026 (Lower Hutt, Dunedin, Counties Manukau) [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/gt-factsheet-target-1-dec25.pdf); separately, digitally-supported "ED Diversion" (redirecting suitable ED presentations to Health NZ-funded urgent care clinics) has been rolled out across all Northern Region EDs (18 funded urgent-care clinics) and in Taupō, Rotorua and New Plymouth. Because most of these go-live dates fall in the last month of, or after, the most recent quarter covered by the baseline finding (Q3 25/26, Jan–Mar 2026), **there has not yet been a full reporting quarter in which their effect on ED wait times could show up in the published national trend** — this is a timing observation, not a criticism: it is too early to assess, not evidence of failure.

ED staffing: figures obtained by the *NZ Herald* under the Official Information Act show the total ED workforce across all 20 districts (nurses, care/support workers, diagnostic staff, junior doctors, senior medical officers, plus corporate roles) rose from 5,527 to 5,821 (+294) between September 2024 and December 2025. The same OIA data show this net national rise **masks falls in frontline nursing and senior-medical-officer numbers in several districts** — Canterbury, Lakes, Nelson Marlborough, Hutt Valley, Auckland, Waitematā, Counties Manukau and Wairarapa all saw modest nursing-staff declines even as the national total rose. Waikato-district total ED nursing numbers also fell over the same 15 months, though the *Herald* reports this masks an increase at Waikato Hospital's own ED offset by declines at the district's smaller EDs (Thames, Te Kūiti, Tokoroa, Taumarunui). NZNO delegate Tracy Chisholm, an 18-year Waikato Hospital ED nurse, said the Minister's public framing ("more frontline staff … than ever before") is "a broad sweeping statement which hides reality in many, many hospitals around the country," naming Rotorua, Christchurch and Whangārei as her particular concerns; the Minister's office responded that the *Herald* was "cherry picking" specific districts and that national totals are the more reliable comparison. [NZ Herald, "Waikato Hospital ED swamped as winter patient surge overwhelms staff", 28 Jun 2026](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/waikato-hospital-ed-swamped-as-winter-patient-surge-overwhelms-staff/5DK3KQIQI5DWNNBK7RFNLVGT3U/) (full article text retrieved via `r.jina.ai` proxy; contrary to this finding's earlier draft, the body is not meaningfully paywalled — the OIA figures and Chisholm's quotes above are drawn from a direct full-text read, not a search-indexed excerpt).

Three days after that article, on 1 July 2026, a Waikato Hospital ED patient in his mid-fifties collapsed and died in a waiting-room toilet after a roughly nine-hour wait, prompting NZNO chief executive Paul Goulter to call for an **independent inquiry** rather than Health NZ's internal review, saying "the public deserve better than obfuscation" and that his members had "little confidence in Te Whatu Ora reviewing its own processes" after 20 months of staffing concerns raised in bargaining. Asked about staffing on the night, Goulter said: "We have been told by staff that they were understaffed. But Te Whatu Ora conceals the figures around how many staff are actually needed, and they staff up to budget." [RNZ, "Waikato Hospital ED death: Mum and daughter describe chaos as staff tried to resuscitate man", 1 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/650382/waikato-hospital-ed-death-mum-and-daughter-describe-chaos-as-staff-tried-to-resuscitate-man); corroborated by [NZ Herald, "Nurses call for independent inquiry after patient's death at Waikato Hospital", 1 Jul 2026](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/waikato-hospital-death-nurses-union-demands-independent-inquiry/VLSRLKK2NVECTOSLQAJL2RVYAY/) (Goulter also there challenges the Minister over whether Waikato Hospital was "deliberately" underfunded to meet "arbitrary" budget cuts). **This finding's earlier draft misattributed this quote to the 28 June staffing-figures article and to "NZNO's chief executive" without naming the death/inquiry context it actually arose from; that has been corrected here.** This is the one case in this research where the underlying raw data (an OIA release) is public and could be re-verified independently of Health NZ's messaging, and where the *existence* of the intervention (staffing increases funded via Budget 2024's $16.68 billion three-Budget frontline package) is well documented, but its *net national effect* is genuinely disputed even in the primary reporting — and, as of this writing, is also the subject of a live patient-death review that this finding flags but does not attempt to adjudicate, since it is under active investigation and outside this issue's scope.

The 140-new-beds programme (Hawke's Bay, Nelson, Middlemore, Wellington Regional, Waikato) and the Palmerston North ED expansion are both funded and dated but scheduled to open **in 2026**, after the most recent quarter in the baseline finding — again too recent to assess against outcomes. [DPMC, Target 1 factsheet](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2026-03/gt-factsheet-target-1-dec25.pdf).

### The one audited signal on measurement integrity: the January 2026 OAG letter on FSA waitlist administration

Separate from the planned-care equity report, the Auditor-General also investigated a specific complaint from ASMS and NZNO that Health NZ's Nelson Marlborough district was using an administrative "dummy clinic" / "do not contact" marker to change 24 long-waiting FSA patients' status from "unbooked" to "booked," raising a concern this could artificially improve the reported FSA percentage. The OAG's audit (finalised 31 October 2025, letter published 30 January 2026) concluded the patients **remained on the waiting list for target-reporting purposes** until actually seen, so there was **no misrepresentation** of the national FSA health-target figure — but it also confirmed Health NZ runs different patient-management systems and processes across districts, creating real variability, and noted Health NZ separately commissioned EY to independently review the accuracy of FSA health-target reporting nationally (this research did not locate the EY report itself, which appears not to be public). [OAG, "Concerns about first specialist appointment waiting lists", 30 Jan 2026](https://oag.parliament.nz/2026/specialist-appointment) (fetched via `r.jina.ai` proxy after a direct WebFetch attempt returned HTTP 403 — OAG's site blocks plain fetchers, consistent with the fetch-ladder guidance in AGENTS.md; full letter text retrieved and confirmed as the primary document, not a summary). This matters for this issue specifically because it shows the reported wait-time percentages that other interventions are credited with moving have themselves been subject to a live, specific integrity challenge — resolved in Health NZ's favour on this particular complaint, but confirming the reporting system is not perfectly uniform nationally.

### The one independent, government-external evaluation body that has looked at this systemically: the Auditor-General's June 2025 planned-care report

This is the single most load-bearing piece of independent evidence in this research, because the OAG is constitutionally independent of Health NZ and DPMC (the bodies making the causal claims above), and its June 2025 report on "Providing equitable access to planned care treatment" directly addresses whether the government's own chosen levers — especially outsourcing — are working as claimed:

- "Significant differences in access remain between districts. People with the same level of clinical need will qualify for treatment in some districts but not in others."
- "There is a risk that some actions designed to provide more timely treatment, like outsourcing treatment to the private sector, could result in less equitable access to treatment. Not all patients (such as people with more complex health needs) are suitable candidates for outsourced treatment."
- Health NZ "needs better information to understand and remove inequities" — the OAG found "significant gaps" in what Health NZ collects, including levels of unmet need and disabled people's access, and said current public reporting "does not convey an accurate picture… of how long it can take to receive treatment."
- Recommendation: Health NZ must "assess[] the impact of outsourcing planned care to the private sector on equitable patient access" — phrased as something still to be done, i.e. **as of June 2025, the equity impact of the single largest lever discussed in this brief had not yet been formally assessed by the agency itself.**
[OAG, "Summary of our report — Providing equitable access to planned care treatment", 25 Jun 2025](https://oag.parliament.nz/2025/planned-care/summary.htm) (fetched via `r.jina.ai` proxy after a direct WebFetch attempt returned HTTP 403; full summary text retrieved).

This does not say the interventions in this brief are failing — the OAG report is about *equity of access*, not overall throughput, and pre-dates most of the 2025/26 activity described above. But it is direct, independent evidence that the government's own external auditor, as of mid-2025, had **not found** the outsourcing-heavy strategy to be a proven net positive, and had specifically flagged it as an *unassessed risk* rather than a validated success — which is a materially different claim than the Beehive/DPMC releases make.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published, quantitative before/after (or interrupted-time-series) analysis from Health NZ, the OAG, or academic researchers isolating one intervention's effect from the others running in the same quarter** — e.g., an orthopaedics-specific FSA percentage trend from before August 2024 to now, or a difference-in-differences comparison of districts that got MSK-pathway staff earlier versus later — would let this finding move from "plausible timing correlation" to "credible attribution" for that specific intervention. This is the single biggest gap; it is flagged as a natural, chunky follow-up question (see fan-out below).
- **Publication of the EY report on FSA health-target reporting accuracy**, referenced but not located by this research, would materially strengthen or weaken confidence in the FSA-related figures underlying several of the interventions discussed here (MSK pathway, outsourcing).
- **An OAG or independent follow-up specifically assessing outsourcing's cost-effectiveness and equity impact** (the gap the OAG itself flagged in June 2025) would be the single most load-bearing piece of missing evidence for judging whether Elective Boost / outsourcing is a credible driver of the elective-treatment improvement, versus procedures that would have happened anyway being relabelled, or complex-case patients being left further behind while easy cases are cleared.
- **A full-text, unpaywalled copy of the OIA release behind the ED-staffing district breakdown** would let this research verify the eight-district nursing decline claim first-hand rather than via a search-indexed excerpt of a paywalled article.
- If a future quarter's data shows the 140-bed programme, the $164m urgent-care rollout, or the Palmerston North ED expansion coinciding with a step-change in their specific districts' ED performance (once those interventions are actually live for a full quarter), that would be new evidence this research could not yet capture, since most of them only went live in December 2025–January 2026 or later.

## Open follow-up questions (proposed fan-out)

Per the fan-out allowance for this issue (depth 1 of 2), this research splits off two genuinely chunky sub-questions rather than attempting shallow coverage of everything:

1. **Does district/specialty-level raw waitlist data (the same Health NZ waitlist-detail extract used in the #732 baseline) show a measurable step-change in orthopaedics FSA performance coinciding with the August 2024 MSK pathway rollout, once the underlying national upward trend is accounted for?** This requires pulling multiple historical vintages of the raw extract (not just the single Dec 2025 snapshot used in the baseline) and is real quantitative work, not a micro-task.
2. **What is the current state of any outsourcing-to-private-sector cost-effectiveness or equity assessment, following the Auditor-General's June 2025 recommendation that Health NZ do one?** This is a live, trackable gap explicitly named by an independent body — worth a dedicated question to check for Health NZ's response, any OAG follow-up audit, and any published cost-per-procedure comparison between outsourced and in-house delivery.

(Sub-issues will be opened per the fan-out instructions if not already covered elsewhere in the stream.)

Other, smaller open questions not split off but worth noting for whoever picks this up next: whether the EY FSA-reporting-accuracy review is ever made public; whether a second full reporting quarter (mid-to-late 2026) shows the urgent-care and bed-capacity interventions beginning to show up in district-level ED trends now that most have gone live.

## What could not be verified

- The exact scope and results of the EY review of FSA health-target reporting accuracy, referenced by the OAG's January 2026 letter but not itself located as a public document.
- A full first-hand read of the underlying OIA release itself on ED staffing (only the *NZ Herald*'s reporting on it, which this research fetched and read in full, was located — the release does not appear to be published as a standalone public document).
- Whether "FSA Accelerate clinics," as named in the issue text, refers to a real, specifically-named programme this research simply failed to find, or is a generic/paraphrased description of the ad hoc additional-clinics efforts documented for Nelson Marlborough and Northland. Treated here as unverified rather than assumed real.
- Any orthopaedics-specific (or cataract-specific) FSA/elective wait-time percentage trend spanning both before and after each intervention's stated start date — the baseline finding and this research both only have single-snapshot specialty data, not a specialty-level time series.
- A published ASMS analysis directly comparing FSA delivery volumes in 2025 versus 2024. This research located ASMS raising a referral-suppression concern and Health NZ's own internal risk flag on private-sector capacity, but no source found here shows FSA delivery volumes flat or falling year-on-year — an earlier version of this finding attributed such a claim to ASMS in error, which has been corrected.
- A cataract waitlist figure dated after 30 June 2024 (when the underlying 3,000-surgery milestone was completed) or after the newer Hutt/Porirua sessions named in the March 2026 factsheet — the only figure located (696-patient growth) is dated March 2024, before both.
- Whether Waikato Hospital's ED was in fact understaffed on the night of the 30 June–1 July 2026 patient death, and what the Serious Adverse Event Review / rapid clinical review Health NZ opened into it eventually finds — both were still underway as of this research and are outside this issue's scope, which is about named 2024–2026 interventions, not this specific incident. An earlier version of this finding misattributed NZNO chief executive Paul Goulter's "conceals the figures... staff up to budget" quote to the 28 June staffing-figures article rather than to his 1 July comments on this death; that has been corrected.
