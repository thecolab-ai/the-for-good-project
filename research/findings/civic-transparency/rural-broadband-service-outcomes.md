# NZ rural broadband programmes closed coverage gaps unevenly; fibre closed quality gaps, while wireless/copper upgrades often left rural users on lower-quality service tiers

## Executive answer

- **UFB is the clear service-outcome success, but mostly for towns and urban areas.** UFB made fibre available to about 87% of New Zealanders by the end of 2022, NIFF reported 1,985,266 homes and businesses with UFB available and 73.1% uptake at 31 December 2025, and the Commerce Commission says fibre coverage generally equals availability because fibre is not affected by congestion in the same way as wireless access [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes); [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf); [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- **RBI, MBSF and RCU materially improved nominal rural coverage and capacity, but the outcome evidence is weaker than the output evidence.** MBIE says RBI1 provided new or improved broadband to more than 300,000 rural homes and businesses and RBI2 targeted more than 84,000 more, while NIFF reported 86,038 rural homes and businesses with improved broadband access, 70,760 broadband capacity upgrades, 572 live mobile towers, 2,219 km of state highways and local roads with mobile coverage, and 197 tourism sites with mobile coverage by 31 December 2025 [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes); [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).
- **The main remaining gap is usable quality, not only footprint.** The Commission's prose says that, under its June 2025 rural definition, 95% of rural addresses were within at least one MNO's 4G FWA footprint; its Table 4.1 separately lists 96% for 2025, so this finding treats the point estimate as an approximate 95-96% and does not use it as an exact count. Coverage was not the same as availability because stop-sells can prevent ordering service, topography can create local gaps, and 4G FWA averaged 56 Mbps download while ADSL averaged 10 Mbps in April 2025 testing [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf); [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/).
- **The strongest rural performance improvement outside fibre is now coming from LEO satellite, not from the named legacy programmes.** The Commission measured Starlink Residential at 226 Mbps and Residential Lite at 200 Mbps in April 2025, around four times faster than rural 4G FWA at 56 Mbps, and Starlink had become the largest rural retail service provider with 27% rural broadband market share by 30 June 2025 [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- **Rural users still paid more and reported worse service experience.** The Commission found rural households paid a weighted average $85.77 per month for broadband versus $72.65 in urban areas, a $13.12 or 18% rural premium, and rural satisfaction trailed urban satisfaction across speed, reliability, billing, product range, pricing, and customer service [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the programme output and 2025 market-outcome evidence is strong, but public data does not cleanly attribute current measured speeds, prices, satisfaction, or orderability back to each intervention.

## Evidence

### What the programmes definitely delivered

UFB is best treated separately from rural wireless programmes because it delivered fibre-to-the-premises in higher-density towns and cities, and MBIE says fibre is most suitable in urban areas, superior to legacy copper, and capable of close to 1,000 Mbps service [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes). NIFF's December 2025 connectivity update reported 1,985,266 homes and businesses with UFB available, 1,445,725 connected, 73.1% uptake, $2.3 billion invested to date, and an average UFB service speed of 576 Mbps [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).

RBI and MBSF delivered large coverage outputs, but their targets were framed mainly as homes, businesses, towers, roads, and tourism sites rather than independent end-user service outcomes [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes). MBIE reports RBI1 as completed in June 2016 with new or improved broadband to more than 300,000 rural households and businesses, over 1,200 Chorus cabinet upgrades, improved fixed-line broadband for more than 110,000 households and businesses, and fibre access for more than 1,000 schools plus 39 hospitals and health centres [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes). MBIE describes RBI2 as extending high-speed broadband to more than 84,000 rural and remote households and businesses, while MBSF targeted about 1,400 km of state highways and more than 168 tourism locations where no coverage existed [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes).

RCU was explicitly a capacity repair programme, not just a footprint programme: NIFF says demand on rural broadband towers had caused some towers to enter stop-sell, where no new users could be supported, and that stop-sells and congestion restricted work from home, home schooling, e-commerce, whanau connection, information access, and entertainment [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/). NIFF reported 70,760 homes and businesses with broadband capacity upgrades by 31 December 2025, equal to 101.3% of the programme target, and its regional tables reported thousands of stop-sells upgraded, including 1,241 in Northland, 3,517 in Waikato, 1,950 in Bay of Plenty, and 2,369 in Canterbury [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).

RUS was narrower and residual by design: NIFF says the Remote Users Scheme targeted rural and remote households with no or poor connectivity at their principal residence, and offered one-off grants of up to $2,000 for eligible households without access to connectivity or planned connectivity after existing government programmes were completed [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/). The 2024 incoming-minister briefing said RUS targeted about 3,800 remote rural residences outside the 99.8% coverage target or with less than 10 Mbps service, concentrated in places such as Marlborough Sounds, the Far North, and Great Barrier Island [CIP/NIFF Briefing for Incoming Minister, May 2024](https://www.nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/CIP-BIM-Media-and-Comms-May-2024-Redacted.pdf).

### What the service outcome evidence shows

The Commerce Commission's 2025 monitoring report defines rural broadband for this analysis as addresses outside the regulated fibre footprint, covering about 352,000 addresses or 15% of New Zealand addresses [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). Under that definition, rural broadband remained a mixed technology tier: 50% of rural addresses had copper coverage, about 95-96% had 4G FWA coverage, 16% had 5G FWA coverage, 71% had WISP non-cellular FWA coverage, and about 1% had WISP rural fibre coverage in 2025 [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The 4G FWA figure is source-inconsistent: the report prose says 95% of rural addresses were in at least one MNO's 4G FWA coverage area, while Table 4.1 lists 96% for 2025 and footnote 126 says the reprocessed 2024 figure should have been 96% rather than the previously reported 95%; I therefore use "about 95-96%" when the exact percentage does not matter and "95%" only when referring to the prose sentence [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Measured performance shows why a coverage count overstates success for non-fibre programmes [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). In April 2025 MBNZ testing, ADSL averaged 10 Mbps down and 1 Mbps up, VDSL averaged 48 Mbps down and 13 Mbps up, 4G FWA averaged 56 Mbps down and 14 Mbps up, Starlink Residential averaged 226 Mbps down and 33 Mbps up, and Starlink Residential Lite averaged 200 Mbps down and 31 Mbps up [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The Commission concluded that ADSL supported only basic online activity, VDSL and 4G FWA supported a fuller online experience for a smaller household, and LEO satellite was a significant step up for larger households, while WISP NC FWA could not be measured because there were not enough volunteers in the MBNZ sample [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

The practical availability gap is clearest for 4G/5G FWA. The Commission says 4G/5G FWA depends on spectrum, tower locations, topography, local load, and network capacity, and that operators may impose stop-sells where performance would degrade or capacity is unavailable [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). NIFF independently describes stop-sell as a condition where a customer has mobile network coverage but cannot access a new broadband connection because the relevant tower has no spare capacity [NIFF Connectivity Update, Dec 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/NIFF_December_2025_Telco_Report_FINAL.pdf).

Consumer outcomes also favour fibre and satellite over copper/FWA in the available data. The Commission found rural broadband satisfaction below urban satisfaction across speed, reliability, billing, product range, pricing, and customer service, with rural scores of 62% overall satisfaction, 60% speed satisfaction, 68% reliability satisfaction, 50% pricing satisfaction, and 50% customer-service satisfaction in Figure 4.15 [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). By technology, rural satellite users reported 81% overall satisfaction and 83% speed satisfaction, while 4G/5G FWA users reported 63% overall satisfaction and 57% speed satisfaction, and copper users reported the weakest overall satisfaction at 57% [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

Price outcomes did not converge with urban service. The Commission's weighted regional price table found rural broadband was more expensive in every listed region, with a national weighted average of $85.77 rural versus $72.65 urban, or $13.12 and 18% extra for rural households [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). Rural 4G FWA was especially expensive relative to urban equivalents: rural 4G FWA averaged $90 per month for plans at or below 120 GB versus $55 urban, and $130 for unlimited plans versus $76 urban [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

MBSF appears effective on the public-safety corridor problem but less evaluable as a service-quality intervention. MBIE says MBSF was intended to improve mobile coverage on state highways and tourism locations with no existing coverage and to support public safety and visitor experience [MBIE broadband and mobile programmes](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/it-communications-and-broadband/digital-connectivity-programmes/broadband-and-mobile-programmes). The Commission reports that MBSF had completed coverage for 192 tourism sites and about 2,000 km of state highways and local roads by 30 June 2025, but also says MBSF does not publish public metrics on the quality of coverage for each site and that black spots are still likely to remain because of geography and low population density [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

## What would change this conclusion

- Address-level before/after data linking each RBI, MBSF, RCU, RUS, and rural fibre build to orderability, measured speed, latency, faults, price, and customer satisfaction would allow a stronger programme-attribution judgement; I could not verify that public dataset exists.
- RUS application, approval, installation, and post-install performance data would materially change the RUS judgement; I found eligibility and grant-design evidence, but not a current public uptake/outcome table.
- Provider-level stop-sell history by tower and date would sharpen the RCU assessment; NIFF reports stop-sells upgraded and ComCom explains stop-sells, but I could not verify how many stop-sells remain.
- Lived-experience review from rural households, WISPs, farmers, schools, health providers, iwi, and emergency services could change the weighting between price, speed, resilience, and local support; this finding uses public sources only.
- A future Commerce Commission report showing rural satisfaction, price, and measured speeds converging with urban fibre outcomes would weaken the conclusion that several programmes moved households into lower-quality tiers rather than closing the real service gap.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which rural addresses remain unable to order a service that meets an explicit speed, latency, reliability, data-cap, and affordability standard?
- How many RUS-eligible households applied, received grants, installed service, and achieved usable performance?
- Which RCU towers remain capacity-constrained or under stop-sell after the reported upgrades?
- How should rural resilience outcomes be measured alongside speed and price as 3G, copper, and landline services retreat?
