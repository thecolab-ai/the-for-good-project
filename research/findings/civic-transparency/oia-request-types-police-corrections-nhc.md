# Police, Corrections and the Natural Hazards Commission (ex-EQC) OIA volume is dominated by individualised operational/personal-record requests, not policy requests — but no agency publishes a request-type breakdown, so the exact proportion stays an inference

## Executive answer

- **The stream's Low-confidence hypothesis survives, and is upgraded to Medium.** The three OIA-volume leaders — NZ Police, the Natural Hazards Commission Toka Tū Ake (ex-EQC), and the Department of Corrections, which together field ~66% of central-government OIA volume ([sibling finding #201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md); [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/oia-statistics)) — each run a request stream whose **dominant, agency-described character is individualised operational/personal-record work** (property claim files, prisoners' own records, and event/incident/crash reports), not the public-interest *policy* requests the "open by default" idea targets. This was previously inferred from a single indirect signal (official volume vs FYI presence); it is now supported by **direct agency statements, an Ombudsman practice review, and the statutory routing structure** — three independent lines instead of one.
- **Natural Hazards Commission (ex-EQC): confirmed most directly.** The Commission states on its own site that **"The most common information request that we receive is for claims information relating to property"** ([Natural Hazards Commission — Request information](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/claims/request-information/)) — i.e. individualised claim-file/property-history requests (buyers, sellers, agents, lawyers doing due diligence, and claimants requesting their own files), not policy. This is *directly stated*, though the exact personal-vs-policy percentage is not published.
- **Corrections: supported in direction, but genuinely unquantifiable from public sources.** The Ombudsman's 2022 practice review confirms a large, structurally-separate **prisoner request stream** — requests arrive "by letter from prisoners" and prisoner requests deemed straightforward are handled by *frontline prison staff*, separate from the National Office OIA hub ([Ombudsman, *OIA compliance and practice in Department of Corrections 2022*](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/oia-compliance-and-practice-department-corrections-2022); [full report .docx](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2022-09/OIA%20practices%20in%20Corrections.docx)). Crucially, the same report says Corrections does **not** capture *what type* of requests prisoners make — the Ombudsman flags this as "a lost opportunity to collect valuable information about the type of requests submitted by prisoners." So the personal-vs-policy split for Corrections **cannot be measured from any published source** — not because the answer is unknown in kind, but because the data isn't collected.
- **Police: the picture is real but more subtle than "personal-record work."** Police *deliberately divert* pure "about yourself" requests to the **Privacy Act** ([FYI Police guidance note](https://fyi.org.nz/body/new_zealand_police): "We do not allow users to make requests about themselves… use the Request for Personal Information online form"), and route most high-volume personal/operational categories — traffic crash reports where you were involved, vetting, criminal disclosure, family-violence disclosure, demerit points, criminal records — through **dedicated statutory channels outside the general OIA** ([Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act); [Police — request a crash report when you were involved (Privacy Act)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-report-when-you-were-involved)). What remains *counted as OIA* is still heavily **individualised operational records** — third-party traffic incident/crash reports, prosecution files, and information about specific events/incidents ([Ombudsman case — Police prosecution file](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/request-police-prosecution-file); FYI "Traffic Incident Report"/"Traffic Crash Report" OIA examples). Police publish **no type breakdown** of their ~64k annual OIAs, so the operational tilt is structurally supported but not quantified.
- **Net effect on the stream:** the load-bearing scoping claim — that most of the top-3 OIA volume is personal/operational work **no open dataset would replace** — **holds and is now better evidenced**, but the specific "~66% is personal-record work" figure remains an *inference*, because the addressable-vs-not split is a request-*type* question that none of the three agencies publishes. The shortlist should keep sourcing "open by default" candidates from the *policy/administrative* request stream (FYI + agency disclosure logs), not from raw PSC volume.

**Overall confidence:** Medium. *Direction* (operational/personal-record tilt for all three) is well-supported — Medium-High for EQC/NHC (direct agency statement), Medium for Corrections and Police (Ombudsman + statutory structure). *Magnitude* (any precise personal-vs-policy percentage) is **Low** for all three: no agency publishes a request-type breakdown, and Corrections explicitly doesn't collect one.

## Evidence

### What the hypothesis was, and what "test it" means

The sibling finding [#201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md) established (High confidence, primary PSC data) that central-government OIA volume is extraordinarily concentrated: in calendar 2025, **Police (64,058), the Natural Hazards Commission/ex-EQC (18,968), and Corrections (17,024)** together made up ~66% of the 151,619 OIA requests completed by the 111 PSC-reporting agencies ([PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/oia-statistics); parsed table committed in that PR as `psc-oia-2025-totals.csv`). It then *hypothesised* — at **Low** confidence, from one indirect signal (these agencies barely appear in the public FYI archive relative to their official volume) — that the three leaders skew to individualised/operational record requests rather than public-interest policy requests. It explicitly flagged that the FYI ratio "does not measure request-type composition" and asked for an **agency request-*type* breakdown** to confirm or refute. This finding is that test, run against Police's own material, agency pages, and the Ombudsman's practice reviews.

### Natural Hazards Commission Toka Tū Ake (ex-EQC): claim-file/property requests are, by the agency's own account, the dominant type

The Commission's public "Request information" page states plainly: **"The most common information request that we receive is for claims information relating to property."** ([Natural Hazards Commission — Request information](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/claims/request-information/)). The page frames these as requests for a specific property's damage/settlement history, made by buyers, sellers, real-estate agents and lawyers doing due diligence, as well as claimants requesting their own claim files — an inherently **individualised, per-property operational record**, not a policy question. A dedicated request path and form exist for exactly this ([Natural Hazards Commission — OIA request form](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/contact-us/official-information-act-request-form/)).

This is corroborated in *direction* by what the ex-EQC request stream visibly looks like on the public archive: the most-viewed FYI requests to the body are claim-file and claims-data requests (e.g. ["EQC Documents"](https://fyi.org.nz/request/10380-eqc-documents), ["Database of claims locations"](https://fyi.org.nz/request/6067-database-of-claims-locations)) ([FYI — Toka Tū Ake / Natural Hazards Commission](https://fyi.org.nz/body/nhc_toka_tu_ake)). The Canterbury-earthquake legacy — a very large stock of individual claim files that owners, buyers and lawyers query per-property — is the structural reason this category dominates. **What I could not find:** a published number putting a percentage on "claims information relating to property" vs everything else. So the *type* leader is directly stated by the agency; the *proportion* is not.

### Department of Corrections: a large, structurally-separate prisoner stream — but the agency doesn't record request type

The Ombudsman's 2022 practice review is the strongest independent source. It describes Corrections' handling model directly: "Corrections operates a partly centralised model of OIA handling wherein the majority of OIA requests are handled by a central OIA hub based in Corrections' National Office, with **some straightforward requests submitted by prisoners handled by frontline staff in prisons**," and separately notes that "OIA requests can be received through multiple channels: by email or letter from members of the public; **by letter from prisoners**; transferred [from other agencies/Ministers]" ([Ombudsman, *OIA compliance and practice in Department of Corrections 2022*](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/oia-compliance-and-practice-department-corrections-2022); text from the [published report .docx](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2022-09/OIA%20practices%20in%20Corrections.docx)). The existence of a whole separate prisoner-request channel, handled by prison-floor staff, is itself evidence that a substantial share of Corrections' OIA volume is prisoners seeking information connected to their own management/records — individualised operational work.

But the same report is candid that Corrections **does not categorise these requests**: it warns that current practices "present a risk of OIA non-compliance [and are] also **a lost opportunity to collect valuable information about the type of requests submitted by prisoners**. This may help to identify areas where OIA training or resources may be needed." In other words, the personal-vs-policy split for Corrections is **not measurable from public sources because the agency itself doesn't collect the request-type data.** This is a genuinely informative negative result for the stream: any "~X% is personal" figure for Corrections is currently unbackable by evidence.

(Corrections' high OIA volume and its Ombudsman scrutiny are independently visible: RNZ reports Corrections among the top OIA-volume agencies and, separately, that [Police and Corrections have the most OIA complaints](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/323503/police,-corrections-have-most-oia-complaints).)

### NZ Police: personal requests are diverted to other regimes, yet the residual OIA stream is still event/incident-record heavy

Police are the case where the naive reading ("Police OIA volume = personal-record work") is *most* in need of correction, and where the honest answer is the most structural:

1. **Pure "about yourself" requests are explicitly not OIA.** Police instruct requesters not to ask for their own information under the OIA — those go via the **Privacy Act**: the Police authority page on FYI carries the note "We do not allow users to make requests about themselves. Instead, use the Request for Personal Information online form" ([FYI — New Zealand Police](https://fyi.org.nz/body/new_zealand_police)), and Police's own guidance separates "official information (OIA)" from "personal information (Privacy Act)" ([Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act)).
2. **Most high-volume personal/operational categories have their own statutory channels, outside the general OIA.** Police route criminal records (to the Ministry of Justice), criminal disclosure (Criminal Disclosure Act 2008), family-violence history (Family Violence Information Disclosure Scheme), deceased-person inquiries (Coroners Act), vetting (visa vetting service), traffic infringements/demerit points, and **traffic crash reports** — each away from the general OIA path. Crash reports are routed by the requester's involvement: a report about a crash **you were involved in** is handled under the **Privacy Act**, while one about a crash **you were not involved in** is handled under the **OIA** — both resting on s211 of the Land Transport Act 1998 ([Police — Request a Traffic Crash Report (TCR)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr); [Police — TCR when you were involved (Privacy Act)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-report-when-you-were-involved); [Police — TCR about a crash you were not involved in (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-0); [Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act)).
3. **What is still counted as OIA is nonetheless dominated by individualised operational records.** Requests for third-party traffic incident/crash reports, prosecution files and information about *specific events/incidents* are made and processed under the OIA (with heavy privacy redaction) — visible both in Ombudsman casework (e.g. [a victim's family requesting a Police prosecution file](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/request-police-prosecution-file)) and in the everyday FYI request titles to Police (e.g. "Traffic Incident Report", "Traffic Crash Report"). These are per-event operational records about specific people and incidents — not policy.

The upshot: Police's ~64k OIAs are **operational-record heavy, but not "personal information about the requester" in the Privacy-Act sense** — the pure-personal ones have been routed elsewhere. Police publish OIA *timeliness/complaint* statistics to PSC but **no breakdown of request subject-matter or type** ([Police — Latest OIA statistics](https://www.police.govt.nz/police-latest-oia-statistics)). So for Police the operational tilt is *structurally* well-supported and the "not policy, therefore not open-dataset-addressable" conclusion holds, but the exact composition is not a published number. A caveat worth flagging for the stream: because personal-to-self requests are diverted to the Privacy Act, one should **not** assume Police's OIA count is interchangeable with "personal-record work" — the mechanism is different even if the "not proactively-publishable" conclusion is the same.

### Why this matters for the stream's scoping

The stream's shortlist logic rests on distinguishing OIA volume that a proactively-published open dataset *could* replace (repeated policy/administrative questions — spending, headcount, contracts, briefings) from volume it *couldn't* (each answer is about a specific person, property, prisoner, or incident). This finding confirms that the three biggest agencies sit overwhelmingly in the **second** bucket by the nature of their dominant request types — claim files (per-property), prisoner records (per-person), and event/crash reports (per-incident). Publishing "open by default" datasets would not touch that volume, because you cannot proactively publish someone's individual claim file, prison record, or crash report. The scoping instinct is therefore sound. What the stream **cannot** legitimately claim is a *precise* addressable-fraction (the "~66% personal" figure), because that is a request-type measurement none of these agencies publishes — and Corrections doesn't even collect it internally.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published request-type breakdown from any of the three agencies.** The cleanest refutation-or-confirmation would be an agency (or Ombudsman) tally splitting OIA requests into "personal/operational record about a specific person/property/incident" vs "policy/administrative." None of the three publishes this today; Corrections' own review says it isn't even collected internally. If such a breakdown showed a large *policy* share hiding inside these agencies' volume, the stream's "look elsewhere for open-by-default candidates" conclusion would weaken.
- **Clarity on exactly what Police's PSC "OIA requests completed" count includes.** My structural argument is that Police divert pure-personal requests to the Privacy Act, so the OIA count is operational-record (event/incident) heavy rather than "about the requester." I could **not** verify from a published source precisely which categories are and aren't rolled into Police's 64,058 PSC figure (e.g. whether some third-party crash-report or event-report requests are counted as OIA while others are not). An OIA to Police for its internal request-category counts would settle both this and the magnitude question — and is the natural next step (noted below, not opened, per the human research→ideate gate).
- **A precise "% personal" figure remains unverifiable and should not be asserted.** The sibling finding's "~66% is personal-record work" conflates *volume concentration* (which is High-confidence: three agencies = ~66% of volume) with *request-type composition* (which is not measured). This finding supports the *direction* strongly but cannot supply the percentage; the stream synthesis should state the ~66% as "share of OIA *volume*," and treat "personal/operational tilt" as a separately-evidenced qualitative claim, not a measured 66%-of-requests-are-personal number.
- **Needs a human / domain check:** whether any *slice* of these operational streams could still be partly served proactively (e.g. NHC publishing aggregate per-property *claim existence/status* flags, or Police publishing more standardised event-report templates) is a legal/privacy judgement for agency OIA/privacy staff and a steward — metadata alone can't decide it.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Request-category counts via OIA to the agencies themselves.** The one reliable way to convert this from "direction" to "magnitude" is to ask each of Police, Corrections and NHC for their internal OIA request-category counts (many agencies tag requests even where they don't publish the tags). *Not opened here* — this is close to an action/recommendation, which sits behind the stream's research→ideate human gate (docs/STREAMS.md).
- **How much of each agency's operational stream is genuinely un-publishable vs standardisable.** Crash reports, claim files and prisoner records can't be published as-is, but the *forms/templates and turnaround* could potentially be standardised or self-served — a question for the ideate stage, not this finding.
- **Do other high-volume agencies (Customs, MPI, Health NZ) show the same operational tilt?** The top-3 dominate, but the next tier (see [#201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md)) may mix policy and operational differently and is where open-by-default candidates more plausibly live.
