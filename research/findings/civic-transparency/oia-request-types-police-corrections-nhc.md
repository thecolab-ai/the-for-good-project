# Police, Corrections and the Natural Hazards Commission (ex-EQC) each run a distinct operational/personal-record OIA request stream — directly confirmed as the most-common type only for the NHC — and none publishes a request-type breakdown, so how much of their OIA volume is operational vs policy cannot be measured

## Executive answer

- **What this finding can and cannot establish.** The three OIA-volume leaders — NZ Police, the Natural Hazards Commission Toka Tū Ake (ex-EQC), and the Department of Corrections, which together field ~66% of central-government OIA volume ([sibling finding #201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md); [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/oia-statistics)) — each visibly run an operational/personal-record request stream (property claim files, prisoners' own records, event/incident/crash reports) alongside whatever policy requests they receive. **What the public sources establish varies sharply by agency, and none of them establishes that operational requests *dominate* the volume:** the NHC states directly that operational (property-claim) requests are its most-common type; Corrections and Police have documented operational request *channels/routing*, but no source quantifies how large those streams are relative to policy work. The original stream hypothesis was that these three skew operational; this finding **partly confirms it for the NHC and leaves it a plausible-but-unquantified hypothesis for Corrections and Police**, because the load-bearing proportion is a request-*type* measurement none of the three publishes.
- **Natural Hazards Commission (ex-EQC): the one directly-supported case.** The Commission states on its own site that **"The most common information request that we receive is for claims information relating to property"** ([Natural Hazards Commission — Request information](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/claims/request-information/)) — i.e. individualised claim-file/property-history requests (buyers, sellers, agents, lawyers doing due diligence, and claimants requesting their own files), not policy. This is *directly stated by the agency*, so "most common request type is operational" is well-supported for the NHC; the exact personal-vs-policy percentage is still not published.
- **Corrections: an operational channel exists, but its size is genuinely unquantifiable from public sources.** The Ombudsman's 2022 practice review confirms a structurally-separate **prisoner request channel** — requests arrive "by letter from prisoners" and prisoner requests deemed straightforward are handled by *frontline prison staff*, separate from the National Office OIA hub ([Ombudsman, *OIA compliance and practice in Department of Corrections 2022*](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/oia-compliance-and-practice-department-corrections-2022); [full report .docx](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2022-09/OIA%20practices%20in%20Corrections.docx)). Crucially, the same report says Corrections does **not** capture *what type* of requests prisoners make — the Ombudsman flags this as "a lost opportunity to collect valuable information about the type of requests submitted by prisoners." So we can confirm a prisoner channel exists, but we **cannot** say what share of Corrections' OIA volume it is, nor that prisoners' requests are mostly about their own records rather than other prison-related matters — the source neither quantifies the channel nor describes what prisoners ask for.
- **Police: personal-to-self requests are diverted elsewhere; the residual OIA stream's composition is unknown.** Police *deliberately divert* pure "about yourself" requests to the **Privacy Act** ([FYI Police guidance note](https://fyi.org.nz/body/new_zealand_police): "We do not allow users to make requests about themselves… use the Request for Personal Information online form"), and route most high-volume personal/operational categories — traffic crash reports where you were involved, vetting, criminal disclosure, family-violence disclosure, demerit points, criminal records — through **dedicated statutory channels outside the general OIA** ([Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act); [Police — request a crash report when you were involved (Privacy Act)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-report-when-you-were-involved)). Operational records *do* still flow through the OIA — third-party traffic crash reports and prosecution files are processed under it ([Ombudsman case — Police prosecution file](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/request-police-prosecution-file)) — but Police publish **no type breakdown** of their ~64k annual OIAs, and their recent FYI request log is visibly mixed (policy, data and process requests alongside operational ones). So "the residual OIA stream is operational-heavy" is a **hypothesis, not a demonstrated fact**, for Police.
- **Net effect on the stream:** the scoping instinct — that a chunk of the top-3 OIA volume is per-person/per-property/per-incident work **no open dataset could replace** — is *supported for the request categories the sources name* (claim files, prisoner records, crash/prosecution files can't be published as open datasets), but the finding **cannot** put a proportion on that volume, and specifically cannot support the sibling finding's "~66% is personal-record work" reading — that figure is *volume concentration*, not *request-type composition*. The shortlist should keep sourcing "open by default" candidates from the visible *policy/administrative* request stream (FYI + agency disclosure logs) rather than assuming the raw PSC volume is un-addressable.

**Overall confidence:** Medium for the *narrow, evidenced* claims — Medium-High for the NHC's most-common-type statement (direct agency source); Medium that Corrections and Police operate distinct operational request channels/routing (Ombudsman + Police pages). **Low** for any claim that operational requests *dominate* Corrections' or Police's volume, and Low for any precise personal-vs-policy percentage for any of the three: no agency publishes a request-type breakdown, and Corrections explicitly doesn't collect one. The headline deliberately no longer asserts dominance.

## Evidence

### What the hypothesis was, and what "test it" means

The sibling finding [#201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md) established (High confidence, primary PSC data) that central-government OIA volume is extraordinarily concentrated: in calendar 2025, **Police (64,058), the Natural Hazards Commission/ex-EQC (18,968), and Corrections (17,024)** together made up ~66% of the 151,619 OIA requests completed by the 111 PSC-reporting agencies ([PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/oia-statistics); parsed table committed in that PR as `psc-oia-2025-totals.csv`). It then *hypothesised* — at **Low** confidence, from one indirect signal (these agencies barely appear in the public FYI archive relative to their official volume) — that the three leaders skew to individualised/operational record requests rather than public-interest policy requests. It explicitly flagged that the FYI ratio "does not measure request-type composition" and asked for an **agency request-*type* breakdown** to confirm or refute. This finding is that test, run against Police's own material, agency pages, and the Ombudsman's practice reviews.

### Natural Hazards Commission Toka Tū Ake (ex-EQC): claim-file/property requests are, by the agency's own account, its most-common request type

The Commission's public "Request information" page states plainly: **"The most common information request that we receive is for claims information relating to property."** ([Natural Hazards Commission — Request information](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/claims/request-information/)). The page frames these as requests for a specific property's damage/settlement history, made by buyers, sellers, real-estate agents and lawyers doing due diligence, as well as claimants requesting their own claim files — an inherently **individualised, per-property operational record**, not a policy question. A dedicated request path and form exist for exactly this ([Natural Hazards Commission — OIA request form](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/contact-us/official-information-act-request-form/)).

This is consistent in *direction* with what the ex-EQC request stream visibly looks like on the public archive: visible examples of FYI requests to the body include claim-file and claims-data requests (e.g. ["EQC Documents"](https://fyi.org.nz/request/10380-eqc-documents), ["Database of claims locations"](https://fyi.org.nz/request/6067-database-of-claims-locations)) ([FYI — Toka Tū Ake / Natural Hazards Commission](https://fyi.org.nz/body/nhc_toka_tu_ake)) — these are illustrative, not a ranked or "most-viewed" sample, and FYI is a self-selected subset of requesters, so it is not a measure of the body's overall request mix. The Canterbury-earthquake legacy — a very large stock of individual claim files that owners, buyers and lawyers query per-property — is a plausible structural reason this category leads. **What I could not find:** a published number putting a percentage on "claims information relating to property" vs everything else. So the *type* leader is directly stated by the agency; the *proportion* is not.

### Department of Corrections: a structurally-separate prisoner channel — but the agency doesn't record request type, so its size is unknown

The Ombudsman's 2022 practice review is the strongest independent source. It describes Corrections' handling model directly: "Corrections operates a partly centralised model of OIA handling wherein the majority of OIA requests are handled by a central OIA hub based in Corrections' National Office, with **some straightforward requests submitted by prisoners handled by frontline staff in prisons**," and separately notes that "OIA requests can be received through multiple channels: by email or letter from members of the public; **by letter from prisoners**; transferred [from other agencies/Ministers]" ([Ombudsman, *OIA compliance and practice in Department of Corrections 2022*](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/oia-compliance-and-practice-department-corrections-2022); text from the [published report .docx](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2022-09/OIA%20practices%20in%20Corrections.docx)). That a separate prisoner-request channel exists, handled by prison-floor staff, tells us prisoners are a distinct and recurring source of OIA requests to Corrections. It does **not**, on its own, tell us how large that stream is relative to policy requests, nor that prisoners are mostly seeking information about their own management/records rather than other prison-related matters — the source establishes the channel, not its size or its content. Treating "prisoner channel exists" as evidence of "operational work dominates Corrections' volume" would be an inference the source does not carry, so it is flagged here as a hypothesis, not a demonstrated proportion.

But the same report is candid that Corrections **does not categorise these requests**: it warns that current practices "present a risk of OIA non-compliance [and are] also **a lost opportunity to collect valuable information about the type of requests submitted by prisoners**. This may help to identify areas where OIA training or resources may be needed." In other words, the personal-vs-policy split for Corrections is **not measurable from public sources because the agency itself doesn't collect the request-type data.** This is a genuinely informative negative result for the stream: any "~X% is personal" figure for Corrections is currently unbackable by evidence.

(Corrections' high OIA volume and its Ombudsman scrutiny are independently visible: RNZ reports Corrections among the top OIA-volume agencies and, separately, that [Police and Corrections have the most OIA complaints](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/323503/police,-corrections-have-most-oia-complaints).)

### NZ Police: personal requests are diverted to other regimes; the composition of the residual OIA stream is not published

Police are the case where the naive reading ("Police OIA volume = personal-record work") is *most* in need of correction, and where the honest answer is the most structural:

1. **Pure "about yourself" requests are explicitly not OIA.** Police instruct requesters not to ask for their own information under the OIA — those go via the **Privacy Act**: the Police authority page on FYI carries the note "We do not allow users to make requests about themselves. Instead, use the Request for Personal Information online form" ([FYI — New Zealand Police](https://fyi.org.nz/body/new_zealand_police)), and Police's own guidance separates "official information (OIA)" from "personal information (Privacy Act)" ([Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act)).
2. **Most high-volume personal/operational categories have their own statutory channels, outside the general OIA.** Police route criminal records (to the Ministry of Justice), criminal disclosure (Criminal Disclosure Act 2008), family-violence history (Family Violence Information Disclosure Scheme), deceased-person inquiries (Coroners Act), vetting (visa vetting service), traffic infringements/demerit points, and **traffic crash reports** — each away from the general OIA path. Crash reports are routed by the requester's involvement: a report about a crash **you were involved in** is handled under the **Privacy Act**, while one about a crash **you were not involved in** is handled under the **OIA** — both resting on s211 of the Land Transport Act 1998 ([Police — Request a Traffic Crash Report (TCR)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr); [Police — TCR when you were involved (Privacy Act)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-report-when-you-were-involved); [Police — TCR about a crash you were not involved in (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-traffic-crash-report-tcr/request-traffic-crash-0); [Police — Request other information (OIA)](https://www.police.govt.nz/advice-services/accessing-information/request-other-information-official-information-act)).
3. **Operational records *do* still flow through the OIA — but the sources show this happens, not that it dominates.** Requests for third-party crash reports and prosecution files are made and processed under the OIA (with heavy privacy redaction): this is directly visible in Ombudsman casework (e.g. [a victim's family requesting a Police prosecution file](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/request-police-prosecution-file)) and the Police pages that route non-involved crash reports to the OIA (source 14 below). What these sources establish is that such per-event operational records *exist within* the OIA stream, not their share of it. Working against a simple "operational-heavy" reading, Police's own recent [FYI request log](https://fyi.org.nz/body/new_zealand_police) is visibly mixed — it includes policy, data and process requests alongside operational ones — and I did not find a specific set of titled crash/incident-report OIA requests prominent enough to characterise the stream. So the residual mix is genuinely unknown from public sources.

The upshot: Police divert pure "about yourself" requests to the Privacy Act, so Police's ~64k OIA count is **not** interchangeable with "personal-record work about the requester." Beyond that, the *composition* of what remains is **not established** — Police publish OIA *timeliness/complaint* statistics to PSC but **no breakdown of request subject-matter or type** ([Police — Latest OIA statistics](https://www.police.govt.nz/police-latest-oia-statistics)). "The residual OIA stream is operational-record heavy" is therefore a **hypothesis** consistent with the routing structure and the casework examples, but not demonstrated; it should be treated as Low-confidence for the stream, and an OIA to Police for its internal request-category counts is the way to settle it (noted under follow-ups).

### Why this matters for the stream's scoping

The stream's shortlist logic rests on distinguishing OIA volume that a proactively-published open dataset *could* replace (repeated policy/administrative questions — spending, headcount, contracts, briefings) from volume it *couldn't* (each answer is about a specific person, property, prisoner, or incident). This finding establishes that all three agencies **do** receive a meaningful stream of the second kind — claim files (per-property), prisoner records (per-person), and crash/prosecution files (per-incident) — and that, by their nature, those specific per-record requests **cannot** be served by an open dataset (you cannot proactively publish someone's individual claim file, prison record, or crash report). What the finding **cannot** establish is that this second kind *dominates* any of the three agencies' volume: only the NHC states that its operational type is most-common, and no agency publishes the personal-vs-policy split. So the practical takeaway is narrower than "these agencies' volume is un-addressable": it is that a real, un-publishable operational core exists in each, its size is unmeasured, and some slices of even that core might still be partly standardisable or proactively served subject to legal/privacy judgement (see below). The stream should therefore keep looking for "open by default" candidates in the visible policy/administrative request stream rather than treating raw PSC volume as either addressable or not — and must **not** carry the sibling finding's "~66% personal" figure, which is *volume concentration*, not *request-type composition*.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A published request-type breakdown from any of the three agencies.** The cleanest refutation-or-confirmation would be an agency (or Ombudsman) tally splitting OIA requests into "personal/operational record about a specific person/property/incident" vs "policy/administrative." None of the three publishes this today; Corrections' own review says it isn't even collected internally. If such a breakdown showed a large *policy* share hiding inside these agencies' volume, the stream's "look elsewhere for open-by-default candidates" conclusion would weaken.
- **Clarity on exactly what Police's PSC "OIA requests completed" count includes.** My structural argument is only that Police divert pure-personal ("about yourself") requests to the Privacy Act, so the OIA count is *not* simply "about the requester." Whether what remains is operational-record heavy is exactly what I could **not** verify: I could not confirm from any published source which categories are and aren't rolled into Police's 64,058 PSC figure (e.g. whether some third-party crash-report or event-report requests are counted as OIA while others are not), nor the mix of policy vs operational within it. An OIA to Police for its internal request-category counts would settle both this and the magnitude question — and is the natural next step (noted below, not opened, per the human research→ideate gate).
- **A precise "% personal" figure remains unverifiable and should not be asserted.** The sibling finding's "~66% is personal-record work" conflates *volume concentration* (which is High-confidence: three agencies = ~66% of volume) with *request-type composition* (which is not measured). This finding supports the operational *direction* strongly only for the NHC (agency-stated), and leaves it an unquantified hypothesis for Corrections and Police; it cannot supply any percentage. The stream synthesis should state the ~66% strictly as "share of OIA *volume*," and treat "operational/personal tilt" as a separately-evidenced qualitative claim (strong for NHC, tentative for the other two), not a measured 66%-of-requests-are-personal number.
- **Needs a human / domain check:** whether any *slice* of these operational streams could still be partly served proactively (e.g. NHC publishing aggregate per-property *claim existence/status* flags, or Police publishing more standardised event-report templates) is a legal/privacy judgement for agency OIA/privacy staff and a steward — metadata alone can't decide it.

## Open follow-up questions

- **Request-category counts via OIA to the agencies themselves.** The one reliable way to convert this from "direction" to "magnitude" is to ask each of Police, Corrections and NHC for their internal OIA request-category counts (many agencies tag requests even where they don't publish the tags). *Not opened here* — this is close to an action/recommendation, which sits behind the stream's research→ideate human gate (docs/STREAMS.md).
- **How much of each agency's operational stream is genuinely un-publishable vs standardisable.** Crash reports, claim files and prisoner records can't be published as-is, but the *forms/templates and turnaround* could potentially be standardised or self-served — a question for the ideate stage, not this finding.
- **Do other high-volume agencies (Customs, MPI, Health NZ) show the same operational tilt?** The top-3 dominate OIA *volume*, but the next tier (see [#201](repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md)) may mix policy and operational differently and is where open-by-default candidates more plausibly live.
