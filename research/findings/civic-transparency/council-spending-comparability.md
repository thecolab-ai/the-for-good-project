# Existing NZ council reporting standards support high-level comparisons, not transaction-level spending comparability

## Executive answer

- New Zealand councils must publish audited annual reports, financial statements, and group-of-activities information, but the Local Government Act 2002 sets reporting obligations and prescribed statements rather than a common chart of accounts for council spending [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/#DLM174786).
- Accounting compliance makes audited financial statements comparable at statement level, because councils report under New Zealand GAAP and the public-benefit-entity standards framework, but those standards do not create a shared transaction taxonomy for "consultants", "parks maintenance", "library programmes", or individual projects [XRB Accounting Standards Framework](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/standards/accounting-standards/accounting-standards-framework/) [Audit Office, 2004](https://ao.parliament.nz/2004/lg-2002-03/part2-01.htm).
- Published aggregate sources do support some cross-council comparisons: Stats NZ's Local Authority Census/Local Authority Financial Statistics publishes income, expenditure, operating income and expenditure by activity, and financial position at total and individual-council level; DIA/MCERT's council profiles compare selected rates, debt, operating, and capital-expenditure metrics within council groups [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).
- A civic spending-legibility tool would still need a mapping layer for council-specific accounts, line items, procurement categories, programmes, and projects, because official sources explicitly note variation in council organisational structures, accounting systems, definitions, and accounting practices [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).

**Overall confidence:** High — the conclusion is supported by legislation, official accounting/reporting sources, Stats NZ metadata, and the current central-government council-performance publication; the main uncertainty is whether any unpublished sector chart of accounts exists inside council finance systems.

## Evidence

### Accounting and audit compliance creates statement-level consistency

Local authorities must prepare planning and reporting documents under the Local Government Act 2002, including long-term plans, annual plans, and annual reports, and Schedule 10 specifies information that must be included in those documents [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/#DLM174786). Schedule 10 requires annual reports to identify activities within each group of activities, compare budgeted and actual capital expenditure for each group of activities, include an audited statement of service provision, and include an audited funding impact statement for each group of activities [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clauses 23-26](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/#DLM174842).

New Zealand's Accounting Standards Framework sets the suites and tiers of accounting standards that apply to entities, including public benefit entities, and the XRB describes the framework as a multi-sector, multi-tier approach for financial statements rather than as a council-operational spending classification [XRB Accounting Standards Framework](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/standards/accounting-standards/accounting-standards-framework/). The Office of the Auditor-General has also described local authorities as being required to present financial statements in accordance with GAAP, with GAAP meaning approved accounting standards and applicable authoritative notices [Audit Office, 2004](https://ao.parliament.nz/2004/lg-2002-03/part2-01.htm).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** These are statutory and official accounting/audit sources; they support comparability of formal financial statements, not a shared operational account code.

### The Local Government Act creates activity-group reporting, but leaves councils room to define most activity structure

Schedule 10 of the Local Government Act requires long-term plans and annual reports to report by "groups of activities" [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clauses 2 and 23](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/#DLM174788). The current Schedule 10 identifies some activities as groups of activities and also says a local authority may treat other activities as a group of activities, subject to water-services exceptions introduced by later legislation [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clause 2](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/#DLM174788).

The Department of Internal Affairs' mandatory non-financial performance measures were designed to improve comparability for five service areas: stormwater drainage, sewerage and sewage disposal, flood protection and control works, water supply, and roads and footpaths [DIA local government policy](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy). DIA says those measures require local authorities to use a standard set of performance measures for those activities, but they are non-financial service measures and do not prescribe service standards or spending categories [DIA local government policy](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** The activity-group obligation is statutory, and DIA's page is explicit about the purpose and scope of the mandatory measures.

### Prudence regulations benchmark financial health, not detailed spending categories

The Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014 prescribe financial prudence statements and benchmarks, including rates affordability, debt affordability, balanced budget, essential services, and debt servicing [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/). The regulation's own template says the purpose is to disclose council financial performance against benchmarks so readers can assess whether the council is prudently managing revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, and general financial dealings [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014, Schedule 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/).

These prudence benchmarks are useful for fiscal sustainability comparisons, but they do not identify suppliers, contracts, projects, or detailed spend categories [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014, Schedule 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** The regulation is primary legal evidence for the benchmark set and its purpose.

### Stats NZ and DIA/MCERT publish comparable aggregates, with explicit caveats

Stats NZ's Local Authority Census is an annual survey sent to city councils, district councils, and non-market council-controlled organisations, and Stats NZ says the resulting Local Authority Financial Statistics publish income and expenditure, operating income and expenditure by activity, and financial position at both total and individual-council level [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101). Stats NZ's local authority data collection says it collects income, expenditure, and financial position data for 78 local authorities and three Auckland Council CCOs, mostly from audited financial accounts, annual reports, and follow-up enquiries to councils [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42).

Stats NZ also warns that local authorities have varying organisational structures and accounting systems, so some council activities and data items are not always readily available and may be estimated by either the council or Stats NZ [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42). Stats NZ's quarterly Local Authority Statistics metadata similarly says accounting procedures vary among local authorities, which can make fully comparable statistics challenging, although Stats NZ does not expect the differences to materially affect the aggregate statistics it produces [Stats NZ quarterly Local Authority Statistics](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/f0e135ea-1e3b-466d-a4dd-5eb83238605c).

MCERT's July 2025 council-performance profiles compare councils within council groups across demographics, rates revenue, debt, and operating and capital expenditure [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/). MCERT says officials sourced the performance information from annual reports, annual plans, long-term plans, Stats NZ, and the Local Government Funding Agency, and it explicitly warns that it cannot guarantee all councils apply the same definitions or accounting practices for metrics such as staffing levels or capital expenditure by activity group [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** Stats NZ and MCERT directly describe both the available aggregate datasets and their comparability limits.

### Transaction- and project-level comparability would require a mapping layer

I found no official source that publishes or mandates a common NZ local-government chart of accounts for public transaction-level spending, and the strongest official evidence points the other way: Stats NZ says council organisational structures and accounting systems vary, and MCERT says it cannot guarantee shared definitions or accounting practices across councils [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).

A civic tool can safely use existing standards for high-level views such as total operating expenditure, capital expenditure, debt, rates, prudence benchmarks, and some activity-level comparisons, but it should not assume that two councils' ledger lines, procurement categories, programme names, or project budgets are semantically equivalent without a council-by-council mapping layer [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).

**Per-claim confidence: Medium-High.** The need for a mapping layer is an inference from official caveats and the absence of a discovered mandated chart of accounts; it would become High if confirmed directly by DIA, Stats NZ, SOLGM/Taituarā, LGNZ, or council finance practitioners.

## What would change this conclusion

- The conclusion would change if DIA, MCERT, Stats NZ, Taituarā, LGNZ, or a statutory instrument published a current common chart of accounts, activity-code hierarchy, procurement-spend taxonomy, or data standard that councils are required to use below the aggregate reporting level.
- The conclusion would change for a narrower civic tool if a subset of councils using the same finance system or open-data portal exposed consistent transaction exports with stable field definitions and licences.
- I could not verify whether councils share any non-public finance-system implementation templates, model charts of accounts, or professional-practice guidance through Taituarā, LGNZ, Audit New Zealand, or finance-system vendors.
- I did not test every council's annual report, annual plan, procurement page, or open-data portal; this finding is about national reporting structures and official aggregate datasets, not a full council-by-council publication census.
- A human council-finance practitioner or local-government auditor should review any proposed mapping rules before a public tool labels detailed spend categories as comparable.
