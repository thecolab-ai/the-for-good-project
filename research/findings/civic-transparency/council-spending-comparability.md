# Existing NZ council reporting standards support high-level comparisons, not transaction-level spending comparability

## Executive answer

- New Zealand councils must publish audited annual reports, financial statements, and group-of-activities information, but the Local Government Act 2002 sets reporting obligations and prescribed statements rather than a public common chart of accounts for council spending [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html).
- Accounting compliance makes audited financial statements comparable at statement level, because councils report under New Zealand GAAP and the public-benefit-entity standards framework, but those standards do not create a shared transaction taxonomy for "consultants", "parks maintenance", "library programmes", or individual projects [XRB submission on local-government reporting](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/dmsdocument/3374/) [XRB Accounting Standards Framework](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/standards/accounting-standards/accounting-standards-framework/).
- Published aggregate sources do support some cross-council comparisons: Stats NZ's Local Authority Census/Local Authority Financial Statistics publishes income, expenditure, operating income and expenditure by activity, and financial position at total and individual-council level; DIA/MCERT's council profiles compare selected rates, debt, operating, and capital-expenditure metrics within council groups [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).
- My inference is that a civic spending-legibility tool should plan for a mapping layer for council-specific accounts, line items, procurement categories, programmes, and projects, because official sources note variation in council organisational structures, accounting systems, definitions, accounting practices, and activity categorisation [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/) [Treasury-hosted Productivity Commission report, 2019](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-lgff-public-sector-performance-lg-accounting-and-financial-management-issues.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the aggregate-reporting part is High confidence, but the detailed-spending/mapping-layer part is a Medium-confidence inference from official caveats, the 2019 Productivity Commission accounting report, and a bounded negative search for a public mandated chart of accounts or transaction taxonomy.

## Evidence

### Accounting and audit compliance creates statement-level consistency

Local authorities must prepare planning and reporting documents under the Local Government Act 2002, including long-term plans, annual plans, and annual reports, and Schedule 10 specifies information that must be included in those documents [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html). Schedule 10 requires annual reports to identify activities within each group of activities, compare budgeted and actual capital expenditure for each group of activities, include an audited statement of service provision, and include an audited funding impact statement for each group of activities [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clauses 23-26](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html).

Section 111 of the Local Government Act requires information in Part 6 or Schedule 10 plans and reports to be prepared in accordance with generally accepted accounting practice where GAAP has developed standards, except for funding impact statements; XRB described the same section as requiring a two-arm reporting regime of GAAP information and other local-government legislative information [Local Government Act 2002, section 111](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM172320.html) [XRB submission on local-government reporting](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/dmsdocument/3374/). New Zealand's Accounting Standards Framework sets the suites and tiers of accounting standards that apply to entities, including public benefit entities, and the XRB describes the framework as a multi-sector, multi-tier approach for financial statements rather than as a council-operational spending classification [XRB Accounting Standards Framework](https://www.xrb.govt.nz/standards/accounting-standards/accounting-standards-framework/).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** These are statutory and official accounting/audit sources; they support comparability of formal financial statements, not a shared operational account code.

### The Local Government Act creates activity-group reporting, but leaves councils room to define most activity structure

Schedule 10 of the Local Government Act requires long-term plans and annual reports to report by "groups of activities" [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clauses 2 and 23](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html). The current Schedule 10 identifies some activities as groups of activities and also says a local authority may treat other activities as a group of activities, subject to water-services exceptions introduced by later legislation [Local Government Act 2002, Schedule 10 clause 2](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/2002/84/en/latest/DLM3418900.html).

The Department of Internal Affairs' mandatory non-financial performance measures were designed to improve comparability for five service areas: stormwater drainage, sewerage and sewage disposal, flood protection and control works, water supply, and roads and footpaths [DIA local government policy](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy). DIA says those measures require local authorities to use a standard set of performance measures for those activities, but they are non-financial service measures and do not prescribe service standards or spending categories [DIA local government policy](https://www.dia.govt.nz/Resource-material-Our-Policy-Advice-Areas-Local-Government-Policy).

The 2019 report "Local Government Funding and Finance - Accounting and Financial Management Issues", prepared for the Productivity Commission and now hosted by Treasury, is closer to the comparability question than the older Audit Office audit-results source: it identifies GAAP financial reporting, additional LGA/prudence disclosures, activity-group information, and financial prudence benchmarks as the four components of the local-government financial disclosure regime, and records submissions saying councils categorise activities differently in the funding impact statement, so comparisons cannot always be made across councils [Treasury-hosted Productivity Commission report, 2019](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-lgff-public-sector-performance-lg-accounting-and-financial-management-issues.pdf). The same report says a proposed definition of "groups of activities" would leave the reporting entity responsible for determining the groups for which it reports, reinforcing that activity-group reporting is not a complete national operational taxonomy [Treasury-hosted Productivity Commission report, 2019](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-lgff-public-sector-performance-lg-accounting-and-financial-management-issues.pdf).

**Per-claim confidence: High.** The activity-group obligation is statutory, and DIA's page is explicit about the purpose and scope of the mandatory measures.

### Prudence regulations benchmark financial health, not detailed spending categories

The Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014 prescribe financial prudence statements and benchmarks, including rates affordability, debt affordability, balanced budget, essential services, and debt servicing [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/). The regulation's own template says the purpose is to disclose council financial performance against benchmarks so readers can assess whether the council is prudently managing revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, and general financial dealings [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014, Schedule 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/).

These prudence benchmarks are useful for fiscal sustainability comparisons; my reading of the Schedule 4 template is that its fields contain benchmark disclosures rather than fields for suppliers, contracts, projects, or detailed spend categories [Local Government (Financial Reporting and Prudence) Regulations 2014, Schedule 4](https://www.legislation.govt.nz/secondary-legislation/pco-drafted/2014/76/en/latest/).

**Per-claim confidence: Medium.** The regulation is primary legal evidence for the benchmark set and its purpose; the absence of supplier/project fields is a direct reading of the template rather than a separately stated proposition in the regulation.

### Stats NZ and DIA/MCERT publish comparable aggregates, with explicit caveats

Stats NZ's Local Authority Census page describes an annual survey sent to city councils, district councils, and non-market council-controlled organisations, and says the resulting Local Authority Financial Statistics publish income and expenditure, operating income and expenditure by activity, and financial position at both total and individual-council level [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101). Stats NZ's separate local authority data collection page says it collects income, expenditure, and financial position data for 78 local authorities and three Auckland Council CCOs, mostly from audited financial accounts, annual reports, and follow-up enquiries to councils [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42). I am treating those as related but distinct Stats NZ collection descriptions, not as identical coverage statements.

Stats NZ also warns that local authorities have varying organisational structures and accounting systems, so some council activities and data items are not always readily available and may be estimated by either the council or Stats NZ [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42). Stats NZ's quarterly Local Authority Statistics metadata similarly says accounting procedures vary among local authorities, which can make fully comparable statistics challenging, although Stats NZ does not expect the differences to materially affect the aggregate statistics it produces [Stats NZ quarterly Local Authority Statistics](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/f0e135ea-1e3b-466d-a4dd-5eb83238605c).

MCERT's July 2025 council-performance profiles compare councils within council groups across demographics, rates revenue, debt, and operating and capital expenditure [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/). MCERT says officials sourced the performance information from annual reports, annual plans, long-term plans, Stats NZ, and the Local Government Funding Agency, and it explicitly warns that it cannot guarantee all councils apply the same definitions or accounting practices for metrics such as staffing levels or capital expenditure by activity group [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/).

**Per-claim confidence: High for aggregate availability; Medium for detailed comparability limits.** Stats NZ and MCERT directly describe both the available aggregate datasets and their caveats, but MCERT partly sources its profiles from Stats NZ, annual reports, plans, and the Local Government Funding Agency, so the Stats NZ/MCERT pair is not fully independent for underlying data even though their caveat wording is each organisation's own editorial statement.

### Sampled open-data portals and LGOIMA do not remove the mapping problem

The review correctly flags public open-data portals and LGOIMA as relevant alternatives to relying only on statutory PDFs and national aggregate datasets. A quick check of the three largest territorial authorities' public data surfaces found open-data infrastructure, but did not find a public, reusable, cross-council supplier-payment or transaction-spend dataset in the sampled portal searches: Auckland Council's ArcGIS open-data site is described as a place to explore and download council-owned geospatial datasets, and ArcGIS public search queries for Auckland Council "expenditure" and "supplier" returned zero items [Auckland Council Open Data](https://data-aucklandcouncil.opendata.arcgis.com/) [Auckland ArcGIS search: expenditure](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/search?f=json&q=orgid%3An4yPwebTjJCmXB6W%20expenditure&num=10) [Auckland ArcGIS search: supplier](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/search?f=json&q=orgid%3An4yPwebTjJCmXB6W%20supplier&num=10). Wellington City Council's data.govt.nz showcase is explicitly about geospatial data, and Christchurch City Council's ArcGIS Hub describes its portal as a spatial open-data portal; ArcGIS public search queries for Christchurch "expenditure" and "supplier" returned zero items [Wellington City Council geospatial open data](https://data.govt.nz/catalogue-guide/showcase/wellington-city-council-geospatial-open-data) [Christchurch City Council Open Data Portal](https://opendata-christchurchcity.hub.arcgis.com/search?layout=grid) [Christchurch ArcGIS search: expenditure](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/search?f=json&q=orgid%3ArW4Z7Dz9Bz87fkds%20expenditure&num=10) [Christchurch ArcGIS search: supplier](https://www.arcgis.com/sharing/rest/search?f=json&q=orgid%3ArW4Z7Dz9Bz87fkds%20supplier&num=10).

This is only a sample and should not be read as a 78-council publication census; it is enough to keep "which councils publish detailed spend data?" as a live follow-up rather than an assumption against the mapping-layer conclusion. The sampled evidence weakens any absolute claim that no detailed council spending data is publicly available, but it does not show a national common transaction taxonomy or a consistent open-spend format across the sampled large councils [Wellington City Council geospatial open data](https://data.govt.nz/catalogue-guide/showcase/wellington-city-council-geospatial-open-data) [Christchurch City Council Open Data Portal](https://opendata-christchurchcity.hub.arcgis.com/search?layout=grid) [Auckland Council Open Data](https://data-aucklandcouncil.opendata.arcgis.com/).

LGOIMA is a real access mechanism for detailed spending records held by councils: Ombudsman guidance says LGOIMA allows people to request official information held by local-government agencies, that the principle of availability applies unless there is good reason to withhold, and that city, district, and regional councils are subject to the Act [Ombudsman, LGOIMA guide for local government agencies](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-07/The%20LGOIMA%20for%20local%20government%20agencies%20A%20guide%20to%20processing%20requests%20and%20conducting%20meetings.pdf). LGOIMA can therefore obtain or test specific council spend questions, but it is reactive access to information held by each council, not evidence of a proactive national open-data standard or shared chart of accounts [Ombudsman, LGOIMA guide for local government agencies](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/sites/default/files/2024-07/The%20LGOIMA%20for%20local%20government%20agencies%20A%20guide%20to%20processing%20requests%20and%20conducting%20meetings.pdf).

**Per-claim confidence: Medium.** The portal checks are deliberately narrow, and LGOIMA establishes a route to request information rather than a ready-to-use comparative dataset.

### Transaction- and project-level comparability would require a mapping layer

I found no official source that publishes or mandates a common NZ local-government chart of accounts for public transaction-level spending. The evidence for a mapping layer is therefore inferential: Stats NZ says council organisational structures and accounting systems vary; MCERT says it cannot guarantee shared definitions or accounting practices across councils; and the Productivity Commission accounting report records activity-categorisation comparability concerns and says a more developed definition of groups of activities would still leave reporting entities responsible for determining their reported groups [Stats NZ local authority data collection](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/66c1d2c4-0ccc-4ff6-8fbd-1e972662c005/42) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/) [Treasury-hosted Productivity Commission report, 2019](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-lgff-public-sector-performance-lg-accounting-and-financial-management-issues.pdf).

A civic tool can use existing standards for high-level views such as total operating expenditure, capital expenditure, debt, rates, prudence benchmarks, and some activity-level comparisons, but should treat semantic equivalence between two councils' ledger lines, procurement categories, programme names, or project budgets as a hypothesis to be mapped and verified council by council [Stats NZ DataInfo+](https://datainfoplus.stats.govt.nz/Item/nz.govt.stats/9a6701e3-f1d3-4480-b879-5eea7716979b/101) [MCERT council performance](https://www.mcert.govt.nz/our-work/local-government/council-performance/) [Treasury-hosted Productivity Commission report, 2019](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2024-05/pc-inq-lgff-public-sector-performance-lg-accounting-and-financial-management-issues.pdf).

**Per-claim confidence: Medium.** The need for a mapping layer is my inference from official caveats, the Productivity Commission accounting report, sampled portal checks, and the absence of a discovered public mandated chart of accounts; it would become High if confirmed directly by DIA, Stats NZ, Taituarā, LGNZ, Audit New Zealand, or council finance practitioners.

## What would change this conclusion

- The conclusion would change if DIA, MCERT, Stats NZ, Taituarā, LGNZ, or a statutory instrument published a current common chart of accounts, activity-code hierarchy, procurement-spend taxonomy, or data standard that councils are required to use below the aggregate reporting level.
- The conclusion would change for a narrower civic tool if a subset of councils using the same finance system or open-data portal exposed consistent transaction exports with stable field definitions and licences.
- I could not verify whether councils share any non-public finance-system implementation templates, model charts of accounts, or professional-practice guidance through Taituarā, LGNZ, Audit New Zealand, or finance-system vendors.
- I did not test every council's annual report, annual plan, procurement page, LGOIMA disclosure log, or open-data portal; this finding is about national reporting structures, official aggregate datasets, and a small portal sample, not a full council-by-council publication census.
- A human council-finance practitioner or local-government auditor should review any proposed mapping rules before a public tool labels detailed spend categories as comparable.

## Open follow-up questions

- Which councils publish transaction-level, supplier-level, contract-level, or project-level spend data in reusable formats, and under what licence?
- Which council spending questions are best answered by LGOIMA requests rather than proactive open data, and what request wording reliably produces reusable machine-readable exports?
- Can Stats NZ Local Authority Financial Statistics activity categories be used as a first-pass taxonomy for a civic spending tool, and which categories are too broad or inconsistently allocated?
- Are there common finance-system vendors or implementation templates across NZ councils that could make semi-automated account mapping feasible for clusters of councils?
- What council-spending questions do residents and local journalists most often ask that can be answered safely from aggregate data alone?
