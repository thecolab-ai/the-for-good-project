# NZ mental-health inpatient inspections: the Wakari Ward 10A findings are primary-sourced and follow a warning trail back to 2014, the "Waikato mental-health ward slipped to 2027" starting fact is a conflation, and the national seclusion record — now read directly from the primary Regulatory Report — shows a ~21-month lag, a 6.6× Māori inequity, and a separate prolonged-seclusion cohort the Director is investigating

This is the **discover baseline for Stream #224**: it verifies the starting facts, corrects the ones that don't hold, and frames the fan-out. It is deliberately not the deep dive — each deep dive is a research child (#230, #231, #233, #234, #235).

> **Re-research note (8 July 2026).** An earlier version of this baseline could not fetch the Ministry of Health's *Regulatory Report 2023/24* — health.govt.nz bot-blocked the tooling then in use, so the national seclusion figures rested only on the Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission's secondary relay and a Wayback snapshot of the publication page. After the 8 July fetch-ladder upgrade (Jina reader + stealth-browser rungs), **both the publication page and the 73-page report PDF now fetch cleanly**. This version reads the primary source directly: it confirms every figure the Commission relayed, corrects one attribution the block had left ambiguous, and recovers a load-bearing finding the block hid entirely — the report's own Appendix 3 on **eight individuals in prolonged/frequent seclusion**. That cohort is an important contextual match for the Wakari Ward 10A allegations, but the report anonymises facilities and covers 1 July 2023 to 30 June 2024, so it does not prove the specific 2026 Ward 10A resident was one of those eight.

## Executive answer

- **The Wakari findings are real and primary-sourced.** The Chief Ombudsman, John Allen, inspected **Ward 10A at Wakari Hospital, Dunedin — a secure inpatient unit for adults with intellectual disabilities — unannounced, 17–20 March 2026**, under the Crimes of Torture Act 1989 (NZ's OPCAT implementation). His 3 July 2026 media release reports "compelling evidence of punitive and coercive treatment": one person secluded in their bedroom for **around 18 months** with almost no meaningful human contact, restraint on an EVAC/bariatric mat causing physical harm (carpet burns), and staff conditioning access to basic necessities — a toilet, dental care, personal money — on "good" behaviour. He called it "some of the worst practices we have observed anywhere" [Ombudsman media release, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need); [RNZ, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/660871/potential-human-rights-abuses-uncovered-at-dunedin-s-wakari-hospital-ombudsman); [ODT, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/horror-conditions-wakari-ward-some-worst-we-have-observed).
- **This was a re-inspection failure, not a surprise discovery.** The Ombudsman inspected the same ward in May 2021 and reported (February 2022) that it was "not fit-for-purpose and was in critical need of upgrade and redevelopment," an issue **unresolved since 2014**, making 15 recommendations [Ombudsman 2021 inspection report page](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin); the ODT reports Allen noting "next to no improvement" since [ODT](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/horror-conditions-wakari-ward-some-worst-we-have-observed). The 2014→2021→2026 warning trail is the spine of this stream.
- **The Ward 10A case resembles a national prolonged-seclusion pattern, but the identity link is unconfirmed.** The *Regulatory Report 2023/24* flags, in **Appendix 3**, **eight individuals who experienced prolonged and/or frequent periods of seclusion**, at three regional intellectual-disability or forensic facilities, whom the Director of Mental Health is separately investigating. Those eight people account for **45,531 of the 105,407 total seclusion hours across all inpatient services (43%)**; in intellectual-disability services alone, including them lifts total seclusion hours from 15,652 to **49,674** [MoH *Regulatory Report 2023/24* PDF, pp. 8, 62–63 (Appendix 3, Table A3)](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). An ~18-month seclusion in a regional ID secure unit is a close contextual match, but the MoH report does not name Ward 10A or any facility, and its 2023/24 data year ends on 30 June 2024 — well before the 17–20 March 2026 Ombudsman inspection.
- **One starting fact does not hold.** The RNZ story the issue cites for "Waikato Hospital's new mental-health ward has slipped to 2027" is about a **general 28-bed assessment and diagnostic ward near the emergency department** — the article contains no mention of mental health [RNZ, Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/659839/waikato-hospital-ward-will-not-be-ready-until-2027-health-minister-says). Waikato's actual new **64-bed adult acute mental-health facility** ($131.2m) was reported by Health NZ on 30 April 2026 as weather-tight and "expected to open in late December this year" (2026) [Health NZ via Scoop, 30 Apr 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/GE2604/S00085/major-milestone-reached-for-waikatos-new-adult-acute-mental-health-facility.htm) — that is the operator's own timeline, a claim, not confirmation. Child #233 should verify the current date rather than inherit the conflation.
- **National seclusion statistics exist, lag ~21 months, and show a stark, entrenched inequity — now read from the primary source.** The Office of the Director of Mental Health and Addiction Services *Regulatory Report 2023/24* covers the year to 30 June 2024 but carries a publication date of **31 March 2026** — the report's own foreword acknowledges the release "has been delayed again" because of data complexity and manual reporting [MoH publication page](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/office-of-the-director-of-mental-health-and-addiction-services-regulatory-report-2023-to-2024); [PDF, p. iv (foreword)](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). The report states directly that **Māori in adult inpatient services were secluded at 62.8 per 100,000 vs 9.6 for non-Māori — 6.6× more likely**, and that Māori made up **55% of all adults secluded despite being 17% of the population** [PDF, pp. 20, 27](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). The Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission's same-day response frames these as "deepening inequities" [Te Hiringa Mahara release, 31 Mar 2026](https://livenews.co.nz/2026/03/31/health-deepening-inequities-in-the-mental-health-system-call-for-action/).

**Overall confidence:** High — every load-bearing claim is now either primary-sourced (Ombudsman; the MoH *Regulatory Report 2023/24* PDF, read directly; Health NZ) or double-sourced, and the two starting facts that failed verification are corrected, not repeated. Agency commitments (ward closure, relocations, independent investigation) are marked as claims throughout.

## Evidence

### What the Ombudsman found at Ward 10A (March 2026)

The inspection was **unannounced, 17–20 March 2026**, of Ward 10A — described in the Ombudsman's release as an inpatient unit for adults with intellectual disabilities at Wakari Hospital, Dunedin — under the Crimes of Torture Act 1989, which implements OPCAT [Ombudsman media release, 3 Jul 2026](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need) ([archived press release text](https://web.archive.org/web/20260703093820/https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/GE2607/S00008/failures-at-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-highlight-need-for-strengthened-commitment-from-health-agencies.htm)). Findings reported by the Chief Ombudsman and carried consistently by RNZ and the ODT:

- "Compelling evidence of punitive and coercive treatment", with some people "subjected to prolonged environmental restraint and/or long-term seclusion" [Ombudsman release](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/660871/potential-human-rights-abuses-uncovered-at-dunedin-s-wakari-hospital-ombudsman).
- One person **secluded in their bedroom for around 18 months**, with an almost complete lack of meaningful human contact and no access to the outdoors [Ombudsman release](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need); [ODT](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/horror-conditions-wakari-ward-some-worst-we-have-observed).
- Restraint using an EVAC/bariatric mat that caused physical harm including carpet burns; restrictions on dental care; denial of access to people's own money; staff "incentivising 'good' behaviour by rewarding people with access to basic necessities including dental treatment or the use of a toilet" [Ombudsman release](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/660871/potential-human-rights-abuses-uncovered-at-dunedin-s-wakari-hospital-ombudsman); [ODT](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/horror-conditions-wakari-ward-some-worst-we-have-observed).
- RNZ additionally reports concerns about **potentially unlawful medication administration** [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/660871/potential-human-rights-abuses-uncovered-at-dunedin-s-wakari-hospital-ombudsman) — this detail was in RNZ's account of the findings; treat as Medium until the full Ombudsman report is published.

No patient or staff member is identified in any source used, and none is identified here.

### The warning trail: 2014 → 2021 → 2026

The Ombudsman (then Peter Boshier) inspected Ward 10a and Helensburgh Cottage on **3–7 May 2021** and published the report on **2 February 2022**, finding "the Ward was not fit-for-purpose and was in critical need of upgrade and redevelopment" — an issue the report noted had been **unresolved since 2014** — alongside concerns about mixing forensic and non-forensic patients and an improperly applied restraint technique; it made **15 recommendations**, including rebuilding or substantially upgrading the ward, and repeated a recommendation from 2014 [Ombudsman 2021 inspection report](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin). The ODT independently anchors the trail: the 2022 report "deemed ward 10a 'not fit for purpose'", and in 2026 Allen noted "next to no improvement" to the physical environment since [ODT](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/dunedin/horror-conditions-wakari-ward-some-worst-we-have-observed). **Confidence: High.** A repeat-findings pattern across the wider ~93-report corpus is exactly what child #230 should quantify.

### Agency responses — claims, not confirmations (docs/METHOD.md)

Per RNZ, Health NZ's director of mental health Phil Grady said Health NZ **ceased admissions to Ward 10A in late April 2026 and began relocating patients in May 2026**, with commitments to strengthen clinical oversight and seclusion-reduction approaches; the Ministry of Health accepted the Ombudsman's recommendation of an **independent investigation**, and a district inspector was already reviewing two complaints [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/health/660871/potential-human-rights-abuses-uncovered-at-dunedin-s-wakari-hospital-ombudsman). The Ombudsman's release confirms the ward is closed to new admissions and says he will monitor both agencies' progress [Ombudsman release](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/news/serious-failures-adult-intellectual-disability-inpatient-ward-dunedin-highlight-need). **These are the agencies' statements about themselves — claims, not verified outcomes** (relocation destinations, investigation terms of reference, and completion are all unverified). **Confidence in "the agencies said this": High; in "this has happened": not assessed — child #234's core job.**

### The inspection regime, and where its outputs actually live

New Zealand ratified OPCAT in 2007; the Ombudsman's inspection powers come from the Crimes of Torture Act 1989, and the mandate covers "prisons, court facilities, mental health facilities, intellectual disability facilities, aged care facilities, immigration detention centres", with announced and unannounced inspections and published expectations documents for health and disability places [Ombudsman: monitoring places of detention](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/what-ombudsman-can-help/monitoring-places-detention); [Ministry of Justice: OPCAT](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/constitutional-issues-and-human-rights/human-rights/international-human-rights/cat/optional-protocol/). The published output is a filterable list of **~93 OPCAT inspection reports, one document per facility inspection** — recent examples include Te Awhina Unit (Whanganui), Ward OPR1 (Waikato), and Tumanako Inpatient Mental Health Unit (Whangārei), all December 2025 [OPCAT reports catalogue](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources?f%5B0%5D=category%3A1993). A site search for "Wakari" returns the 2022 Ward 10a report and 2024 follow-ups for Wards 9A/9B, but **no 2026 Ward 10A report as of 8 July 2026** [Ombudsman resources search](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources?search=Wakari) — the most consequential inspection finding of the year is currently legible only through a media release. Two refinements to the issue's premise: (1) Ward 10A patients are subject to the **Intellectual Disability (Compulsory Care and Rehabilitation) Act 2003** compulsory-care regime (the 2021 report describes the units as secure units for patients with intellectual disabilities requiring compulsory care), so the stream spans both that Act and the Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992; (2) whether the Ombudsman's *annual* OPCAT reporting or the Human Rights Commission's central-NPM reporting already aggregates findings usefully is unassessed here — child #230 must check before asserting "no national picture" as more than "no facility-level roll-up found". **Confidence: High** on regime and catalogue facts.

### National seclusion/restraint statistics: read directly from the primary Regulatory Report

The national statistics vehicle the issue calls the "Director of Mental Health Annual Report" is now the **Office of the Director of Mental Health and Addiction Services: Regulatory Report**; the 2023/24 edition covers 1 July 2023 – 30 June 2024, spans the Mental Health Act 1992, the IDCCR Act 2003 and the Misuse of Drugs Act 1975, includes seclusion, compulsory treatment orders and ECT, and carries a publication date of **31 March 2026** — a ~21-month lag that the Director's foreword explicitly acknowledges ("the release of this report has been delayed again … First, the data is complex. Second, some regulatory data are still reported to the Ministry via manual processes") [MoH publication page](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/office-of-the-director-of-mental-health-and-addiction-services-regulatory-report-2023-to-2024); [PDF, p. iv](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).

The primary figures, read directly from the report (all sourced to PRIMHD data extracted 10 June 2025, plus manual data from four districts):

- **Adult inpatient services (excluding forensic/ID/youth):** 7,042 people were accommodated over 220,898 bed nights; **561 people (8%) were secluded**, across **1,050 seclusion events** (1.9 events per person on average). The national rate was **17.9 people secluded per 100,000 population** and **33.6 seclusion events per 100,000**; the average event lasted **23.4 hours** (down from 27.5 the prior year) [PDF, pp. 20, 24–26](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).
- **Across all inpatient services (adult, forensic, ID, youth): 755 people experienced at least one seclusion event** (69% male, 31% female) [PDF, p. 20](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). *(Note: The Spinoff attributes "755 people" to 2024/25; the primary report is unambiguous that 755 is the 2023/24 all-services figure — treat the Spinoff's year label as a slip.)*
- **The Māori inequity, verbatim:** "The rate of seclusion for Māori in adult inpatient services was **62.8 people per 100,000** … Māori were **6.6 times more likely to be secluded than non-Māori, who had a rate of 9.6 people per 100,000**." Pacific peoples were secluded at 15.7 and other ethnicities at 9.0 per 100,000. Māori made up **55% of all adults secluded** against **17% of the general population** [PDF, pp. 20, 27–28](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).
- **The inequity is entrenching in relative terms.** Since 2009 (when seclusion-reduction policy began), the total number of people secluded in adult inpatient services fell **48%**, and total seclusion hours fell **73%** — but the number of **Māori secluded fell only 18%** over the same period [PDF, pp. 18–19, 29](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). Overall seclusion continued to fall year-on-year (people down 20%, hours down 24% vs 2022/23) [PDF, pp. 18, 20](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).
- **Compulsory treatment orders (correction to the issue's framing).** 5.8% of Māori accessed specialist MH/addiction services vs 2.8% of non-Māori; Māori men were the group most likely to be on a CTO. The report's precise multipliers are Māori **vs Pacific and vs other ethnicities**, not vs non-Māori: Māori were **1.9× more likely than Pacific and 2.3× more likely than other ethnicities** to be on a community CTO (s29), and **1.6× / 2.1×** respectively for an inpatient CTO (s30) [PDF, p. 8](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). The earlier "1.6–2.3× more likely to be under CTOs" phrasing conflated these into a Māori-vs-non-Māori comparison it does not support.

The Mental Health and Wellbeing Commission (Te Hiringa Mahara) responded the same day, framing the picture as "deepening inequities in the mental health system" and echoing the 6.6× figure [Te Hiringa Mahara release, 31 Mar 2026](https://livenews.co.nz/2026/03/31/health-deepening-inequities-in-the-mental-health-system-call-for-action/) ([archived](https://web.archive.org/web/20260331040332/https://livenews.co.nz/2026/03/31/health-deepening-inequities-in-the-mental-health-system-call-for-action/)); The Spinoff (citing Commission monitoring) repeats the 6.6× figure [The Spinoff, 16 Jun 2026](https://thespinoff.co.nz/the-bulletin/16-06-2026/our-mental-health-services-are-improving-unless-youre-young-maori-pasifika-or-disabled) ([archived](https://web.archive.org/web/20260629185343/https://thespinoff.co.nz/the-bulletin/16-06-2026/our-mental-health-services-are-improving-unless-youre-young-maori-pasifika-or-disabled)); Te Ao Māori News carries the Commission's Director of Māori Health citing Māori as 17% of the population but 55% of those in seclusion [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/). **Confidence: High — these are now verified against the primary report, not relayed at one remove.** The full seclusion/CTO time series is child #231's job.

### The eight individuals in prolonged seclusion — national context relevant to Ward 10A

The single most consequential thing the block had hidden is in the report's body and **Appendix 3**. The report states that "the analysis of data on the use of seclusion during 2023/24 showed that **eight individuals experienced prolonged and/or frequent periods of seclusion**", raising "significant issues around trauma, dignity and human rights"; the Office of the Director "is undertaking deeper analysis of the circumstances and factors that led to these prolonged or frequent periods of seclusion" and has "commissioned a specific piece of work" on them [PDF, pp. 18, 62](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). Appendix 3 specifies that the **eight individuals are at three Health New Zealand regional facilities in the categories of regional intellectual disability or regional forensic units**, each subject to one of three Acts (Mental Health Act, IDCCR Act, or Criminal Procedure (Mentally Impaired Persons) Act 2003), and the Director "will consider … any areas that require escalation to other agencies" [PDF, p. 62](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf).

Their weight on the totals is extreme (Table A3) [PDF, p. 63](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf):

| Measure | Excluding the 8 | Including the 8 | The 8 alone |
|---|---|---|---|
| People secluded, all services | 755 | 763 | 8 |
| **Seclusion hours, all services** | **59,876** | **105,407** | **45,531 (43%)** |
| Seclusion events, all services | 1,787 | 2,772 | 985 |
| **Seclusion hours, intellectual-disability services** | **15,652** | **49,674** | **34,022** |
| Seclusion events, ID services | 229 | 1,143 | 914 |
| People secluded, ID services | 22 | 27 | 5 |

So **eight people accounted for 43% of all seclusion hours in the New Zealand inpatient system in 2023/24**, and five of them were in intellectual-disability services — where their inclusion more than triples total seclusion hours. Ward 10A is a regional intellectual-disability secure unit, and the Ombudsman found a resident secluded for ~18 months; that makes the Wakari case a close match to the type of prolonged/frequent seclusion problem Appendix 3 describes. It does **not** prove identity: the report anonymises by design, does not name facilities, and ends its data year on 30 June 2024, while the Ombudsman inspection was 17–20 March 2026. The specific resident may fall in the 2024/25 successor-year equivalent, or possibly in the 2023/24 cohort if the seclusion began early enough, but that is unconfirmed. **Confidence: High on the figures and the regime; Medium on the contextual match; Low on any person-level or facility-level identity link until the full 2026 Ward 10A report or a successor Regulatory Report identifies enough timing detail.**

### Corrections and refinements to the issue's starting facts

1. **"Waikato Hospital's new mental-health ward has slipped to 2027" — does not hold as stated.** The cited RNZ article concerns "an assessment and diagnostic centre near the emergency department, providing brief observations and treatment outside the main wards" — a 28-bed general ward, originally expected in the second half of 2026, now expected Q2 2027; the article never mentions mental health [RNZ, Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/659839/waikato-hospital-ward-will-not-be-ready-until-2027-health-minister-says). Waikato's actual mental-health build — the 64-bed, $131.2m adult acute inpatient facility replacing Henry Rongomau Bennett Centre capacity — was reported by Health NZ on 30 April 2026 as weather-tight and expected to open **late December 2026** [Health NZ via Scoop, 30 Apr 2026](https://www.scoop.co.nz/stories/GE2604/S00085/major-milestone-reached-for-waikatos-new-adult-acute-mental-health-facility.htm). That timeline is the operator's own claim; child #233 verifies it. **Confidence in the correction: High** (the article text itself is the evidence).
2. **"The Mental Health Act 1992 governs the units" — incomplete.** Ward 10A is an intellectual-disability compulsory-care unit under the **IDCCR Act 2003** [Ombudsman 2021 report](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/report-unannounced-inspection-ward-10a-and-helensburgh-cottage-wakari-hospital-dunedin); the Regulatory Report explicitly covers both Acts (and, for the prolonged-seclusion cohort, the Criminal Procedure (Mentally Impaired Persons) Act 2003 as well) [PDF, pp. iii, 62](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf). **Confidence: High.**
3. **"Director of Mental Health Annual Report" — renamed.** Now the "Office of the Director of Mental Health and Addiction Services: Regulatory Report" [MoH publication page](https://www.health.govt.nz/publications/office-of-the-director-of-mental-health-and-addiction-services-regulatory-report-2023-to-2024). **Confidence: High.**

### The law in motion

The Mental Health Bill — repealing and replacing the 1992 Act — was introduced **1 October 2024** and reported back by the Health Committee on **17 April 2025** (the Regulatory Report's foreword independently confirms this report-back date) [PDF, p. iv](https://www.health.govt.nz/system/files/2026-03/odmhas-regulatory-report-2023-24.pdf); as of mid-2026 it had not had its second reading, the Commission judges its coercive-practice provisions (including on seclusion) "do not go far enough", and advocates and the Commission warn the stall risks the planned **July 2027** commencement [Te Hiringa Mahara: "New Mental Health Bill — are we there yet?"](https://www.mhwc.govt.nz/news-and-resources/new-mental-health-bill-are-we-there-yet/); [Te Ao Māori News, 1 May 2026](https://www.teaonews.co.nz/2026/05/01/advocates-urge-the-government-to-pass-mental-health-bill/) (which quotes the Minister for Mental Health attributing the delay to legislative congestion). **Confidence: High** on the dates and stage; the Bill's substantive effect on seclusion/restraint is child #235's question.

## What would change this conclusion

- **Publication of the full 2026 Ward 10A report.** Everything on the 2026 Ombudsman findings currently rests on the Ombudsman's release and same-day reporting from it. The full report could refine details (e.g. the medication-administration concern RNZ carried) and would let child #234 test whether Ward 10A can be linked to the Regulatory Report's 2023/24 "eight individuals" or to a successor-year equivalent; it should be re-checked immediately by children #230/#234, and could also falsify the "not yet published" claim at any moment.
- **Evidence of an existing national roll-up.** If the Ombudsman's annual OPCAT reporting, the Human Rights Commission's central-NPM reporting, or Te Hiringa Mahara monitoring already aggregates facility-level inspection findings legibly, the stream's "no legible national picture" premise weakens from "doesn't exist" to "exists but isn't reaching decision-makers" — a different problem. Child #230 must check this first.
- **The successor-year (2024/25) Regulatory Report.** The seclusion figures here end at 30 June 2024, ~21 months before the Ward 10A inspection. The 2024/25 edition (not yet published as of this writing) will show whether the "eight individuals" cohort persisted, grew, or changed composition — and is where a resident secluded through 2024–2026 would land. That is the more current denominator child #231 needs.
- **Verification of agency follow-through.** If Health NZ's relocations, the MoH independent investigation, the Director's "specific piece of work" on the eight individuals, or ward remediation are confirmed by records rather than statements, the claims-vs-confirmed picture changes materially (in either direction).
- **Could not verify:** the full 2026 Ombudsman report text; whether Ward 10A's ~18-month resident can be linked to the report's counted "eight" or to a successor-year equivalent (the report anonymises facilities and individuals by design); the current legislative stage beyond "awaiting second reading" from a primary parliamentary source (bills.parliament.nz returned an application error to non-browser fetches at the time of writing — tooling, not absence; the 17 Apr 2025 report-back date is independently confirmed by the MoH report foreword); which specific facility the relocated Ward 10A patients went to (no attempt was made to trace this at an individual level — see below).
- **A human gate is required here.** This is a maximally sensitive domain: detained disabled people, allegations at the threshold of torture, and live agency responses. Per Constitution Art. III, a person with clinical and/or human-rights authority should review before any synthesis of this stream is treated as settled, and lived-experience/whānau perspectives are essential before anything moves past research.
