# NZ government digital-services spend: what the "not effective" review actually says, and following the money

This is the **discover baseline for Stream #255**: it verifies the starting facts from the 2 July 2026 Public Service Commission (PSC) review, pins the load-bearing numbers to primary sources, distinguishes government *claims* from confirmed outcomes, and frames the fan-out. It is deliberately a survey, not the deep dive — each deep dive is a research child ([#268](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/268), [#269](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/269), [#270](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/270), [#271](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/271)). It is a natural sibling to the OIA open-by-default stream ([#199](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/199)).

## Executive answer

- **The review's charge is real and primary-reported.** A review for the Public Service Commission, released by Commissioner Sir Brian Roche on **Thursday 2 July 2026**, found the digital revamp of government services "is not as effective as it should be" and that investment "so far is fragmented, with duplication and limited coordination"; it found the government's key **Digital Delivery Agency** has "limited influence" over key decisions on funding, design and procurement, and signalled a "reset" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). The review reports Cabinet's own stated view that "the public service had a history of waste, duplication, and poor delivery on technology projects" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). (Note: the issue's cited link uses the path `/news/political/653166/`; the live article is at `/news/politics/653166/` — same story.)
- **Follow the money — but most of the money here is a forecast or an aspiration, not audited actuals.** The two headline figures are both **government claims about the future**, not confirmed spend or savings: (1) the government "could save almost $6 billion in the next 10 years by centralising investment and agencies sharing IT" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds); and (2) at the Digital Delivery Agency's announcement, centralising procurement could save "up to 30 per cent" of a projected "$13 billion technology spend across the public sector in the next five years" — i.e. up to **$3.9 billion** [Reseller News, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.reseller.co.nz/article/4107339/nz-government-to-centralise-digital-investment-and-procurement-under-new-digital-delivery-agency.html); [cfotech, Dec 2025](https://cfotech.co.nz/story/new-government-digital-delivery-agency-to-cut-nzd-spending). The **$13bn/5yr** figure is the closest thing to a stated spend baseline, and it is a *forecast*. There is no single published figure for actual annual government digital/IT spend — quantifying that from Estimates and agency reporting is child [#268](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/268).
- **One programme is held up as the success: IRD.** The review notes "IRD had cracked it with its digital revamp — it cost over a billion dollars" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). Nailing the exact IRD Business Transformation total to a primary source is part of child [#268](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/268).
- **Three failures are confirmed and primary-sourced, and they rhyme.** (a) **Novopay**, the schools payroll — original build ≈$30m, then tens of millions more to stabilise; thousands of staff mis-paid; $6m compensation to schools [Wikipedia: Novopay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novopay); [NZ Herald: schools get $6m compensation](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/novopay-schools-get-6m-compensation/XCDBNFKY3Y6TZGUAIV2BQO6NBA/). (b) **Te Ara Manaaki's Civil Registration (births, deaths, marriages) rebuild** — Internal Affairs terminated it and **wrote off $22.9m**, part of a $300m five-year programme whose second phase was scoped near $150m; DIA declined to state the total cost to taxpayers [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project); [RNZ, 5 Jun 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/563154/internal-affairs-to-have-another-go-at-modernising-births-deaths-and-marriages-registry). (c) **Immigration NZ's Biometric Capability Update** — ~$35m over roughly seven years from 2018, quietly axed in late 2025, now before Parliament's Privileges Committee over whether officials misled MPs; an independent review found MBIE was over-optimistic and ignored assurance warnings [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed); [Immigration NZ, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/biometric-capability-update-project/). Full catalogue is child [#269](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/269).
- **The transparency gap is the through-line.** In each failure the public number arrived late, partially, or only under pressure — DIA "refused to say" the births-deaths-marriages total [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project); the Immigration write-off surfaced via a Privileges Committee process, not routine disclosure [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed). There is no live public register of which major digital projects are on track. Framing an open-by-default standard for digital spend/delivery, tied to [#199](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/199), is child [#271](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/271).

**Overall confidence:** High — the review's findings and the three failures are each primary-sourced or double-sourced. The savings and spend figures are marked throughout as **government claims/forecasts, not audited outcomes** (per the issue's guardrail: a party's statement about itself is a claim, not confirmation). The one thing this baseline cannot yet give is a verified actual-spend denominator — that is deliberately handed to child #268.

## Evidence

### 1. What the review actually says (the PSC review, 2 July 2026)

The review was conducted **for the Public Service Commission** and released by Commissioner **Sir Brian Roche** on Thursday 2 July 2026. Its findings, as reported by RNZ:

- Investment in the digital revamp "so far is fragmented, with duplication and limited coordination" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).
- The key **Digital Delivery Agency** "has 'limited influence' over key decisions on funding, design and procurement" [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).
- The system it must fix is "poorly informed", "has poor coordination", and "is not playing its critical part in giving agencies expert support"; engagement with tech providers is "patchy" and "leadership was lacking" [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).
- Between **August 2025 and February 2026** Cabinet made "a series of decisions aimed at reducing the cost of digital in government"; Cabinet's stated view was that "the public service had a history of waste, duplication, and poor delivery on technology projects" [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).
- Roche said the report "provides a solid base for a reset"; the recommended reset centres on prioritising high-impact projects, repositioning the Digital Delivery Agency as a strategist/broker, and reforming the broader system [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).

**Method note (claims vs confirmation).** These are the review's findings as relayed by RNZ; the full PSC report text was not fetched for this baseline. RNZ is reporting a government-commissioned review — the *findings* are the reviewer's, and the "reset" is a signalled intention, not a delivered outcome. Reading the full PSC report verbatim, and the government's formal response, is folded into this baseline at survey depth and deepened in children #268–#271. **Confidence: High** on the findings as reported; the review is the primary artefact.

### 2. The spend — a $13bn forecast, a ~$6bn savings claim, and no published actuals figure

Two forward-looking numbers dominate the public discussion, and it is important to keep them apart:

- **"Almost $6 billion in the next 10 years"** — the government's claimed saving from "centralising investment and agencies sharing IT" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds).
- **"$13 billion technology spend across the public sector in the next five years"**, of which "up to 30 per cent" (~**$3.9 billion**) is the claimed centralisation saving — stated at the Digital Delivery Agency's announcement on 17 December 2025 [Reseller News, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.reseller.co.nz/article/4107339/nz-government-to-centralise-digital-investment-and-procurement-under-new-digital-delivery-agency.html); [cfotech](https://cfotech.co.nz/story/new-government-digital-delivery-agency-to-cut-nzd-spending); [securitybrief](https://securitybrief.co.nz/story/new-government-digital-delivery-agency-to-cut-nzd-spending).

Both are **the government's own statements about its own programme** — claims and forecasts, not audited actuals or realised savings. The $13bn/5yr figure is the only stated approximation of *spend*, and it is a projection. The primary government announcement (Beehive) is bot-blocked from this environment (fetch ladder exit 3 — tooling, not a dead link; see Sources), so these figures are cited to the government-facing trade press that carried the announcement plus the primary digital.govt.nz establishment page below; a Treasury/Estimates-grounded actual-spend figure is exactly what child [#268](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/268) must produce. **Confidence in "the government said these figures": High; in the figures as realised spend/savings: not assessed — by design.**

**The Digital Delivery Agency itself:** operational **1 April 2026**, sitting **within the Public Service Commission**, with functions transferred from the Department of Internal Affairs, and incorporating the **Government Chief Digital Officer (GCDO)** role; announced by Hon **Judith Collins KC**, Minister for Digitising Government, on 17 December 2025 [digital.govt.nz: Government Digital Delivery Agency established](https://www.digital.govt.nz/news/government-digital-delivery-agency-established); [Reseller News, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.reseller.co.nz/article/4107339/nz-government-to-centralise-digital-investment-and-procurement-under-new-digital-delivery-agency.html). The PSC review's "limited influence" finding is a judgement on *this* agency, months after it stood up.

**The success case:** the review singles out IRD — "IRD had cracked it with its digital revamp — it cost over a billion dollars" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). The exact IRD Business Transformation total (widely reported in the ~$1.5bn range, not verified to primary here) is a child-#268 task; this baseline states only what the review states.

### 3. The failures — three confirmed, and they share a shape

**(a) Novopay (Ministry of Education schools payroll).** New Zealand's most famous IT failure: an Australian-built payroll (Talent2) that, from its first pay run in 2012, mis-paid thousands of school staff — some overpaid, some underpaid, some paid nothing — with tens of thousands of reported errors, prompting ministerial intervention and a shift of the system to full ministry operation [Wikipedia: Novopay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novopay); [Office of the Auditor-General: Novopay and school audits](https://ao.parliament.nz/2014/school-audits/part3.htm). Schools received **$6m in compensation** for the extra work [NZ Herald: Novopay — schools get $6m compensation](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/novopay-schools-get-6m-compensation/XCDBNFKY3Y6TZGUAIV2BQO6NBA/). Costs quoted range from the ~$30m original build to $45m+ in remediation and higher political estimates; pinning a defensible total is child #269. **Confidence: High** that Novopay was a major, costly failure; **Medium** on any single total figure (contested across sources).

**(b) Te Ara Manaaki — the Civil Registration / births, deaths and marriages rebuild (Internal Affairs).** DIA's $300m, five-year "Te Ara Manaaki" programme modernised identity/life-event services in phases; its second-phase **Civil Registration replacement (births, deaths and marriages, ~$150m scope)** ran into trouble and was **terminated**, with the department **writing off $22.9m** in capital costs and remaining in a contract dispute with Australian vendor **DWS** [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024: Internal Affairs terminates BDM IT project](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project); [RNZ, 5 Jun 2025: Internal Affairs to have another go](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/563154/internal-affairs-to-have-another-go-at-modernising-births-deaths-and-marriages-registry). DIA **"refused to say how much the project's failure had cost taxpayers"** beyond the write-off [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project). **Confidence: High** on the $22.9m write-off and the $300m programme scope (both stated in RNZ, sourced from DIA); the *total* cost is undisclosed — a transparency-gap data point for child #271.

**(c) Immigration NZ — the Biometric Capability Update (BCU), MBIE.** Intended to replace Immigration NZ's ageing biometric systems for identity checks at the border, the BCU ran from around 2018, suffered roughly seven years of delays and cost blowouts, and was **quietly terminated in late 2025** at a cost commonly reported around **$35 million** (with a related project reported at ~$8.5m more, and a Budget 2026 write-off reported near $31.2m; BusinessDesk put the figure at $33.4m) [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed); [Biometric Update, Jun 2026](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202606/failed-nz33m-border-biometrics-project-sparks-integrity-investigation); [Immigration NZ, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/biometric-capability-update-project/). An **independent review** found MBIE "was overly optimistic about delivery, failed to follow appropriate governance processes, and ignored repeated warnings from assurance reviews"; MBIE Chief Executive **Nic Blakeley** acknowledged the department's "decisions, oversight and governance failings led to the project's overall failure", and Sir Brian Roche appointed an independent investigator [Immigration NZ, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/biometric-capability-update-project/); [Biometric Update, Jun 2026](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202606/new-zealand-probes-whether-officials-misled-ministers-over-failed-biometrics-project). Separately, Parliament's **Privileges Committee** is examining whether MPs were misled: the head of Immigration NZ did not tell a select committee in March 2026 that the project had already been axed [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed); [Stuff, 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/politics/360996743/mbie-officials-told-mps-they-were-re-working-33m-immigration-upgrade-project-was-already-dead). **Confidence: High** on the failure, the governance findings, and the Privileges Committee process; the *precise* dollar figure varies by source ($31.2m write-off / $33.4m / ~$35m + $8.5m related) and is a child-#269 reconciliation.

**The shared shape** — over-optimism, weak governance, ignored assurance warnings, and cost surfacing late — is the PSC review's implicit thesis about "waste, duplication, and poor delivery" [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). Whether it is a genuine base-rate pattern (vs a few salient cases) is what child #269 must test against a fuller catalogue, including historical cases like INCIS.

### 4. Duplication and the coordination premise

The review's word is "duplication", and the whole centralisation case — and the Digital Delivery Agency's mandate to bring "system leadership, delivery support and capability functions" together and move agencies onto common IT platforms — assumes agencies are building the same things separately [digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/news/government-digital-delivery-agency-established); [Reseller News, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.reseller.co.nz/article/4107339/nz-government-to-centralise-digital-investment-and-procurement-under-new-digital-delivery-agency.html). This baseline does not attempt to prove or size the duplication — that is child [#270](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/270), which must distinguish genuine overlap (e.g. multiple biometric/identity builds) from appropriate agency-specific federation, and assess whether an agency the PSC review itself says has "limited influence" over funding/design/procurement is positioned to consolidate anything. **Confidence: High** that duplication is the government's and reviewer's stated diagnosis; **not assessed** here as a measured fact.

### 5. The transparency gap (ties to #199)

Across all three failures, the public cost picture was thin or late: DIA declined to state the births-deaths-marriages total [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project); the Immigration write-off and the misleading-MPs question surfaced through a Privileges Committee process rather than routine reporting [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed); and even the "how much does government spend on digital" question has no single published answer. New Zealand has no equivalent of a public, project-level IT dashboard with live status ratings. Framing what *should* be open-by-default — business cases, Treasury Gateway assurance ratings, project status, cost-to-date, write-offs — and connecting it to the OIA open-by-default dataset shortlist ([#199](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/199), [open-by-default shortlist](./open-by-default-dataset-shortlist.md)) is child [#271](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/271). **Confidence: High** that the specific opacity instances above are real; the systemic characterisation is child #271's to substantiate.

## What would change this conclusion

- **The full PSC review text and the government's formal response.** This baseline rests on RNZ's reporting of the review. The full report could sharpen or soften the "not effective" framing, quantify the duplication, or name specific programmes — it should be fetched and read verbatim by child #268/#270, and the government's own response read as a claim, not confirmation.
- **A verified actual-spend figure.** The only stated spend number ($13bn over five years) is a forecast, and the savings figures ($6bn/$3.9bn) are aspirations. If Treasury Estimates or GCDO reporting show a materially different actual annual spend, the "follow the money" picture changes. Child #268 is the denominator.
- **Reconciliation of the failure figures.** The Immigration figure is quoted as $31.2m (write-off) / $33.4m (BusinessDesk) / ~$35m (+$8.5m related); Novopay's total is genuinely contested ($30m–$100m+ depending on what's counted). If the reconciled totals are much lower (or higher), the "waste" narrative shifts. Child #269.
- **Evidence that duplication is small or already federated appropriately.** If the "duplication" is mostly legitimate agency-specific systems rather than redundant builds, the centralisation savings case weakens from "obvious" to "unproven". Child #270 must measure, not assume.
- **Could not verify:** the full PSC report text (only RNZ's account); the primary Beehive announcement (bot-blocked, exit 3 — tooling, not dead; a Wayback snapshot or residential egress would confirm the $13bn/$3.9bn primary wording); the exact IRD Business Transformation total; a single official figure for total annual government digital/IT spend (I believe none is routinely published — that belief is itself child #271's to confirm or refute).
- **No human gate is triggered** by domain sensitivity (this is public spending, not vulnerable-people data, and no personal data appears). But any *external-facing* synthesis or recommendation from this stream is an always-human trigger per [docs/STREAMS.md](../../../docs/STREAMS.md), and the load-bearing savings claims are government self-assessments that a steward should treat sceptically.
