# NZ government digital-services spend: what the "not effective" review says, and following the money

This is the **discover baseline for Stream #255**. It verifies the starting facts from the **2 July 2026 Public Service Commission (PSC) rapid review / Digital Reset Plan**, pins the headline money figures to primary government sources, distinguishes government *claims* from confirmed outcomes, and frames the fan-out. It is deliberately a survey, not the deep dive: each deep dive is a research child ([#268](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/268), [#269](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/269), [#270](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/270), [#271](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/271)). It is a natural sibling to the OIA open-by-default stream ([#199](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/199)).

## Executive answer

- **The review's charge is real and primary-sourced.** Public Service Commissioner Sir Brian Roche released the rapid review on **2 July 2026**; the PSC summary says the overall digital-delivery system is underperforming, and lists key findings including fragmented digital investment, duplication, limited coordination, weak system-level prioritisation, and limited Government Digital Delivery Agency (GDDA) influence over funding, design, and procurement decisions [PSC, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/rapid-review-proposes-reset-for-digital-delivery). The PDF says Cabinet's August 2025-February 2026 decisions reflected concern about waste, duplication, and poor technology-project delivery across the public service [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf).
- **The headline money figures are government forecasts or savings claims, not audited outcomes.** The Beehive release establishing the GDDA says a centralised approach "could save up to 30 percent" on a projected **$13 billion** five-year public-sector technology spend, or **$3.9b** in potential savings, and says the GDDA/GCDO functions would move to the PSC on **1 April 2026** [Beehive, 16 Dec 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/public-service-digital-transformation-accelerates). RNZ separately reports the review/government position as a potential saving of "almost $6 billion in the next 10 years" from centralising investment and agencies sharing IT [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). These figures should be treated as the government's stated forecast/claim unless child #268 verifies actual spending and realised savings from budget and agency sources.
- **The review itself supports a transparency-gap lead, but this baseline does not prove every negative claim.** The Digital Reset Plan says the system lacks complete, timely information; that it was hard to get fundamental metrics to judge agency technology-investment performance; that there is limited understanding of baseline technology spend versus project funding; and that there is limited central visibility of what agencies are actually spending on technology [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). It recommends consistent reporting of technology cost, performance outcomes, and benefits across agencies [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). I did not verify a current public project-level dashboard or a single public actual-spend denominator; that absence should be treated as a **research lead for #268/#271**, not as fully proven here.
- **Three failures are enough to frame, not enough to prove a base-rate pattern.** Novopay, Te Ara Manaaki's Civil Registration rebuild, and Immigration NZ's Biometric Capability Update are documented failures or terminated projects, but the exact failure pattern and cost reconciliation belong in child #269. This baseline treats their common theme as a hypothesis to test: late/thin public cost visibility, governance/assurance issues in at least some cases, and difficulty reconciling total cost from public material.

**Overall confidence:** High on the PSC review findings, the Beehive/RNZ headline figures as *claims*, the GDDA establishment facts, and the existence of the three named failures. Medium on the systemic transparency characterisation, because it is supported by the PSC review but still needs a publication-set search across Treasury, PSC/GDDA, Estimates, and agency reporting. Not assessed here: actual annual government digital/IT spend, realised savings, or a reconciled failure catalogue.

## Evidence

### 1. What the PSC review says

The PSC page released on **2 July 2026** says Sir Brian Roche commissioned a rapid review into digital delivery across government, and that the review found the overall system was underperforming [PSC, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/rapid-review-proposes-reset-for-digital-delivery). The PSC's listed key findings are that digital investment is fragmented with duplication and limited coordination; system-level prioritisation is not strong enough; the GDDA has limited influence over funding, design, and procurement; funding and approval processes fit large capital projects better than fast-moving technology investments; provider engagement is inconsistent; and stronger system leadership is needed [PSC, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/rapid-review-proposes-reset-for-digital-delivery).

The PDF's executive summary adds the review's diagnosis that the system is poorly informed about the nature and value of technology investment across government, has poor coordination for trade-offs in complex projects, and has a weak central digital function focused more on functional than strategic work [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). It recommends three tracks: reset prioritisation, reposition the GDDA as central strategist/technical authority/commercial broker while agencies remain accountable for delivery, and reform system settings such as risk appetite, funding model, digital leadership/accountability, mandates, and reporting [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf).

RNZ independently reported the same release on 2 July 2026, including the "not effective" framing, the fragmentation/duplication/limited coordination finding, the GDDA "limited influence" finding, and Roche's statement that the review provides a base for a reset [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). The issue's original RNZ path used `/news/political/653166/`; the live story resolves at `/news/politics/653166/`.

### 2. The spend and savings figures

The primary Beehive announcement, **Public service digital transformation accelerates**, says the government would progressively establish logical digital groupings of agencies to share common IT platforms, and that centralised digital investment and procurement could save up to 30% on a projected **$13 billion** five-year public-sector technology spend, creating potential savings of **$3.9b** [Beehive, 16 Dec 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/public-service-digital-transformation-accelerates). The same release says the new GDDA would sit at the PSC, with GCDO functions transitioning from DIA to PSC on **1 April 2026** [Beehive, 16 Dec 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/public-service-digital-transformation-accelerates).

The digital.govt.nz establishment page corroborates the transition mechanics: from 1 April 2026, the GDDA would be a new functional agency within the PSC, taking over digital functions from DIA and incorporating the GCDO role [digital.govt.nz](https://www.digital.govt.nz/news/government-digital-delivery-agency-established). Reseller News also reported the $13bn/30%/$3.9bn Beehive figures and the 1 April transition [Reseller News, 17 Dec 2025](https://www.reseller.co.nz/article/4107339/nz-government-to-centralise-digital-investment-and-procurement-under-new-digital-delivery-agency.html).

The **almost $6bn over 10 years** saving is reported by RNZ in its story on the PSC review [RNZ, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/653166/digital-revamp-of-government-services-not-effective-review-finds). I did not find that exact 10-year figure in the PSC web summary or the PDF during this baseline check, so the current support for that number is RNZ's reporting of the review/government position; child #268 should pin it to Cabinet, Budget, PSC, or GDDA material before using it as a load-bearing denominator.

### 3. The transparency and reporting gap

The strongest primary evidence for a transparency problem is inside the PSC Digital Reset Plan, not in a proved absence of a public dashboard. The plan says the system is fragmented and lacks complete, timely information; says it was hard to get fundamental metrics to judge technology-investment performance; says existing reports/surveys were thinly populated and focused on inputs rather than value or impact; and says there is limited central visibility of what agencies are actually spending on technology, including spend hidden in budgets and financial reporting [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). Its reform recommendations include consistent reporting of technology cost, performance outcomes, and benefits across agencies and, in the proposed GDDA operating model, managing internal reporting on digital spend and performance and considering reinstated public targets [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf).

Treasury's public Gateway material shows that New Zealand has an assurance process for major investments, including digital/ICT-enabled projects, and that Treasury publishes lessons/findings from Gateway reports at aggregate level [Treasury Gateway review findings](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/information-and-services/public-sector-leadership/investment-management/investment-management-system-reporting/gateway-review-findings); [Treasury Gateway Review 1 workbook, Nov 2023](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2023-11/gateway-review-1-nov23.pdf). Older Treasury major-projects releases show project-level dashboards existed for significant investment projects in 2015/16, including budget/timeline/benefits confidence ratings and actual expenditure fields [Treasury Major Projects Performance Information Release, Jul 2016](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2016-09/mppr-jul16.pdf). This baseline did not verify whether an equivalent current public, digital-specific live dashboard exists; child #271 should test that directly across Treasury, PSC/GDDA, digital.govt.nz, data.govt.nz, and agency publication sets.

### 4. Three failures to carry into the failure-catalogue child

**Novopay (schools payroll).** The Beehive release for the 2013 Ministerial Inquiry says the inquiry produced a 113-page report and found many factors over many years contributed to the Novopay problems [Beehive, 4 Jun 2013](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/ministerial-inquiry-report-novopay-released). The Office of the Auditor-General says the Ministry introduced Novopay in August 2012 and that problems were evident from the first pay round, with about 5,000 staff underpaid, about 700 overpaid, and 15 not paid at all; the Auditor-General also reported audit effects for schools [OAG, 2014](https://ao.parliament.nz/2014/school-audits/part3.htm). Schools received a $6m compensation package for additional administration costs, reported contemporaneously by the NZ Herald and tied to the 2013 technical review response [NZ Herald, 19 Mar 2013](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/novopay-schools-get-6m-compensation/XCDBNFKY3Y6TZGUAIV2BQO6NBA/). This baseline does **not** settle Novopay's total cost; child #269 should use the Ministerial Inquiry report, OAG material, Ministry releases, and appropriation/annual-report data to reconcile build, operating, remediation, settlement, and school-administration costs.

**Te Ara Manaaki / Civil Registration (births, deaths and marriages).** RNZ reported that DIA terminated the Civil Registration replacement part of Te Ara Manaaki, that the broader programme was a $300m five-year modernisation effort, that the Civil Registration phase was scoped near $150m, and that DIA declined to state how much the failure cost taxpayers beyond the write-off [RNZ, 3 Apr 2024](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/513360/internal-affairs-terminates-birth-death-and-marriage-registrations-it-project). RNZ later reported DIA wrote off **$22.9m** and was preparing another attempt at modernising the register [RNZ, 5 Jun 2025](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/563154/internal-affairs-to-have-another-go-at-modernising-births-deaths-and-marriages-registry). Confidence is High on the termination and $22.9m write-off as reported from DIA-facing material; total cost remains unverified.

**Immigration NZ Biometric Capability Update (BCU).** Immigration NZ says an independent review of the BCU found MBIE was overly optimistic about delivery, failed to follow appropriate governance processes, and ignored repeated assurance warnings; MBIE Chief Executive Nic Blakeley accepted that decisions, oversight, and governance failings led to the project's overall failure [Immigration NZ, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/about-us/news-centre/biometric-capability-update-project/). RNZ reported the project had been axed after roughly seven years and about **$35m**, and that Parliament's Privileges Committee was considering whether MPs were misled because the head of Immigration NZ did not tell a select committee in March 2026 that the project had already been axed [RNZ, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/politics/616552/immigration-new-zealand-head-didn-t-tell-select-committee-35m-it-project-had-been-axed). Biometric Update and Stuff also reported the integrity/Privileges Committee angle and differing dollar figures around $33m-$35m [Biometric Update, 2026](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202606/failed-nz33m-border-biometrics-project-sparks-integrity-investigation); [Stuff, 2026](https://www.stuff.co.nz/politics/360996743/mbie-officials-told-mps-they-were-re-working-33m-immigration-upgrade-project-was-already-dead). Confidence is High on the failure/governance findings and Medium on the exact total pending child #269.

### 5. Duplication and the coordination premise

The duplication premise is the government's and reviewer's diagnosis, not a measured conclusion from this baseline. The Beehive release says agencies will be grouped to share common IT platforms, reducing duplication and improving efficiency [Beehive, 16 Dec 2025](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/public-service-digital-transformation-accelerates). The PSC page says digital investment is fragmented, with duplication and limited coordination [PSC, 2 Jul 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/rapid-review-proposes-reset-for-digital-delivery). The PDF gives disguised examples including a large agency pursuing its own payments system rather than reusing an existing mature system, multiple agencies pursuing digital-identity initiatives with limited coordination, and related agencies tendering independently for service-delivery modules before the process was stopped [PSC Digital Reset Plan 2026](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/Digital-Reset-Plan-2026.pdf). Child #270 should test whether these examples represent material, current duplication and whether the GDDA has enough mandate over funding/design/procurement to fix it.

## What would change this conclusion

- **A primary source for the almost $6bn/10yr figure.** The $13bn/$3.9bn figures are pinned to Beehive; the almost-$6bn figure is currently pinned to RNZ reporting. A Cabinet paper, Budget document, PSC/GDDA document, or ministerial release could raise confidence and clarify whether it is additional to, or a restatement of, the $3.9bn five-year claim.
- **A verified actual-spend denominator.** The PSC review says baseline visibility is limited, but child #268 needs to quantify annual actual digital/IT spend from Estimates, agency annual reports, procurement data, GDDA/GCDO reporting, and Treasury material.
- **A current-publication map for project status and assurance.** Treasury has aggregate Gateway findings and older public major-project dashboards. If child #271 finds a current, public, project-level digital/ICT dashboard with status ratings, cost-to-date, assurance state, write-offs, and benefits tracking, the transparency-gap framing should narrow.
- **Reconciliation of failure figures.** Immigration figures vary across reporting; Novopay's total depends on whether build, 10-year service envelope, remediation, compensation, settlement, and ongoing payroll remediation are counted; DIA disclosed a $22.9m write-off but not a total cost in the cited reporting. Child #269 should reconcile these.
- **Evidence that duplication is small or appropriate.** If #270 finds the PSC's duplication examples are isolated, dated, or mostly legitimate agency-specific federation, the centralisation-savings logic weakens.
- **No human gate is triggered** by domain sensitivity in this baseline because it uses public-spending material and no personal data. Any external-facing synthesis or recommendation from the stream remains subject to the stream human gates in [docs/STREAMS.md](../../../docs/STREAMS.md).
