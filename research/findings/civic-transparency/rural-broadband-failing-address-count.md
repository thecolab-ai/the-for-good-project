# Issue #681 is answered by the existing rural broadband orderability-gap finding

## Executive answer

- This finding is a reconciliation note, not a second independent count: issue #681 asks which rural addresses fail a defined usable broadband standard, and issue #586 asked which rural addresses still cannot order usable broadband at a clear service standard before the already-merged #586 finding answered the same practical question for Stream #433 [GitHub issue #681](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/681), [GitHub issue #586](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/586), [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md).
- I did not find a genuine public-data delta that changes #586's answer: New Zealand still has public coverage, performance, price, stop-sell, and programme evidence, but no public address-level dataset joining live orderability, speed, latency, data allowance, price, and reliability into one failing-address count [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- The best public denominator remains the Commerce Commission's about 352,000 rural addresses outside local fibre-company fibre areas, or 15% of New Zealand addresses; applying the Commission's 95-96% rural 4G FWA coverage to that denominator gives only a narrow 14,000-18,000 address footprint-risk band, not a count of addresses failing usable broadband [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).
- The main correction to the earlier draft of this PR is factual: the April 2025 MBNZ rural-speed table reports ADSL at 10/1 Mbps, VDSL at 46/13 Mbps, and 4G FWA at 56/21 Mbps; these figures support the same conclusion that ADSL fails a 50/10 working standard while averages for VDSL and 4G FWA do not identify every address that fails speed, latency, or reliability [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Measuring Broadband New Zealand programme](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - confidence is High that this issue duplicates the already-merged #586 question and that no public all-criteria address count is identified in the cited official sources, but Low for any inferred failing-address number beyond the narrow 4G FWA footprint arithmetic.

## Evidence

### Reconciliation with the earlier finding

Issue #586 asked which rural addresses still cannot order usable broadband, while issue #681 asks which rural addresses fail a defined usable broadband standard [GitHub issue #586](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/586), [GitHub issue #681](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/681). Those are not materially different when "usable" is defined as orderable fixed-location service with speed, latency, data, price, and reliability tests [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md). The #586 finding already used that working standard, identified the Commerce Commission's 352,000-address rural denominator, tested the same public coverage and programme sources, and concluded that public data can show risk classes but not a definitive address list [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md).

I therefore treat #586 as the controlling answer for #681 rather than restating the same research as new work. The answer remains negative: public sources do not publish a reproducible count of rural addresses failing a combined standard of live orderability, at least 50 Mbps down and 10 Mbps up, unlimited or effectively uncapped data, video-call-usable latency, urban-comparable price, and practical reliability [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).

**Claim confidence: High** that the two questions overlap; **Medium** that the negative answer remains the best public answer, because it depends on absence across public sources rather than a single affirmative dataset.

### What remains countable from public sources

The Commerce Commission says its rural broadband analysis covers about 352,000 addresses outside areas where local fibre-company fibre is available, or 15% of New Zealand addresses [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report gives overlapping rural technology coverage of about 50% copper, 95-96% 4G FWA, 16% 5G FWA, 71% WISP non-cellular FWA, and about 1% HFC or rural WISP fibre [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

That supports only limited arithmetic. Applying 95-96% 4G FWA coverage to 352,000 rural addresses leaves about 14,000-18,000 addresses outside that one technology footprint, but this is not a failing-address count because copper, WISP fixed wireless, 5G FWA, satellite, HFC, or rural fibre may overlap, and because coverage is not live orderability [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

The Commission's Connectivity Map reinforces that limit: it is provider-supplied, updated annually rather than live, assigns wireless parcel coverage when at least 50% of a parcel is inside a coverage area, excludes satellite because obstacles can block the signal, omits some providers, hides provider names, and can differ from broadbandmap.nz or provider websites [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/). Those caveats prevent using the public map as a live address-level pass/fail dataset for a usable broadband standard [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/).

**Claim confidence: High** that public coverage data is not a live all-criteria orderability count; **Low** for any inferred failing-address number beyond the narrow 4G FWA footprint band.

### Risk classes still lack national public counts

The address classes most likely to fail remain the same as #586 identified: outside terrestrial footprints, inside nominal wireless coverage but blocked by stop-sell or capacity, dependent on ADSL or other weak/congested technology, technically served but facing price or installation barriers, or reliant on satellite where line of sight or equipment cost makes service impractical [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md), [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).

NIFF's Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme proves the stop-sell class exists: it says demand on rural broadband towers caused some towers to go into stop-sell, where no new users can be supported [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/). NIFF's January-June 2025 connectivity update defines stop-sell as a customer having mobile network coverage but being unable to access a new broadband connection because the tower has no spare capacity, and reports upgraded stop-sells including 3,517 in Waikato, 1,935 in Bay of Plenty, and 2,320 in Canterbury [NIFF Connectivity Update, Jan-Jun 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/June-2025-Connectivity-Update-FINAL_new.pdf). That is useful supporting evidence for the orderability-failure class, but it is not a current national count of remaining stop-sell addresses [NIFF Connectivity Update, Jan-Jun 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/June-2025-Connectivity-Update-FINAL_new.pdf), [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md).

The MBNZ performance evidence also supports risk classes rather than counts. In April 2025 MBNZ testing, rural ADSL averaged 10 Mbps down and 1 Mbps up, VDSL averaged 46 Mbps down and 13 Mbps up, and 4G FWA averaged 56 Mbps down and 21 Mbps up [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf). The same report found only 12% of measured ADSL households could reliably stream one UHD Netflix stream and did not have enough volunteers for WISP non-cellular FWA performance results, so public performance evidence identifies weak technologies but cannot map every failing address [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf), [Commerce Commission Measuring Broadband New Zealand programme](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/monitoring-new-zealands-broadband/).

The affordability evidence has the same limit. The Commission's June 2025 weighted price table found rural households paid $85.77 per month for broadband on average versus $72.65 in urban areas, a $13.12 or 18% rural premium, but the public table does not identify which addresses or households cannot afford service [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).

**Claim confidence: Medium.** The risk classes are well supported by official sources, but their national address counts are not public.

The earlier #586 finding records successful live CKAN searches for those terms on 6 July 2026 and found no catalogue package that filled the gap, so I cite #586 for the catalogue-search result and treat this PR's blocked access as a tooling limitation under ADR-0006 [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md).

**Claim confidence: Low** for my direct catalogue-access result, because it is environment-dependent; **Medium** for the broader no-public-count conclusion, because it rests on ComCom, NIFF, and the existing #586 catalogue checks.

## What would change this conclusion

- A public Commerce Commission, MBIE, or NIFF table joining address or parcel identifiers to live orderability, technology, stop-sell status, expected speed, measured or modelled latency, data allowance, monthly price, setup cost, outage history, and satellite obstruction status would replace both #586 and #681's evidence-gap answer with a count of failing addresses [Commerce Commission Connectivity Map](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/regulated-industries/telecommunications/monitoring-the-telecommunications-market/telecommunications-connectivity-map/), [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- A published RUS outcome table with applications, eligible addresses, approved grants, completed connections, and post-install performance would sharpen the residual no-service or poor-service count; the public page confirms address-level applications existed, but not a public outcome count [NIFF Remote Users Scheme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/remote-users-scheme-rus/).
- Provider or agency publication of remaining tower stop-sells, failed installs, and capacity-constrained service areas would change the orderability finding because NIFF proves the class exists but reports upgrades rather than a current residual count [NIFF Rural Capacity Upgrades Programme](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/rural-capacity-upgrades-programme/), [NIFF Connectivity Update, Jan-Jun 2025](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/wp-content/uploads/June-2025-Connectivity-Update-FINAL_new.pdf).
- A formal NZ service standard could change the threshold: the 50/10 unlimited component here comes from the Canadian comparator used in #586, while the price, latency, orderability, and reliability parts are a research construction from NZ monitoring evidence [Rural broadband orderability gap finding](rural-broadband-orderability-gap.md), [Commerce Commission 2025 Telecommunications Monitoring Report](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/2025-Telecommunications-Monitoring-Report-29-June-2026.pdf).
- I could not verify live provider orderability, provider-specific address results from broadbandmap.nz or RSP order portals, Starlink obstruction results, household income-linked affordability, or outage histories without using individual addresses or non-public data. Those fields should be handled through an authorised agency data-sharing process or privacy-preserving aggregate publication, not by publishing identifiable rural household examples.
