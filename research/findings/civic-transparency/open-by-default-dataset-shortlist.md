# Open-by-default candidates are disclosure-log metadata, advice-title registers, richer procurement data, workforce/contractor details, council project/service data, and surveillance-technology registers

## Executive answer

- This finding **answers issue #203** by consuming both sibling findings that #203 depended on, now merged to `main`: the repeat-request analysis (#201) and the OIA cost/save analysis (#202). It maps candidate open-by-default datasets against what New Zealand already proactively releases, uses #201's committed topic clusters to rank them, and applies #202's marginal-cost rule instead of leaving cost/save pending [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [issue #203](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/203).
- The shortlist targets **gaps in an existing proactive-release system**, not a claim that New Zealand has no system: the 2011 Declaration says government should actively release high-value public data, NZDIMP says government data should be open unless lawful protection grounds apply, NZGOAL supplies reuse/licensing guidance for open data releases, Cabinet material must normally be published within 30 business days, and PSC/Ombudsman guidance already encourages proactive release of OIA responses [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy), [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles), [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal), [DPMC CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements), [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release), [Ombudsman proactive-release guide](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/proactive-release-good-practices-proactive-release-official-information).
- The candidate set is **central OIA disclosure-log metadata**, **all-agency ministerial/agency advice-title registers**, **richer procurement award/contract/spend data**, **workforce/contractor detail below the current aggregate level**, **council project/service/rates-to-service data**, and **public CCTV/ANPR/surveillance-technology registers**. #201's committed FYI sample found recurring public-interest topics that cluster around spending/budgets, contracts/consultants, staffing/headcount, ministerial briefings, roading/transport, education, and health — the reusable-record categories these candidates cover [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [council repeat-demand finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-data-questions.md).
- **A key scoping lesson from #201:** OIA volume is dominated by a few agencies whose requests skew to individualised/operational records — Police completed ≈42% of 2025 central-government OIA volume and the top three ≈66% — so open-by-default publishing of *reusable* datasets addresses the recurring public-interest topics, not that high-volume personal-record tail. That is consistent with MoJ's own point in #202 that high-volume-agency savings come from cheaper service channels (e.g. camera footage), not from open-by-default publication of reusable public records [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md).
- **Cost/save now uses #202's marginal-cost rule, not a placeholder:** the published FY2024/25 baseline is about $183.6m, and Tregaskis Brown's model implies every 1,000 measured avoidable routine requests is worth about $0.35m of gross response cost avoided and every 1,000 avoidable complex requests about $3.53m; an illustrative "one avoided routine repeat per published response" scenario across the 5,417 responses agencies published in that year is about $1.91m. But #202 is explicit that public data does not measure how many repeats any given dataset would avoid, so these are scenario arithmetic and a per-dataset dollar estimate is still not supportable [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md).
- The highest-confidence "already open but incomplete" cases are procurement and workforce: GETS award notices and PSC workforce/contractor aggregates are already published, but recurring public questions often need contract documents, variations, supplier spend, org charts, role/pay bands, and project/vendor context that current aggregate releases do not expose [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data), [Procurement Rule 32](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/government-procurement-framework/government-procurement-rules/awarding-the-contract/contract-award-notice/), [PSC workforce size](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/workforce-data/public-sector-composition/workforce-size).
- The highest-sensitivity case is surveillance technology: public bodies and councils can publish equipment/policy/retention/location-level registers without publishing footage or operationally sensitive details, but CCTV/ANPR involves personal information and should require privacy, safety, and enforcement-risk review before any default-release rule [OPC CCTV guidance](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/), [FYI Christchurch CCTV/ANPR request](https://fyi.org.nz/request/33756-cctv-and-anpr-cameras-signage-and-monitoring-policies), [FYI Hamilton CCTV/ANPR request](https://fyi.org.nz/request/32029-cctv-and-anpr-cameras).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the policy regime and "already open" map are well sourced and High-confidence; the candidate ranking is now grounded in #201's committed FYI topic clusters rather than a single council sample; and the cost/save case uses #202's primary-source marginal-cost rule. It stays Medium overall because #201 shows the *magnitude* of repetition is not pinnable from public metadata and #202 shows the *avoidable-request count* per dataset is not measured, so no candidate gets a per-dataset savings estimate.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This finding narrows issue #203 by mapping candidate open-by-default dataset categories against what is already proactively released in New Zealand and what gap remains for a human steward to weigh at G1 [issue #203](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/203). It is written after the two siblings #203 depended on landed on `main`, so it consumes their evidence rather than deferring to them: #201 measured which bodies and topics recur across the OIA corpus, and #202 estimated the cost baseline and the marginal cost of a response [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md). I treated a "candidate dataset" as a reusable structured release or register that could answer a repeated public-information question without requiring a fresh OIA/LGOIMA request each time, because PSC guidance says proactive release promotes openness and encourages agencies to publish OIA responses and Cabinet material online [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release).

The demand evidence is now grounded in two committed findings, not a single sample. The merged council-data finding found recurring observable demand for costs, rates/fees, contracts/procurement, decision documents, service/asset performance, governance/staff accountability, and CCTV/ANPR surveillance transparency from FYI request titles and Local Democracy Reporting examples [council repeat-demand finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-data-questions.md). The merged #201 national analysis found, from a committed and reproducible FYI title sample, that recurring topics cluster around spending/budgets, roading/transport, education, policy/advice, health/waitlists, correspondence/meetings, statistics ("number of…"), staffing/headcount, contracts/consultants, and ministerial briefings, with 44% of sampled titles matching at least one such cluster [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md). #201 is explicit that the *magnitude* of repetition is Low-confidence from public title metadata (only ~2% of titles strictly recur, and the cleanest signal is cross-body campaigns), so the ranking below uses which topics recur, not a precise repeat rate [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md).

The cost evidence is now the merged #202 result, applied as a marginal-cost rule rather than a placeholder. #202 takes Tregaskis Brown's November 2025 model for the Ministry of Justice as the primary baseline: about $183.6m for FY2024/25, from 158,862 completed requests, with a routine response costed at $352.97 and a complex response at $3,529.66 [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [MoJ stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/). From those unit costs, #202 derives a reusable rule — every 1,000 measured avoidable routine requests is worth about $0.35m and every 1,000 avoidable complex requests about $3.53m of gross response cost avoided — and an illustrative $1.91m scenario for one avoided routine repeat per each of the 5,417 responses agencies published that year [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md). Crucially, #202 says the public data does not record request type, repeat status, or how many later requests a release avoided, so "proactive release saves money" is supportable only as scenario arithmetic; I therefore give each candidate the applicable unit-cost frame and an honest confidence mark, not a per-dataset dollar figure [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [PSC OIA statistics planning](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/OIA-Statistics-Planning-Jan-June-2026.pdf).

One scoping consequence of #202 and #201 together is that the biggest OIA volume is not the open-by-default target. #201 shows volume is concentrated in Police, the Natural Hazards Commission, and Corrections, whose requests plausibly skew to individualised/operational records, and #202 notes MoJ's own view that savings for those high-volume agencies come from cheaper service channels (such as camera footage) rather than open-by-default publication of reusable public records [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md). The candidates below are therefore chosen from the recurring *public-interest, reusable-record* topics, where open-by-default release plausibly substitutes for repeat requests.

### Existing proactive-release regime

New Zealand already has a broad open-data and proactive-release policy stack. Data.govt.nz says Cabinet made the Declaration on Open and Transparent Government in 2011 and that it sets a government-wide approach to actively releasing high-value public data collected and held on behalf of taxpayers [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy). NZDIMP says government-held data and information should be open for public access unless OIA grounds or other limits apply, should be proactively released online without discrimination, should have an authoritative source where possible, and should remain protected where personal, confidential, or classified [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles). Data.govt.nz also says New Zealand adopted the International Open Data Charter in 2017, including principles that data should be open by default, timely and comprehensive, accessible and usable, comparable and interoperable, and useful for governance and citizen engagement [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy).

NZGOAL matters because proactive publication is not enough if the dataset is not legally reusable. Data.govt.nz describes NZGOAL as all-of-government guidance for agencies releasing copyright works and non-copyright or public-domain material for reuse, and says it is guidance to use when releasing open data [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal). The quick NZGOAL guide says NZGOAL seeks to standardise licensing of government copyright works for reuse using Creative Commons licences and recommends "no-known rights" statements for non-copyright material; it also says the purpose is to let people legally reuse government material and contribute to better policy development and financial performance through access, manipulation, and feedback [Quick NZGOAL guide for agencies](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal/quick-nzgoal-guide-for-agencies-january-2015). Therefore each candidate below needs both a release rule and a default licence/reuse statement, with exceptions for material that cannot safely be released under an open licence.

Cabinet material has a specific proactive-release rule. DPMC's CO (23) 4 says all Cabinet and Cabinet committee papers and minutes must be proactively released and published online within 30 business days of final Cabinet decisions, unless there is good reason not to publish all or part of the material or to delay release; the circular also introduced six-monthly reporting of Cabinet-paper release numbers to the Public Service Commission [DPMC CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements). PSC's proactive-release page repeats that Cabinet papers lodged from 1 January 2019, excluding Appointments and Honours papers, are covered by the policy and that normal official-information assessments and due diligence still apply before publication [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release).

The official-information side is also not blank. PSC's OIA statistics cover requests completed, timeliness, OIA-response publication, Ombudsman complaints, extensions, transfers, refusals, and average response time; the latest December 2025 media release says 102 agencies completed 43,183 requests in July-December 2025, the highest volume since reporting began in 2016 [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [PSC Dec 2025 media release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/latest-oia-statistics-released-december-2025). PSC says its OIA statistics now include agencies' proactive publication of responses, and its July-December 2024 all-data CSV showed 3,106 published OIA responses across 57 publishing agencies in that six-month period when fetched with `curl` on 3 July 2026 [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [PSC all-data CSV](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/v_OIAStatisticsAllDataResults-1.csv). The Ombudsman's proactive-release guide says it explains proactive release, describes its benefits, and identifies good practices, complementing PSC and Cabinet Office guidance [Ombudsman proactive-release guide](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/proactive-release-good-practices-proactive-release-official-information).

The Law Commission's R125 chapter on proactive release could not be fetched directly in this environment: `node scripts/fetch.mjs` classified the live URL as BLOCKED, not dead, and `node scripts/archive-cite.mjs` returned a 29 May 2022 Wayback snapshot after a fresh capture failed. I therefore treat it as a contextual policy-history source rather than a load-bearing source for this shortlist [Law Commission R125 ch.12 live](http://r125.publications.lawcom.govt.nz/chapter+12:+proactive+release+and+publication/chapter+12:+the+trend+towards+proactive+release+and+open+data), [archived snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20220529232443/http://r125.publications.lawcom.govt.nz/chapter+12%3A+proactive+release+and+publication/chapter+12%3A+the+trend+towards+proactive+release+and+open+data).

### Evidence-based ordering for a steward

Using #201's recurring topic clusters, #202's marginal-cost rule, existing release infrastructure, and release-risk judgement, the present evidence supports reviewing the candidates in this order: (1) **central OIA disclosure-log metadata**, because it is the measurement layer #202 says is missing and would let the avoidable-repeat count — and therefore every other candidate's saving — finally be sized; (2) **richer procurement/contract/spend data**, because #201's contracts/spending clusters recur, the release infrastructure already exists, and the gap is concrete; (3) **all-agency advice-title registers**, because #201 confirms briefings/advice recur, Treasury and DPMC already prove the workflow, and avoided complex-request cost is high per #202; (4) **workforce/contractor detail**, because #201's staffing cluster recurs and PSC already publishes aggregates, though privacy and employment sensitivity require careful aggregation; (5) **council project/service/rates-to-service data**, because local demand is directly evidenced but it sits under LGOIMA, so #202's central-government cost rule does not transfer and standardisation across 78 authorities is harder; and (6) **surveillance registers**, because the transparency case is real and cross-body-campaign-shaped but the privacy/security review burden is highest and dollar savings are not the main case [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data), [Treasury titles of advice](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/other-official-information/titles-advice-ministers).

This ordering is a G1 discussion aid, not a measured national frequency ranking or a cost/save ranking. #201 is explicit that the magnitude of repetition is not pinnable from public title metadata, and #202 is explicit that the avoidable-request count per dataset is not collected in public statistics, so the order rests on which topics recur and where release infrastructure already exists, not on a per-dataset dollar saving. NZGOAL reuse fit and release-risk judgement then temper the order for sensitive categories [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md), [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal).

## What would change this conclusion

- A response-level disclosure-log dataset (candidate 1) that finally measures how many requests repeat already-published information would replace #202's scenario arithmetic with a measured avoided-cost figure per candidate, and could reorder the shortlist [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md).
- #201 flags, but does not prove, that high-volume agencies skew to individualised requests; agency request-*type* breakdowns could confirm or overturn that, and if some high-volume category is actually reusable public records it would add a candidate [#201 repeat-request finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/repetitive-oia-requests-bodies-topics.md).
- A current central government source proving that any candidate gap is already published in a complete, machine-readable, all-agency way would remove that candidate or narrow it to quality/discoverability rather than release [data.govt.nz](https://data.govt.nz/), [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics).
- TBL's 2026 follow-up research on OIA cost drivers, which #202 says will survey about 40 agencies, could materially change the cost/save case if it measures repeat-request cost directly or shows most cost is unavoidable case-by-case judgement [#202 cost finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/oia-cost-proactive-release.md), [MoJ stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/).
- A legal or domain-expert review could change the surveillance and workforce recommendations, because privacy, employment, law-enforcement, security, and commercial-sensitivity trade-offs are not matters an agent should finally decide [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles), [OPC CCTV guidance](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/).
- I could not verify a current all-agency catalogue of advice-title publication practice, a central register of all agency disclosure logs, a local-government project-level open-data standard, or a public count of how many repeated OIA/LGOIMA requests each candidate category would avoid — #202 confirms that last count is not in public statistics.
