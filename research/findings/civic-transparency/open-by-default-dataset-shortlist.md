# Interim open-by-default candidates are disclosure-log metadata, advice-title registers, richer procurement data, workforce/contractor details, council project/service data, and surveillance-technology registers

## Executive answer

- This is an **interim note for issue #203**, not the final close-out payload: issue #203 explicitly depends on the repeat-request sibling (#201) and cost sibling (#202), and both are still open as of 3 July 2026 [issue #203](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/203), [issue #201](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/201), [issue #202](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/202).
- The eventual shortlist should target **gaps in an existing proactive-release system**, not argue that New Zealand has no system: the 2011 Declaration says government should actively release high-value public data, NZDIMP says government data should be open unless lawful protection grounds apply, NZGOAL supplies reuse/licensing guidance for open data releases, Cabinet material must normally be published within 30 business days, and PSC/Ombudsman guidance already encourages proactive release of OIA responses [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy), [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles), [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal), [DPMC CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements), [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release), [Ombudsman proactive-release guide](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/proactive-release-good-practices-proactive-release-official-information).
- The current candidate set is **central OIA disclosure-log metadata**, **all-agency ministerial/agency advice-title registers**, **richer procurement award/contract/spend data**, **workforce/contractor detail below the current aggregate level**, **council project/service/rates-to-service data**, and **public CCTV/ANPR/surveillance-technology registers**. These map to observed repeat-demand categories in the FYI/council sample and to existing policy-data gaps, but they should be treated as hypotheses until issue #201 completes a corpus-level repeat-topic analysis [council repeat-demand finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-data-questions.md), [issue #201](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/201), [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics).
- The highest-confidence "already open but incomplete" cases are procurement and workforce: GETS award notices and PSC workforce/contractor aggregates are already published, but recurring public questions often need contract documents, variations, supplier spend, org charts, role/pay bands, and project/vendor context that current aggregate releases do not expose [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data), [Procurement Rule 32](https://www.procurement.govt.nz/government-procurement-framework/government-procurement-rules/awarding-the-contract/contract-award-notice/), [PSC workforce size](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/workforce-data/public-sector-composition/workforce-size).
- The highest-sensitivity case is surveillance technology: public bodies and councils can publish equipment/policy/retention/location-level registers without publishing footage or operationally sensitive details, but CCTV/ANPR involves personal information and should require privacy, safety, and enforcement-risk review before any default-release rule [OPC CCTV guidance](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/), [FYI Christchurch CCTV/ANPR request](https://fyi.org.nz/request/33756-cctv-and-anpr-cameras-signage-and-monitoring-policies), [FYI Hamilton CCTV/ANPR request](https://fyi.org.nz/request/32029-cctv-and-anpr-cameras).
- Savings should be framed as **unmeasured mechanisms, not accepted estimates**, until #202 is resolved: the Ministry of Justice says the official-information-system cost baseline is over $183m and that cost drivers are not well understood, but this finding does not independently measure what share of that cost repeat requests cause or what any dataset would save [MoJ stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/), [issue #202](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/202).

**Overall confidence:** Low-Medium - the policy regime and "already open" map are well sourced, and the council repeat-demand categories are auditable, but the stream's national FYI repeat-topic sibling (#201) and cost sibling (#202) are still open, so this is a provisional candidate map rather than a completed national ranking or cost/save answer.

## Evidence

### Scope and method

This interim finding narrows issue #203 by mapping candidate open-by-default dataset categories against what is already proactively released in New Zealand and what gap remains for a human steward to weigh at G1; it should not be read as resolving #203 until the repeat-request and cost siblings are complete [issue #203](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/203). I treated a "candidate dataset" as a reusable structured release or register that could answer a repeated public-information question without requiring a fresh OIA/LGOIMA request each time, because PSC guidance says proactive release promotes openness and encourages agencies to publish OIA responses and Cabinet material online [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release).

The demand evidence is necessarily provisional. The merged council-data finding found recurring observable demand for costs, rates/fees, contracts/procurement, decision documents, service/asset performance, governance/staff accountability, and CCTV/ANPR surveillance transparency from FYI request titles and Local Democracy Reporting examples; it explicitly did not claim a statistically representative national ranking [council repeat-demand finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-data-questions.md). Issue #201 is the sibling meant to test repeat topics across the FYI corpus; while that issue remains open, this finding can only rank policy-fit candidates provisionally [issue #201](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/201).

The cost evidence is also bounded. The Ministry of Justice says Tregaskis Brown modelled official-information-system costs at over $183m in 2024/25 and says the cost drivers are not well understood; issue #202 is the sibling meant to estimate response costs and proactive-release savings, so this finding treats dataset-level savings as Low-confidence mechanisms unless directly supported by an existing publication workflow [MoJ stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/), [issue #202](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/202).

### Existing proactive-release regime

New Zealand already has a broad open-data and proactive-release policy stack. Data.govt.nz says Cabinet made the Declaration on Open and Transparent Government in 2011 and that it sets a government-wide approach to actively releasing high-value public data collected and held on behalf of taxpayers [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy). NZDIMP says government-held data and information should be open for public access unless OIA grounds or other limits apply, should be proactively released online without discrimination, should have an authoritative source where possible, and should remain protected where personal, confidential, or classified [NZDIMP](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/new-zealand-data-and-information-management-principles). Data.govt.nz also says New Zealand adopted the International Open Data Charter in 2017, including principles that data should be open by default, timely and comprehensive, accessible and usable, comparable and interoperable, and useful for governance and citizen engagement [data.govt.nz open-data policy](https://www.data.govt.nz/toolkit/policies/open-data-policy).

NZGOAL matters because proactive publication is not enough if the dataset is not legally reusable. Data.govt.nz describes NZGOAL as all-of-government guidance for agencies releasing copyright works and non-copyright or public-domain material for reuse, and says it is guidance to use when releasing open data [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal). The quick NZGOAL guide says NZGOAL seeks to standardise licensing of government copyright works for reuse using Creative Commons licences and recommends "no-known rights" statements for non-copyright material; it also says the purpose is to let people legally reuse government material and contribute to better policy development and financial performance through access, manipulation, and feedback [Quick NZGOAL guide for agencies](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal/quick-nzgoal-guide-for-agencies-january-2015). Therefore each candidate below needs both a release rule and a default licence/reuse statement, with exceptions for material that cannot safely be released under an open licence.

Cabinet material has a specific proactive-release rule. DPMC's CO (23) 4 says all Cabinet and Cabinet committee papers and minutes must be proactively released and published online within 30 business days of final Cabinet decisions, unless there is good reason not to publish all or part of the material or to delay release; the circular also introduced six-monthly reporting of Cabinet-paper release numbers to the Public Service Commission [DPMC CO (23) 4](https://www.dpmc.govt.nz/publications/co-23-4-proactive-release-cabinet-material-updated-requirements). PSC's proactive-release page repeats that Cabinet papers lodged from 1 January 2019, excluding Appointments and Honours papers, are covered by the policy and that normal official-information assessments and due diligence still apply before publication [PSC proactive release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/guidance/official-information/proactive-release).

The official-information side is also not blank. PSC's OIA statistics cover requests completed, timeliness, OIA-response publication, Ombudsman complaints, extensions, transfers, refusals, and average response time; the latest December 2025 media release says 102 agencies completed 43,183 requests in July-December 2025, the highest volume since reporting began in 2016 [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [PSC Dec 2025 media release](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/news/latest-oia-statistics-released-december-2025). PSC says its OIA statistics now include agencies' proactive publication of responses, and its July-December 2024 all-data CSV showed 3,106 published OIA responses across 57 publishing agencies in that six-month period when fetched with `curl` on 3 July 2026 [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [PSC all-data CSV](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/assets/DirectoryFile/v_OIAStatisticsAllDataResults-1.csv). The Ombudsman's proactive-release guide says it explains proactive release, describes its benefits, and identifies good practices, complementing PSC and Cabinet Office guidance [Ombudsman proactive-release guide](https://www.ombudsman.parliament.nz/resources/proactive-release-good-practices-proactive-release-official-information).

The Law Commission's R125 chapter on proactive release could not be fetched directly in this environment: `node scripts/fetch.mjs` classified the live URL as BLOCKED, not dead, and `node scripts/archive-cite.mjs` returned a 29 May 2022 Wayback snapshot after a fresh capture failed. I therefore treat it as a contextual policy-history source rather than a load-bearing source for this shortlist [Law Commission R125 ch.12 live](http://r125.publications.lawcom.govt.nz/chapter+12:+proactive+release+and+publication/chapter+12:+the+trend+towards+proactive+release+and+open+data), [archived snapshot](https://web.archive.org/web/20220529232443/http://r125.publications.lawcom.govt.nz/chapter+12%3A+proactive+release+and+publication/chapter+12%3A+the+trend+towards+proactive+release+and+open+data).

### Provisional ordering for a steward

If the steward needs an interim discussion order before #201 and #202 land, the present evidence supports reviewing the candidates in this order: (1) central OIA disclosure-log metadata, because it would create a measurement layer for repeat OIA friction; (2) richer procurement/contract/spend data, because the release infrastructure already exists and the gap is concrete; (3) all-agency advice-title registers, because Treasury and DPMC already prove the workflow; (4) council project/service/rates-to-service data, because local demand is directly evidenced but standardisation is harder; (5) workforce/contractor detail, because public interest is clear but privacy and employment sensitivity require careful aggregation; and (6) surveillance registers, because the transparency case is real but the privacy/security review burden is highest [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics), [MBIE procurement open data](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/cross-government-functions/new-zealand-government-procurement-and-property/open-data), [Treasury titles of advice](https://www.treasury.govt.nz/publications/other-official-information/titles-advice-ministers), [council repeat-demand finding](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/blob/main/research/findings/civic-transparency/council-data-questions.md), [OPC CCTV guidance](https://www.privacy.org.nz/resources-and-learning/a-z-topics/privacy-and-cctv/).

This ordering is intentionally not a final shortlist, a national frequency ranking, or a cost/save ranking. It is a provisional review order using policy fit, existing release infrastructure, observed repeat-demand examples, NZGOAL reuse fit, and release-risk judgement; issue #201 could move categories up or down if it finds a different national pattern, and issue #202 could change the cost/save priority [issue #201](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/201), [issue #202](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/202), [NZGOAL](https://www.data.govt.nz/manage-data/policies/nzgoal).

## What would change this conclusion

- A completed issue #201 corpus analysis showing different nationally dominant repeat topics would change the shortlist order and could add or remove categories [issue #201](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/201).
- A current central government source proving that any candidate gap is already published in a complete, machine-readable, all-agency way would remove that candidate or narrow it to quality/discoverability rather than release [data.govt.nz](https://data.govt.nz/), [PSC OIA statistics](https://www.publicservice.govt.nz/data/oia-statistics).
- TBL's 2026 follow-up research on OIA cost drivers could materially change the cost/save case if it measures repeat-request cost directly or shows most cost is unavoidable case-by-case judgement [MoJ stewardship page](https://www.justice.govt.nz/justice-sector-policy/regulatory-stewardship/regulatory-systems/constitutional/transparency-and-scrutiny-of-executive-action/).
