# NZ hospital waiting-time comparisons need separate ED-flow, specialist-referral and elective-surgery frames

This is research child **#733 of Stream #434**. It answers one question: **how should New Zealand be compared with Australia, England and OECD countries once hospital waiting-time definitions are made comparable?**

## Executive answer

- **Do not publish a single "NZ rank" across ED and elective waits.** New Zealand's current hospital-wait targets are three different measures: ED attendance completed within six hours, first specialist assessment (FSA) from referral within four months, and elective treatment after commitment-to-treatment within four months [Ministry of Health, Health targets](https://www.health.govt.nz/monitoring-statistics/system-monitoring/health-targets); [Health NZ Q1 2025/26 results definitions](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/Q1-2025-26-health-target-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality.pdf).
- **For ED flow, the closest headline comparator is not Australia's triage "seen on time" figure; it is England's four-hour A&E flow standard and Australia's four-hour ED visit-completion measure, both with a different clock length from NZ's six-hour target.** NZ reported 74.4% within six hours for January-March 2026, England reported 75.7% within four hours across all A&E types and 61.9% for Type 1 departments in May 2026, and Australia reported 53% of ED visits completed within four hours in 2024-25 [Beehive, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets); [NHS England A&E May 2026 commentary](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Statistical-commentary-May-2026-sDl1e.pdf); [AIHW ED care 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments).
- **For elective care, compare two separate layers: pre-treatment pathway access and surgery-list waits.** NZ's FSA target starts at referral, NZ's elective-treatment target starts after commitment to treatment, England's RTT standard starts at referral and covers consultant-led elective pathways, Australia's elective-surgery wait starts when the patient is ready for surgery, and OECD's elective-surgery measures start after specialist assessment or describe patients still on the list [Health NZ Q1 definitions](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/Q1-2025-26-health-target-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality.pdf); [NHS England RTT April 2026 press notice](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Apr26-RTT-statistical-press-notice-PDF-566K-X7gGnn.pdf); [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times); [OECD Health at a Glance 2025 waiting-times definition](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html).
- **The defensible headline is: NZ looks under target and pressured, but the comparator story is metric-dependent.** OECD's 2025 waiting-times chapter says New Zealand and Slovenia were the countries where elective waiting times continued to increase on both OECD-presented metrics after the pandemic; the same OECD figure data show NZ's latest cataract-surgery waits are worse than England on median days but slightly better than Australia on the share waiting over three months, while NZ's hip-replacement waits are worse than Australia and England on the share waiting over three months but shorter than England on median days [OECD waiting-times chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html); [OECD Figure 5.11 Statlink](https://stat.link/vgz50q); [OECD Figure 5.12 Statlink](https://stat.link/gfstj5).

**Overall confidence:** Medium - definitions and current headline figures are high-confidence official sources; the cross-country interpretation is medium because ED case mix, hospital inclusion, clock starts and reporting periods are not harmonised.

### ED comparison

NZ's current ED headline is the share of patients admitted, discharged or transferred within six hours; Health NZ's own definition describes this as patients completed within six hours as a percentage of all patients who attended ED, and the latest ministerial release reports 74.4% for January-March 2026 [Health NZ Q1 definitions](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/Q1-2025-26-health-target-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality.pdf); [Beehive, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets). The baseline finding for sibling issue #732 reconciles the same quarter across Health NZ products and records the national ED target result as 74.4% with 333,104 ED attendances in Q3 2025/26 [#732 baseline finding](hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md).

Australia's most visible ED waiting-time headline, "seen on time", is not the same construct: AIHW defines ED waiting time as presentation to commencement of clinical care, then judges timeliness against triage category thresholds from immediate care through 120 minutes [AIHW ED waiting-times overview](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments/waiting-times). AIHW's separate throughput measure is closer to NZ/England: 53% of Australian ED visits were completed within four hours in 2024-25, compared with 55% in 2023-24 and 67% in 2020-21 [AIHW ED care 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments).

England's A&E four-hour measure is closer again because it uses arrival to admission, transfer or discharge, but it still uses a four-hour clock rather than NZ's six-hour clock [NHS England A&E statistics](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/ae-waiting-times-and-activity/). NHS England's May 2026 commentary reports 75.7% within four hours across all A&E departments, 61.9% within four hours in Type 1 major A&E departments, and an operational standard of 95% within four hours [NHS May 2026 commentary](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Statistical-commentary-May-2026-sDl1e.pdf).

**Interpretation:** NZ's 74.4% within six hours cannot be called better than England's 75.7% within four hours or worse than Australia's 53% within four hours without standardising the clock, department mix and case mix. The fair ED statement is that all three systems are materially below their nominal flow standards or four-hour throughput benchmarks, and that England Type 1 and Australia four-hour completion show severe hospital-ED flow pressure on shorter clocks [NHS May 2026 commentary](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Statistical-commentary-May-2026-sDl1e.pdf); [AIHW ED care 2024-25](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/emergency-departments); [Beehive, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets). **Confidence: Medium** because the definition mapping is strong, but no public harmonised four-hour/six-hour NZ-England-Australia patient-level extract was found.

### Specialist referral and elective-treatment comparison

NZ splits planned-care pressure into a referral-to-FSA target and a post-commitment elective-treatment target: FSA is the proportion waiting less than four months from referral, while elective treatment is the proportion of people given a commitment to treatment waiting less than four months for a procedure [Health NZ Q1 definitions](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/Q1-2025-26-health-target-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality.pdf). The latest national results are 61.2% for FSA within four months and 64.9% for elective treatment within four months in January-March 2026 [Beehive, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.beehive.govt.nz/release/progress-continues-across-all-five-health-targets).

England's RTT standard is broader than NZ's elective-treatment target because it starts at referral and includes consultant-led elective pathways at different stages, including diagnostics, outpatient appointments and admission waits [NHS RTT April 2026 press notice](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Apr26-RTT-statistical-press-notice-PDF-566K-X7gGnn.pdf). NHS England reported that 65.0% of RTT pathways were within 18 weeks at the end of April 2026, against the 92% incomplete-pathway standard; the same press notice says this represented 7.2 million pathways waiting, with about 6.1 million unique patients estimated from WLMDS [NHS RTT April 2026 press notice](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Apr26-RTT-statistical-press-notice-PDF-566K-X7gGnn.pdf).

Australia's elective-surgery waiting-time measure is narrower than England RTT and differently shaped from NZ's current target: AIHW measures the elapsed time from being ready for surgery to admission for surgery, and reports distribution measures for admitted patients and overdue waits by clinical urgency category [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times). In 2024-25, AIHW reported a national elective-surgery median wait of 45 days, 90th percentile wait of 329 days, and 6.0% waiting more than 365 days for surgery [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times).

**Interpretation:** For planned care, the least misleading comparator set is: NZ FSA vs England RTT incomplete pathways for broad referral-to-specialist/elective access, and NZ elective-treatment vs Australia/OECD elective-surgery waits for post-specialist surgery-list pressure. The comparison still needs caution because NZ's current target is a current-list under-four-month percentage, Australia publishes admitted-surgery distribution measures, and England counts pathways rather than unique patients [Health NZ Q1 definitions](https://static.info.content.health.nz/docs/publications/Q1-2025-26-health-target-results-with-ethnicity-and-rurality.pdf); [AIHW elective-surgery waiting times](https://www.aihw.gov.au/hospitals/topics/elective-surgery/waiting-times); [NHS RTT April 2026 press notice](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/06/Apr26-RTT-statistical-press-notice-PDF-566K-X7gGnn.pdf). **Confidence: Medium-high** for the mapping; **Medium** for any implied country ordering.

### OECD elective-surgery frame

OECD Health at a Glance 2025 is useful for selected elective procedures, not for ED flow. OECD says its waiting-times section presents two elective-procedure measures: share of patients waiting more than three months from specialist assessment to treatment, and median days for patients still on the list at a given point in time [OECD waiting-times definition](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html). OECD also warns that Norway is overestimated because its clock starts from doctor referral rather than post-specialist-assessment waiting-list addition, which shows why clock starts matter even inside OECD's own figure set [OECD waiting-times definition](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html).

OECD's strongest NZ-specific warning is trend-based: after pandemic increases, most countries' waiting times had returned or started returning toward pre-pandemic levels, but New Zealand and Slovenia continued to increase on both OECD-presented metrics [OECD waiting-times chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html). The OECD Statlink data behind Figure 5.11 reports New Zealand cataract surgery at 54.0% waiting more than three months and 104 median current-list days in the latest year, while Australia is 57.8% waiting over three months and England's latest median is 74 days, with some country series footnoted as latest data from 2022-23 [OECD Figure 5.11 Statlink](https://stat.link/vgz50q). The OECD Statlink data behind Figure 5.12 reports New Zealand hip replacement at 126 median days and 70.3% waiting more than three months; the same figure reports Australia at 62.7% waiting over three months and England at 174 median days and 63.5% waiting over three months [OECD Figure 5.12 Statlink](https://stat.link/gfstj5).

**Interpretation:** OECD supports "NZ elective-surgery waits are worsening and look poor on selected procedures," but it does not support a simple "NZ is worst" headline. In the OECD figure data checked here, NZ is worse than Australia on hip-replacement share waiting over three months, slightly better than Australia on cataract share waiting over three months, worse than England on cataract median days, and better than England on hip-replacement median days [OECD Figure 5.11 Statlink](https://stat.link/vgz50q); [OECD Figure 5.12 Statlink](https://stat.link/gfstj5). **Confidence: Medium** because the Statlink values are official OECD figure data, but the countries' exact reference years differ where footnoted.

## What would change this conclusion

- A harmonised ED dataset that reports NZ, Australia and England on both four-hour and six-hour arrival-to-departure clocks, with comparable inclusion of urgent-care/minor-injury units and comparable admission/discharge case mix, would allow a stronger ED ranking.
- A Health NZ publication that reports NZ elective-treatment waits as median/90th percentile admitted-surgery waits by procedure would make NZ directly comparable to AIHW and OECD elective-surgery outputs.
- A Health NZ publication that combines referral-to-FSA, diagnostics, decision-to-treat and treatment into a single RTT-like pathway measure would make NZ more directly comparable to England RTT.
- I could not verify a current OECD ED waiting-time benchmark comparable to NZ's ED six-hour target; OECD Health at a Glance 2025's waiting-times chapter used here is elective-procedure and survey-access focused, not ED-flow focused [OECD waiting-times chapter](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/11/health-at-a-glance-2025_a894f72e/full-report/waiting-times_3a1021fa.html).
- I did not contact Health NZ, AIHW, NHS England, OECD analysts, clinicians, or patient groups. A human operational reviewer should check whether the proposed grouping matches how hospitals themselves understand flow bottlenecks.

## Open follow-up questions

- What would NZ's ED result be under a four-hour arrival-to-admission/discharge/transfer clock, and what would England/Australia be under a six-hour clock?
- Can Health NZ's detailed waitlist extract be transformed into OECD-style procedure-specific median and over-three-month elective-surgery measures for cataract, hip and knee replacement?
- How much of the NZ-England-Australia difference is explained by admissions from ED, older-patient mix, urgent-care diversion, bed occupancy and discharge delay rather than ED process alone?
