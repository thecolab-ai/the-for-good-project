# A pre-retrofit earthquake turns council seismic risk into a cash, insurance and service-continuity problem before it becomes a rebuild programme

## Executive answer

- A significant earthquake before retrofits are complete would first create a triage problem: councils would need to keep emergency management, water, wastewater, stormwater, roads and public information running while closing or reassessing damaged public buildings, because lifeline utilities include transport, water, energy and telecommunications and CDEM Groups are local-authority partnerships with lifeline and welfare organisations. [NEMA, elected officials guidance](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/elected-officials); [NEMA, lifeline utilities](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/lifeline-utilities/lifelines-role)
- Fiscal exposure would not be solved by Natural Hazards Commission cover: NHCover is a capped first layer for insured residential dwellings and limited residential land, while council buildings, public facilities and lifeline infrastructure sit in council insurance, debt, reserves, Crown recovery support and rates decisions. [NHC Toka Tu Ake, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/); [NEMA, infrastructure repair/rebuild funding](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/guidelines/factsheets/infrastructure-repair-rebuild)
- The best general rule for damaged council lifeline assets is "insurance first, then possible 60:40 Crown/local split, then council headroom": NEMA says the Government funds 60% of eligible repair/rebuild costs above a local-authority threshold, subject to Cabinet approval, and insurance for the damaged asset is considered when funding is determined. [NEMA, infrastructure repair/rebuild funding](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/guidelines/factsheets/infrastructure-repair-rebuild)
- Wellington is the most fiscally stressed case in my synthesis of the three public LTPs checked, but that ranking is Medium confidence and not independently benchmarked: Wellington's own LTP says many public assets such as libraries, pools and waste systems cannot be covered by insurance, net debt rises to $2.9 billion including $272 million of insurance headroom by 2033/34, and its forecast exceeds its debt-to-revenue limit for the first eight years when insurance headroom is included. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1, extracted via Jina text on 2026-07-05 after local fetch ladder classified the PDF as blocked/tooling](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)
- Hutt is the clearest operational-outage case in this sample because its LTP says a major seismic event could disrupt single-access routes, roads, pipes and stormwater networks, while its financial strategy sets a 250% net-debt-to-revenue limit below the LGFA 280% covenant specifically to retain natural-disaster headroom. [Hutt City Council 10 Year Plan 2024-2034, fetched by curl and parsed locally 2026-07-05](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)
- Dunedin is a "debt headroom but core-asset crowding" case: its financial strategy says DCC remains within the LGFA 280% debt-to-revenue limit and its 2026/27 update estimated debt at $829 million or 190% of revenue by 30 June 2027, but its capital plan already includes $1.856 billion over nine years and specific seismic work at Moana Pool and the Town Hall/Municipal Chambers. [Dunedin City Council Financial Strategy 2025-34](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/1271062/9-year-plan-2025-34-Financial-Strategy.pdf); [Dunedin City Council CEO Overview Report, Annual Plan 2026/27](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0004/1288444/CEO-Overview-Report-Annual-Plan-2026-27.pdf); [Dunedin City Council Capital Expenditure Report 2025-34, extracted via Jina text after local fetch ladder classified direct PDF fetch as blocked/tooling](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)

**Overall confidence:** Medium - the fiscal channels and council case inputs are from official sources, but actual post-event costs would depend on event location, asset damage, insurance schedules, deductibles, Cabinet recovery decisions, and engineering assessments that are not public at portfolio level.

## Evidence

### Scenario boundary

This finding models a local or regional earthquake large enough to damage council-owned public buildings and lifeline networks before all planned seismic remediation is complete, but it does not estimate casualties, private-building losses, or exact engineering damage because those depend on shaking, ground failure, building condition and occupancy at the time of the event. [MBIE, how the earthquake-prone building system works](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/how-the-earthquake-prone-building-system-works); [Hutt City Council 10 Year Plan 2024-2034](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)

The operational baseline is that elected officials can declare a state of local emergency, while the Civil Defence Controller is the response decision-maker for public information and response actions. [NEMA, elected officials guidance](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/elected-officials)

The finance baseline is that councils are already under cost pressure: the Audit Office says council insurance costs have increased significantly, with sharper increases in earthquake-risk regions, and 58 councils forecast $91.9 billion of capital expenditure during 2024-34. [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)

LGFA is the main debt constraint checked here because LGFA lends to local authorities and sets financial covenants including net debt/total revenue below 280% as a foundation-policy covenant, net interest/total revenue below 20%, net interest/annual rates income below 30%, and liquidity above 110%. [LGFA, risk management](https://www.lgfa.co.nz/about-lgfa/risk-management)

### Interpretation

The fiscal consequence of a pre-retrofit earthquake is not simply "new retrofit debt"; it is a forced reprioritisation between emergency response, immediate service restoration, insurance shortfalls, the local share of eligible infrastructure rebuilds, and the already-planned capital programme. [NEMA, infrastructure repair/rebuild funding](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/guidelines/factsheets/infrastructure-repair-rebuild); [Office of the Auditor-General, 2025](https://ao.parliament.nz/2025/long-term-plans/part2.htm)

Wellington appears to have the most acute balance-sheet vulnerability in this sample on the public documents checked, but this is a single-source synthesis from council disclosures rather than an independently benchmarked fiscal ranking. The basis is Wellington's LTP, which says the council is underinsured and uses a perpetual investment fund proposal to reduce disaster-recovery funding risk, while its debt forecast only stays inside the debt-to-revenue limit when insurance headroom is excluded. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)

Hutt has the clearest network-continuity warning in this sample because its own LTP identifies the risk of major disruption to single-access routes, pipes, stormwater and sanitation after a significant seismic event, and it has deliberately set a lower debt limit than LGFA to retain disaster headroom. [Hutt City Council 10 Year Plan 2024-2034](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)

Dunedin appears to have more debt headroom on the public documents checked, but that does not mean a pre-retrofit earthquake is cheap: the visible risk is that emergency works and closures would crowd an already large programme of renewals, water compliance, Moana Pool seismic upgrades and civic-building investigations. [Dunedin City Council Financial Strategy 2025-34](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0019/1271062/9-year-plan-2025-34-Financial-Strategy.pdf); [Dunedin City Council Capital Expenditure Report 2025-34](https://www.dunedin.govt.nz/__data/assets/pdf_file/0007/1126870/Capital-expenditure-report-2025-34.pdf)

For all three councils, NHCover is mainly relevant to household recovery and community displacement rather than council asset recovery because it covers insured residential dwellings and limited residential land, not council-owned public facilities or network infrastructure. [NHC Toka Tu Ake, about NHCover](https://www.naturalhazards.govt.nz/insurance-and-claims/about-nhcover/)

## What would change this conclusion

- Council insurance schedules, deductibles, probable-maximum-loss modelling and exclusions for Wellington, Hutt and Dunedin would materially change the fiscal ranking because public LTPs summarise insurance risk but do not disclose full policy terms. [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en); [Hutt City Council 10 Year Plan 2024-2034](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans)
- A council-by-council asset register that joins %NBS, EPB status, occupancy, service criticality, insurance status, replacement value and retrofit stage would change the operational ranking because public registers and LTPs do not expose all fields consistently. [Hutt City Council council-owned buildings register](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/property-and-building/seismic-information/council-owned-buildings); [MBIE EPB Register](https://www.building.govt.nz/managing-buildings/managing-earthquake-prone-buildings/epb-register)
- A real event's location and ground effects could reverse the ranking: for example, Hutt's scenario is especially sensitive to fault rupture, liquefaction, landslides and route loss, while Wellington's fiscal risk is especially sensitive to uninsured public-asset damage. [Hutt City Council 10 Year Plan 2024-2034](https://www.huttcity.govt.nz/council/annual-plans-and-publications/annual-plans-and-long-term-plans); [Wellington City Council LTP volume 1](https://wellington.govt.nz/-/media/your-council/plans-policies-and-bylaws/plans-and-policies/longtermplan/2024-34/2024-34-longtermplan-volume-one.pdf?hash=7A079DF8F37BF4EF479605EEA7CF6D766F0F1D3F&la=en)
- I could not verify full council insurance schedules, exact deductible/excess levels, unpublished engineering assessments, current contractor capacity after a regional event, or Cabinet's likely funding decision for a hypothetical earthquake. [NEMA, infrastructure repair/rebuild funding](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/guidance-training/guidelines/factsheets/infrastructure-repair-rebuild)
- A local-government finance specialist, council emergency manager, structural engineer and insurance broker would be needed before using this scenario for council-specific financial planning.
