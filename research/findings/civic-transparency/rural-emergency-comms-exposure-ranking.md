# Real-world storm data and official hazard assessments let five rural clusters be ranked for emergency-communications exposure, even though no single public index scores them

## Executive answer

- No public agency publishes a composite score that ranks rural communities by combined copper/mobile/road/power/device exposure — that conclusion from the prior round of this stream [Rural mobile and emergency resilience gaps remain visible after copper and 3G transitions, but public data still cannot rank exposed communities (#588)](../civic-transparency/rural-resilience-gaps-after-transitions.md) still holds. **But** three independent official sources, plus repeated real storms across 2023–2026, consistently point at the *same* geography, which is enough to rank exposure with Medium confidence rather than declare the question unanswerable.
- **Tier 1 — highest, best-documented, recurring:** Tairāwhiti/Gisborne (especially East Cape settlements Te Araroa, Hicks Bay, Wharekahika, Onepoto) had 90% of cell sites offline for two days during Cyclone Gabrielle (Feb 2023) — the worst of any region — and was cut off again by road closures and power loss in a January–February 2026 flooding event (declared a state of emergency 21 January, extended to 11 February), though that later event is documented as road/power isolation, not a confirmed telecommunications outage (see below) [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf), [RNZ, "New technology could keep communications going during disasters"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/535819/new-technology-could-keep-communications-going-during-disasters), [RNZ, "State of emergency in Tai Rāwhiti extended"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/585954/state-of-emergency-in-tai-rawhiti-extended).
- Hawke's Bay is a close second in raw severity (80% of cell sites offline in Gabrielle) but is the *only* region with a quantified, funded remediation plan (167 towers, 48-hour battery target, ~$20m ask; 13 towers/60,000 addresses already committed under a 2025 Budget package) — meaning its forward exposure is now better characterised and partly funded, unlike the other regions on this list [Brewer, "Improving Telecommunications Resilience in Hawke's Bay" (2024)](https://www.hawkesbayrecovery.nz/assets/Uploads/Communications-Resilience-Report-July-24.pdf), [NZ Herald, "Upgrades for hundreds of cell towers to protect 111 calls"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/hawkes-bay-today/news/hundreds-of-cell-towers-to-be-upgraded-to-protect-111-calls-in-natural-disasters/premium/7OC6ZEQJ2JDXVJOFO44J2DZUYI/).
- **Tier 2 — structurally exposed, less remediated:** Northland (Far North east coast), the West Coast of the South Island, and the Southern Lakes are among the regions NEMA's own lifelines assessment gives as examples of having "limited points of [electricity] supply" — the same assessment also names Hawke's Bay in this group (already covered under Tier 1 above), and it does not rank these regions against each other or state they are the *least* redundant nationally, only that they are examples where funding thresholds for added redundancy may not be met [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C, p.12](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf). This is still relevant to telecoms exposure specifically because the same assessment separately states the telecoms sector is more vulnerable to electricity outage than to direct physical damage [same source, p.63]. Northland's exposure has since gotten *worse*, not better: its Civil Defence chair says towers failed faster in an April 2025 storm than in Gabrielle, despite telcos' legal resilience obligations [RNZ, "Northland Civil Defence leader..."](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/regions_local-democracy-reporting/558495/northland-civil-defence-leader-govt-must-wave-the-big-stick-over-telcos-storm-failure).
- **Tier 3 — low-probability, total-isolation risk:** the Chatham Islands' entire mobile and internet backhaul routes through a single satellite link to a Wellington ground station — two independent sources describe only this one link and neither mentions a submarine cable or any other route, though neither explicitly states no alternative exists — and West Coast communities behind the Alpine Fault risk road isolation "for weeks to months" in a major quake — both are singular, catastrophic-but-rare risks distinct from the East Coast/Northland pattern of frequent, shorter outages [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, "Chatham Islands Receive First-Ever Mobile Network"](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/chatham-islands-receive-first-ever-mobile-network/), [Bill Bennett, "RCG flips switch on Chatham Islands 4G network"](https://billbennett.co.nz/rcg-flips-switch-on-chatham-islands-4g-network/), [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf).

**Overall confidence:** Medium — the regional ranking is corroborated by three independent official/primary sources (a national lifelines assessment, a regional engineering audit, and a regulator's investigation) plus telecommunications-specific outage data from two real storms two years apart (Cyclone Gabrielle, Feb 2023, and ex-Tropical Cyclone Tam, April 2025). A third, more recent Tairāwhiti event (Jan/Feb 2026) corroborates the same geography but only through road-closure and power-loss reporting — it does not itself contain telecommunications-outage figures, so it is treated as supporting evidence for recurring physical exposure in that location, not as a third instance of confirmed comms outage. This remains a synthesis rather than an official published index, and cannot resolve to individual households, roads, or towers.

## Evidence

### The prior finding's gap, and what's different this time

Round 1 of this follow-up ([#588](https://github.com/thecolab-ai/the-for-good-project/issues/588)) found real counts for each risk category — rural copper premises, 3G-only addresses, MBSF sites, 111-vulnerable consumers — but concluded no public dataset joins them at community level [Rural mobile and emergency resilience gaps... (#588)](../civic-transparency/rural-resilience-gaps-after-transitions.md). That remains true for a *formal composite index*. What this round adds is that a national hazard assessment and repeated real storms already identify the same handful of places as consistently worst-affected, which is a usable — if less precise — answer to "which communities."

### Cyclone Gabrielle (Feb 2023) as the best available stress test

The clearest evidence comes from NEMA and the NZ Lifelines Council's national assessment, which states that across Northland, Auckland, Hawke's Bay and Gisborne there were 1,645 cell sites in total; only two were physically damaged by the cyclone, but 20% were offline at the outage's peak nationally — and "the worst hit region was Gisborne, where for two days (14/15 February) 90% of cell sites were offline" [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment, 2023 Edition, Part C: Sectors and Hazards, p.61](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf). This is independently corroborated: RNZ reports "in the worst hit region of Tairāwhiti about 90% of mobile towers were offline over the 14-15 February period," while cell towers came down once backup batteries went flat, leaving people "completely isolated" for three to four weeks in the worst-affected settlements [RNZ, "New technology could keep communications going during disasters"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/535819/new-technology-could-keep-communications-going-during-disasters); a NZ Herald editorial repeats the same 90% figure [NZ Herald, "Hawke's Bay and Tairāwhiti storms a wake-up call"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/editorial-hawkes-bay-and-tairawhiti-storms-a-wake-up-call-for-nz-infrastructure/OD42SIL2TZGCNMC2XBOY7CKR64/).

Hawke's Bay was the second-worst hit, and is separately documented in detail: an independent engineering report commissioned by the Hawke's Bay Regional Economic Development Agency found "on Tuesday, February 14, 2023 there were 185 cell sites offline in New Zealand. Most of these were in the Hawke's Bay Region where only 20% of the region's cell sites remained online," restoration being hampered by roads blocked by flooding, slips and bridge washouts [Brewer, "Improving Telecommunications Resilience in Hawke's Bay," July 2024, p.4](https://www.hawkesbayrecovery.nz/assets/Uploads/Communications-Resilience-Report-July-24.pdf). That report recommends extending battery backup at key cellular sites to a 48-hour minimum, covering roughly 167 towers at an estimated cost of under $20m (individual batteries costing around NZ$135,000 each) [Brewer (2024), pp.14, 19–24](https://www.hawkesbayrecovery.nz/assets/Uploads/Communications-Resilience-Report-July-24.pdf); [NZ Herald, "Cell towers in Hawke's Bay need $20m battery upgrades"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/cell-towers-in-hawkes-bay-need-20m-battery-upgrades-to-protect-against-natural-disasters-report/WPDYSS7KJZAKHAI2X6GQAZ3OBE/).

Copper-specific damage in the same event is quantified by the regulator: "During Cyclone Gabrielle, 37,500 copper broadband and voice services were disrupted and hundreds of street cabinets were knocked out across regions including Northland, Napier, Auckland, and Coromandel" [Commerce Commission, Copper Services Investigation Final recommendation report, 21 August 2025, para 3.142](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf). The Commission also rejects the common assumption that copper is more resilient than the alternatives: rural copper broadband fault restoration averages around 25 hours, and copper's own street-cabinet batteries last only 4–8 hours in a power cut, the same as mobile cell-site batteries [Commerce Commission (2025), paras 3.140–3.142](https://www.comcom.govt.nz/assets/Documents/copper-services-investigation/Copper-Services-Investigation-Final-recommendation-report-21-August-2025.pdf). This partially links the copper and mobile risk categories the issue asks about: Northland and Auckland appear on both the copper-disruption list (Northland, Napier, Auckland, Coromandel) and the mobile-outage list (Northland, Auckland, Hawke's Bay, Gisborne) quoted above, and Napier sits within the Hawke's Bay region, so three of the four named copper-affected regions overlap with the mobile-affected list. Coromandel is named only on the copper side and Gisborne only on the mobile side in the sources found, so this is overlapping — not identical — regional exposure; no source located here shows copper disruption specifically in Gisborne/Tairāwhiti or mobile disruption specifically in Coromandel at a comparable scale.

### The government's own targeting is a second, independent ranking signal

Separately from the historical stress test, the government's most recent resilience investment is itself evidence of a de facto priority order. A NZ$6.4 million Budget 2025 allocation is funding battery-capacity upgrades (4–8 hours to 24 hours) at 295 cell towers across 16 regions, selected by Connexa (the tower company delivering the work) on "vulnerability to power outages," "historical outage data," "the coverage of critical facilities," and "access constraints" [NZ Herald, "'Every extra hour helps': Upgrades for hundreds of cell towers to protect 111 calls in natural disasters"](https://www.nzherald.co.nz/hawkes-bay-today/news/hundreds-of-cell-towers-to-be-upgraded-to-protect-111-calls-in-natural-disasters/premium/7OC6ZEQJ2JDXVJOFO44J2DZUYI/). Of the 295 towers, only Hawke's Bay's allocation is broken out publicly — 13 towers protecting roughly 60,000 addresses — which is consistent with Hawke's Bay being the most *quantified*, if not the most severely affected, region [NZ Herald, same article]. Work is not scheduled to begin until the second half of 2026 and finish by June 2027, meaning the exposure identified below is still live as of this finding's writing [NZ Herald, same article].

A separate, ongoing programme specifically targets Tairāwhiti: 295 towers nationally include a Gisborne allocation funded via a $6.4 million Budget 2025 line to Spark and tower owner Connexa, upgrading battery capacity to 24 hours; Tairāwhiti's own civil defence group has additionally built a standalone "mesh node" system — solar-powered nodes that a cellphone connects to via Bluetooth to send text messages, explicitly designed to work independently of Starlink, Vodafone, or any other internet/cellphone provider — piloted at a station 100km from Gisborne, after commercial network restoration in the region took three to four weeks during Cyclone Gabrielle — the one documented multi-week outage found in this search, not a claim of repeated multi-week outages across the region's 16 extreme-weather events since 2017 [RNZ, "New technology could keep communications going during disasters"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/535819/new-technology-could-keep-communications-going-during-disasters).

### Northland: the region where exposure is documented as worsening, not improving

Northland was one of the four regions hit in Gabrielle (60,000 Northlanders lost power, mobile phone and/or broadband service), with specific east-coast settlements named: Huruiki (near Helena Bay), Karikari Peninsula, Mangōnui, Matapōuri, Taupō Bay, Tikitikioure (Bay of Islands), and Ruakākā [RNZ, "Northland Civil Defence leader: Govt must 'wave the big stick' over telcos' storm failure"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/regions_local-democracy-reporting/558495/northland-civil-defence-leader-govt-must-wave-the-big-stick-over-telcos-storm-failure). The same article reports that during ex-Tropical Cyclone Tam in April 2025, cellphone towers in the same settlements failed *faster* than they had in Gabrielle two years earlier, and Northland Civil Defence chair Kelly Stratford stated that despite telcos' legal obligation to build in resilience, nothing had visibly been done since 2023 [RNZ, same article]. This is the strongest available evidence that exposure in at least one region is trending in the wrong direction rather than converging toward the national remediation programme.

Separately, NEMA's own national lifelines assessment names Northland (along with West Coast, Southern Lakes and Hawke's Bay) as one of the regions with "limited points of [electricity] supply," where "there are opportunities to provide more redundancy... [but] many of these may not meet funding criteria thresholds" [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C, p.12](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf). Since the same assessment states the telecommunications sector is "most vulnerable to electricity outage" rather than direct physical damage [same source, p.63], a region's electricity-supply redundancy is a structural proxy for its telecoms exposure even before a storm hits.

### Tairāwhiti/Gisborne remains exposed in 2026, not just 2023

The East Cape was cut off again well after Gabrielle: in a late January/February 2026 flooding event, coastal settlements Te Araroa, Onepoto, Wharekahika and the area around Punaruku were cut off when State Highway 35 was closed by flooding and slips in two separate places, with more than 250 homes losing power overnight and roughly 16 properties red-stickered; the state of emergency for Tairāwhiti was extended to 11 February 2026 [RNZ, "Gisborne coastal communities remain cut off after a deadly storm"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/584755/gisborne-coastal-communities-remain-cut-off-after-a-deadly-storm); [RNZ, "State of emergency in Tai Rāwhiti extended"](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/585954/state-of-emergency-in-tai-rawhiti-extended). Neither article reports telecommunications-specific outage figures for this event, so the causal link to communications loss (rather than just road/power loss) is inferred from the 2023 pattern, not directly confirmed for 2026 — this is flagged as unverified below. SH35 is the only road link for these settlements; NEMA's national roads table separately flags East Cape as an immediate Waka Kotahi priority area for resilience investment [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C, p.29](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf).

### West Coast and Southern Lakes: a different, lower-frequency but higher-consequence profile

NEMA's assessment separately singles out the West Coast for a distinct, catastrophic risk: in a major Alpine Fault (AF8) earthquake, State Highways 6, 7 and 73 are "the only links to the West Coast," and "large parts of the South Island (notably the West Coast) accessed through alpine passes or steep valleys near the Alpine Fault will be inaccessible by road, potentially for weeks to months" [NZ Infrastructure Vulnerability Assessment 2023, Part C, pp.29–30](https://www.civildefence.govt.nz/assets/Uploads/documents/lifelines/NVA-Part-C-Sectors-and-Hazards-v1.0-Sept-2023.pdf). The same document notes that smaller local telephone exchanges (it names Whataroa, on the West Coast) are being progressively shut down, which removes communities' ability to make local calls if the link to the rest of the network fails, pushing them toward satellite as the only fallback [same source, p.63]. This is a lower-probability, higher-consequence risk profile than the East Coast/Northland pattern of frequent storm outages, and the two should not be ranked on the same scale without qualification — West Coast has not experienced a Gabrielle-scale communications failure in the period reviewed, but its structural isolation-in-a-major-quake scenario is worse than anywhere else assessed.

### Chatham Islands: a single point of failure by design

The Chatham Islands are a distinct case: until a Rural Connectivity Group (RCG) build, the islands had no mobile network at all. The National Infrastructure Funding and Financing agency states the build "allowed the build of a new satellite dish and satellite link, providing 8.5 times the capacity of the current satellite link serving the island," and that the five island cell sites are "linked with diverse paths, so any fault on a particular site can be isolated and still allow the other sites to operate" — that redundancy is described only *between* the five local cell sites, not for the satellite backhaul itself [National Infrastructure Funding and Financing, "Chatham Islands Receive First-Ever Mobile Network"](https://nationalinfrastructure.govt.nz/our_project/chatham-islands-receive-first-ever-mobile-network/). An independent industry account of the same build corroborates that the backhaul is a single satellite dish pointed at the Eutelsat 172B satellite, linking to "a ground station at Gateway Teleport in Wellington," describing the same "eight times the capacity" upgrade and the same inter-tower path diversity, but likewise not mentioning a submarine cable or any second backhaul route [Bill Bennett, "RCG flips switch on Chatham Islands 4G network"](https://billbennett.co.nz/rcg-flips-switch-on-chatham-islands-4g-network/). Neither source states outright that no alternative backhaul exists, so "single point of failure" here is an inference from two sources independently describing only one link, not a directly sourced claim — flagged accordingly in the load-bearing claims table below. No storm-outage data specific to the Chathams was found in this search; the exposure claim here rests on this backhaul-design inference, not on an observed failure, and is flagged as lower-confidence for that reason.

### Device risk after the 3G shutdown is national, not regionally concentrated

New Zealand's 3G shutdown completed by 31 March 2026, and Spark has confirmed that any remaining 3G-only phones cannot make 111 calls [Bill Bennett, "New Zealand's 3G era ends, emergency call concerns remain," 29 March 2026](https://billbennett.co.nz/new-zealands-3g-era-ends-emergency-call-concerns-remain/). Industry estimates put roughly 30,000 handset variants still active nationally with some degree of call incompatibility, with affected users "likely thousands in a market of millions" — disproportionately falling on "harder-to-reach and more vulnerable groups," often because their phones were parallel-imported or bought outside normal carrier channels rather than because they live in a particular region [Bill Bennett, same article]. This is consistent with round 1's finding that device risk is a distinct axis from geographic/network risk, and this search found no evidence that residual 3G-dependent devices cluster specifically in the five places identified above, rather than nationally among older or lower-income users generally.

## What would change this conclusion

- A public, address- or community-level dataset that actually joins copper status, mobile coverage quality, 3G-device migration, road isolation risk, 111-vulnerable-consumer counts, and tower backup duration would upgrade this from a Medium-confidence, evidence-based ranking to a High-confidence, precise one — this is the same gap round 1 identified, and it is still open [Rural mobile and emergency resilience gaps... (#588)](../civic-transparency/rural-resilience-gaps-after-transitions.md).
- Confirmation (or denial) that the January/February 2026 Tairāwhiti flooding caused a communications outage comparable to 2023 — this finding infers it from the 2023 pattern and from road/power loss reported in 2026, but found no telecoms-specific reporting for the 2026 event itself.
- Publication of the full list and regional breakdown of the 295 towers being upgraded under the Budget 2025 programme (only Hawke's Bay's 13-tower share is public) would let the "de facto priority ranking" claim be checked directly rather than inferred from partial disclosure.
- Any observed West Coast communications failure at a scale comparable to Gabrielle would confirm (or complicate) treating it as equally exposed to the East Coast/Northland cluster, since its risk case here rests on a modelled earthquake scenario rather than an observed event.
- Confirmation (or denial) of whether the Chatham Islands' mobile/internet backhaul has any route besides the one Eutelsat satellite link described by both sources found here — neither source states outright that no alternative exists, so the "single point of failure" framing is currently an inference from two sources' silence on a second route, not a directly sourced fact.
- A breakdown of residual 3G-dependent devices and 111-Contact-Code-registered consumers by region — neither ComCom's monitoring report nor the 111 Contact Code compliance report (both cited in round 1) publishes this — would show whether device risk actually concentrates in the five places identified here or is genuinely dispersed nationally as the device-fragmentation evidence in this finding suggests.
- I could not verify a per-community or per-marae ranking within Tairāwhiti, Northland or the West Coast; the finding operates at regional/settlement-cluster level because that is the finest grain the public sources support. A human review by Tairāwhiti and Te Tai Tokerau civil defence staff, iwi/marae representatives, and the Hawke's Bay Regional Recovery Agency would be needed to confirm whether this regional ranking matches on-the-ground experience at finer resolution.

## Open follow-up questions

- No sub-issues were opened. The question was answerable within this issue's scope once a cross-region synthesis of Cyclone Gabrielle outage data, the NEMA/Lifelines Council national hazard assessment, and the Hawke's Bay-specific engineering report was assembled — it did not require further chunky research to answer at the regional/settlement-cluster grain this finding operates at.
- Does the January–February 2026 Tairāwhiti flood event have a documented telecommunications-outage component, and if so, at what scale relative to Gabrielle?
- Can MBIE/NIFF publish the regional breakdown of the 295-tower Budget 2025 battery-upgrade programme, so the "de facto priority ranking" inferred here can be checked against the government's actual selection?
- Do residual 3G-dependent devices and 111-Contact-Code-registered vulnerable consumers cluster geographically in the five places identified here, or are they genuinely dispersed nationally?
