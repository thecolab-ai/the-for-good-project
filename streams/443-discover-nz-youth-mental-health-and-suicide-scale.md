# Youth mental health and suicide in New Zealand — the crisis is real and unequal, but nobody can yet show which services are working

## The problem, in plain language

Nearly one in four young New Zealanders aged 15–24 now reports high psychological distress — roughly double the level of five years ago — and New Zealand sits among the worst wealthy countries for adolescent suicide. Māori, Pacific, disabled, rainbow and care-experienced young people carry a much heavier share of this burden, and where a young person lives matters too: distress and going without wanted care vary roughly two-fold across regions. Government has expanded free early-support services, but the public record cannot yet show whether that money and effort is actually improving young people's lives.

## What we've learned so far

- **Youth distress has roughly doubled since 2019/20 and stayed high.** About 22.9% of 15–24-year-olds (roughly 156,000 young people) had high or very high psychological distress in 2024/25, up from 11.2% in 2019/20; young people also have the highest hospital-admission rate for intentional self-harm of any age group (confidence: Medium) — [baseline finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-baseline.md)
- **The burden is starkly unequal.** Reported distress in 2024/25 was 27.1% for Māori young people, 30.8% for Pacific young people and 70.5% for disabled young people aged 15–24; young people who have been involved with Oranga Tamariki were more than four times as likely to report a past-year suicide attempt as peers (confidence: Medium) — [baseline finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-baseline.md), [identity-responsive care finding](../research/findings/other/identity-responsive-youth-care.md)
- **Where a young person lives now visibly matters — new since the last update.** Official pooled survey data (2022/23–2024/25), recovered in July 2026 after earlier fetching problems, shows youth distress ranges roughly two-fold across the four health regions — from about 15% in Midland | Te Manawa Taki up to about 31% in Central | Te Ikaroa — and the two regions with the highest rates of youth distress (Central and the South Island) are also where the largest share of young people report going without wanted mental-health care. District-level figures spread even wider, from about 13% (Waikato) to about 35% (Capital and Coast) (confidence: Medium) — [baseline finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-baseline.md)
- **New Zealand is among the worst high-income countries for adolescent and under-30 suicide — but "worst at everything" overstates it.** UNICEF ranked New Zealand last of 36 wealthy countries on child mental wellbeing, driven by the highest adolescent suicide rate in that comparison; distress, self-harm and service measures are counted too differently across countries to rank fairly (confidence: Medium) — [international comparison finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-comparators.md)
- **The evidence supports layered prevention, not one hero programme.** The strongest evidence backs combining population-wide measures (like restricting access to means of suicide and safe media reporting), early low-barrier youth support, and direct clinical care for those in crisis; school programmes and digital tools like the NZ-made SPARX app show promise but depend on sustained engagement and referral pathways (confidence: Medium) — [interventions finding](../research/findings/other/youth-suicide-early-mental-health-interventions.md)
- **Free early support now exists in every district, but specialist care is the bottleneck.** Only 69.9% of under-19s were seen by specialist services within three weeks in 2024/25 (worst of any age group), their referrals were declined most often (14.9%), and 19–24-year-olds were the only group whose specialist access *fell* over five years (confidence: Medium) — [service access finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-service-access.md)
- **Services are reaching Māori and Pacific young people, but no one can show the care is working.** Māori and Pacific youth use the new early-support services above their population share, yet outcome measurement is inconsistent, national collection is unreliable, and no evaluation has directly tested whether culturally tailored programmes improve symptoms more than mainstream ones (confidence: Medium) — [identity-responsive care finding](../research/findings/other/identity-responsive-youth-care.md)
- **Funding is moving in an evidence-consistent direction, but alignment to need is unproven.** The big investments (Access and Choice's $664 million, ~$36 million a year for suicide prevention, Gumboot Friday's $24 million) match what the evidence broadly recommends, but public data cannot show whether spend matches need by age, ethnicity, region, disability or severity — and the Auditor-General criticised how the Gumboot Friday contract was awarded (confidence: Medium) — [funding alignment finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-funding-alignment.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether any of the funded services actually improve outcomes.** Every finding that touched services hit the same wall: reach and wait times are published, but outcome data is inconsistently collected and nationally unreliable. This is the load-bearing gap across the whole stream.
- **Exact youth suicide rates for small subgroups and regions.** Official suicide counts for youth subgroups are small, often suppressed or provisional, and the confirmed-death data runs years behind — so subgroup "rankings" should not be trusted cell-by-cell. (Youth *distress* by region, previously listed here as unknown, has now been recovered — see above.)
- **Whether the new regional pattern holds up.** The two-fold regional spread in youth distress and unmet need comes from a single official Ministry of Health pooled release — no independent source corroborates it yet, some district-level figures are flagged low-quality, and the public tables cannot cross region with ethnicity or deprivation for young people.
- **How bad New Zealand's adolescent suicide rate really is versus peers.** UNICEF's headline figure (17.1 per 100,000) and the WHO's harmonised estimate (9.7) differ because they use different datasets and periods; both say New Zealand is high, but the exact international rank is method-dependent.
- **Rainbow and disabled young people's service coverage.** They are highly visible in the need data and in suicide-prevention policy, but nearly invisible in public service-coverage and outcome reporting.
- **What young people and whānau themselves say works.** Several findings flag that the public numbers can't speak to trust, cultural safety or lived experience — this needs people with that authority, not more desk research.
- A single figure worth flagging as single-sourced: the 370-per-100,000 self-harm hospitalisation rate for 15–24-year-olds comes from one national monitoring report, though other official sources independently support the broader pattern.

## What we could do about it

- **Publish a plain-language "state of youth mental health" explainer that stitches the scattered official numbers into one readable picture — now including the regional breakdown.** Helps: charity workers, funders, journalists and councils who currently have to read six government reports to get the baseline; the newly recovered region and district figures make it useful to local audiences too. Effort: Small. Supported by [baseline finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium) and [international comparison finding](../research/findings/other/nz-youth-mental-health-comparators.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful handling of suppressed/small-count figures, clear flagging that the regional pattern rests on one official release, and a refresh plan when new surveys land.
- **Build a public map of who funds what in youth mental health, and what outcome reporting each stream does (or doesn't) publish.** Helps: funders and advocates deciding where money or pressure is most useful. Effort: Medium. Supported by [funding alignment finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-funding-alignment.md) (confidence: Medium) and [service access finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-service-access.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: provider-level contract data that is only partly public today, possibly via official information requests.
- **Write a plain-language "what the evidence supports" guide for community groups choosing youth mental-health activities.** Helps: small NGOs, schools and marae-based groups picking interventions without overclaiming what any programme can do. Effort: Small. Supported by [interventions finding](../research/findings/other/youth-suicide-early-mental-health-interventions.md) (confidence: Medium) and [identity-responsive care finding](../research/findings/other/identity-responsive-youth-care.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by people with clinical, Māori, Pacific and lived-experience authority before publication.
- **Document the youth outcome-data gap as a specific, evidence-cited advocacy case for publishing what is already collected.** Helps: anyone pushing for accountability — the services collect outcome tools now, but the data never surfaces publicly. Effort: Medium. Supported by [service access finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-service-access.md) (confidence: Medium), [identity-responsive care finding](../research/findings/other/identity-responsive-youth-care.md) (confidence: Medium) and [funding alignment finding](../research/findings/other/youth-mental-health-funding-alignment.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: engagement with data-privacy and Māori data-sovereignty considerations the findings flag.
