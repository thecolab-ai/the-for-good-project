# Aged care in New Zealand: quality, staffing, and who pays for rest-home care

## The problem, in plain language

Around 34,500 New Zealanders live in rest homes and other residential aged care, and that number is projected to grow by roughly half within a decade. Families face a confusing payment system, care quality is hard to compare between homes, and the sector cannot hold onto enough nurses to keep all its beds open. The government is deciding now — through 2026 and 2027 — how to reform the system, so good public evidence matters at this moment.

*This overview is a draft awaiting a human steward's direction decision — no direction has been set yet.*

## What we've learned so far

- **What families pay is capped, but the system is a maze.** Residents pay a means-tested contribution up to a regional weekly cap ($1,513-$1,634 from July 2026); the government subsidises people under the asset threshold and offers interest-free loans against the family home. Premium room charges of $10-$85 a day are not publicly funded. In 2022/23, the observed residential-care split was about $1.35 billion public funding and $1.01 billion resident fees, roughly 57/43. (confidence: Medium) — [funding split finding](../research/findings/other/nz-residential-care-funding-split.md)
- **For families, the hardest moments are practical and come in a fixed sequence.** The journey runs from an emotional "is home still safe?" decision, through a formal needs assessment, to comparing homes using scattered signals, then a paperwork chain — subsidy application, asset test, loan. Families can be liable for the full cost of care while assessments are processed, with refunds backdated at most 90 days, and the interest-free loan can take 6-8 weeks. Premium room charges are the clearest out-of-pocket trap, and Māori, Pacific and Asian families face added cultural-safety and navigation barriers. (confidence: Medium) — [family pain points finding](../research/findings/other/aged-care-family-pain-points.md)
- **Demand is likely to grow sharply.** Health NZ's public model projects average residents rising from about 34,500 now to 48,100-52,200 by 2035/36, with pressure concentrated in the oldest age groups and in places such as Canterbury, Nelson Marlborough, Waitematā and Auckland. A Sapere review warns of an almost 12,000-bed shortfall by 2032 if building continues at historic rates. (confidence: Medium) — [demand baseline finding](../research/findings/other/arc-demand-baseline-2035.md)
- **Nurse shortages are the clearest operational constraint.** In 2023, 83% of facilities were not fully staffed with registered nurses, annual registered-nurse turnover was about one third, and more than 1,200 beds had closed because of nurse shortages. The likely link to quality is strong, but no public NZ study yet proves the chain from staffing levels to complaints, audit failures or resident harm. (confidence: Medium) — [staffing constraints finding](../research/findings/other/arc-staffing-constraints.md)
- **Quality is regulated, but not turned into a clear public picture.** Every home is audited, complaints are tracked, and enforcement powers exist, but audits, complaints, enforcement and resident outcomes are not publicly joined into one defensible comparison. A 2012 Auditor-General review found the audit process was still more about compliance than outcomes, and the stream did not find a newer review overturning that. (confidence: Medium) — [quality variation finding](../research/findings/other/arc-quality-variation.md)
- **Complaint numbers are falling, but the themes are stubborn — and nobody can prove which one now leads.** A decade ago the health watchdog found poor communication with family was the single most common issue in rest-home complaints (58% of 2010-2014 cases). Complaints naming a rest home fell to 154 in 2024/25, and today's public reporting no longer breaks rest-home themes down the old way — so it cannot be said whether family communication still ranks first. What recurs now is a cluster: care and treatment quality, communication with families, slow escalation when a resident deteriorates, safety events such as falls, dementia-related distress, and workforce strain. (confidence: Medium) — [complaint themes finding](../research/findings/other/arc-complaint-themes.md)
- **The public audit data is more usable than it first looked, but still incomplete.** A first aggregation found the Ministry's public rest-home data can support a facility-level dataset of premises, beds, services, legal provider names, auditor, certification period, audit links and current corrective actions. It cannot, from public sources alone, become a full provider-level audit-and-complaints dataset or a clean ultimate-owner/brand comparison. (confidence: Medium) — [audit aggregation finding](../research/findings/other/rest-home-audit-aggregation.md)
- **Reform decisions are live.** A Ministerial Advisory Group started in January 2026 and is expected to report around mid-2026 on funding, cost-sharing, contracting and regulation, with funding-model changes anticipated in 2027. A Parliamentary inquiry has already made 14 recommendations, the Aged Care Commissioner has made 20, and a 4% funding uplift for 2026/27 was announced in June 2026. (confidence: High) — [reform decision points finding](../research/findings/other/aged-care-reforms-decision-points.md)
- **Australia and England show useful design patterns and warnings.** Both publish public aged-care quality comparison layers, and Australia's ratings include a staffing component. They also show that "no rating" cases must be explained clearly, and that means testing does not by itself remove out-of-pocket hardship. (confidence: Medium) — [overseas models finding](../research/findings/other/nz-lesson-from-australia-england-oecd-ltc-models.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether short-staffing is causing measurable resident harm.** The staffing link is plausible and repeatedly asserted, but the stream found no NZ study statistically connecting staffing levels to complaints, audit failures or resident outcomes.
- **What families actually complain about today.** No public dataset codes current rest-home complaints the way the 2010-2014 analysis did, and complaints flow through several channels — the home itself, the health watchdog, the Ministry, disputes bodies — that no one publicly joins up. Falling watchdog numbers therefore cannot be read as falling dissatisfaction.
- **How often the payment traps actually bite.** The rules on interim full-cost liability, the 90-day refund limit, loan processing time and premium-room rights are official, but nothing public says how many families are caught by them — advice-line enquiry counts (such as Citizens Advice) are not published.
- **What a fair public comparison should look like.** The audit aggregation finding narrows the problem: facility-level audit evidence can be gathered, but complaint data, resident outcomes, serious-complaint inspections, issues-based audits and ultimate-owner groupings are not publicly joinable from current sources.
- **Whether audit signals match resident and whānau experience.** Corrective actions and certification periods show compliance variation, but a simple score could mislead if it ignores acuity, complaints that never become public, staffing reality, culture, or "no data" cases.
- **Why Māori, Pacific and Asian older people show lower recorded use of residential care.** Māori under-representation is better corroborated than the Pacific and Asian comparisons; lower use could reflect access barriers, cultural safety concerns, cost, family care, preference for care at home, or a mix. The pain-points research adds direct signals — culturally safe care, means-testing that fits poorly with collectively held Māori land, and a language/culture navigation gap for older Asian New Zealanders — but they are mostly qualitative.
- **How current the funding split is.** The observed public/resident dollar split is from 2022/23; later figures are modelled or whole-system rather than observed residential-care-only flows. Premium-room prevalence is also not publicly quantified.
- Several load-bearing figures remain single-sourced: the 83% registered-nurse understaffing rate, the almost 12,000-bed shortfall, the Health NZ projection range, the Ministry CSV aggregation, the 6-8 week loan processing time, and the unverified claim that a guarantor cannot be required for admission.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language family guide to paying for residential care** — one clear explainer of the needs assessment, subsidy, asset test, loan, weekly cap, premium charges and the 90-day refund rule, which currently live across many official pages. Helps: families facing a placement decision under stress. Effort: Small. Supported by the [funding split finding](../research/findings/other/nz-residential-care-funding-split.md) (confidence: Medium) and the [family pain points finding](../research/findings/other/aged-care-family-pain-points.md) (confidence: Medium), which maps where families struggle most — the assessment gate, interim payment liability, premium-room rights — and so gives the guide its shape. Would need: annual checking against current official rates, and a home where families can actually find it.
- **A rest-home audit evidence panel, not a "best home" score** — show each facility's public audit basics, corrective actions, risk levels, certification period and audit links with clear caveats about what is missing. Helps: families choosing a home; advocates, researchers and journalists scrutinising the sector. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [audit aggregation finding](../research/findings/other/rest-home-audit-aggregation.md) (confidence: Medium), [quality variation finding](../research/findings/other/arc-quality-variation.md) (confidence: Medium), [overseas models finding](../research/findings/other/nz-lesson-from-australia-england-oecd-ltc-models.md) (confidence: Medium), and the [complaint themes finding](../research/findings/other/arc-complaint-themes.md) (confidence: Medium), which confirms complaint data cannot currently be joined in and the panel must say so. Would need: a defensible "no score unless justified" design, stable extraction from public pages, and review by people who understand aged-care audits.
- **A reform watch-brief through the 2026-2027 decision window** — track what the Ministerial Advisory Group recommends, what ministers accept, and which existing recommendations are implemented, delayed or dropped. Helps: older people's advocates, funders and community organisations trying to respond while decisions are live. Effort: Small. Supported by the [reform decision points finding](../research/findings/other/aged-care-reforms-decision-points.md) (confidence: High), [funding split finding](../research/findings/other/nz-residential-care-funding-split.md) (confidence: Medium), and [staffing constraints finding](../research/findings/other/arc-staffing-constraints.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: someone to maintain it past a single snapshot.
- **An equity-of-access deep dive before any community-facing solution** — investigate why Māori, Pacific and Asian older people show lower recorded use, including lived-experience and cultural-safety perspectives the current public evidence lacks. Helps: communities the current system may be quietly failing. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [demand baseline finding](../research/findings/other/arc-demand-baseline-2035.md) (confidence: Medium overall; Low-Medium for some exact Pacific and Asian comparisons), [funding split finding](../research/findings/other/nz-residential-care-funding-split.md) (confidence: Medium), and the [family pain points finding](../research/findings/other/aged-care-family-pain-points.md) (confidence: Medium), which surfaces concrete barriers — cultural safety, collectively held land in the means test, and language navigation gaps. Would need: partnership with people who hold that lived experience; public documents alone are not enough.
