# Mental-health inpatient conditions in New Zealand

## The problem, in plain language

People held in mental-health and intellectual-disability inpatient units can be in very restrictive settings, but the public record about those places is scattered across individual Ombudsman inspection reports, delayed national statistics, and agency statements. That makes it hard for advocates, funders, public officials, whanau, and the public to see whether serious failures are isolated events or part of a wider pattern. This stream is about making the existing public evidence legible without identifying patients or staff, and it is now waiting on a human steward to decide its direction.

## What we've learned so far

- **The Ombudsman's inspection record is public, but it is not a clear national picture.** At least 41 relevant public report pages were verified, with repeat issues around buildings, seclusion and restraint, over-capacity, staffing, privacy, and mixed legal status; there is no clean facility-level roll-up in the public record found so far. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/opcat-inspection-catalogue.md)
- **Wakari Ward 10A is a live, serious case, but much of the 2026 detail is still not in a full public report.** The Ombudsman's March 2026 findings are public through the Ombudsman release and reporting, while the full report, investigation terms, relocation outcomes, and replacement capacity were not yet verified in the merged evidence. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/wakari-10a-claims-confirmed.md)
- **The starting facts needed correction before any public work uses them.** The Wakari findings are primary-sourced, but the "Waikato mental-health ward slipped to 2027" story was a conflation with a different general ward; the actual Waikato mental-health facility had a separate operator-stated timeline. (confidence: High) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/mh-inpatient-inspections-baseline.md)
- **Capacity is hard to describe because the national bed count changes depending on what is counted.** The adult acute series was about 600 beds and static through 2022, Health NZ later cited a broader 900-plus acute figure, the infrastructure programme is real but was reset for cost and timing problems, and Ward 10A's secure intellectual-disability beds were closed to new admissions while acute wards were over capacity. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/mh-inpatient-capacity-closures-pipeline.md)
- **The discharge bottleneck may be as important as the number of acute beds.** The merged evidence found no public national count of community, residential, or step-down mental-health and addiction beds, while public reporting repeatedly points to lack of discharge placements as a driver of acute-ward over-capacity. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/community-mh-bed-discharge-bottleneck.md)
- **Official seclusion data is improving nationally, but still shows major inequity and arrives late.** Adult-inpatient seclusion has fallen in the Ministry's long-run series, but Maori remain much more likely to be secluded than non-Maori, restraint reporting is weaker than seclusion reporting, and public statistics can lag the events by 15-21 months. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seclusion-restraint-nz-trends.md)
- **There is a legal framework, but public evidence does not yet prove whether it worked in practice.** Mental-health compulsory treatment and intellectual-disability compulsory care sit under different Acts; Ward 10A is under the intellectual-disability law, the Ombudsman's detention inspections are recommendation-based, and the Mental Health Bill does not replace the intellectual-disability law. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-inpatient-mh-id-rights-and-law.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- The full March 2026 Ward 10A inspection report was not in the public evidence used here. That weakens any detailed claim about the exact findings, legal framing, recommendations, or agency response beyond the Ombudsman's release and same-day reporting.
- Health NZ and Ministry statements say admissions stopped, patients were being relocated, closure was agreed, and investigations or reviews were planned. The merged evidence does not independently verify completed relocation, equivalent replacement beds, investigation terms, patient or whanau experience, or whether remedial actions worked.
- The public bed picture is still not apples-to-apples: adult acute, all-age acute, forensic, secure intellectual-disability, and community/residential beds are counted differently or not published together. This makes a simple national "bed shortage" number unreliable.
- The national seclusion trend and the inspection record point in different directions at the same time: official adult-inpatient seclusion has fallen, but inspection reports still show severe local failures, recording problems, and current cases that lagged statistics may not capture.
- The Ombudsman catalogue work is strong enough to show fragmentation, but not complete enough to be a structured national database. A full export or text extraction could change counts by theme, facility, and recommendation status.
- The legal finding maps duties and safeguards; it does not decide whether any particular Ward 10A practice breached the law. That needs legal authority and, given the subject matter, disability-rights, clinical, Maori health, and lived-experience review.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language inspection map could show each public Ombudsman report by facility, date, theme, and follow-up status.** -- helps: advocates, funders, public officials, journalists, and community organisations trying to see the pattern. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/opcat-inspection-catalogue.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/mh-inpatient-inspections-baseline.md) (confidence: High). Would need: careful source checking, no patient or staff identifiers, and clear labels where follow-up status is incomplete.
- **A capacity-and-flow explainer could separate acute beds, secure intellectual-disability beds, community residential beds, and discharge bottlenecks.** -- helps: people trying to understand why wards can be over capacity even when new buildings are being built. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/mh-inpatient-capacity-closures-pipeline.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/community-mh-bed-discharge-bottleneck.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: either official data or very clear "unknown" labels for unpublished community and replacement-bed counts.
- **A seclusion, restraint, and rights briefing could translate the official statistics and legal frameworks into plain language.** -- helps: charities, rights groups, and decision-makers who need to understand what is measured, what is missing, and why Ward 10A sits under a different law from many mental-health units. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seclusion-restraint-nz-trends.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-inpatient-mh-id-rights-and-law.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: legal, clinical, disability-rights, Maori health, and lived-experience review before public use.
- **A Wakari claims-versus-confirmed tracker could follow only public, de-identified facts about the full report, investigations, closure, and replacement capacity.** -- helps: watchdogs and community organisations avoid mixing confirmed findings with agency promises. Effort: Small. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/wakari-10a-claims-confirmed.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/mh-inpatient-capacity-closures-pipeline.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: regular re-checks of official publications and strict privacy guardrails.
