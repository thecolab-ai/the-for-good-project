# NZ plastic recycling: what we know, and what we still can't prove

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealanders are asked to sort plastic carefully, but the public evidence does not support one simple answer to "how much really gets recycled?" The clearest current number is for plastic packaging, not all plastic, and even that number leaves major gaps about landfill, stockpiles, rejected material and what happens after exports arrive overseas. This research round is complete and the stream is now **waiting on a human steward to decide its direction**.

## What we've learned so far

- **The best current number is narrow: in 2022, about 17.1% of plastic packaging was recorded as reprocessed onshore or exported for recycling.** The same estimate says 263,231 tonnes of plastic packaging were placed on the NZ market, 28,954 tonnes were reprocessed onshore, 16,159 tonnes were exported, and 218,118 tonnes were not recycled through those reported channels. It does not prove where that residual all went. (confidence: Medium) — [mass-balance finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastics-mass-balance.md)
- **A whole-of-plastics answer is not available from public data.** The older all-plastics figures are useful context, but they mix dated sources and big unknowns; current public data still cannot cleanly split all plastic by collection, onshore processing, export, landfill and leakage. (confidence: Medium) — [mass-balance finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastics-mass-balance.md)
- **Plastic-waste exports did not end after China's restrictions; they shifted.** Public trade data show HS 3915 plastic-waste exports around 21,000 tonnes in 2022 and around 27,000 tonnes in 2024 and 2025, mainly to Malaysia and Indonesia in recent years. The finding is much stronger on declared destination countries than on what happened after arrival. (confidence: Medium) — [export-destinations finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-export-destinations.md)
- **NZ appears behind better-performing plastic-packaging systems, but comparisons must be handled carefully.** The closest available comparison puts NZ's 17.1% packaging rate below Australia at about 19-20% and well below the EU average of 42.1%, while the best EU performers are above 50%. These are not perfectly like-for-like because countries use different scopes and calculation points. (confidence: Medium) — [comparators finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-comparators.md)
- **The strongest live NZ policy change is clearer household collection, not a complete recycling system.** Since February 2024, council household kerbside collections have standardised accepted plastic to bottles, trays and containers numbered 1, 2 or 5. That can reduce confusion and contamination, but it does not by itself make producers redesign packaging, pay the full costs, or buy recycled plastic. (confidence: Medium) — [policy-levers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-policy-levers.md)
- **The main barriers are structural, not just household behaviour.** The evidence repeatedly points to small domestic scale, long transport distances, uneven sorting and reprocessing capacity, contamination costs, weak demand for some recycled plastics, and hard-to-recycle designs such as mixed films, coloured or lined PET, small lids and caps. (confidence: Medium) — [economics finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-economics.md)
- **The bigger system levers are still unfinished or only modelled.** Better-performing systems tend to combine producer responsibility, public reporting, deposit return for beverage containers, recycled-content demand and reliable data. NZ has proposed or partial versions of several of these, including plastic packaging product stewardship, but they are not yet an operating regulated system. (confidence: Medium) — [policy-levers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-policy-levers.md), [comparators finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-comparators.md), [economics finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-economics.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The 17.1% packaging rate is load-bearing and not independently measured twice.** MfE repeats the rounded figure, but the finding treats that as likely derived from the same PPPS/Valpak estimate rather than a separate official measurement.
- **The fate of the unrecycled packaging residual is not known publicly.** The evidence can calculate the gap between packaging placed on market and packaging reprocessed or exported, but cannot split that gap into landfill, rejected bales, stockpiles, litter, energy recovery or other losses.
- **Export destination is not final treatment.** Stats NZ and trade data can show declared destination countries; MfE says there is no ability to track waste after it leaves New Zealand, so claims that exported material was actually recycled overseas remain weak without audits or processor records.
- **HS 3915 does not capture every exported plastic material.** The export finding says flaked or shredded plastic sits outside the plastic-waste category used in the main destination table, so the export picture is useful but incomplete.
- **The comparison with Australia and Europe is directionally useful, not exact.** NZ's best number is packaging placed on market, Australia's strongest current dataset is all-plastics end-of-life, EU figures use generated plastic packaging, and deposit-return rates cover only beverage containers.
- **No public evidence yet shows the tonne impact of the 2024 kerbside standardisation or proposed product-stewardship changes.** The policy direction is visible, but measured effects after implementation are not.
- **Several practical costs are still hidden.** Public sources do not expose contract-level council costs, material recovery facility rejection rates, processor yields, freight rates, commercial margins or destination-country recycling yields.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language "where does NZ plastic go?" explainer** — explain the 2022 packaging flow, what 17.1% means, what it does not mean, and why plastics 1, 2 and 5 are treated differently at kerbside. Helps: households, educators, councils and funders who need a careful baseline. Effort: Small. Supported by the [mass-balance finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastics-mass-balance.md) (Medium), [policy-levers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-policy-levers.md) (Medium), and [export-destinations finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-export-destinations.md) (Medium). Would need: careful wording so it does not imply we know the final fate of the residual or exported material.
- **An export transparency tracker** — keep a simple public table of plastic-waste export volumes and declared destination countries, with clear warnings that destination is not proof of recycling. Helps: journalists, councils, researchers and advocates checking claims about overseas recycling. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [export-destinations finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-export-destinations.md) (Medium) and [mass-balance finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastics-mass-balance.md) (Medium). Would need: a reproducible Stats NZ workflow and a decision about whether to also chase flaked and shredded plastic categories.
- **A policy and data-gap brief for product stewardship** — turn the evidence into a short brief on what public reporting, producer responsibility, recycled-content demand and deposit-return design would need to prove progress. Helps: policy teams, NGOs, councils and funders deciding where to focus attention. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [policy-levers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-policy-levers.md) (Medium), [comparators finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-comparators.md) (Medium), and [economics finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-economics.md) (Medium). Would need: a human decision on audience and tone, because this can easily become advocacy rather than evidence.
- **A bottleneck and demand map for recyclable plastics** — map where public evidence points to sorting, transport, reprocessing and buyer-demand constraints for PET, HDPE, PP, soft plastics, lids and caps. Helps: councils, community recyclers, procurement teams and potential funders looking for practical constraints rather than slogans. Effort: Large. Supported by the [economics finding](../research/findings/other/plastic-recycling-economics.md) (Medium) and [policy-levers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-plastic-policy-levers.md) (Medium). Would need: operator validation, because the public record does not reveal enough contract-level cost or yield data.
