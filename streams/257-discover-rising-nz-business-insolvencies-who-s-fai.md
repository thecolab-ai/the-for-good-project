# Rising business failures in New Zealand

## The problem, in plain language

More New Zealand companies are being formally wound up than a few years ago. That matters because each failure can leave workers, suppliers, Inland Revenue, owners, and local communities trying to recover money or jobs after the business has run out of workable options.

## What we've learned so far

- **Company liquidations have roughly doubled since 2021, and the first half of 2026 is still elevated, but the rise is not literally month-by-month.** Liquidations rose from 1,379 in 2021 to 2,867 in 2025, and January-June 2026 was higher than the same period in recent years; the safer wording is that 2026 is running above recent years, not that every month keeps rising. (confidence: High) — [trend finding](../research/findings/other/nz-insolvency-trend-primary-registers.md)
- **This is mainly a liquidation story, not a broad surge in every insolvency process.** Receiverships and voluntary administrations are much smaller than liquidations and both peaked in 2024 rather than 2025 or 2026. (confidence: High) — [trend finding](../research/findings/other/nz-insolvency-trend-primary-registers.md)
- **The best current public cut points to construction, accommodation and food services, and Auckland as areas of higher exposure, but the size picture is still only a proxy.** The sector and region pattern is supported by Companies Office-derived Deloitte figures checked against Stats NZ denominators, but the finding could not reproduce the full cut directly from Companies Office bulk files — a re-check on 8 July 2026 confirmed those files sit behind a formal access-request gate that no automated tool can pass — and company size cannot be joined cleanly from the public fields available here. (confidence: Medium) — [exposure finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-exposure.md)
- **Inland Revenue debt enforcement is the clearest evidenced near-term driver, but it does not explain every failure.** IRD reports statutory demands, company liquidations, and a large overdue-tax pipeline; wider pressure from interest rates, demand, costs, and post-Covid clean-up is plausible context, not a proven cause for each appointment. (confidence: Medium) — [drivers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-drivers.md)
- **The payment order in liquidation is clear; the real-world recovery picture is not.** Employees have preferential claims up to a cap, IRD has some preferential tax claims, and ordinary unsecured creditors sit late in the queue, but no official public NZ dataset shows aggregate creditor losses, employee shortfalls, or cents-in-the-dollar recovery. (confidence: High) — [ripple-effects finding](../research/findings/other/liquidation-creditor-employee-recovery-gaps.md)
- **Help exists before insolvency, but a small operator has to piece it together.** IRD, ITS, Companies Register guidance, RBP, Business Mentors NZ, and paid practitioners all cover pieces of the journey; the gap appears to be early, visible, affordable restructuring help, especially for incorporated micro-operators. (confidence: Medium) — [support-gap finding](../research/findings/other/pre-insolvency-support-gaps-small-business.md)
- **This topic can be studied without naming failed businesses or people.** The framing work found public routes for trend, sector, region, and support research using aggregate data only. (confidence: Medium) — [framing finding](../research/findings/other/nz-business-insolvencies-framing.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **How much of the rise is actually caused by IRD action.** IRD enforcement is the strongest evidenced driver, but the public register data used here does not attribute each liquidation to IRD, another creditor, a shareholder decision, or a secured creditor.
- **Whether the sector and region pattern holds after a primary bulk-data reproduction.** The current concentration finding is useful but still leans on Deloitte's Companies Office-derived cuts, and the business-size answer is weaker because public company records do not expose employee count or turnover in a clean join. A re-attempt on 8 July 2026 settled *why* this is stuck: the underlying Companies Office bulk files are only released after a named person or organisation completes an access request and signs a data agreement, so this gap can only close if a human on the project applies for that access — better research tools will not fix it.
- **How much workers and creditors actually lose.** The law and payment order are clear, but recovery rates, employee shortfalls, and unsecured-creditor losses are not published as aggregate NZ statistics.
- **How existing support works in practice.** The support-gap finding reads published guidance well, but it does not include lived experience from owners, accountants, financial mentors, insolvency practitioners, or frontline advisers.
- **Whether NZ truly lacks a wage-guarantee-style backstop is less firmly sourced than the payment-order findings.** The comparison with Australia and the UK is useful, but the NZ absence framing is explicitly weaker and partly single-sourced.
- **The Official Assignee data is only a subset of liquidations.** The Companies Office register is the better whole-market trend source, while the ITS/Official Assignee series needs care and still has some reconciliation questions.

## What we could do about it

- **Create a plain-language "business in trouble: start here" pathway map** — helps: small business owners, accountants, financial mentors, and community advisers trying to find the right next step before liquidation. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [support-gap finding](../research/findings/other/pre-insolvency-support-gaps-small-business.md) (confidence: Medium) and [drivers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-drivers.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful wording that signposts existing help without giving legal or insolvency advice.
- **Build an aggregate insolvency watch brief for local and sector support planning** — helps: councils, funders, business associations, and advice services see where formal failures are rising without naming individual businesses. Effort: Large. Supported by the [trend finding](../research/findings/other/nz-insolvency-trend-primary-registers.md) (confidence: High), [exposure finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-exposure.md) (confidence: Medium), and [drivers finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-drivers.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: Companies Office bulk-data access (now confirmed to require a formal access request and signed data agreement by a person or organisation — it cannot be automated), hidden tiny counts, and clear notes on what is primary versus proxy data.
- **Run a privacy-safe recovery-rate pilot from liquidator reports** — helps: worker advocates, supplier-support groups, funders, and policymakers understand whether the formal payment order translates into real recovery. Effort: Large. Supported by the [ripple-effects finding](../research/findings/other/liquidation-creditor-employee-recovery-gaps.md) (confidence: High). Would need: a sampling method, removal of identifying details, and a first check that reports record recovery outcomes consistently enough to aggregate.
- **Test a targeted early-support pilot for the most exposed groups** — helps: small operators in sectors or regions showing higher exposure, such as construction, accommodation and food services, or Auckland, if the steward accepts the current Medium-confidence targeting or asks for primary reproduction first. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [exposure finding](../research/findings/other/nz-company-insolvency-exposure.md) (confidence: Medium) and [support-gap finding](../research/findings/other/pre-insolvency-support-gaps-small-business.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a human decision on whether the evidence is strong enough to target now or whether to verify the Companies Office bulk-data cut first.
