# NZ dairy farming and tightening freshwater rules: how much milk production is actually at risk?

## The problem, in plain language

Dairy is one of New Zealand's biggest export earners, and almost every dairy
farm depends on permissions ("consents") from its regional council to take
water and manage effluent. Those permissions are being renewed under stricter
freshwater rules, with a big wave of decisions due around the end of 2027.
Nobody — including the government — currently publishes a clear picture of how
much milk production those tightening permissions actually put at risk, which
makes it hard for communities, councils and farmers to plan.

This overview synthesises the stream's research to date. **The stream is
now waiting on its human steward to decide direction.**

## What we've learned so far

- **The headline question still can't be answered from public data.** No public
  dataset joins consent expiry dates to milk production, so "how much
  production is at risk" is not currently computable nationally — councils
  publish consent records and the dairy industry publishes production figures,
  but nothing public connects the two (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/dairy-consent-expiry-denominator.md)
- **Where counting is possible, the exposure is large.** In Waikato, roughly
  2,374 dairy-purpose water consents covering about 212 million cubic metres a
  year expire by 2031; in Canterbury, 200 dairy-effluent consents covering
  about 219,000 consented animals expire by 2031 (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/dairy-consent-expiry-denominator.md)
- **Expiring dairy consents can now be counted in five of the six major dairy
  regions — but only counted, not linked to production.** New classification
  work shows Southland (roughly 818 dairy water-take and 1,106 dairy-effluent
  consents expiring by the end of 2036), Otago (roughly 116 and 294) and Bay of
  Plenty (38 and 215) can be screened from their public consent maps, joining
  Waikato and Canterbury. Northland's public layers lack expiry dates entirely,
  and each region uses a different data format, so the counts are screening
  estimates rather than comparable measurements (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/four-region-dairy-consent-classification.md)
- **Much of the pressure is bunched at the end of 2027.** Many expiring
  consents have been extended only to 31 December 2027, councils must notify
  new freshwater plans by that same date, and water-related consents cannot be
  extended past a 35-year total life (confidence: High) —
  [finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-consent-comparators.md)
- **The first look at actual renewal decisions found no refusals — but no
  free passes either.** In the only three public dairy renewal decisions found
  and read (in Canterbury and Southland), every one was granted, yet each
  carried tightening in another form: stricter conditions such as river-flow
  cut-offs, or a shorter term than the farmer asked for. These three were
  publicly notified (i.e. more contested) cases and no register records renewal
  outcomes nationally, so no national "renewal success rate" can be drawn from
  them (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/other/dairy-renewal-outcomes.md)
- **The rules that really bite are regional limits, not national paperwork.**
  Catchment-level caps on nitrogen and water (Lake Rotorua, Canterbury's
  Selwyn-Waihora, Otago's water-permit transition) set numbers farms must stay
  within; national metering, stock-exclusion and farm-plan rules are mostly
  reporting and practice requirements (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-dairy-headroom-rules.md)
- **So far there is no proven nationwide production drop.** National
  milksolids rose 2.9% in 2024/25 even as cow numbers fell; where caps exist,
  the observed effects are changed nitrogen allowances, consents and land use
  — the big projected dairy losses (e.g. Rotorua) are forecasts, not measured
  results (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-caps-production-effects.md)
- **Farmers are adapting, but the costs are farm-specific and hard to
  untangle.** The verifiable published costs are narrow (e.g. a freshwater
  farm plan at roughly $1,500–$10,000 per farm); the bigger cost is changing
  the farm system — modelled nitrogen-reduction bundles cut profit 11–16% —
  and it's genuinely hard to tell freshwater-driven changes apart from
  responses to milk prices, interest rates and climate policy (confidence:
  Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/other/dairy-freshwater-adaptation-costs.md)
- **Compared with Australia, California and the Netherlands, New Zealand
  offers farmers the least formal security and the least visible adjustment
  support.** An NZ consent isn't property, there's no national water market or
  buy-back scheme, and NZ has the weakest public picture of who bears the
  adjustment costs (confidence: Medium) —
  [finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-consent-comparators.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The stream's central number is still missing.** We can now count expiring
  dairy consents in five regions, but nothing public links a consent to the
  milk production behind it, so "how much production is at risk" remains
  uncomputable. Northland is a blind spot (its public data has no expiry
  dates), and the five countable regions use different formats — Otago's dairy
  labels are free text, and in several regions one consent can appear as many
  map records — so cross-region totals shouldn't be added up without council
  confirmation.
- **What happens at renewal is now sampled, not measured.** Three decisions
  is far too few to generalise from, and because they were publicly notified
  they likely over-represent contested cases; routine, unnotified renewals —
  the majority — leave no accessible public record of what was asked for
  versus what was granted. Whether renewal typically bites hard or is mostly
  routine is still open.
- **Forecast versus observed.** The most dramatic numbers in the evidence
  (Lake Rotorua modelling of ~40% less milksolids by 2032) are
  pre-implementation forecasts. Observed data so far shows production holding
  up. These aren't contradictory — the caps mostly haven't bitten yet — but
  the gap between them is exactly what a future reader will want resolved.
- **The Canterbury water-take numbers are an over-count.** They use "pasture
  irrigation" as a stand-in for dairy and may double-count consents with
  multiple take points, so they're an upper bound, not a measurement.
- **Everything is provisional until the 2027 plan wave.** New regional
  freshwater plans (due to be notified by December 2027) and the replacement
  of the Resource Management Act could change both the rules and the
  timelines these findings describe.

## What we could do about it

- **Publish a public consent-expiry dashboard for the five countable regions
  (Waikato, Canterbury, Southland, Otago and Bay of Plenty)**, showing
  aggregate dairy consent numbers and expiry years without identifying any
  farm — helps: councils, sector groups and journalists who currently have no
  visibility of the 2027–2031 expiry wave. Effort: Medium. Supported by
  [the denominator finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/dairy-consent-expiry-denominator.md)
  (confidence: Medium) and
  [the four-region classification finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/four-region-dairy-consent-classification.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: council confirmation of what the data
  fields mean, de-duplication so multi-record consents aren't double-counted,
  and either Northland data in usable form or an honest "no data" marker for
  Northland.
- **Ask the five priority councils, through official information requests,
  for what farmers applied for versus what was granted in dairy renewals since
  2020** (volumes, discharge loads and consent terms, in aggregate) — helps:
  anyone who needs to know whether renewal actually tightens consents, which
  no public register currently records. Effort: Medium. Supported by
  [the renewal-outcomes finding](../research/findings/other/dairy-renewal-outcomes.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: councils to hold this in extractable form,
  and careful aggregation so no individual farm is identifiable.
- **Write a plain-language explainer on which freshwater rules actually
  constrain dairy farms and when**, distinguishing the regional limits that
  bite from the national reporting rules that don't, ahead of the December
  2027 plan deadline — helps: farmers, rural advisers and community groups
  navigating a confusing rule stack. Effort: Small. Supported by
  [the headroom-rules finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-dairy-headroom-rules.md)
  (confidence: Medium) and
  [the comparators finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-consent-comparators.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by someone with resource-management
  expertise, since the findings are explicit they aren't legal advice.
- **Track forecast-versus-observed outcomes in the three live capped
  catchments** (Lake Taupō, Lake Rotorua, Selwyn-Waihora) as councils publish
  audit and allowance data, testing whether the modelled production losses
  materialise — helps: policy-makers deciding whether caps of this design cost
  as much production as models predict. Effort: Medium. Supported by
  [the production-effects finding](../research/findings/other/freshwater-caps-production-effects.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: councils to actually publish audit
  outcomes and allowance-trading data over time.
