# Small charities miss grants they qualify for

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealand has hundreds of organisations that give out grants — lottery and government funds, pokie trusts, councils, and charitable trusts — but there is no single place where a community group can see them all. Small charities, most of them run entirely by volunteers, have to hunt across paid databases, library computers, and dozens of separate websites to find money they may be entitled to. The groups with the least spare time face the most work just to find out what exists.

## What we've learned so far

- **There is no official register of New Zealand's grant funders, and the information is genuinely scattered.** Funding rules live across at least three separate government systems, 32 pokie-trust websites, 67 local councils, trustee-company portals, and private databases — no one source covers them all. (confidence: High for the fragmentation; Medium for "no register exists") — [where grant information lives](../research/findings/grant-access/nz-grant-source-map.md), [is there a register of funders?](../research/findings/grant-access/nz-grant-funder-count-and-eligibility-data.md)
- **Nobody can say exactly how many funders or grants there are.** The only published funder count comes from one commercial database (1,650 funders); estimates of the number of grant schemes range from about 1,200 to about 3,400 depending on who is counting. The scale is "low thousands", but there is no audited figure. (confidence: Medium for the scale; Low for any exact count) — [is there a register of funders?](../research/findings/grant-access/nz-grant-funder-count-and-eligibility-data.md)
- **Finding grants today means a patchwork: paid databases, a library card, funder link lists, and volunteer labour.** The main databases charge subscriptions; the free route depends on whether your local council's library or a council-sponsored portal has paid for access — effectively a postcode lottery. After discovery, the work is still manual: spreadsheets of deadlines, document packs, and different paperwork for every funder. (confidence: Medium overall; High that the free-via-library channel exists, Low on how many councils actually provide it) — [how small charities find grants](../research/findings/grant-access/how-small-nz-charities-find-grants.md), [where the workflow breaks down](../research/findings/grant-access/small-charity-grant-workflow-failures.md)
- **The people expected to do this work are volunteers.** In the most recent official count (2018), 89% of New Zealand's roughly 116,000 non-profit organisations had no paid staff at all, and a sector programme exists specifically because grant writing takes time and skill volunteer groups often don't have. (confidence: High for the 2018 figure; Medium that it still holds today) — [how small charities find grants](../research/findings/grant-access/how-small-nz-charities-find-grants.md)
- **Grant money is highly concentrated:** one analysis found 9% of charities receive 91% of philanthropic and grant-maker giving. Discovery friction is one plausible contributor, not a proven cause. (confidence: Medium — a single analysis, using 2020-era data) — [how small charities find grants](../research/findings/grant-access/how-small-nz-charities-find-grants.md)
- **Public grants data records who got money, not what's on offer.** Government datasets cover past awards and declined applications; no official dataset lists current grant opportunities with their eligibility rules, deadlines, and application links. Building that would require assembling it from funder websites by hand (or licensing it from a commercial vendor). (confidence: Medium) — [what public data exists and what's missing](../research/findings/grant-access/grants-data-schema-gaps.md)
- **A public starting list of likely grant-giving charities is buildable.** The Charities Register's free open data identifies roughly 5,500 registered charities that both say they make grants and reported paying grants — about NZ$1.2 billion a year — though it misses government, council and corporate funders, and the figures come from self-reported filings. (confidence: Medium for the method; Low for the exact numbers, which are a single unverified calculation) — [a census of charitable grantmakers](../research/findings/grant-access/charities-register-grantmaker-census.md)
- **The size of the "missed grants" pool cannot be measured from public data.** What can be measured is unmet demand among groups that did apply — for example, in one six-month pokie-grants dataset, $70 million of requests were declined and half of successful applicants got less than they asked for. Groups that never applied leave no public trace. (confidence: Medium) — [can we measure missed funding?](../research/findings/grant-access/charity-grant-underapplication-measures.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The stream's core claim — that small charities miss grants *because discovery is hard* — is still plausible rather than proven.** Two findings independently rate it Low confidence: everything gathered so far shows the tools and the friction, not measured behaviour. No New Zealand survey asks charities how they actually find grants (the main sector survey measures funding adequacy, not discovery).
- **We don't know where groups actually lose out:** never discovering a grant, discovering it too late, misreading the rules, running out of volunteer time, or deciding it isn't worth the effort. Only interviews, a survey, or funder application logs can separate these.
- **Several load-bearing numbers rest on a single source:** the "9% of charities get 91% of giving" figure (one 2020 analysis), the 1,650-funder count (one vendor), and the 5,500-grantmaker / $1.2 billion census (one unreplicated data pull). None has been independently confirmed.
- **Free access coverage is unmapped.** The claim that "most" council libraries provide free database access is the vendor's own; nobody has checked which of the country's councils actually do.
- **The volunteer-capacity figure is from 2018** — the official statistics behind "89% have no paid staff" have not been updated since.
- **The findings agree with each other overall**, with one nuance worth noting: the commercial databases' own counts of what exists diverge widely (1,200 vs ~2,000 vs 3,361), which the findings treat as evidence that no ground truth exists — not as a resolved number.
