# NZ Super: what it costs, where it's heading, and what change would take

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealand pays a universal pension — NZ Super — to almost everyone aged 65
and over, with no income or asset test. As the population ages, its cost is
projected to grow faster than the economy for decades, which means future
governments will face pressure to raise taxes, cut other services, or change
the pension itself. Any change would land hardest on particular groups of
people, so understanding the real numbers and the real trade-offs matters
before anyone argues for a fix.

## What we've learned so far

- **NZ Super costs about $26.5 billion this year (about 5.6% of the economy) and is projected to reach about 8% of the economy by 2065.** That's roughly $27,400 per recipient per year today. Treasury itself says the current path is not sustainable without change. (confidence: Medium — the headline current cost is High, but the after-tax figures come from a single Treasury model) — [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md)
- **NZ Super is unusual in design, not in generosity.** It is one of only two OECD pension systems with no means test and no compulsory workplace pension, but a middle earner gets a smaller pension relative to their wage than in most OECD countries, and NZ currently spends *less* of its economy on public pensions than the OECD average. (confidence: Medium) — [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md)
- **The alarming international poverty figure for older New Zealanders is mostly a measurement warning, not a hardship count.** The OECD reports 33.7% of over-65s in "income poverty" — more than double its average — but New Zealand's own data finds only about 3% of older people (roughly 25,000) clearly in material hardship, with another 3–4% (roughly 30,000) close to it. Much of the gap exists because the OECD measure ignores housing wealth and housing costs, and because NZ Super sits just below a moving income line. (confidence: Medium) — [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md), [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md)
- **The hardship that does exist is real and concentrated.** Older renters are in material hardship at 16–18%, versus 1% of mortgage-free homeowners; people living alone fare worse than couples; Pacific (14%) and Māori (8%) older people fare far worse than European older people (2%). And with home ownership falling among people now aged 45–64, the next retiring generation is likely to be more exposed, not less. (confidence: Medium) — [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md)
- **No single reform fixes the cost curve painlessly.** Switching the annual increase from wages to prices saves the most but would steadily push pension-reliant retirees below the rest of society; raising the age to 67 or 68 saves money but the cost still keeps rising unless the age reached 72 by 2065; income-testing savings depend entirely on design choices no one has publicly costed; and the NZ Super Fund only trims the future bill modestly. (confidence: Medium) — [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md)
- **Each reform sends the bill to a different group.** Raising the age hits Māori, Pacific peoples, manual workers and disabled people who can't work longer; slower rate increases hit renters, single people and those with no savings; leaning on KiwiSaver hits women, carers and the self-employed, who have much smaller balances; doing nothing hits current and future taxpayers. (confidence: Medium) — [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md)
- **Reform is technically feasible — NZ has done it before — but only durable with cross-party and public trust.** The age was raised from 60 to 65 in the 1990s and stuck; a means-test-like surcharge was introduced in 1985 and abolished in 1998 because it "could not stand the political heat". Parties are currently split on the age, and surveys and seniors' groups strongly favour keeping the pension universal. (confidence: Medium) — [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The after-tax cost figures rest on one source.** The exact "net" cost of NZ Super (what it costs after retirees pay tax back) comes only from Treasury's own model workbook, with no independent check — [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md).
- **We can't fully break down the high OECD poverty number from public data.** The reconciliation shows most of it is measurement, but exactly how much is home-owning retirees with low cash income versus genuinely struggling renters needs secure access to individual-level data, and the group breakdowns come from surveys pooled over 2019–2024 rather than one current snapshot. There is also no like-for-like international comparison of *material hardship* (rather than income) to show how New Zealand's older people really rank — [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md).
- **Two framings that look contradictory but aren't.** NZ spends less on pensions than the OECD average, *and* Treasury says the path is unsustainable. Both are true — one compares countries today, the other projects NZ's own future — but the tension is easy to misuse in public debate.
- **Income-testing and asset-testing have no current public price tag.** Every official source stops at "savings depend on the threshold chosen" — so the fairest-sounding options are also the least quantified — [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md).
- **Some current party positions are thinly sourced.** Labour's stance rests on one media podcast summary and the Greens have no specific published position on the age or means-testing — [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md).
- **No lived experience yet.** All six findings are from public documents; none include the voices of older renters, Māori and Pacific communities, disabled people, carers or the self-employed — every finding flags this as a gap.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language public explainer that reconciles the numbers** — one page showing what NZ Super actually costs (gross vs after-tax, per person, vs other countries), why "below OECD average" and "unsustainable" can both be true, and why "one-third of retirees in poverty" and "3% in hardship" can both be true. Helps: journalists, community organisations, and anyone navigating the debate. Effort: Small. Supported by [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium), [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md) (confidence: Medium) and [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful sourcing kept up to date as Treasury releases new forecasts.
- **A "who pays under each option" guide to reform choices** — a neutral side-by-side of the main reform levers showing the savings evidence and which groups each one exposes, tested against the groups the data shows are already struggling (older renters, single households, Māori and Pacific older people). Helps: seniors' organisations, unions, advocacy groups and submitters to the Retirement Commission's proposed 10-year roadmap process. Effort: Medium. Supported by [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md) (confidence: Medium), [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md) (confidence: Medium) and [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by people from the affected groups before publication.
- **A "who is actually struggling" brief on older-person hardship** — the public-data side of the poverty puzzle is now largely answered, so this would turn it into a short brief showing where older-person hardship actually concentrates (renters, people living alone, Māori and Pacific older people) and why headline poverty rankings mislead, so support and reform debates aim at the right groups. Helps: anyone deciding whether reform should prioritise cost or adequacy, and services supporting older people. Effort: Small. Supported by [poverty measurement reconciliation](../research/findings/other/older-poverty-measurement-reconciliation.md) (confidence: Medium) and [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: acceptance that the exact split behind the OECD number needs individual-level data no volunteer team can access, and lived-experience review.
- **A short history brief on why past reforms stuck or collapsed** — what the 1990s accord, the surcharge's rise and fall, and the 1997 referendum teach about making change durable, timed for the current cross-party roadmap discussion. Helps: people engaging with the political process. Effort: Small. Supported by [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: nothing beyond the existing evidence, though a political scientist's review would strengthen it.
