# NZ Super: what it costs, where it's heading, and what change would take

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealand pays a universal pension — NZ Super — to almost everyone aged 65
and over, with no income or asset test. As the population ages, its cost is
projected to grow faster than the economy for decades, which means future
governments will face pressure to raise taxes, cut other services, or change
the pension itself. Any change would land hardest on particular groups of
people, so understanding the real numbers and the real trade-offs matters
before anyone argues for a fix.

## What we've learned so far

- **NZ Super costs about $26.5 billion this year (about 5.6% of the economy) and is projected to reach about 8% of the economy by 2065.** That's roughly $27,400 per recipient per year today. Treasury itself says the current path is not sustainable without change. (confidence: Medium — the headline current cost is High, but the after-tax figures come from a single Treasury model) — [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md)
- **NZ Super is unusual in design, not in generosity.** It is one of only two OECD pension systems with no means test and no compulsory workplace pension, but a middle earner gets a smaller pension relative to their wage than in most OECD countries, and NZ currently spends *less* of its economy on public pensions than the OECD average. (confidence: Medium) — [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md)
- **Despite the universal pension, the OECD reports over-65 income poverty in NZ at 33.7% — more than double the OECD average.** This measure ignores housing wealth and housing costs, so it should not be read as a full hardship ranking, but it is a striking result. (confidence: Medium) — [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md)
- **No single reform fixes the cost curve painlessly.** Switching the annual increase from wages to prices saves the most but would steadily push pension-reliant retirees below the rest of society; raising the age to 67 or 68 saves money but the cost still keeps rising unless the age reached 72 by 2065; income-testing savings depend entirely on design choices no one has publicly costed; and the NZ Super Fund only trims the future bill modestly. (confidence: Medium) — [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md)
- **Each reform sends the bill to a different group.** Raising the age hits Māori, Pacific peoples, manual workers and disabled people who can't work longer; slower rate increases hit renters, single people and those with no savings; leaning on KiwiSaver hits women, carers and the self-employed, who have much smaller balances; doing nothing hits current and future taxpayers. (confidence: Medium) — [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md)
- **Reform is technically feasible — NZ has done it before — but only durable with cross-party and public trust.** The age was raised from 60 to 65 in the 1990s and stuck; a means-test-like surcharge was introduced in 1985 and abolished in 1998 because it "could not stand the political heat". Parties are currently split on the age, and surveys and seniors' groups strongly favour keeping the pension universal. (confidence: Medium) — [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The after-tax cost figures rest on one source.** The exact "net" cost of NZ Super (what it costs after retirees pay tax back) comes only from Treasury's own model workbook, with no independent check — [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md).
- **How bad is old-age poverty really?** The 33.7% OECD poverty figure is a single income-only indicator that ignores whether people own their homes. NZ data suggests renters and single households are genuinely struggling, but no one has reconciled the OECD number with NZ's own hardship measures — [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md).
- **Two framings that look contradictory but aren't.** NZ spends less on pensions than the OECD average, *and* Treasury says the path is unsustainable. Both are true — one compares countries today, the other projects NZ's own future — but the tension is easy to misuse in public debate.
- **Income-testing and asset-testing have no current public price tag.** Every official source stops at "savings depend on the threshold chosen" — so the fairest-sounding options are also the least quantified — [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md).
- **Some current party positions are thinly sourced.** Labour's stance rests on one media podcast summary and the Greens have no specific published position on the age or means-testing — [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md).
- **No lived experience yet.** All five findings are from public documents; none include the voices of older renters, Māori and Pacific communities, disabled people, carers or the self-employed — every finding flags this as a gap.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language public explainer that reconciles the numbers** — one page showing what NZ Super actually costs (gross vs after-tax, per person, vs other countries) and why "below OECD average" and "unsustainable" can both be true. Helps: journalists, community organisations, and anyone navigating the debate. Effort: Small. Supported by [cost baseline](../research/findings/other/nz-super-cost-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium) and [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful sourcing kept up to date as Treasury releases new forecasts.
- **A "who pays under each option" guide to reform choices** — a neutral side-by-side of the main reform levers showing the savings evidence and which groups each one exposes. Helps: seniors' organisations, unions, advocacy groups and submitters to the Retirement Commission's proposed 10-year roadmap process. Effort: Medium. Supported by [reform options](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-options.md) (confidence: Medium) and [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by people from the affected groups before publication.
- **Deeper research into the old-age poverty puzzle** — dig into NZ's own income, housing-cost and hardship data to establish how much of the alarming OECD poverty figure is real hardship versus a measurement artefact. Helps: anyone deciding whether reform should prioritise cost or adequacy. Effort: Medium. Supported by [OECD comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-super-oecd-pension-profile.md) (confidence: Medium) and [who bears the burden](../research/findings/other/nz-super-reform-burden.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: public data to be granular enough to separate renters, single households and ethnicity.
- **A short history brief on why past reforms stuck or collapsed** — what the 1990s accord, the surcharge's rise and fall, and the 1997 referendum teach about making change durable, timed for the current cross-party roadmap discussion. Helps: people engaging with the political process. Effort: Small. Supported by [politics of reform](../research/findings/other/nz-super-accord-politics.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: nothing beyond the existing evidence, though a political scientist's review would strengthen it.
