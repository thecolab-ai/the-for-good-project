# NZ's new school assessment system — different from National Standards on paper, unproven in practice

## The problem, in plain language

In 2026 the Government began requiring most primary and intermediate schools to assess every Year 3-8 child twice a year in reading, writing and maths, using one of three approved tools, and to report progress to parents in a more common format. New Zealand tried a related promise before: National Standards (2010-2017) aimed to make progress clearer, but became associated with unreliable comparisons, extra workload, a narrowed curriculum and children being labelled "below standard". The question for this stream is whether the new system is better evidenced and better protected from the same ranking-and-labelling harms.

## What we've learned so far

- **The 2026 system is not a straight replay of National Standards.** Schools must assess Year 3-8 students twice a year, but they can use SMART, PATs or e-asTTle in 2026; reports use five progress descriptors; and no current rule was found requiring every school to submit annual "below/at/above" whole-school results like National Standards did. SMART still creates a central data path where schools use it. (confidence: High) — [what the 2026 system requires](../research/findings/other/nz-2026-school-assessment-reporting.md)
- **National Standards were mixed: useful local information, no clear achievement lift, and real implementation harms.** Schools used the data to identify children needing help, but the evidence does not show a clear national achievement gain, and the recurring harms were unreliable school comparisons, extra workload, curriculum narrowing and repeated "below" labelling. (confidence: Medium) — [what National Standards actually did](../research/findings/other/national-standards-effects.md)
- **The new system has a real official paper trail, but the public proof is still incomplete.** Business-case, Treasury, procurement, privacy and assurance records exist, and the cited school trial now appears traceable to a November 2025 item-testing exercise. But the trial analysis, independent review and AI-marking validation have not been published, and two key official-information responses are still lawfully pending. (confidence: Medium) — [the SMART evidence record](../research/findings/other/smart-evidence-assurance-record.md), [the trial and AI-marking evidence](../research/findings/other/smart-trial-validation-and-ai-marking-evidence.md)
- **The main international lesson is about stakes and use, not whether assessment is standardised.** Assessment can support equity when it is low-stakes, avoids public ranking, and triggers extra support; it becomes risky when results feed league tables, sanctions or narrow accountability. (confidence: Medium) — [assessment without ranking](../research/findings/other/standardised-assessment-without-ranking.md)
- **The league-table safeguard is still indirect.** No central public school-level SMART dataset was found, and privacy and official-information checks create barriers, but no current published rule was found that clearly bans school league tables from 2026 assessment data if comparable school-level aggregates are released or published elsewhere. (confidence: Medium) — [assessment data and league tables](../research/findings/other/assessment-data-league-tables.md)
- **The system is clearer as measurement than as guaranteed support.** Funded support exists through learning support, teacher aides, tutoring, professional learning and school-level funding, but no public rule was found saying a child below expectation automatically receives a named funded intervention. (confidence: Medium) — [assessment and support pathways](../research/findings/other/assessment-support-pathways.md)
- **Early sector reaction repeats old concerns and adds new digital ones.** "Rushed", workload, labelling and league-table fears echo National Standards. New 2026 concerns are AI marking, data storage/backups, and trust in a vendor-run digital tool. The reaction evidence is still early: it is position statements, small surveys and official use counts, not a representative national survey. (confidence: Medium) — [sector reactions to the rollout](../research/findings/other/sector-reactions-2026-assessment-rollout.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether the trial and validation evidence will stand up once released.** The trial is now traceable to a real item-testing exercise, but the analysis and independent review are still not public. The key official-information requests are pending under lawful extensions, not yet overdue.
- **Whether SMART's AI marking is accurate enough for writing.** Two unions report a quantity-over-quality problem, while the Ministry says teachers can review or change AI-assisted scores. No independent technical audit was found.
- **Whether data-use rules will prevent ranking in practice.** One finding found no National Standards-style annual return; another found no explicit published ban on league tables if school-level aggregates become available. That is not a contradiction, but it is a live governance gap.
- **Whether a low result leads to help quickly enough.** International evidence says assessment is safer when it is tied to support, but the NZ public record shows support programmes beside the assessment system rather than an automatic pathway from result to funded help.
- **Whether the system works for Māori-medium settings and whānau.** Timing is delayed to 2027 for specified kura, and there is Māori-medium reporting guidance, but Treasury's aromatawai and data-sovereignty concerns remain unresolved in public evidence.
- **How representative the early reaction is.** Sector concerns are credible as statements from named organisations, but there is no representative 2026 teacher, principal, parent or whānau survey yet. The reported 60% school-uptake figure remains Low confidence.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language guide for parents and whānau** explaining what is required, what SMART is and is not, what the five descriptors mean, what AI marking may touch, and what does not automatically happen when a child is assessed as behind. Helps: parents, whānau, school boards. Effort: Small. Supported by [what the 2026 system requires](../research/findings/other/nz-2026-school-assessment-reporting.md) (confidence: High), [assessment and support pathways](../research/findings/other/assessment-support-pathways.md) (confidence: Medium), and [the trial and AI-marking evidence](../research/findings/other/smart-trial-validation-and-ai-marking-evidence.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by someone with school-leadership or learning-support experience.
- **A public evidence-watch page** tracking the missing proof points: trial analysis, independent review, AI-marking audit, data-use rules, official-information responses, uptake evidence, and any representative sector survey. Helps: journalists, educators, advocates, funders. Effort: Medium. Supported by [the SMART evidence record](../research/findings/other/smart-evidence-assurance-record.md) (confidence: Medium), [the trial and AI-marking evidence](../research/findings/other/smart-trial-validation-and-ai-marking-evidence.md) (confidence: Medium), and [sector reactions to the rollout](../research/findings/other/sector-reactions-2026-assessment-rollout.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: ongoing maintenance as official documents are released.
- **A safeguards checklist for boards and community advocates** covering no league tables, no one-test judgements, progress-over-rank reporting, visible untested curriculum areas, clear data-use rules, and results tied to support. Helps: school boards, community advocates, parent groups. Effort: Medium. Supported by [assessment without ranking](../research/findings/other/standardised-assessment-without-ranking.md) (confidence: Medium), [assessment data and league tables](../research/findings/other/assessment-data-league-tables.md) (confidence: Medium), and [assessment and support pathways](../research/findings/other/assessment-support-pathways.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: checking by assessment, privacy and education-law specialists.
- **A local conversation pack for schools and communities** with neutral questions about AI marking, data storage, parent reporting, support follow-through and Māori-medium readiness. Helps: schools, boards, whānau, community groups. Effort: Medium. Supported by [sector reactions to the rollout](../research/findings/other/sector-reactions-2026-assessment-rollout.md) (confidence: Medium) and [assessment and support pathways](../research/findings/other/assessment-support-pathways.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: human review from educators, whānau and Māori-medium voices before public use.
