# Flood-risk homes, insurance retreat, and who is left exposed

## The problem, in plain language

Some homes exposed to flooding may still be rising in value, even as insurers start charging more for flood risk and, in some places, becoming harder to get. That matters because a cheaper purchase price can hide a much bigger future cost: higher premiums, less cover, harder lending, or no clear public buyout path if the risk becomes intolerable. This research round asks who can see the risk before buying, who is most exposed, and what happens if private insurance keeps pulling back.

This overview is a first synthesis. The research round is complete and the stream is now **waiting on a human steward to decide its direction**.

## What we've learned so far

- **The price-growth paradox is real as a reported Cotality/RNZ finding, but not yet independently proven from public data.** Auckland is the only public, quantified example found: flood-zoned houses were reported as selling at a $70,000-$100,000 discount while growing 18% since 2020, compared with 13% for nearby unaffected homes. But the underlying flood scores, sale-price tables, sample sizes, and clean post-2023 time window are not public, so this should not be treated as a settled market fact. (confidence: Medium for the reported finding; Low-Medium for the market conclusion) — [value paradox](../research/findings/other/flood-risk-value-paradox.md)
- **Insurance retreat is showing up mainly through price and thinner availability, not widespread non-renewal.** Insurers are moving toward property-level flood pricing; some high-risk quotes already carry extra flood premiums, and availability is tighter in places where flood and earthquake risk overlap. Full withdrawal is still uncommon in the public evidence, but a temporary pause on new policies in Westport shows partial geographic retreat is no longer theoretical. Natural Hazards Cover does not cover building flood damage, only land damage. (confidence: Medium) — [insurance retreat](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-insurance-retreat-pricing.md)
- **New Zealand's public retreat policy is still ad hoc.** There is no standing national managed-retreat or buyout law. Category 3 buyouts after recent weather events were voluntary, council-led, event-specific deals, commonly co-funded by central and local government, with different homeowner offer rules by region. The national adaptation framework still leaves the future "who pays" question open. (confidence: Medium) — [managed retreat policy](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-managed-retreat-adaptation-policy-baseline.md)
- **Exposure is large and uneven, but we do not yet have a public all-decile deprivation overlay.** Public national modelling reports hundreds of thousands of people and large building value exposed to current-climate rainfall flooding, with the highest exposed shares in places such as the West Coast, Nelson, Hawke's Bay, Marlborough, Canterbury, and Gisborne. The best deprivation evidence found is an older NZDep2018 decile-10 vulnerable-community list, not a current NZDep2023 table of flood-exposed homes by deprivation decile. (confidence: Medium) — [exposed communities](../research/findings/other/flood-risk-housing-communities.md)
- **Buyer disclosure has improved, but the practical warning system is still patchy.** Since 2025, LIMs must include clearer natural-hazard sections, but a LIM still only discloses information councils already hold; it is not a property-specific risk assessment. Banks also do not yet systematically track whether mortgaged homes stay insured, and hazard-aware lending appears uneven. In practice, the clearest flood-risk price signal may be the insurance quote, which a buyer might only confront late in the purchase process. (confidence: Medium) — [lending and disclosure](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-lending-buyer-disclosure.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether the market is truly under-pricing flood risk.** The stream's headline paradox rests on one public source chain. It may be a real signal, a rebound from discounted prices, a timing artefact from using a 2020 baseline, or something that varies sharply by region.
- **Who is most financially exposed, not just geographically exposed.** The research can say where flood exposure is concentrated, but not which households are recent buyers, renters, low-equity owners, uninsured, underinsured, elderly, disabled, or least able to absorb a premium shock.
- **How much flood risk a buyer actually sees before becoming committed.** The disclosure finding shows what information channels exist, but not whether ordinary buyers order and understand a LIM, get insurance quotes early enough, or adjust their offer price.
- **Whether retreat is hiding in policy details rather than headline withdrawal.** The insurance finding found stronger evidence for premium loading and thinner availability than for exclusions, special excesses, or non-renewals, because insurer-by-insurer public data is thin.
- **How future public support will work.** Managed-retreat precedents exist, but they are event-specific. Future adaptation funding, buyout triggers, and the role of the coming flood map or climate-law changes are still unsettled.
- **Several load-bearing numbers are single-source.** Examples include the Cotality/RNZ Auckland value comparison, Treasury/Finity flood-premium loadings, Climate Sigma regional residential-property counts, and RBNZ's high-flood-risk property estimate. They are useful, but should travel with their caveats.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language guide for flood-risk home buyers and advisers** — explain what to check before buying, what a LIM does and does not mean, why the insurance quote matters, and what Natural Hazards Cover will not cover. Helps: buyers, budgeting services, community advisers, and conveyancers working with non-expert households. Effort: Small. Supported by [lending and disclosure](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-lending-buyer-disclosure.md) (confidence: Medium), [insurance retreat](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-insurance-retreat-pricing.md) (confidence: Medium), and [managed retreat policy](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-managed-retreat-adaptation-policy-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: review by someone with conveyancing or insurance expertise so it informs without becoming legal or financial advice.
- **A public evidence brief on who is exposed** — summarise the regional flood-exposure evidence, the older vulnerable-community list, and the missing NZDep2023 overlay in one careful, privacy-safe briefing. Helps: charities, councils, funders, and local media deciding where further attention is warranted. Effort: Small-Medium. Supported by [exposed communities](../research/findings/other/flood-risk-housing-communities.md) (confidence: Medium) and [managed retreat policy](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-managed-retreat-adaptation-policy-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: clear caveats that regional exposure is not the same as household vulnerability, and no property-level examples.
- **An insurance and lending watchlist** — track public signals of flood-risk pricing, new-business pauses, excess or exclusion changes, bank lending responses, and the insurance-affordability review. Helps: community organisations and councils see whether price pressure is turning into practical loss of cover or credit. Effort: Medium. Supported by [insurance retreat](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-insurance-retreat-pricing.md) (confidence: Medium) and [lending and disclosure](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-lending-buyer-disclosure.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a strict public-source method, because many insurer and bank decisions are not visible.
- **A reproducibility request for the value paradox** — publish a simple specification for the non-identifying aggregate table needed to test the reported faster-growth claim by flood-risk band, region, sample size, and post-2023 time window. Helps: journalists, data holders, and researchers separate a real market signal from an unverified headline. Effort: Small-Medium. Supported by [value paradox](../research/findings/other/flood-risk-value-paradox.md) (confidence: Medium/Low-Medium) and [insurance retreat](../research/findings/other/nz-flood-insurance-retreat-pricing.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: cooperation from Cotality or another data holder, or access to aggregate sales and hazard data that can be published without identifying properties.
