# New Zealand's teacher shortage is about matching the right teachers to the right schools

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealand has enough teachers in some places and not enough in others. The sharpest problems are in secondary maths, science, technology and te reo Māori, in Māori-medium education, and in rural or high-need schools where vacancies are hard to fill. Students feel this when schools use unqualified staff, ask teachers to teach outside their subject, or cancel classes.

## What we've learned so far

- **There is no single teacher-shortage number.** Primary schooling is projected to have a small national surplus, while secondary is projected to be short about 710 teachers in 2026; the student-facing pain shows up in unfilled roles, untrained staff, out-of-field teaching and cancelled classes. (confidence: Medium) — [How the shortage looks on five different measures](../research/findings/other/teacher-shortage-signal-by-measure.md)
- **Retention pressure is real, but not mainly a national exit wave.** The 2024 teacher leaving rate was low by the Ministry's own series; the stronger signals are school-to-school movement, long hours, change fatigue, early-career support gaps, and local subject/place mismatch. (confidence: Medium) — [What drives retention and working conditions](../research/findings/other/teacher-retention-working-conditions.md)
- **Māori-medium and te reo Māori supply is constrained by qualified, language-proficient kaiako, especially at secondary level.** Māori-medium learner numbers are rising, first-time Māori-medium training enrolments improved in 2025, but the latest public completion count was 135 graduates in 2024, with only 10 at secondary level. (confidence: Medium) — [The Māori-medium teaching pipeline](../research/findings/other/maori-medium-teaching-pipeline.md)
- **The pipeline loses certainty after enrolment.** Public data shows training enrolments and completions, but not a clear cohort path through registration, first job, subject, region, school setting and retention; employment-based routes look well targeted by design but are not yet outcome-proven at scale. (confidence: Medium) — [Where the teacher pipeline loses people](../research/findings/other/teacher-pipeline-loss-points.md)
- **New evidence makes the incentive gap more precise.** TeachNZ scholarships have older public outcome evidence, including a roughly 22-23% default rate; EBITE has promising but small pilot evidence; the newer Teacher Bonding Scheme, Go Rural and scaled School Onsite Training Programme mostly publish places and inputs, not completion, placement or retention outcomes. (confidence: Medium) — [What is known about teacher-supply incentive outcomes](../research/findings/other/teacher-supply-incentive-outcomes.md)
- **"Decile 1-3" is the wrong current targeting label.** Decile has been replaced by the Equity Index, and the hardest-to-staff picture is sharper when priority-staffing, high Equity Index, rural/isolation and Māori-medium flags are combined; one public join found 65 schools carrying all four flags. (confidence: Medium) — [Who the hard-to-staff schools actually are](../research/findings/other/hard-to-staff-equity-index.md)
- **Overseas examples transfer as tools, not whole systems.** Australia, England and Singapore point to targeted incentives, earn-while-training routes, workload relief, mentoring and better workforce data, but New Zealand would need to adapt those tools to local school boards, kura, collective agreements and existing Ministry schemes. (confidence: Medium) — [What overseas policies could transfer to NZ](../research/findings/other/teacher-shortage-policy-transfer.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether current incentives actually fill and keep teachers in shortage roles.** The stream now has partial evidence, but the public record is still thin for the current Teacher Bonding Scheme, Go Rural, scaled School Onsite Training Programme and EBITE beyond its small pilot.
- **Why teachers move, stay or leave in specific schools.** Public data does not link moves or exits to pay, workload, housing, leadership, fixed-term work, subject options or local support.
- **The Māori-medium picture still relies on proxies.** There is no public current dataset matching vacancies, LAT use, qualified kaiako, training completions, placement and retention by Māori-medium setting, region, year level and subject.
- **School targeting is good enough for a public map, not for causal diagnosis.** The priority-staffing list does not publish which school met which criterion, so it cannot separate retention problems, repeated readvertising, regional nomination and kaupapa Māori nomination.
- **Several key numbers are single-origin.** The Ministry projections, the secondary principals' survey, the EBITE pilot outcome figure, and the joined 65-school group are useful but should not be treated as independently confirmed from multiple public datasets.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language explainer that reconciles the competing shortage numbers** — helps: journalists, funders, school communities and officials who need to understand why different sources seem to disagree. Effort: Small. Supported by [shortage measures](../research/findings/other/teacher-shortage-signal-by-measure.md) (confidence: Medium) and [retention conditions](../research/findings/other/teacher-retention-working-conditions.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: someone to refresh it when annual figures change.
- **An open "hard-to-staff schools" dataset or map using public flags** — helps: charities, iwi organisations and funders looking for schools where staffing, rurality, socioeconomic barriers and Māori-medium indicators stack together. Effort: Medium. Supported by [hard-to-staff school targeting](../research/findings/other/hard-to-staff-equity-index.md) (confidence: Medium) and [Māori-medium pipeline evidence](../research/findings/other/maori-medium-teaching-pipeline.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful wording so public proxies are not mistaken for a judgement about school quality.
- **A public incentive-evidence tracker or OIA-ready brief** — helps: advocates and funders see which teacher-supply schemes publish real uptake, completion, placement and retention outcomes, and which still only publish places or spending. Effort: Medium. Supported by [incentive outcomes](../research/findings/other/teacher-supply-incentive-outcomes.md) (confidence: Medium), [pipeline loss points](../research/findings/other/teacher-pipeline-loss-points.md) (confidence: Medium) and [policy transfer](../research/findings/other/teacher-shortage-policy-transfer.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: Ministry annual reports, possible OIA requests, and neutral handling of schemes that are too new to judge.
- **A case for de-identified teacher-workforce data** — helps: researchers, unions, school leaders and the Ministry answer questions that are currently guessed from separate datasets. Effort: Medium. Supported by [pipeline loss points](../research/findings/other/teacher-pipeline-loss-points.md) (confidence: Medium), [policy transfer](../research/findings/other/teacher-shortage-policy-transfer.md) (confidence: Medium), [Māori-medium pipeline evidence](../research/findings/other/maori-medium-teaching-pipeline.md) (confidence: Medium) and [incentive outcomes](../research/findings/other/teacher-supply-incentive-outcomes.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: privacy-by-design framing and small-cell protections.
