# Government digital spending: no one can say how much, on what, or what worked

## The problem, in plain language

The New Zealand government spends very large sums on computer systems and digital
services — the government's own projection is $13 billion over the next five years —
but nobody, inside or outside government, can point to a published figure for what
is *actually* spent each year, on what, and what the public got for it. In July 2026
a Public Service Commission review found the digital effort fragmented, duplicated,
and poorly coordinated. Without real numbers, the public debate about whether that
money is well spent runs on headlines and anecdotes rather than evidence.

This synthesis draws on five completed pieces of research. **The stream is now
waiting on its human steward to decide direction** (see "Where this is heading").

## What we've learned so far

- **There is no published total of what government actually spends on digital.**
  The only official all-of-government figure ever published is a 2006 estimate of
  roughly $3.0 billion a year — now twenty years old. Today's headline numbers
  ($13 billion over five years; "almost $6 billion" of savings over ten) are
  forward projections and modelled savings, not measured spend. The government's
  own 2026 review confirms it cannot see its own baseline either.
  (confidence: Medium) — [spend baseline](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-government-digital-it-spend-baseline.md)
- **Rebuilding the number from public budget papers only gets you a partial floor.**
  A line-by-line reconstruction across the biggest digital-spending agencies
  (Internal Affairs, MBIE, Inland Revenue, Health NZ) surfaces roughly $460–594
  million of visible digital/IT spend in 2024/25 — far below the ~$2.6 billion a
  year the government's projection implies. The difference isn't proof of error:
  most everyday IT spending (cloud subscriptions, maintenance, contractors,
  embedded tech staff) simply isn't tagged as "digital" anywhere the public can
  see. (confidence: Medium) — [reconstructed actuals](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-government-digital-it-actuals.md)
- **Big IT failures keep happening for the same reasons, decades apart.** Four
  well-documented cases — Police's INCIS (about $82.5m net cost in 1999), the
  Novopay school payroll ($56.8m to 2013), the halted births/deaths/marriages
  system rebuild ($22.9m written off), and Immigration NZ's biometrics project
  (about $35m plus $8.5m on a related project, delivering nothing) — share the
  same diagnosed causes: weak governance, over-optimistic business cases, and
  ignored quality-assurance warnings. Even the failure costs are reported in
  inconsistent, hard-to-compare ways. (confidence: Medium) — [failure catalogue](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-govt-it-failure-catalogue.md)
- **The transparency gap is a choice, not a technical limit.** Government already
  collects project-level status, cost, and assurance data internally (quarterly
  investment reporting, Gateway reviews) — it just doesn't publish it. New Zealand
  briefly published a public major-projects dashboard (2015–2017) and stopped for
  ministerial-priority reasons; the UK and US publish comparable registers today.
  (confidence: Medium) — [transparency gap](../research/findings/civic-transparency/digital-project-transparency-gap.md)
- **Duplication is clearest in shared "building blocks", not specialist systems.**
  The government's own review describes agencies separately building payments,
  identity, and service-delivery systems that others already run. A new central
  agency (the Government Digital Delivery Agency) now exists to fix this, but the
  review itself says the function it inherited had only "paper mandates" — the
  model works only if its funding and procurement levers are actually enforced.
  (confidence: Medium) — [duplication](../research/findings/civic-transparency/digital-capability-duplication.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **What's inside the $13 billion projection.** Its agency coverage and method
  can't be verified from public documents, and a related $12.5 billion (2024)
  figure rests on a single secondary source. Since the projection is the main
  public reference point, this weakness matters.
- **The gap between the visible floor (~$0.5–0.6b/yr) and the implied projection
  (~$2.6b/yr) is unexplained**, not contradictory: it likely sits in untagged
  operating IT spend, with Health NZ's digital operating costs the single largest
  unresolved hole.
- **Several load-bearing dollar figures rest on one source each:** the INCIS
  $82.5m settlement-adjusted figure (one 1999 government release) and the exact
  $35m/$8.5m biometrics split (one independent review, whose author says total
  costs were hard to establish).
- **The duplication examples are anonymised** — the public record doesn't say
  which agencies built what twice, so the scale of waste can't be quantified.
- **Whether government fixes this itself.** The reset plan recommends creating
  exactly the missing spend baseline; if that's published, parts of this stream's
  evidence would be superseded. Separately, an Official Information Act request
  for a current actuals total needs a human with standing — an agent can't file one.

## What we could do about it

- **Publish a plain-language public tracker of the visible spend floor** — turn
  the line-by-line reconstruction into a maintained, citizen-readable table of
  what digital spend *is* visible in public accounts, updated as annual reports
  land. Helps: journalists, researchers, and anyone arguing from numbers rather
  than vibes. Effort: Medium. Supported by [reconstructed actuals](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-government-digital-it-actuals.md)
  (confidence: Medium) and [spend baseline](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-government-digital-it-spend-baseline.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful presentation of accounting caveats so
  the floor isn't misread as a total.
- **Build a public catalogue of major government IT failures** — plain-language
  case cards with sourced costs, diagnosed causes, and clear notes on what each
  dollar figure does and doesn't include. Helps: the public and media, who
  currently rely on episodic news coverage. Effort: Small–Medium. Supported by
  the [failure catalogue](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-govt-it-failure-catalogue.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: discipline about single-sourced figures and
  the differing cost categories.
- **Draft a worked proposal for a public digital-project register** — a concrete
  specification (fields, thresholds, redaction rules) drawing on the data
  government already collects, New Zealand's own discontinued 2015–17 dashboard,
  and the UK/US registers, usable as an advocacy document. Helps: anyone pushing
  for open-by-default publication. Effort: Medium. Supported by the
  [transparency gap](../research/findings/civic-transparency/digital-project-transparency-gap.md)
  finding (confidence: Medium). Would need: a human with official-information
  expertise to judge what can safely be default-public.
- **Run a claims-versus-outcomes watch on the government's own promises** — a
  dated ledger tracking whether the projected savings ($3.9b over five years, up
  to $5.9b over ten) and the promised spend baseline actually materialise. Helps:
  future scrutiny; the claims are dated and checkable. Effort: Small, ongoing.
  Supported by [spend baseline](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-government-digital-it-spend-baseline.md)
  (confidence: Medium) and [duplication](../research/findings/civic-transparency/digital-capability-duplication.md)
  (confidence: Medium). Would need: patience — the claimed savings run out to
  roughly 2036.
