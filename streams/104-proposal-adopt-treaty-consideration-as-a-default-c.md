# Should every project ask the Treaty question by default, not just the "Māori-looking" ones?

## The problem, in plain language

Everything this project works on happens in Aotearoa New Zealand — council spending, grocery prices, grant access, child and family support. Right now, the project only asks "does this affect Māori, or involve Māori data, or raise Treaty of Waitangi obligations?" when a piece of work already *looks* like it might (for example, child welfare). The worry is that this misses the projects where Treaty and Māori interests are real but not obvious. This stream researched whether a short Treaty-consideration question should become a standard step for *every* piece of work, and if so, how to do that without overloading the small volunteer team.

## What we've learned so far

- **A short default check for every piece of work, with a deeper look only where the stakes are real, is the most workable model for a small team.** A one-sentence note ("no obvious Māori data or Treaty question here" or "this needs escalating because…") costs reviewers very little, while full reviews stay reserved for genuinely sensitive work. (confidence: Medium — the effort estimate is a judgement call, not measured) — [steward-attention finding](../research/findings/other/steward-attention-treaty-check.md)
- **Nobody ever explicitly decided to make Treaty consideration conditional.** The current "only when it looks Māori-related" approach fits the project's general habit of saving scarce human attention, but no record shows it was a deliberate Treaty-specific choice — so changing it would not be overturning a considered decision. (confidence: Medium) — [steward-attention finding](../research/findings/other/steward-attention-treaty-check.md)
- **The NZ government treats Treaty consideration as a standing step, not a special-case trigger.** Official guidance for policy-makers builds Treaty analysis into every stage of the policy process, and one key source says it matters *most* where Māori interests are not obvious — direct support for the "default, not trigger" idea, though that strongest point comes from a single source. (confidence: Medium) — [placement finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-checklist-placement.md)
- **If adopted, the rule should be written once and referenced everywhere, not copy-pasted into every document.** The recommended shape: one new line in the project's core method, a matching tick-box on the review checklist (which physically lives in two files that must be kept in sync by hand), and the detailed "how to actually do the check" kept in the one existing reference document. (confidence: Medium — this is a drafted option for a human to decide, not a settled fact) — [placement finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-checklist-placement.md)
- **No automated tool can enforce this — only human reviewers can.** The project's automatic checks verify document structure, not substance, so without clear guidance for reviewers the check risks becoming an empty box-ticking exercise. (confidence: High) — [placement finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-checklist-placement.md)
- **If the check is adopted, it should apply to new work going forward, with a light one-time checkpoint for work already in flight — not a rewrite of everything finished.** Every current stream already has a natural upcoming moment where a human looks at it; the check can ride those moments instead of triggering a costly re-review of merged work. (confidence: Medium) — [retroactivity finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-check-retroactivity.md)
- **The live child-welfare stream deserves the strongest of those checkpoints.** It touches vulnerable families, still has active research underway, and existing work there has not applied this lens — a gap in scope, not a defect in what was done. (confidence: Medium-High) — [retroactivity finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-check-retroactivity.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **No Māori voice has reviewed any of this.** All three findings say the same thing independently: the wording, thresholds, and what counts as "enough" Treaty consideration need validation by someone with Māori data-governance or Te Tiriti expertise. Right now this is entirely a desk exercise by AI agents reading public sources.
- **The cost to reviewers is guessed, not measured.** The claim that a default check adds only trivial effort per review is explicitly Low confidence — the project keeps no data on how long reviews take.
- **The strongest external justification rests on a single source.** The claim that a Treaty check matters *most* where Māori interests aren't obvious comes from one government agency's guidance only; the other official source examined actually frames its own test in the conditional, trigger-shaped way this stream questions.
- **How to stop the check becoming box-ticking is unsolved.** Two findings warn about it; neither offers a tested fix beyond "reviewers must challenge weak 'nothing to see here' sentences."
- **Whether this needs a formal governance decision first.** One finding raises the possibility that adopting a default lens is a change to how the project works at a constitutional level, which may need its own recorded decision before any document is edited.
- **A separate dedicated Māori-data-governance workstream** (one of the original questions this stream was meant to explore) was not directly researched by any of the three findings.

## What we could do about it

- **Adopt a light default check everywhere, escalating to a deep review only when it flags something** — one sentence recorded on every piece of work, with full Treaty/Māori-data review reserved for sensitive or flagged cases. Helps: Māori communities whose interests might otherwise be missed in "ordinary-looking" work, and reviewers who get a clear, bounded task. Effort: Small. Supported by the [steward-attention finding](../research/findings/other/steward-attention-treaty-check.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a Māori domain expert to approve the wording, and reviewer guidance so the sentence doesn't become boilerplate.
- **Place any adopted check as one method line plus a review tick-box, with a section in the output templates to record the answer** — the requirement stated once in the core method, mirrored on the review checklist, and a space in each research document where the actual answer gets written down. Helps: contributors and reviewers, who see one consistent rule instead of scattered copies. Effort: Small–Medium (touches roughly four documents and two hand-synced checklists). Supported by the [placement finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-checklist-placement.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a steward decision on where the detailed guidance canonically lives, since its current home is a document the project classes as advisory.
- **Apply the check prospectively, with a one-time checkpoint for in-flight streams at their next natural human review moment** — new work follows the new rule; existing streams get the question asked once, when a human was going to look at them anyway; finished work is left intact unless a specific gap is identified. Helps: the volunteer team, by avoiding a costly re-review backlog, while still covering live work. Effort: Small. Supported by the [retroactivity finding](../research/findings/other/treaty-check-retroactivity.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: agreement that the checkpoint wording doesn't imply older work was deficient.
- **Before adopting anything, seek review from someone with Māori data-governance or Te Tiriti expertise** — treat the current three findings as a starting brief and have the wording, thresholds, and the child-welfare stream's checkpoint validated by an appropriate person. Helps: the project's credibility and the communities the check is meant to serve. Effort: Medium (finding and engaging the right person is the work; all three findings flag this gap — [steward-attention](../research/findings/other/steward-attention-treaty-check.md), [placement](../research/findings/other/treaty-checklist-placement.md), [retroactivity](../research/findings/other/treaty-check-retroactivity.md), each Medium confidence on this point). Would need: a suitable expert willing to engage with a small volunteer project.
