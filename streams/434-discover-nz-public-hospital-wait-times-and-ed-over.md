# NZ hospital waits are improving slowly but remain far from target

## The problem, in plain language

People are waiting too long at several points in the public hospital system: in emergency departments, for first specialist appointments, and for planned treatment such as surgery. The latest public data shows some improvement, but results are still far below the health system's own targets, and the pressure is not evenly spread across districts, patient groups, or specialties. Delays can harm patients, but the public evidence still does not clearly show which interventions are doing the most to reduce waits.

## What we've learned so far

- **New Zealand is still well short of its hospital-wait targets.** In the most recent quarter studied, 74.4% of emergency department patients were finished within six hours, 61.2% of first specialist appointments were within four months, and 64.9% of planned treatments were within four months; all were below their in-year milestones and far below the 2030 target of 95%. (confidence: High) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md)
- **The pressure is uneven, not one national queue.** District results vary widely, first-specialist data has no ethnicity or rurality breakdown, and orthopaedics is a particular pressure point across first specialist appointments and planned treatment. (confidence: High) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md)
- **International comparisons are possible, but a simple "NZ rank" would mislead.** New Zealand, Australia, England, and OECD sources use different clocks and patient groups, so emergency flow, specialist referral waits, and surgery waits need to be compared separately. (confidence: Medium) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/wait-time-comparator-definitions.md)
- **Staffing, beds, ambulance demand, and aged-care discharge pressure are real, but public data cannot yet prove they explain district wait-time spikes.** The available data is national, annual, or otherwise misaligned with the district-by-quarter wait-time data; one aged-care discharge signal even runs against a simple "aged-care delay equals worse emergency waits" story. (confidence: Medium) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/workforce-beds-ambulance-agedcare-discharge-wait-time-spikes.md)
- **Delayed care causes harm, but New Zealand does not yet have one public national cost estimate.** The strongest evidence is for emergency department crowding and mortality, cancer-service delay harm, and hip-surgery waiting costs; national mental-health and productivity costs remain only partly measured. (confidence: Medium) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/delayed-care-costs-nz.md)
- **Named interventions have output evidence, but little independent proof of what they changed.** Elective Boost and the musculoskeletal physiotherapy pathway report real volumes delivered, but public evidence does not isolate how much they improved wait times; orthopaedics did not show a clear positive national first-specialist step-change after the pathway rollout. (confidence: Medium) - [intervention finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-hospital-wait-intervention-evidence-2024-2026.md), [orthopaedics finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/orthopaedics-fsa-waitlist-inflection.md)
- **Private-sector planned care is central to the current volume plan, but public transparency is incomplete.** Health NZ planned most Elective Boost treatments through private providers, while the public record still lacks a direct outsourcing-equity assessment and a usable procedure-level cost comparison. (confidence: Medium) - [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/health-nz-private-planned-care-assessment.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- We still cannot say which named intervention worked best, or whether some mainly shifted easier patients through while leaving complex patients waiting.
- The strongest missing data is practical rather than abstract: district-by-quarter bed capacity, occupancy, nursing vacancies, ambulance handover delays, and first-specialist equity breakdowns.
- One research file could not locate the EY first-specialist reporting review, while a later file found Health NZ's published EY report and summarised it as finding no intentional misrepresentation but real district process variability. The later file appears to update that narrow point, but the wider warning about inconsistent reporting remains.
- The outsourcing evidence is load-bearing but incomplete: public sources show planned private-provider volume and procurement controls, but not the equity impact or clear cost-per-procedure comparison the Auditor-General asked Health NZ to assess.
- The cost of delay is undercounted in public evidence. Clinical harm is visible, but national mental-health, family, work, tax, welfare, and ACC costs are not yet pulled into one New Zealand estimate.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain public wait-times brief or dashboard** - helps: charities, funders, councils, journalists, and patient advocates see where waits are worst without having to understand health-target spreadsheets. Effort: Medium. Supported by [baseline finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md) (confidence: High) and [comparator finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/wait-time-comparator-definitions.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: regular refresh from official data and careful wording so unlike measures are not ranked as if they were the same.
- **A public data-gap and OIA package** - helps: researchers and advocates ask for the missing measures that would make the causes testable. Effort: Medium. Supported by [workforce and capacity finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/workforce-beds-ambulance-agedcare-discharge-wait-time-spikes.md) (confidence: Medium), [baseline finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md) (confidence: High), and [private planned-care finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/health-nz-private-planned-care-assessment.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: official agencies to hold releasable aggregate data, especially for beds, vacancies, ambulance handover, and first-specialist equity breakdowns.
- **A planned-care equity and outsourcing watchlist** - helps: patient advocates and community groups track whether extra treatment volume is improving access fairly, not only increasing totals. Effort: Medium. Supported by [private planned-care finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/health-nz-private-planned-care-assessment.md) (confidence: Medium), [intervention finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/nz-hospital-wait-intervention-evidence-2024-2026.md) (confidence: Medium), and [baseline finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md) (confidence: High). Would need: Health NZ or Auditor-General updates on outsourcing equity, cost, case selection, and complex-patient outcomes.
- **A delayed-care harm briefing** - helps: funders and public-interest groups explain why wait times matter beyond headline percentages. Effort: Small. Supported by [delayed-care costs finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/delayed-care-costs-nz.md) (confidence: Medium) and [baseline finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/hospital-wait-times-baseline-2024-2026.md) (confidence: High). Would need: honest caveats that New Zealand has strong harm signals but not a full national dollar-cost model.
