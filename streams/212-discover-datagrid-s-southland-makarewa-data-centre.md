# DataGrid's Southland data centre: the verifiable facts in one place

## The problem, in plain language

A company called DataGrid plans to build one of New Zealand's largest data centres at Makarewa, just north of Invercargill. The public debate about it is muddy: campaigners, media, and the company quote different sizes, costs, and impacts, and DataGrid says critics are describing "quite different projects". When numbers this big are contested, people in Southland — and the councils and funders who serve them — need one place where the confirmed facts are separated from the claims. This stream assembled that factual baseline from primary records. All research is now merged and the stream is **awaiting a direction decision from its human steward**.

## What we've learned so far

- **The environmental approvals are real and now verified from the council's own record, not just the company's press release.** Environment Southland granted nine resource consents on 11 March 2026 — covering groundwater, wastewater, generator air discharges, wetland removal, and the subsea cable landing — for 35 years, decided without public notification (so there were no public submissions). (confidence: Medium overall — see the gap below) — [consent record finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-consents.md)
- **The project was not approved through the government's fast-track regime.** DataGrid applied for fast-track listing but was not chosen; the March 2026 approvals came through the ordinary council consenting route. (confidence: High) — [consent record finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-consents.md)
- **The water numbers are now exact.** The operating consent allows up to 7 litres per second, about 605,000 litres a day and 220.75 million litres a year of groundwater for evaporative cooling and drinking supply — about the same daily volume as one small Southland town's supply, and roughly 2.6 times the largest nearby dairy-farm groundwater consent. Day-to-day wastewater discharge is small (5,000 litres a day, like 2–3 large households). (confidence: High) — [water consents finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-water-consents.md)
- **The wetland story has two parts that the public debate blurs.** The consent allows removal of a small (0.24 ha) wetland the council assessed as very low ecological value — but a separate, high-value wetland nearby (Taylor Road Wetland) could dry out from the groundwater take without the required monitoring and artificial recharge, and the council's own reviewer flagged uncertainty about that mitigation. (confidence: High) — [water consents finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-water-consents.md)
- **The power claim "twice the Tiwai smelter" does not describe the approved project.** The announced campus is 280 MW — about half of Tiwai's 572 MW. "Up to 1 GW" (about 1.75× Tiwai) is a stated company ambition with no consent, power contract, or grid arrangement behind it. The only confirmed supply deal is a 15-year, 140 MW *option* agreement with Mercury, and grid planning documents show a staged first connection of 100–120 MW. (confidence: High for the 280 MW/half-Tiwai arithmetic; Medium for the staged grid picture) — [energy finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-energy-phase-load.md), [factual baseline finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-factual-baseline.md)
- **Who owns and pays for it is only partly visible.** The NZ companies are held through a New Zealand/Singapore corporate chain whose ultimate owners can't be confirmed from the NZ register; the widely-quoted costs ($3.5b vs $5.1b) appear to be the same project in different currencies, not different builds; and the jobs figures (550 vs 1,200 vs 5,400 construction jobs; 50–72 permanent) measure different things and can't be reconciled without the unpublished economic assessment. (confidence: Medium) — [economics and ownership finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-economics-ownership.md)
- **Neither side of the public debate is simply right.** The opposition petition (26,027 signatures) points at real consented impacts — water take, power demand, wetland removal — but some of its claims are unsupported, including that the government spent $5.1 billion on the build (no evidence found; it's a private project). Equally, DataGrid's consent and power-deal claims check out, while its climate, jobs, and latency benefit claims have no independent verification yet. (confidence: Medium) — [claims catalogue finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-claims-status.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Two of the three council approvals are still unverified.** The Southland District Council and Invercargill City Council decision documents could not be found publicly, so "full consent from three councils" still partly rests on DataGrid's own announcement. This is the biggest remaining hole in the baseline.
- **The consented size may not be the announced size.** The council record describes "up to 240 MW of IT capacity" while DataGrid announces "280 MW of power" — probably different metrics (computing load vs total electricity draw), but no source confirms that, and the findings disagree on which number to headline.
- **Who ultimately owns DataGrid** after a 2025 upstream share transaction is not visible in any NZ public register.
- **Whether the Taylor Road Wetland protection will actually work** is a technical judgement the consent itself defers to future monitoring; the findings flag it needs a hydrologist or ecologist, not more desk research.
- **What DataGrid actually meant by critics "describing quite different projects"** is unknown — the article is paywalled and no research agent could retrieve its text.
- The Wellington-electricity comparison, the "half a million cars" emissions claim, and the University of Otago anchor-tenancy are each reported but not independently verified.

## What we could do about it

- **Publish the baseline as a plain-language public fact sheet** — a one-page "what's approved, what's claimed, what's unverified" summary of the six findings that anyone in the debate can cite. Helps: Southland residents, journalists, councillors, submitters on both sides. Effort: Small. Supported by all six findings, especially the [claims catalogue](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-claims-status.md) (Medium) and [consent record](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-consents.md) (Medium). Would need: a maintainer's editorial sign-off and a decision on where to host it.
- **Close the consent gap first, then publish.** Request the Southland District Council and Invercargill City Council decision documents (both councils must release them on request under official-information law), reconcile the 240 MW/280 MW discrepancy, and upgrade the baseline before any public output. Helps: anyone relying on the baseline's accuracy. Effort: Small–Medium (requests are cheap; waiting and analysis take time). Supported by the [consent record finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-consents.md) (Medium), which names exactly which documents are missing. Would need: a person or agent to lodge and track the requests.
- **Maintain the claims table as a living tracker** — keep the confirmed/contested/unverifiable catalogue updated as the project moves toward its 2028 target, so the baseline doesn't go stale the way the public debate did. Helps: future researchers and decision-makers over the multi-year build. Effort: Medium (ongoing). Supported by the [claims catalogue finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-claims-status.md) (Medium). Would need: a recurring review cadence and someone to own it.
- **Hand the wetland question to a domain expert.** The one open environmental question the documents can't settle — whether the Taylor Road Wetland mitigation is adequate — needs a hydrologist or wetland ecologist to review the consent conditions and technical reviews. Helps: Environment Southland's monitoring accountability and local environmental groups. Effort: Small for us (a briefing pack from the findings), but depends on finding the expert. Supported by the [water consents finding](../research/findings/other/datagrid-makarewa-water-consents.md) (High on what the documents say; the adequacy judgement is explicitly outside our method). Would need: a willing, qualified volunteer expert.
