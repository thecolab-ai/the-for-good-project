# NZ housing supply is blocked between capacity, consents, and homes people can live in

## The problem, in plain language

Housing policy can look successful on paper while people still cannot get a home. Councils may enable land for housing, developers may get consents, and public programmes may fund homes, but the homes still need infrastructure, finance, construction, and the right match to local need.

This matters for renters, people waiting for public housing, councils trying to plan growth, and funders deciding where help would actually change outcomes.

## What we've learned so far

- **There is no single current national "homes built" target to compare against delivery.** The measurable gap is a chain: enabled housing capacity, then building consents, then net dwelling-stock additions. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-capacity-consents-completions-gap.md)
- **Consents are not completed homes.** The latest national baseline was 39,737 new homes consented in the year ended May 2026, while Stats NZ's stock method lags and discounts consents; applying the current national proxy would imply about 35,400 net private-dwelling additions, not 39,737. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-capacity-consents-completions-gap.md)
- **The hardest national constraint appears to be infrastructure funding and servicing, with land rules close behind.** Development finance, construction capacity, and consent processing matter too, but current public evidence does not show building-consent processing as the main national bottleneck. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-constraint-ranking.md)
- **Different places have different problems.** Auckland is the main volume engine, Canterbury and Queenstown-Lakes have high consent intensity, Wellington City is a low-consent capital-city outlier, and Hamilton and Tauranga look like pressure cases where raw consent counts can hide weak per-person delivery. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/regional-supply-dynamics.md), [finding](../research/findings/other/council-housing-supply-outliers.md)
- **High consenting can still sit beside serious pressure.** Queenstown-Lakes and Selwyn have very high per-person consenting but also infrastructure or long-term capacity warnings; Wellington shows the opposite pattern, where capacity reform evidence has not yet translated into strong current consenting. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/council-housing-supply-outliers.md), [finding](../research/findings/other/regional-supply-dynamics.md)
- **Public and affordable housing programmes are targeted to high-need places, but they are not large enough to close the national high-need gap on their own.** They matter greatly for the households reached, but programme volumes and delivery to date are small beside the Housing Register and national consent flow. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/public-affordable-housing-gap.md)
- **The strongest completed-home evidence is for direct public or social-housing procurement.** Infrastructure funding has good evidence for unlocking capacity, while fast-track approvals, granny-flat exemptions, modular construction, and council incentive payments have weaker or newer evidence for actual occupied homes. (confidence: Medium) -- [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-delivery-levers.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- We do not have a current public council-by-council register showing whether Tier 1 and 2 councils meet the new Housing Growth Target method.
- We do not have a clean public dataset linking enabled capacity, consents, starts, completions, occupation, affordability, and local need in the same places and dates.
- The evidence repeatedly separates "enabled" or "approved" homes from completed homes, but many delivery levers are still measured mainly by approvals, expected capacity, or process savings.
- Some findings point in opposite directions unless read carefully: Auckland and Wellington can show large enabled-capacity figures while still facing feasibility, infrastructure, or consent-flow problems; Queenstown-Lakes can show very high consenting while still having short-term infrastructure-limited shortages.
- Public and affordable housing evidence is strong on targeting and programme size, but weaker on net new supply once purchases, leases, renewals, demolitions, and sales are separated.
- The local cause of the gap is still unresolved in key places, especially Wellington's low current consenting and Queenstown-Lakes' split between resident housing, visitor accommodation, second homes, and worker housing.

## What we could do about it

- **Create a plain-language explainer that separates housing capacity, consents, and completed homes.** -- helps: charity workers, funders, journalists, and council officers avoid comparing the wrong numbers. Effort: Small. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-capacity-consents-completions-gap.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/other/regional-supply-dynamics.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: clear caveats that the national stock proxy is not a local completion dataset.
- **Build a council comparison briefing for the main outliers: Auckland, Canterbury/Selwyn, Queenstown-Lakes, Wellington, Hamilton, and Tauranga.** -- helps: local advocates and funders see which places need different questions rather than one national story. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/other/council-housing-supply-outliers.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/other/regional-supply-dynamics.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: keeping the briefing framed as an outlier screen, not a formal compliance audit.
- **Map the main constraints and delivery levers into a simple decision guide.** -- helps: councils, funders, and policy advocates ask whether a place needs infrastructure, land, public procurement, or delivery tracking before arguing for process reform. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-constraint-ranking.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-delivery-levers.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: not overclaiming causal evidence where the sources only show approvals or expected capacity.
- **Produce a high-need housing programme brief comparing targeted public and affordable supply with visible need.** -- helps: community housing providers, social-service charities, and funders understand where public programmes are material but insufficient. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/other/public-affordable-housing-gap.md) (confidence: Medium) and [finding](../research/findings/other/housing-delivery-levers.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a careful distinction between funded homes, delivered homes, and net new local supply.
