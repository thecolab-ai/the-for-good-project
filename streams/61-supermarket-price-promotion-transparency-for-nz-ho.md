# Shoppers can't tell a real supermarket deal from a manufactured one

## The problem, in plain language

Food is one of the biggest costs in a New Zealand household budget, and food prices have kept rising. Official statistics show the national *trend*, but not what a shopper actually faces on the shelf: how a specific "special" changed over time, whether a "was" price was ever the real price, and how promotion cycles are timed. Without that, people can't tell a genuine saving from a manufactured one.

## What we've learned so far

- **This affects almost every household, not a niche group.** Food is about $300 a week — nearly a fifth of average household spending — and food prices rose 3.2% in the year to May 2026. (confidence: Medium) — [framing research](../research/findings/other/supermarket-transparency-framing.md)
- **The official numbers can't answer the shopper's question, and real shelf prices can move differently from the national trend.** Stats NZ's food price indexes measure national price *change*, not the shelf price of a product in a store — and a small three-item test basket in Papakura actually got about 2.5% *cheaper* over a year in which the national index rose 3.2%. (confidence: High that the indexes can't be read as shelf prices; Medium for the basket arithmetic, Low for any generalisation) — [shelf prices vs official index](../research/findings/civic-transparency/grocery-shelf-prices-vs-fpi.md)
- **The shelf-level data we'd need exists publicly — but we don't have the right to republish it in bulk.** A community price-tracking service (Grocer) publishes current prices for ~465 stores and ~109,000 products, with history back to 2022 for some products, yet its terms grant no redistribution licence: the conservative position is to publish analysis, methods and small cited examples only. (confidence: Medium for the data; Medium-Low for the rights reading) — [data audit](../research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md)
- **The high "regular" price is rarely the price people actually pay.** Across 15 packaged staples at two Papakura supermarkets over two years, the highest observed price was charged less than half the time for *every* item, and the average item spent about two-thirds of its time below the near-ceiling price band. Regulator guidance says that when a promotional price becomes routine, *it* is the real usual price — which makes "was/now" comparisons against a rarely-charged high price legally sensitive. (confidence: Medium for the measurements; High for the regulator guidance) — [staples promotion cycling](../research/findings/other/staples-promo-cycling.md)
- **The two big chains don't simply move in lockstep — and sometimes they alternate.** In a nine-product, two-year Papakura comparison, only two products shared an identical lowest price across chains. Coca-Cola 1.5L changed price at both chains almost every Monday, but the chains took turns being the cheap one — a shopper could often save 35–53% just by picking the right chain that week. Milk moved near-identically at both, in a pattern more consistent with shared supplier costs than coordination — though the data cannot prove *why* prices move. (confidence: Medium) — [cross-chain price comparison](../research/findings/other/supermarket-cross-chain-price-lockstep.md)
- **The same product can cost wildly different amounts on the same day.** In a 20-product snapshot, the same lemons were $1.99 at one store and $10.49 at another (5.3×), and within a few kilometres in Papakura the same can of baked beans ranged $1.79 to $4.19 (2.3×). Notably, this check also found its own earlier examples had gone stale within days — supermarket prices move fast. (confidence: Medium) — [same-day price gaps](../research/findings/other/same-day-grocery-price-gaps.md)
- **Shrinkflation can hide a price rise behind an unchanged shelf price.** When a juice bottle shrank from 3L to 2.4L and a toilet-paper pack lost sheets, the shelf price stayed the same while the price per litre / per sheet rose 25% and 10% at the sampled stores. The practical defence is the unit price ("per 100g") that supermarkets are now legally required to display — in store since August 2024, online since August 2025. (confidence: Medium for the examples; High for the unit-pricing rules) — [shrinkflation and unit prices](../research/findings/other/shrinkflation-pack-size-unit-price.md), [pricing law map](../research/findings/other/nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md)
- **"Cheapest store" reputations are real but partial.** In one Papakura check, PAK'nSAVE had the cheapest 25-item basket total ($141 vs $151–$153) — but was cheapest on only 16 of the 25 items, and rivals' loyalty and sale prices clawed back most of the line-by-line gap. (confidence: Medium) — [cheapest-basket check](../research/findings/other/paknsave-cheapest-basket.md)
- **Even the same chain can charge different prices at different branches.** Across four branches of each chain in Auckland, one chain showed no branch-to-branch difference on a 15-item basket, while another's central-city branch was dearest on all 15 items ($18.70 more for the basket), and a single body wash varied by $7.50 between branches of the same discount chain. (confidence: Medium) — [within-chain price spread](../research/findings/other/within-banner-store-price-spread.md)
- **Misleading pricing is already illegal, and enforcement is live.** The Fair Trading Act prohibits false or misleading price claims, a "was/now" comparison can mislead if the "was" price was inflated, stale or rarely charged, and major supermarkets currently face criminal charges over alleged pricing inaccuracies and misleading specials. A price *rise* on its own is lawful. (confidence: High) — [pricing law map](../research/findings/other/nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md)
- **There is a defensible way to measure "suspicious" promotions without accusing anyone.** A drafted method turns regulator guidance into measurable signals, with strict evidence thresholds before any retailer is ever named — though its specific numeric thresholds remain untested assumptions. (confidence: Medium) — [measurement method](../research/findings/other/supermarket-pricing-method.md)
- **Another price-comparison app is not the gap — but a regulator route exists.** Existing tools already compare prices; what's missing is a no-login, plain-English, citable layer: education on promotion mechanics and evidence packs usable by regulators, journalists or complainants. The Grocery Commissioner's remit covers pricing and promotional transparency, with new complaint-disclosure duties on supermarkets from 2026. (confidence: Medium — the regulator's focus is well evidenced; that they'd *use* our work is untested) — [tool gap analysis](../research/findings/other/supermarket-tool-transparency-gap.md), [pricing law map](../research/findings/other/nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether the measured patterns generalise beyond one suburb.** Almost every measured result — promotion cycling, chain alternation, basket comparisons — comes from small baskets (9–25 products) in Papakura, plus one 20-product national snapshot. The findings themselves warn against generalising; nobody has tested a large basket across regions, and the method's evidence thresholds are still uncalibrated guesses.
- **What kind of price each recorded price actually was.** The price history records only a price, a store and a time — not whether it was a shelf, sale, loyalty-card or online price, and not what the shelf ticket said. Several findings repeat this limit: the data shows *that* prices cycled, not what a shopper was told, so none of it yet proves a misleading representation.
- **Why prices move the way they do.** The alternating specials and near-identical milk moves are patterns, not explanations — the data cannot distinguish independent promotion calendars, shared supplier costs, or chains watching each other, and no finding claims intent.
- **Whether we may publish anything derived from Grocer's data.** The no-bulk-republication conclusion is a conservative non-lawyer reading (Medium-Low confidence, single finding); no lawyer has reviewed it and Grocer has not been asked. This still gates most public-facing options below.
- **How reliable the single data source is — and how fast claims go stale.** All price results are reproducible from one aggregator's files, not independently verified against shelves; two findings weight that reliability differently. One check had to withdraw its own headline examples after prices changed within days — any published example needs a re-verification discipline.
- **What real households actually need, and whether the regulator would act.** No shoppers, regulators, retailers, or Grocer itself have been spoken to, and there's no evidence yet the Commerce Commission would accept an outside evidence pack.

## What we could do about it

- **Test whether the patterns hold at scale, and calibrate the method.** Repeat the promotion-cycling and price-gap measurements on a much larger basket across several regions, and use the results to set the method's evidence thresholds, with false-positive checks — results staying internal until the rights question is settled. Helps: the stream itself — it turns one-suburb examples into a defensible picture. Effort: Medium. Supported by [measurement method](../research/findings/other/supermarket-pricing-method.md) (Medium), [staples promotion cycling](../research/findings/other/staples-promo-cycling.md) (Medium), [cross-chain price comparison](../research/findings/other/supermarket-cross-chain-price-lockstep.md) (Medium) and [data audit](../research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md) (Medium). Would need: enough dense product histories outside Papakura.
- **A plain-English education explainer on supermarket promotion mechanics — now with real examples.** Teach how "was/now" pricing, loyalty prices, promo cycles and shrinkflation work, using the measured examples (a "regular" price rarely charged, chains alternating weekly specials, unit prices exposing shrunken packs, cheapest-store nuance) as small cited illustrations, plus what the law requires. Helps: households trying to spot a real deal. Effort: Small–Medium. Supported by [tool gap analysis](../research/findings/other/supermarket-tool-transparency-gap.md) (Medium), [pricing law map](../research/findings/other/nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md) (High), [staples promotion cycling](../research/findings/other/staples-promo-cycling.md) (Medium), [shrinkflation and unit prices](../research/findings/other/shrinkflation-pack-size-unit-price.md) (Medium) and [cheapest-basket check](../research/findings/other/paknsave-cheapest-basket.md) (Medium). Would need: consumer-law review of wording, and care to stay within the small-cited-examples limit from the [data audit](../research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md) (Medium-Low on rights).
- **A resolution of the data-rights question first.** Ask Grocer for written permission (and/or get a legal opinion) before any public artifact that uses its data. Helps: every other option — it's the common blocker. Effort: Small in volunteer time, but depends on outside parties. Supported by [data audit](../research/findings/other/grocer-nz-data-audit.md) (Medium-Low) and [measurement method](../research/findings/other/supermarket-pricing-method.md) (Medium). Would need: a contactable, willing counterparty at Grocer, or pro-bono legal help.
- **A 2-page brief for the Grocery Commissioner on the shelf-vs-index transparency gap.** Present the method and the first measured signals — rarely-charged "regular" prices, same-day price gaps, shrinkflation unit-price rises, and the basket-vs-index demonstration — mapped to the regulator's own complaint categories; evidence and method only, no breach allegations. Helps: the regulator's stated transparency work; indirectly all shoppers. Effort: Medium. Supported by [pricing law map](../research/findings/other/nz-supermarket-pricing-law.md) (High), [staples promotion cycling](../research/findings/other/staples-promo-cycling.md) (Medium), [same-day price gaps](../research/findings/other/same-day-grocery-price-gaps.md) (Medium), [shrinkflation and unit prices](../research/findings/other/shrinkflation-pack-size-unit-price.md) (Medium), [shelf prices vs official index](../research/findings/civic-transparency/grocery-shelf-prices-vs-fpi.md) (Medium) and [tool gap analysis](../research/findings/other/supermarket-tool-transparency-gap.md) (Medium). Would need: honesty that the examples are small-sample and that regulator uptake is untested.
