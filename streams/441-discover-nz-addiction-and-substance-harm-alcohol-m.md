# Alcohol, methamphetamine, and gambling harm in New Zealand — how big is it really, and what actually helps

## The problem, in plain language

Alcohol, methamphetamine, and gambling each cost New Zealand billions of dollars a year in health damage, lost work, crime, and harm to families — and the burden falls hardest on already disadvantaged communities. Public debate often runs on outdated or mismatched numbers, so this stream set out to establish what the real scale is, how New Zealand compares internationally, and which responses have genuine evidence behind them. A round of research is now complete and the stream is **waiting on its human steward to decide direction**.

## What we've learned so far

- **Alcohol is the largest measured burden, but the three harms can't be added into one total.** Official estimates put alcohol harm at $6.0–9.1 billion for 2023 (roughly 1.45–2.21% of GDP), gambling at $4.2 billion for 2023/24 (about 1.0%), and methamphetamine at a conservative $1.5 billion for 2024 (about 0.35%) — each uses a different accounting method, so they rank but don't sum. (confidence: Medium) — [economic burden finding](../research/findings/other/alcohol-meth-gambling-economic-burden.md)
- **New Zealand is not a heavy-drinking outlier, and consumption is falling — but risky drinking patterns remain large.** We drink slightly less per adult than the OECD average and less than Australia or the UK, and domestic supply fell again in 2025; yet 16.6% of adults still drink at hazardous levels, and how we rank internationally on harm flips depending on which measure is used. (confidence: Medium) — [OECD benchmarks finding](../research/findings/other/nz-alcohol-oecd-benchmarks.md)
- **Methamphetamine consumption roughly doubled in 2024 and has stayed high into 2026 — but surveys don't show many more users.** Wastewater testing shows a sustained jump from 732 kg (2023) to 1,751 kg (2025) at tested sites; official analysis suggests existing users using more, not a wave of new users. Harm is concentrated in deprived, mostly North Island communities. Links to crime are real associations, but public data can't prove meth *caused* specific crime trends. (confidence: Medium) — [methamphetamine data finding](../research/findings/other/nz-methamphetamine-public-data.md)
- **The interventions with the strongest evidence are mostly structural, not individual.** Alcohol pricing and availability controls have the strongest cross-country evidence; "contingency management" (structured incentive-based treatment) is the best-supported methamphetamine treatment; for gambling, supply and product controls beat voluntary tools like self-exclusion. Prescribing stimulants as substitution therapy is *not* supported as a general policy. (confidence: Medium) — [interventions finding](../research/findings/other/addiction-harm-interventions-nz.md)
- **Government policy is active but split across many agencies, and councils are a practical pressure point.** Health, Justice, Police, Customs, Internal Affairs, and councils each hold different levers; 2023 law changes made it easier for communities to influence local alcohol decisions, and a new online-casino licensing regime starts phasing in from December 2026. A small volunteer team can responsibly work on navigation, evidence-packaging, and civic participation — not clinical care, enforcement, or regulation. (confidence: Medium) — [policy landscape finding](../research/findings/other/nz-addiction-policy-landscape.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The methamphetamine burden number is the shakiest of the three.** The government's $1.5 billion figure has no published breakdown, estimates range from $0.9 billion to an implied ~$2 billion depending on method, and it moves whenever wastewater volumes move.
- **Is the meth surge permanent or a passing supply glut?** Two elevated years isn't enough to tell; full-year 2026 and 2027 data would settle it. Notably, cheaper meth and a global oversupply are plausible drivers — meaning the surge could partly reverse on its own.
- **Treatment capacity looks like it's going the wrong way, but the data are blunt.** Specialist addiction services saw about 5,000 *fewer* people in 2023/24 than five years earlier, and declined referrals nearly doubled — yet there is no public methamphetamine-specific series on demand, wait times, or outcomes to line up against the surge.
- **The best-evidenced fixes are untested here.** There is no published New Zealand outcome data on contingency management for meth, on whether the 2023 local alcohol policy reforms reduced harm, or on whether online-casino licensing will reduce gambling harm rather than displace it.
- **Every finding relies solely on public documents.** No lived experience, Māori or Pacific provider, clinical, council, or enforcement voices have been heard yet — all five findings flag this as needed before any prioritisation.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language "where to get help" navigator** across alcohol, drug, and gambling support, since services and directories are scattered across at least six agencies and websites — helps: people seeking help and frontline community workers. Effort: Medium. Supported by [policy landscape finding](../research/findings/other/nz-addiction-policy-landscape.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful boundaries (no clinical or crisis advice; route high-risk cases to official services) and a maintenance plan so it doesn't go stale.
- **A council-participation toolkit** — plain explainers, deadline trackers, and citation-backed evidence packs for local alcohol policies and pokie venue policies, where 2023 law changes gave communities more say — helps: residents, community groups, and councillors. Effort: Medium. Supported by [interventions finding](../research/findings/other/addiction-harm-interventions-nz.md) and [policy landscape finding](../research/findings/other/nz-addiction-policy-landscape.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: identifying which councils have live policy reviews, and staying strictly on process guidance rather than advocacy.
- **A public harm-data explainer or dashboard** that presents the alcohol, meth, and gambling burden numbers with their caveats — countering the common mistake of adding incompatible totals, and tracking the wastewater series as new quarters land — helps: journalists, funders, advocates, and officials. Effort: Medium. Supported by [economic burden finding](../research/findings/other/alcohol-meth-gambling-economic-burden.md) and [methamphetamine data finding](../research/findings/other/nz-methamphetamine-public-data.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: agreement on honest presentation of uncertainty, and refresh discipline as sources update.
- **A watching brief on the online-casino rollout** — defining and tracking public indicators from December 2026 to show whether the new licensing regime reduces gambling harm or pushes gamblers to unlicensed sites — helps: gambling-harm services and policy watchers. Effort: Small. Supported by [interventions finding](../research/findings/other/addiction-harm-interventions-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: the regime to publish usable data, and patience — effects won't be visible until 2027.
