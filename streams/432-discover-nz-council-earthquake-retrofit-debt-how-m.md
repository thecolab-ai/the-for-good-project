# Council earthquake retrofit costs are visible in pieces, not as one national bill

## The problem, in plain language

Councils own public buildings, water systems, roads, venues and community facilities that may need earthquake strengthening or other seismic risk work. The public can see some projects and some risky assets, but not one reliable national number for what councils still owe, what is already funded, and what would happen if a damaging earthquake arrived first.

## What we've learned so far

- **There is no reliable public national total for remaining council seismic retrofit liability.** Public sources show individual assets and projects, but not a complete all-council cost total. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/council-seismic-remediation-exposure.md), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-retrofit-balance-sheet-visibility.md)
- **The national earthquake-prone building register is useful, but it is not an ownership or cost register.** It shows buildings territorial authorities have issued notices for, not whether those buildings are council-owned or how much remediation remains. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/council-seismic-remediation-exposure.md)
- **Seismic costs are visible mostly as project and risk disclosures, not as a clean balance-sheet liability.** Stats NZ and council financial data show debt, assets and broad provisions, but not the seismic share of those lines. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-retrofit-balance-sheet-visibility.md)
- **The legal rules are moving.** The current earthquake-prone building system still applies, but a bill before Parliament would narrow mandatory remediation to higher-risk building types in medium and high seismic zones if it passes in the form researched. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/epb-bill-council-asset-scope.md)
- **Councils disclose seismic risk unevenly.** Hutt publishes strong asset-level information, Wellington is strong on major project delivery updates, and other sampled councils are more patchy or less current. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-risk-transparency-delivery.md)
- **Seismic work competes inside larger capital programmes dominated by water, stormwater, transport and renewals.** It is usually a named project or subcomponent, not a standard budget category that can be compared neatly with climate adaptation. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/ltp-capex-competition.md)
- **If a major earthquake hits before retrofits are done, the first problem is service continuity and cash pressure, not a tidy rebuild plan.** Councils would face emergency response, closures, infrastructure repairs, insurance gaps, borrowing room and possible Crown cost-sharing decisions. (confidence: Medium) — [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/earthquake-before-retrofits.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- The total remaining national cost is still unknown. The evidence supports "not publicly comparable yet", not a defensible all-council dollar figure.
- Some evidence uses "earthquake-prone" as a legal Building Act category, while some council asset pages use it more broadly for seismic risk. That matters for assets such as wharves and other infrastructure that may not fit the same legal pathway.
- The proposed law change could remove some buildings from mandatory remediation, bring some places such as coastal Otago into medium-zone scope, or change mitigation tiers. Asset-level conclusions may change once the Act, regulations and methodology are final.
- Costs are often bundled with heritage restoration, fire, accessibility, asbestos, roof, HVAC, public-realm work or general renewal. A named project budget can overstate or understate the true seismic-only cost.
- The strongest public comparisons are still sample-based. There may be stronger internal council registers, insurance schedules, project ledgers or engineering assessments that are not publicly exposed.
- A post-earthquake financial ranking is especially uncertain because it would depend on the event, damage pattern, insurance terms, deductibles, council borrowing room and Cabinet recovery decisions.

## What we could do about it

- **Build a small public register of council-owned seismic-risk assets.** Helps: funders, residents, journalists and council officers see which public assets are visible, what status is known, and where cost is missing. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/council-seismic-remediation-exposure.md) (confidence: Medium), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-risk-transparency-delivery.md) (confidence: Medium), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-retrofit-balance-sheet-visibility.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a cautious standard table that separates ownership, legal status, risk rating, budget, work stage and unknown fields.
- **Create a plain-language budget comparison for seismic work, water, stormwater, transport and renewals.** Helps: community groups and decision-makers understand what seismic work is competing with in long-term plans. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/ltp-capex-competition.md) (confidence: Medium), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-retrofit-balance-sheet-visibility.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: conservative coding rules, because councils do not label mixed projects consistently.
- **Draft a pre-earthquake service and finance risk briefing for selected councils.** Helps: emergency planners, funders and local advocates see which public services and financial buffers may be stressed if a damaging event happens before remediation is complete. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/earthquake-before-retrofits.md) (confidence: Medium), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/seismic-risk-transparency-delivery.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: clear warnings that it is a scenario based only on public documents, not a loss model.
- **Track how the earthquake-prone building bill changes council-owned asset obligations.** Helps: councils and community groups distinguish mandatory remediation from optional risk reduction as the law changes. Effort: Medium. Supported by [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/epb-bill-council-asset-scope.md) (confidence: Medium), [finding](../research/findings/civic-transparency/council-seismic-remediation-exposure.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: the final Act, regulations and methodology, plus public details on what each building is made of and how many storeys it has.
