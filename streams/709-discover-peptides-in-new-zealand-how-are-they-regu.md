# Peptides in New Zealand: regulation, grey-market supply, and harm signals

## The problem, in plain language

Peptides are being sold and used in New Zealand for weight loss, recovery, body image, anti-ageing and other promised benefits, often through online sellers, gyms, social media, and some private health channels. Some products are approved prescription medicines, but many grey-market products are not quality-checked, not approved, and may be imported or sold in ways that normal health safeguards do not reach. This matters because people can be harmed before any regulator, clinician, pharmacy, sport body, or drug-checking service sees what they used.

## What we've learned so far

- **The legal picture still turns on whether a specific peptide is classified as a prescription medicine.** Before classification, Medsafe's own submission says intercepted parcels may be released with a warning letter because it has no seizure power; after classification, import, possession or supply without authority can be unlawful. (confidence: Medium) - [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md), [classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classification-status.md)
- **Medsafe has tried to move from naming one substance at a time to covering peptide groups, but the stream evidence conflicts on whether that group move has legally taken effect.** One finding says the June 2025 group proposal was still deferred and ungazetted; two others rely on Medsafe/news statements saying many peptides became prescription medicines in December 2025. (confidence: Medium) - [classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classification-status.md), [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md), [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md)
- **The grey-market supply pattern is mainly personal parcels, online sellers, gyms, social media, and informal networks, not a clearly documented licensed-pharmacy supply chain.** "For research purposes only" labels do not make human-use sales lawful, but they are still used as cover by sellers. (confidence: Medium) - [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md), [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md)
- **New Zealand has no peptide-specific clinical oversight regime.** Doctors, compounding pharmacists and cosmetic or performance-related prescribing are governed by general professional standards; gyms, influencers and non-clinical sellers sit largely outside those professional systems. (confidence: Medium) - [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md)
- **The harm evidence is real, but NZ-specific harm data is mostly missing.** International studies and Australian regulator statements document contamination, mislabelling and serious adverse events; in New Zealand, public poison-centre data, CARM searches and drug-checking services do not yet provide a clear substance-level picture for these grey-market peptides. (confidence: High) - [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md)
- **Sports integrity data shows a narrow but useful signal.** Peptides and related substances are prohibited in sport, and the NZ cases found in the evidence all began with Customs parcel interceptions rather than positive drug tests, often involving injury recovery, gym use or recreational-level sport rather than elite competition. (confidence: Medium) - [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md)
- **Overseas examples offer levers, not a full copy-paste answer.** Australia shows a possible joint compliance-priority model; the United States mainly governs compounding pharmacies; the United Kingdom shows larger enforcement capacity against illegal medicines. None directly solves New Zealand's specific border gap for unscheduled peptides. (confidence: Medium) - [overseas lessons finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-regulation-au-us-uk-lessons-for-nz.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The current legal status of many peptide groups is unresolved across the findings.** The most detailed classification finding could not find a Gazette notice or later committee record showing the June 2025 group classification had taken effect, while the border and supply findings cite Medsafe statements and news reporting that many peptides have been prescription medicines since December 2025. This affects what can be seized, what remains unscheduled, and how strong any policy recommendation can be.
- **Some named substances remain uncertain or single-sourced.** Growth-hormone secretagogues are explicitly contested in the classification finding, and TB-500's exact NZ classification date rests on one primary Medsafe source rather than a located Gazette notice or committee minute.
- **The size of the market is not known.** Medsafe has snapshot counts of intercepted parcels, but neither Customs nor Medsafe could be shown to measure the number of parcels that get through, the number of users, or how much domestic resale occurs.
- **The size of the harm burden in New Zealand is not known.** The National Poisons Centre may hold custom data, CARM may need a formal query, and drug-checking services say they cannot currently test peptides; none of these gives a public count of NZ peptide harms.
- **The role of legitimate clinical or compounding pathways is still blurry.** The mechanism exists in principle under general medicines rules, but the evidence did not show how often NZ clinics or compounding pharmacies are actually supplying wellness or anti-ageing peptides.
- **The link between unfunded GLP-1 medicines and grey-market substitution is plausible but thin.** The evidence includes NZ testimony about cost-driven retatrutide use, but not a dataset showing how common that pattern is.

## What we could do about it

- **Create a plain-language public briefing on what is known, what is uncertain, and where people can get safe help without giving usage advice.** Helps: community health workers, gyms, parents, youth services, and funders. Effort: Small. Supported by [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High), [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md) (confidence: Medium), and [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful wording that avoids medical advice and clearly flags the unresolved classification question.
- **Prepare a small official-data request pack for Customs, Medsafe, CARM and the National Poisons Centre.** Helps: researchers, advocates, regulators, and journalists who need a clearer NZ evidence base. Effort: Medium. Supported by [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) (confidence: Medium) and [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High). Would need: a human-approved request process and agreement on which substance names and date ranges matter most.
- **Draft a neutral policy-options memo on enforceable levers for New Zealand.** Helps: policy teams, public-health advocates, and MPs' offices comparing realistic options. Effort: Medium. Supported by [overseas lessons finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-regulation-au-us-uk-lessons-for-nz.md) (confidence: Medium), [classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classification-status.md) (confidence: Medium), and [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: the current legal-status contradiction resolved or explicitly treated as the memo's first caveat.
- **Explore a gym and recreational-sport outreach pathway about risks, anti-doping consequences, and the limits of testing.** Helps: recreational athletes, coaches, gym communities, and harm-reduction services. Effort: Medium. Supported by [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md) (confidence: Medium), [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High), and [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: partner appetite from sport integrity, drug-checking or harm-reduction groups, and strict avoidance of sourcing or dosing guidance.
