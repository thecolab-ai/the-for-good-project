# Peptides in New Zealand: regulation, grey-market supply, and harm signals

## The problem, in plain language

Peptides are being sold and used in New Zealand for weight loss, recovery, body image, anti-ageing and other promised benefits, often through online sellers, gyms, social media, and some private health channels. Many of the peptide groups the research focused on are now prescription medicines, but people can still buy grey-market products whose contents, dose, sterility and legal status may be unclear. This matters because harm can happen before a clinician, regulator, pharmacy, sports body, poison centre or drug-checking service sees what was used.

## What we've learned so far

- **A wide list of peptide groups is now a prescription medicine in New Zealand — and an apparent disagreement between two of our findings is resolved.** One finding, reading Medsafe's committee papers and Medsafe's own (out-of-date) notices page, concluded the June 2025 reform was still stuck in committee and unenacted as of April 2026. A second, later finding found the actual 19 December 2025 Gazette notice and Medsafe's Classification Database (updated March 2026), which show the peptide groups and named peptides were declared prescription medicines. The later, primary-source finding supersedes the earlier one for the listed entries: the reform is in force, not deferred. (confidence: High for the in-force status; the "still deferred" reading was Medium and rested on a stale Medsafe page) - [in-force classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classifications-in-force.md), [classification-status finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classification-status.md)
- **The earlier border gap was real, and it still matters for anything outside the listed entries.** Before a peptide was scheduled, Medsafe's own submission said intercepted parcels could be released with a high-risk letter because Medsafe had no seizure power; scheduling changes that footing for listed prescription entries, but new look-alike compounds can restart the same gap until they too are captured. (confidence: Medium) - [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md)
- **The grey-market supply pattern is mainly personal parcels, online sellers, gyms, social media and informal networks.** "For research purposes only" labels do not make human-use sales lawful, but sellers still use them; the evidence does not show a licensed-pharmacy supply chain as the main route. (confidence: Medium) - [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md), [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md)
- **New Zealand has no peptide-specific clinical oversight regime.** Doctors, compounding pharmacists and cosmetic or performance-related prescribing are governed by general professional standards; gyms, influencers and non-clinical sellers sit largely outside those professional systems. (confidence: Medium) - [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md)
- **The harm evidence is strong internationally, but weak inside New Zealand's public data systems.** International studies and Australian regulator statements document contamination, mislabelling and serious adverse events; in New Zealand, poison-centre data, CARM searches and drug-checking services do not yet provide a clear substance-level picture for these grey-market peptides. (confidence: High) - [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md)
- **Sports integrity data shows a narrow but useful signal.** Peptides and related substances are prohibited in sport, and the NZ cases found in the evidence all began with Customs parcel interceptions rather than positive drug tests, often involving injury recovery, gym use or recreational-level sport rather than elite competition. (confidence: Medium) - [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md)
- **Overseas examples offer levers, not a ready-made answer.** Australia shows a possible joint compliance-priority model; the United States mainly regulates compounding pharmacies; the United Kingdom shows larger enforcement capacity against illegal medicines. Which parts New Zealand could copy depends on powers and resources not fully verified here. (confidence: Medium) - [overseas lessons finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-regulation-au-us-uk-lessons-for-nz.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **The two classification findings reached opposite conclusions, and the disagreement is only resolved because one used a later, better source.** The in-force reading rests on the December 2025 Gazette notice and Medsafe's live database; the "still deferred" reading rested on Medsafe's own notices page, which the later finding shows was months out of date. The resolution is sound, but it is a reminder that the fast-moving official record here can mislead anyone checking the wrong page.
- **A few specific compounds are still not cleanly pinned down.** The growth-hormone-secretagogue class (for example CJC-1295, ipamorelin, tesamorelin) was flagged as genuinely contested — one finding could find no primary source confirming their status either way (confidence: Low), and the later database check only partly closes this. Product-specific analogues and borderline substances may still turn on Medsafe interpretation.
- **The size of the market is not known.** Medsafe has snapshot counts of intercepted parcels, but neither Customs nor Medsafe could be shown to measure the number of parcels that get through, the number of users, or how much domestic resale occurs.
- **The size of the harm burden in New Zealand is not known.** The National Poisons Centre may hold custom data, CARM may need a formal query, and drug-checking services say they cannot currently test peptides; none of these gives a public count of NZ peptide harms.
- **The role of legitimate clinical or compounding pathways is still blurry.** The mechanism exists in principle under general medicines rules, but the evidence did not show how often NZ clinics or compounding pharmacies are actually supplying wellness or anti-ageing peptides.
- **The link between unfunded GLP-1 medicines and grey-market substitution is plausible but thin.** The evidence includes NZ testimony about cost-driven retatrutide use, but not a dataset showing how common that pattern is.
- **Several useful facts are single-sourced.** The "22 websites taken down" claim, the statement that Customs cannot say how many parcels get through, and the exact wording of the high-risk letter all need firmer evidence before they carry policy weight.

## What we could do about it

- **Create a plain-language public briefing on what is known, what is uncertain, and where people can get safe help without giving usage advice.** Helps: community health workers, gyms, parents, youth services, and funders. Effort: Small. Supported by [in-force classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classifications-in-force.md) (confidence: High), [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High), [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md) (confidence: Medium), and [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: careful wording that avoids medical advice, sourcing guidance or product-by-product legal claims.
- **Prepare a small official-data request pack for Customs, Medsafe, CARM and the National Poisons Centre.** Helps: researchers, advocates, regulators, and journalists who need a clearer NZ evidence base. Effort: Medium. Supported by [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) (confidence: Medium), [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High), and [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: a human-approved request process and agreement on which substance names, date ranges and agencies matter most.
- **Draft a neutral policy-options memo on enforceable levers for New Zealand after the December 2025 classifications.** Helps: policy teams, public-health advocates, and MPs' offices comparing realistic options. Effort: Medium. Supported by [in-force classification finding](../research/findings/other/nz-peptide-classifications-in-force.md) (confidence: High), [border finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-import-border-enforcement-gap.md) (confidence: Medium), and [overseas lessons finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-regulation-au-us-uk-lessons-for-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: clear caveats about products outside the listed entries, legal powers not verified here, and resourcing.
- **Explore a gym and recreational-sport outreach pathway about risks, anti-doping consequences, and the limits of testing.** Helps: recreational athletes, coaches, gym communities, and harm-reduction services. Effort: Medium. Supported by [sport finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sports-integrity-anti-doping-nz.md) (confidence: Medium), [harm-data finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-sarm-harm-evidence-and-nz-data-gap.md) (confidence: High), and [supply finding](../research/findings/other/peptide-supply-oversight-nz.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: partner appetite from sport integrity, drug-checking or harm-reduction groups, and strict avoidance of sourcing or dosing guidance.
