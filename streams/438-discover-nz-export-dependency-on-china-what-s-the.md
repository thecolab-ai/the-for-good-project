# How exposed is New Zealand to a downturn in trade with China — and could we cope?

## The problem, in plain language

China is New Zealand's biggest export customer, but the risk is not spread evenly across the whole economy. It sits heavily in a few products and people-movement services: dairy, logs, meat, fruit, international students, and visitors. This research round asks how large that exposure is, what has changed over the past decade, and whether other markets could realistically soften a sudden fall in Chinese demand.

## What we've learned so far

- **New Zealand is exposed, but unevenly.** China takes about a quarter of goods exports overall, with much higher exposure in logs/wood, dairy, fruit, and meat/offal; China is also a major student source and visitor-spend market (confidence: Medium) — [exposure baseline](../research/findings/other/china-export-exposure-baseline.md). Among selected peers, New Zealand is high-exposure but below Australia and Chile, and a full OECD rank was not verified (confidence: Medium) — [peer comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-china-exposure-oecd-peer-comparison.md).
- **The peak has already partly unwound.** China's goods share rose from 17.6% in 2015 to 31.6% in 2021, then fell back to about 24-25%; the rise was mostly four sectors, while services were disrupted by COVID and are rebuilding. The same rise-and-partial-unwind shape now also shows up in official education records: Ministry of Education levy data puts China's share of fee-paying students at roughly 27% in 2015, a COVID-era peak near 41% in 2021, and about 30% in 2025 (confidence: Medium) — [decade movement](../research/findings/other/china-share-decade-movement.md).
- **The hardest goods to replace are logs, meat by-products, and dairy powder.** Sheepmeat has the clearest spare European quota headroom; EU/UK growth in other exposed products is mostly extra trade, not redirected China-bound volume, and meat redirection looks much smaller in kilos than in dollars (confidence: Medium) — [goods substitutability](../research/findings/other/china-export-substitutability-goods.md), [redirection versus additive growth](../research/findings/other/eu-uk-export-growth-redirection-vs-additive.md).
- **Students and visitors can be partly replaced, but slowly and unevenly.** Education can absorb a small China shock only over time, and China-origin students appear to generate more tuition revenue per enrolment than several smaller growth markets (confidence: Medium) — [student shock absorption](../research/findings/other/international-student-china-shock-absorption.md), [education revenue by country](../research/findings/other/education-export-revenue-per-student-by-country.md). Tourism can probably absorb a small spend shock across the United States, Europe, Australia, and India, but not a large fast one (confidence: Medium) — [tourism shock absorption](../research/findings/other/china-tourism-shock-absorption.md).
- **Policy is mixed.** Goods-market diversification is happening in some numbers, but partly because China demand weakened; education has not clearly diversified; tourism policy is actively trying to regrow China demand (confidence: Medium) — [diversification efforts and outcomes](../research/findings/other/china-diversification-efforts-outcomes.md).
- **A shock would be material, but the exact damage is still scenario-based.** A 25% China-facing demand fall puts roughly 0.6-1.0% of GDP at risk in the first-round stress test, and a 50% fall roughly 1.2-2.0%; real-world evidence says replacement rates should be treated sector by sector, not as one national number (confidence: Low for the stress test; Medium for sector-specific replacement evidence) — [shock transmission](../research/findings/other/china-demand-shock-transmission.md), [recapture evidence](../research/findings/other/china-shock-recapture-rate-evidence.md).

## What we're not sure about yet

- The headline direction is clear, but **precision is still weak**: GDP, jobs, and public-revenue impacts depend on how much lost demand is replaced, at what price, and how quickly. The follow-up evidence improves this by showing replacement is sector-specific, but it still does not give a clean official forecast.
- **Regional exposure is still mostly a proxy.** Education and tourism exposure can be placed more confidently in Auckland, Queenstown-Lakes, Christchurch, Mackenzie, and other visitor/student centres, but public data does not directly map China-bound goods by production region.
- **Education revenue by country is estimated, not directly published.** This is now confirmed as a genuine gap in what government publishes, checked across two annual editions of the official workbook — it has country headcounts and fee rates by provider type, but never tuition revenue crossed by country — so the China-versus-other-market revenue gap remains a bounded estimate, and only an official request to the Ministry of Education could turn it into a published figure.
- **Goods diversification is real in some numbers but easy to overstate.** Europe and UK export growth is largely additive; for meat, dollar growth can look like major redirection while physical volumes show a much smaller shift.
- **There is a live policy tension.** The exposure problem asks how to reduce reliance on China, but current tourism policy, and parts of education strategy, are deliberately trying to grow China-linked demand again. The evidence describes that tension; it does not decide whether it is good or bad.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language China exposure explainer and live indicator pack** — helps: funders, councils, community organisations, and journalists. Effort: Medium. Supported by [exposure baseline](../research/findings/other/china-export-exposure-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium), [decade movement](../research/findings/other/china-share-decade-movement.md) (confidence: Medium), and [peer comparison](../research/findings/other/nz-china-exposure-oecd-peer-comparison.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: regular data refreshes.
- **A sector-by-sector "what could actually be replaced" briefing** — helps: policy staff, exporters, education providers, tourism bodies, and regional economic teams. Effort: Medium. Supported by [goods substitutability](../research/findings/other/china-export-substitutability-goods.md) (confidence: Medium), [redirection versus additive growth](../research/findings/other/eu-uk-export-growth-redirection-vs-additive.md) (confidence: Medium), [recapture evidence](../research/findings/other/china-shock-recapture-rate-evidence.md) (confidence: Medium), [student shock absorption](../research/findings/other/international-student-china-shock-absorption.md) (confidence: Medium), and [tourism shock absorption](../research/findings/other/china-tourism-shock-absorption.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: clear separation between fast replacement, slow replacement, lower-price replacement, and no realistic replacement.
- **Regional preparedness notes for exposed places** — helps: councils and local charities planning for export, visitor, and student-income shocks. Effort: Large. Supported by [shock transmission](../research/findings/other/china-demand-shock-transmission.md) (confidence: Low), [exposure baseline](../research/findings/other/china-export-exposure-baseline.md) (confidence: Medium), and [tourism shock absorption](../research/findings/other/china-tourism-shock-absorption.md) (confidence: Medium). Would need: strong caveats because goods-region exposure is not directly measured.
