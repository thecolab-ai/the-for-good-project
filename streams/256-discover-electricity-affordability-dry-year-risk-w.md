# Why New Zealand power bills keep climbing — and who gets hurt

## The problem, in plain language

New Zealand households have faced two years of sharp power-price rises, and because our electricity system depends heavily on hydro lakes, a dry year can send wholesale prices soaring and keep the pressure on. This stream set out to build a plain-English, evidence-based picture of what actually drives power prices, how a dry year turns into a bigger bill, and who carries the heaviest burden. The research round is complete and the stream is now **waiting on a human steward to decide its direction**.

## What we've learned so far

- **The "prices keep climbing" story is really a last-two-years story.** Over 10 years, power prices only slightly outpaced inflation nationally (about +2% in real terms) — but from early 2024 to early 2026 they jumped about 13% above inflation, with big regional differences, and fixed daily charges rose much faster than per-unit rates. Network (lines) costs, about 30% of a bill, are expected to keep rising until at least 2030. (confidence: Medium) — [price-trend finding](../research/findings/other/residential-electricity-real-price-trends.md)
- **A dry year is huge at the wholesale level — roughly a 20-fold price swing.** In the dry August of 2024 the wholesale price averaged around 48 cents per unit; three months later, after rain, it was about 2.4 cents. But households don't pay that price directly: retailers buy insurance-like contracts, so the spike reaches home bills later and smaller, as next year's price rise. (confidence: High for the wholesale swing; Medium for how much reaches households) — [dry-year finding](../research/findings/other/dry-year-spot-price-spikes-quantified.md)
- **Four companies generate about nine-tenths of NZ's electricity and also sell it at retail.** In the tense second half of 2024 their combined "energy margin" averaged $76 million a week — a large gross figure, but the regulator is clear it is not the same as profit (two of the four actually reported net losses that half-year). The regulator itself found some evidence, for 2019–2021, that wholesale prices may not have been set in a fully competitive environment. (confidence: Medium) — [market-concentration finding](../research/findings/other/gentailer-concentration-margins.md)
- **Lots of new solar and wind is genuinely being built, and it lowers prices when the sun shines or wind blows — but it does not fix the dry-year problem.** That problem is about storing energy for a whole season (officials sized the gap at 3–5 terawatt-hours), while the batteries being built store about two hours' worth. The one project sized for the seasonal gap was cancelled in 2023; a private revival is at least a decade away. (confidence: Medium) — [new-generation finding](../research/findings/other/renewable-pipeline-hydro-marginal-pricing.md)
- **Rooftop solar is booming but mostly reaches better-off homeowners.** Nearly 87,000 properties now have solar, and for an owner-occupier with the right roof and daytime power use it can pay for itself in 7–10 years. But renters — a third of households, and three to four times more likely to be in energy hardship — generally can't get it: they don't control the roof and often can't access the finance. (confidence: Medium) — [solar-economics finding](../research/findings/other/nz-solar-deployment-economics.md)
- **The burden falls hardest on the poorest.** The lowest-income tenth of households spends about 7.7% of income on electricity versus 1.3% for the highest tenth. Prepay customers bear the sharpest edge: about 27,000 prepay disconnections a month in 2025, touching roughly a third of all prepay customers, and government hardship grants for power and gas ran at about 8,200 payments in a single quarter. (confidence: Medium) — [energy-hardship finding](../research/findings/other/energy-hardship-electricity-burden.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **How much of a wholesale spike actually lands on household bills, and when.** Two findings independently flag this as the key gap: the [dry-year finding](../research/findings/other/dry-year-spot-price-spikes-quantified.md) could only cite the regulator's qualitative "lagged and dampened" description, and the [price-trend finding](../research/findings/other/residential-electricity-real-price-trends.md) could not build any price history by named retailer (that data stopped being published in 2014). Without it, the chain from "dry year" to "your bill" stays a mechanism, not a number.
- **An apparent tension worth keeping in view:** the stream's framing says prices "keep climbing", but the 10-year inflation-adjusted trend is close to flat — the pain is concentrated in 2024–2026 and in fixed daily charges. The hardship numbers (7.7% of income) also rest on 2019 survey data, so today's burden after the recent surge is not actually measured anywhere public.
- **Single-sourced, load-bearing details:** the company-by-company margin split comes from a third-party copy of a regulator dashboard that stopped updating ([market-concentration finding](../research/findings/other/gentailer-concentration-margins.md)); the "current dry-year risk is low" read relies on model output about six months old ([dry-year finding](../research/findings/other/dry-year-spot-price-spikes-quantified.md)).
- **Whether the renewables build-out lowers average prices overall** depends on demand growing too (data centres, electrification) — no finding modelled the net balance. And whether wind actually blows during the weather patterns that cause dry years is an open scientific question ([new-generation finding](../research/findings/other/renewable-pipeline-hydro-marginal-pricing.md)).
- **No public data breaks down electricity burden by neighbourhood deprivation**, only by income band ([energy-hardship finding](../research/findings/other/energy-hardship-electricity-burden.md)).

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language "why did my power bill go up?" explainer** — a short public guide translating this stream's evidence (fixed vs per-unit charges, lines costs, dry years, who profits) for households and budgeting services. Helps: any household trying to understand its bill; frontline money advisers. Effort: Small. Supported by the [price-trend](../research/findings/other/residential-electricity-real-price-trends.md) (Medium), [dry-year](../research/findings/other/dry-year-spot-price-spikes-quantified.md) (High/Medium) and [market-concentration](../research/findings/other/gentailer-concentration-margins.md) (Medium) findings. Would need: careful wording checked by someone with electricity-market expertise, which the dry-year finding itself asks for.
- **A regional power-price and hardship tracker** — a small tool that regularly pulls the regulator's public datasets (regional prices, disconnections) to show, by region, how fixed and per-unit charges and prepay disconnections are moving. Helps: community organisations and local media spotting where pressure is building. Effort: Medium. Supported by the [price-trend](../research/findings/other/residential-electricity-real-price-trends.md) (Medium) and [energy-hardship](../research/findings/other/energy-hardship-electricity-burden.md) (Medium) findings. Would need: the regulator's datasets to stay public and monthly; the disconnections dashboard currently excludes the smallest retailers.
- **A prepay-hardship evidence brief** — assemble the disconnection, hardship-grant and burden evidence into a briefing for budgeting services and consumer advocates, including the specific data gaps (burden by deprivation, repeat disconnections) the findings say officials could fill. Helps: services supporting households at the sharpest edge; advocates asking regulators for better data. Effort: Small. Supported by the [energy-hardship finding](../research/findings/other/energy-hardship-electricity-burden.md) (Medium). Would need: input from people with frontline experience — the finding is explicit that aggregate data can't say whether short prepay disconnections are low-harm budgeting or hidden hardship.
- **A community/shared-solar options paper for renters and low-income households** — map what a shared-solar model could look like in NZ, building on the equity gap and the existing government community-energy funds the research identified. Helps: renters and others locked out of rooftop solar. Effort: Medium–Large. Supported by the [solar-economics finding](../research/findings/other/nz-solar-deployment-economics.md) (Medium). Would need: a workable legal/billing model for tenants to receive credits — flagged in the finding as an unanswered question.
