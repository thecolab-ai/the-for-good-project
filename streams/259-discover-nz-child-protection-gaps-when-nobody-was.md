# Children at risk are still falling through the gaps in NZ's child-protection system

## The problem, in plain language

Every so often New Zealand learns of children who were harmed while living "right under our noses" — known to schools, doctors, or police, yet helped by no one. Official reviews say this is not bad luck but a pattern: the agencies each hold a piece of the picture, nobody holds the whole child, and the reforms meant to fix it are moving slowly. This round of research maps how the system is supposed to work, what the official record says about where it fails, and what has already been recommended — using only public, aggregate sources, never any child's case.

*The research round is complete and the stream is waiting on its human steward to set direction. This overview was refreshed on 8 July 2026 after the legal evidence behind one finding was re-verified against the law itself; no conclusion changed.*

## What we've learned so far

- **The system's design is clear.** Anyone can report a worry about a child to Oranga Tamariki; each report is triaged to a response within 24 hours (critical), 48 hours (very urgent), or 10 working days (urgent), then assessed, taken to a family decision-making conference, or closed. (confidence: High) — [how the pathway works](../research/findings/child-welfare/nz-child-protection-pathway.md)
- **Reports are at record levels, but part of the rise is bookkeeping.** 95,422 reports of concern came in during 2024 — about 35% more than 2023. Oranga Tamariki itself says at least about a quarter of that jump came from a change in how reports are recorded, not necessarily more harm. (confidence: High on the numbers, Medium on what drove the rise) — [the numbers](../research/findings/child-welfare/roc-numbers-timeframe-outcomes.md)
- **The most urgent reports are increasingly answered late.** The target is to respond to critical and very-urgent reports on time at least 95% of the time; actual performance has slid from 89% to 80% over the past two years, and Oranga Tamariki marks itself "off-track". Responses to Māori children are less timely than for non-Māori in every region. (confidence: High) — [the numbers](../research/findings/child-welfare/roc-numbers-timeframe-outcomes.md), [what oversight bodies found](../research/findings/child-welfare/child-protection-oversight-gaps.md)
- **Around 4 in 10 reports lead to no further action — and no one can yet say whether that's right.** The rate is highest for reports made by health professionals and NGOs. Officially, it is unresolved whether this reflects sound triage or missed risk. (confidence: High on the figures; the meaning is expressly unsettled) — [the numbers](../research/findings/child-welfare/roc-numbers-timeframe-outcomes.md)
- **Every independent watchdog is critical, each from a different angle.** The Independent Children's Monitor says minimum care standards still aren't being met after five years of reporting; the Ombudsman could not assure that Oranga Tamariki consistently follows good administrative practice; the Auditor-General found its 2024/25 contracting of front-line services was mostly not open, transparent, or competitive. (confidence: High) — [what oversight bodies found](../research/findings/child-welfare/child-protection-oversight-gaps.md)
- **The "nobody's job" gap is between agencies, and it isn't a legal problem.** The law itself — now checked directly against the Oranga Tamariki Act as well as the Privacy Commissioner's guidance — already allows agencies to share child-safety information proactively, even on their own initiative. In practice they often don't, and they still mostly review failures in isolation from each other. A new inter-agency hub exists but covers only children whose sole parent is imprisoned, and local multi-agency forums don't exist in every community. (confidence: High that the gap persists; Medium on coverage; Low on whether the new fixes work) — [cross-agency handoffs](../research/findings/child-welfare/nz-child-protection-handoffs.md)
- **Everything above was already diagnosed and recommendations accepted — but little is complete.** The 2022 Poutasi review's 14 recommendations were all accepted by Cabinet in September 2025, yet by February 2026 the Monitor graded only two complete, the flagship mandatory-reporting reform still has no legislation date, and the Monitor concluded children are "still no more likely to be seen" — recording 24 children killed by a carer between December 2021 and June 2025. (confidence: High) — [recommendation implementation status](../research/findings/child-welfare/poutasi-recommendations-implementation-status.md), [stream framing](../research/findings/child-welfare/nz-child-protection-gaps-framing.md)

## What we're not sure about yet

- **Whether more reports means more harm.** The estimate that at least ~24% of the 2024 rise was a recording change comes only from Oranga Tamariki's own analysis, with no independent check — it is the weakest link in the "surging demand" story.
- **Whether "no further action" decisions are safe.** The aggregate data cannot distinguish correct triage from missed risk; the sources themselves say so. Judging this needs people with clinical, legal, or lived-experience authority — not more aggregate statistics.
- **What happens to children after a report.** Longer-term recovery and wellbeing outcomes are not published; they sit in restricted government microdata. Timeliness for the largest, 10-working-day "urgent" tier is also not published.
- **Whether the new fixes change anything.** The inter-agency hub, mandatory training, and Privacy Commissioner sessions are so far measured only by activity counts (reports handled, workers trained), not by whether children are seen sooner or kept safer.
- **How patchy local coverage really is.** The claim that multi-agency forums don't operate in every community rests on the Monitor's evidence alone; no public national coverage map exists.
- Across all findings, "the system is failing" remains a judgement the evidence informs but does not settle — the project's rules require a human with domain authority to weigh it before it is treated as settled.

## What we could do about it

- **A plain-language public explainer of the reporting pathway and its documented gaps**, so people who work near at-risk children understand what happens after they report and where the system's known weak points are — helps: charity and community workers, teachers, health staff. Effort: Small. Supported by [how the pathway works](../research/findings/child-welfare/nz-child-protection-pathway.md) (High) and [what oversight bodies found](../research/findings/child-welfare/child-protection-oversight-gaps.md) (High). Would need: careful wording so it informs without discouraging reporting, and review by someone with child-protection practice experience.
- **An independent public tracker of the 14 Poutasi recommendations**, showing side-by-side what the government counts as done and what the Monitor grades as achieved, updated as each new official report lands — helps: journalists, advocates, funders holding the reform to account. Effort: Medium. Supported by [recommendation implementation status](../research/findings/child-welfare/poutasi-recommendations-implementation-status.md) (High). Would need: a sustainable update rhythm tied to official publications, and strict sourcing so it never outruns the record.
- **A small public dashboard of the aggregate system numbers** — report volumes, on-time response rates, no-further-action rates — refreshed from Oranga Tamariki's quarterly reports, with the caveats (recording change, unpublished tiers) carried visibly — helps: anyone needing the current state of the system without reading PDFs. Effort: Medium. Supported by [the numbers](../research/findings/child-welfare/roc-numbers-timeframe-outcomes.md) (High). Would need: the caveats to travel with every figure, and quarterly upkeep.
- **A targeted follow-up research round on the documented data gaps** — e.g. official-information requests for the professional-vs-public reporting split and urgent-tier timeliness, and a public map of which communities have a multi-agency forum — helps: this stream and anyone else working the problem. Effort: Medium. Supported by [the numbers](../research/findings/child-welfare/roc-numbers-timeframe-outcomes.md) (High) and [cross-agency handoffs](../research/findings/child-welfare/nz-child-protection-handoffs.md) (Medium). Would need: the requests to stay strictly aggregate and de-identified.
